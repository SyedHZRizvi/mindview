4.1 — Wave Properties & the Wave Equation
Transverse vs. longitudinal, amplitude, wavelength, frequency, period, \( v = f\lambda \)
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

A wave is a disturbance that transfers energy through a medium (or, for electromagnetic waves, through empty space) without the medium being permanently displaced — water ripples carry energy across a lake but the water itself stays roughly in place. Two main types appear in nature. In a transverse wave, particles of the medium oscillate perpendicular to the direction of energy propagation (a wave on a string, light, all electromagnetic waves). In a longitudinal wave, particles oscillate parallel to the direction of energy propagation (sound in air, compressions on a Slinky). Surface water waves are a combination of both.

Every periodic wave is described by four key quantities. Amplitude A is the maximum displacement from equilibrium — related to the energy carried (energy ∝ A² for most waves). Wavelength λ is the distance between two consecutive identical points (crest to crest), measured in metres. Period T is the time for one full oscillation, in seconds. Frequency f = 1/T is the number of oscillations per second, in hertz (Hz). For periodic waves these are linked by the universal wave equation \( v = f\lambda \): wave speed = frequency × wavelength.

The wave speed depends on the medium, not on the source. A piano string and a violin string vibrating at the same 440 Hz produce different wavelengths because their wave speeds are different. Light in vacuum has \( c = 3.00 \times 10^8\ \text{m/s} \); in glass it slows to about \( 2 \times 10^8\ \text{m/s} \) — slower medium → shorter wavelength at the same frequency (the basis of refraction in Snell's law).

  • Wave — disturbance carrying energy through space
  • Transverse — oscillation ⊥ to propagation (string, light)
  • Longitudinal — oscillation ∥ to propagation (sound)
  • Amplitude A — max displacement; units depend on wave (m for string, Pa for sound)
  • Wavelength λ — m, distance for one full cycle
  • Period T — s, time for one full cycle
  • Frequency f = 1/T — Hz; 1 Hz = 1 cycle/s
  • Wave speed v = fλ — m/s; depends only on medium

Worked Example — Radio wave wavelength

A CHUM 104.5 FM radio station broadcasts at 104.5 MHz. Find the wavelength of the radio wave (in air, v ≈ c = 3.00 × 10⁸ m/s).

Step 1. Convert frequency: \( f = 104.5\ \text{MHz} = 1.045 \times 10^8\ \text{Hz} \).

Step 2. Apply \( v = f\lambda \): \( \lambda = v/f = (3.00 \times 10^8)/(1.045 \times 10^8) = 2.87\ \text{m} \).

Step 3. Sanity check: FM antennas are typically a half- or quarter-wavelength, so about 1.4 m or 0.7 m — matches the size of a real car antenna.

Step 4. Compare to a 60 Hz AC power-line wave: \( \lambda = 3 \times 10^8/60 = 5 \times 10^6\ \text{m} = 5000\ \text{km} \) — vastly larger because frequency is much smaller. The product fλ is fixed by the medium.

  • Confusing wave speed with particle speed — particles oscillate locally; the wave moves forward
  • Mixing wavelength and amplitude — wavelength is spatial period, amplitude is height
  • Assuming louder = faster sound — louder = larger amplitude, same speed
  • Forgetting units in v = fλ — Hz × m = 1/s × m = m/s ✓
  • Using v = fλ for a wave on a stretched string when v changes — must use the actual v in that string

Curriculum expectation: Strand E, Expectation E2.2 — "analyse, in qualitative and quantitative terms, the properties of mechanical waves and sound (e.g., wavelength, frequency, period, amplitude, phase, speed), using the universal wave equation."

4.2 — Sound Waves & the Speed of Sound
\( v_\text{sound} = 331 + 0.6 T_C \) m/s, intensity, decibel scale, hearing range
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Sound is a longitudinal mechanical wave consisting of pressure variations propagating through a medium. In air, a vibrating source (vocal cords, loudspeaker cone, guitar string) alternately compresses and rarefies the surrounding gas, sending a chain of compressions and rarefactions outward at the speed of sound. Because sound is mechanical, it cannot travel through vacuum — astronauts on EVA communicate by radio, not by yelling. The speed of sound in air depends primarily on temperature; a useful empirical equation is \( v = 331 + 0.6\, T_C \) m/s, where \( T_C \) is the Celsius temperature. At 20 °C, sound travels at about 343 m/s.

Sound has the usual wave properties — wavelength, frequency, amplitude — plus a few unique attributes. The pitch of a sound corresponds to its frequency: higher frequency → higher pitch. Humans typically hear from about 20 Hz to 20 000 Hz (kHz); below 20 Hz is infrasound, above 20 kHz is ultrasound. Different animals hear different ranges: dogs to about 40 kHz, bats to 100 kHz (essential for echolocation). The loudness of a sound corresponds to its intensity, measured in W/m². Because our ears respond logarithmically, loudness is reported on the decibel scale: \( \beta = 10\log_{10}(I/I_0) \), where \( I_0 = 10^{-12}\ \text{W/m}^2 \) is the threshold of human hearing.

Sound speeds depend strongly on medium. In water sound travels at about 1500 m/s (4× air); in steel at 5100 m/s (15× air). This is why you can hear a train coming by putting your ear to the rail much earlier than through the air.

  • Sound — longitudinal mechanical wave, pressure variation
  • Speed of sound in air v = 331 + 0.6 T_C (m/s)
  • Audible range — 20 Hz to 20 kHz for healthy young humans
  • Infrasound — < 20 Hz (earthquakes, whale song)
  • Ultrasound — > 20 kHz (medical imaging, bat sonar)
  • Intensity I — W/m²; \( I = P/(4\pi r^2) \) for a point source
  • Sound level β = 10 log(I/I₀) — dB; +10 dB ≈ 10× the intensity

Worked Example — How far away is the thunderstorm?

You see a flash of lightning, then hear thunder 4.5 s later. Air temperature is 22 °C. Find your distance to the lightning strike.

Step 1. Speed of sound at 22 °C: \( v = 331 + 0.6(22) = 331 + 13.2 = 344.2\ \text{m/s} \).

Step 2. Light reaches you essentially instantly (\( c = 3 \times 10^8 \) m/s, so 1 km takes 3 μs).

Step 3. Distance from sound time: \( d = v \cdot t = 344.2 \times 4.5 = 1549\ \text{m} \approx 1.55\ \text{km} \).

Step 4. Rule of thumb: divide seconds by 3 for kilometres (or by 5 for miles) — 4.5/3 = 1.5 km. ✓

Step 5. Decibel sanity check: a thunderclap at 1.5 km is about 70 dB at your ear (loud conversation level); at 10 m it would be about 120 dB (painful).

  • Treating sound as a transverse wave — sound in fluids is purely longitudinal
  • Using v = 343 m/s in a hot or cold environment — apply the temperature correction
  • Confusing intensity (W/m²) with sound level (dB) — sound level is logarithmic
  • Saying "louder sound travels faster" — speed depends on medium, not amplitude
  • Forgetting that sound cannot travel in vacuum

Curriculum expectation: Strand E, Expectation E3.3 — "explain the components and characteristics of sound waves (e.g., compression, rarefaction, longitudinal motion of particles), including the relationship between intensity and amplitude."

4.3 — Interference, Resonance & Standing Waves
Constructive/destructive interference, harmonics on strings (\( f_n = nv/(2L) \)) and in pipes
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

When two waves overlap in the same medium, they superpose: the displacement at every point is the sum of the displacements each wave would produce alone. This is the principle of superposition. If two crests arrive at the same point at the same time, they add to make a bigger crest — constructive interference. If a crest meets a trough of equal amplitude, they cancel — destructive interference. After overlapping the two waves pass through each other unchanged; the interference is momentary.

A standing wave forms when a wave reflects from a boundary and interferes with itself. At specific frequencies (the resonant or natural frequencies), the interference produces a stationary pattern of nodes (points of zero displacement) and antinodes (points of maximum oscillation). On a string fixed at both ends (like a guitar string of length L), the fundamental wavelength is \( \lambda_1 = 2L \), giving fundamental frequency \( f_1 = v/(2L) \). The harmonics are integer multiples: \( f_n = nv/(2L) \) for n = 1, 2, 3, … The series f₁, 2f₁, 3f₁ … is what gives musical instruments their characteristic timbre.

For an air column open at both ends (a flute), the harmonic series is the same: \( f_n = nv/(2L) \). For an air column closed at one end (a clarinet, or a bottle), only odd harmonics exist: \( f_n = nv/(4L) \) with n = 1, 3, 5… Resonance occurs when an external driving force matches a natural frequency, producing dramatically large amplitude — the basis of musical instruments, but also of the Tacoma Narrows bridge collapse (1940).

  • Principle of superposition — total displacement = sum of individual displacements
  • Constructive interference — in-phase waves; amplitudes add
  • Destructive interference — out-of-phase waves; amplitudes subtract
  • Standing wave — stationary pattern of nodes and antinodes
  • Node — fixed point of zero displacement
  • Antinode — point of maximum oscillation
  • Harmonic — integer multiple of the fundamental frequency
  • Resonance — large response when driven at a natural frequency

Worked Example — Frequencies of a guitar string

A guitar string of length L = 0.65 m has a wave speed of 286 m/s. Find the first three harmonic frequencies.

Step 1. Use the formula for a string fixed at both ends: \( f_n = nv/(2L) \).

Step 2. Fundamental (n = 1): \( f_1 = (1)(286)/(2 \times 0.65) = 286/1.30 = 220\ \text{Hz} \) — this is musical A3, the open A string.

Step 3. Second harmonic (n = 2): \( f_2 = 2 f_1 = 440\ \text{Hz} \) — concert pitch A4, one octave up.

Step 4. Third harmonic (n = 3): \( f_3 = 3 f_1 = 660\ \text{Hz} \) — about a perfect fifth above A4.

Step 5. Visualise: at n = 1 the string has one antinode in the middle; at n = 2 it has two antinodes and one node in the middle; at n = 3 it has three antinodes and two interior nodes. The mix of these harmonics gives a guitar its rich tone.

  • Counting nodes as harmonics — n is the antinode count (string fixed both ends)
  • Using both-ends formula for a closed pipe — closed pipes use \( f_n = nv/(4L) \) with odd n only
  • Forgetting that resonance requires matching frequency, not amplitude
  • Treating interference as a permanent change — waves emerge intact
  • Adding wave speeds when waves overlap — superposition adds displacements, not speeds

Curriculum expectation: Strand E, Expectation E2.3 — "investigate, through laboratory inquiry or computer simulation, the properties of mechanical waves and sound, and use the universal wave equation to determine various characteristics of waves (e.g., the wavelength of standing waves)."

4.4 — The Doppler Effect & Applications
Source/observer motion, ultrasound imaging, sonar, weather radar
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The Doppler effect is the change in observed frequency of a wave when the source and the observer are moving relative to each other. Discovered by Christian Doppler in 1842, it explains why a passing ambulance siren drops in pitch as it goes by, why police radar can measure car speeds, and why astronomers can detect the expansion of the universe by red-shifted starlight. The fundamental cause is geometric: when the source approaches, successive wave fronts are emitted closer together (shorter wavelength → higher frequency); when it recedes, wave fronts are spread out (longer wavelength → lower frequency).

For sound waves with stationary observer and source moving toward the observer at speed \( v_s \): \( f_\text{obs} = f_\text{src}\,v/(v - v_s) \). When the source recedes, the denominator becomes \( v + v_s \). When the observer moves toward a stationary source at speed \( v_o \): \( f_\text{obs} = f_\text{src}(v + v_o)/v \). The general form is \( f_\text{obs} = f_\text{src}(v \pm v_o)/(v \mp v_s) \), with signs chosen so that motion toward increases the frequency. Note: the wave speed v depends only on the medium and is unchanged by source or observer motion.

Applications are everywhere. Medical ultrasound Doppler measures blood flow velocity by comparing emitted and reflected ultrasound frequencies. Sonar uses underwater Doppler to detect submarine motion. Weather radar uses Doppler to identify tornadoes by detecting rotating wind patterns. Speed-trap radar bounces microwaves off a car. In astronomy, red-shift of distant galaxies established that the universe is expanding — a discovery by Edwin Hubble in 1929 that revolutionised cosmology.

  • Doppler effect — shift in observed frequency due to relative motion
  • Source velocity \( v_s \) — speed of source relative to medium
  • Observer velocity \( v_o \) — speed of observer relative to medium
  • Wave speed v — depends only on medium (343 m/s for sound in air at 20 °C)
  • Blue-shift / red-shift — light from approaching / receding objects
  • Sonic boom — shock wave when source exceeds wave speed

Worked Example — Ambulance siren

An ambulance siren emits a 920 Hz tone. The ambulance approaches you at 25 m/s through still air (T = 20 °C, v = 343 m/s). What frequency do you hear (a) as it approaches and (b) after it passes?

Step 1. Approaching: source moves toward observer, observer stationary. \( f_\text{obs} = f_\text{src}\,v/(v - v_s) = 920 \times 343/(343 - 25) = 920 \times 343/318 \).

Step 2. Compute: \( 343/318 = 1.0786 \), so \( f_\text{obs} = 920 \times 1.0786 = 992\ \text{Hz} \) — pitched up by about 72 Hz.

Step 3. Receding: \( f_\text{obs} = f_\text{src}\,v/(v + v_s) = 920 \times 343/(343 + 25) = 920 \times 343/368 \).

Step 4. Compute: \( 343/368 = 0.9321 \), so \( f_\text{obs} = 920 \times 0.9321 = 858\ \text{Hz} \) — pitched down by about 62 Hz.

Step 5. Total shift heard as the ambulance passes: 992 → 858 Hz, a drop of 134 Hz — easily detected by the ear (corresponds to about 2 semitones).

  • Thinking the wave speed changes — it does not; only frequency and wavelength shift
  • Forgetting the medium matters — Doppler shift in air ≠ in water
  • Confusing the source and observer formulas — keep careful track of who is moving
  • Using sign convention inconsistently — "toward" always increases f
  • Assuming Doppler only applies to sound — it works for all waves, including light
