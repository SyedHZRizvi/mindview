🔥 Thermochemistry
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Thermochemistry is the quantitative study of heat in chemical reactions. The first law of thermodynamics states \(\Delta U = q + w\), where \(q\) is heat absorbed by the system and \(w\) is work done on the system. At constant pressure (the usual lab condition), the heat is the change in enthalpy: \(q_p = \Delta H\). An exothermic reaction releases heat (ΔH < 0); an endothermic reaction absorbs heat (ΔH > 0). Heat transfer is measured by calorimetry using \(q = mc\Delta T\), where \(c\) is specific heat capacity (water: 4.18 J/(g·°C)).

Standard enthalpies of formation, \(\Delta H_f^\circ\) (kJ/mol, at 1 bar and stated T, usually 298 K), are tabulated for substances in their standard states; the value is zero for an element in its most stable form. The enthalpy of any reaction can be calculated from \(\Delta H_{\text{rxn}}^\circ = \sum n\Delta H_f^\circ(\text{products}) - \sum n\Delta H_f^\circ(\text{reactants})\). Hess's Law permits the algebraic combination of step reactions: enthalpy is a state function, so its change depends only on initial and final states. Bond-energy calculations \(\Delta H_{\text{rxn}} \approx \sum E(\text{bonds broken}) - \sum E(\text{bonds formed})\) give an approximate value when formation enthalpies are unavailable.

Thermochemical equations show ΔH alongside the balanced equation, with phases noted, e.g., \(CH_4(g) + 2O_2(g) \rightarrow CO_2(g) + 2H_2O(\ell), \;\Delta H = -890\,\text{kJ}\).

  • System / surroundings — region of interest / everything else.
  • Enthalpy (\(H\)) — heat content at constant pressure; units kJ or kJ/mol.
  • Specific heat capacity (\(c\)) — J/(g·°C).
  • Heat of combustion / formation / neutralization — standard enthalpies for specific reactions.
  • Hess's Law — ΔH is path-independent.
  • State function — depends only on state, not path (H, U, S, G).

Worked Example: Hess's Law

Given (1) \(C(s) + O_2(g) \rightarrow CO_2(g)\), ΔH₁ = −393.5 kJ; (2) \(H_2(g) + \tfrac{1}{2}O_2(g) \rightarrow H_2O(\ell)\), ΔH₂ = −285.8 kJ; (3) \(CH_4(g) + 2O_2(g) \rightarrow CO_2(g) + 2H_2O(\ell)\), ΔH₃ = −890.4 kJ. Find ΔH for \(C(s) + 2H_2(g) \rightarrow CH_4(g)\) (the standard enthalpy of formation of methane).

Target = (1) + 2·(2) − (3): ΔH = −393.5 + 2(−285.8) − (−890.4) = −393.5 − 571.6 + 890.4 = −74.7 kJ/mol, matching the tabulated \(\Delta H_f^\circ\) of CH₄.

Common mistakes:

  • Sign errors: heat absorbed by the system is +q (endothermic); heat released, −q (exothermic).
  • Forgetting to multiply ΔH by the stoichiometric coefficient when scaling a reaction.
  • Confusing specific heat capacity (per gram) with molar heat capacity (per mole).
  • Treating bond-energy estimates as exact — they are average values over many compounds.

Curriculum expectation: Strand D, Expectation D2.3 — "solve problems related to enthalpy change in a chemical reaction (e.g., problems involving the application of Hess's law, calorimetry, and standard enthalpies of formation)."

⚡ Reaction Rates
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Chemical kinetics deals with the speed of reactions and the molecular pathways by which reactants become products. For a general reaction \(aA + bB \rightarrow cC + dD\), the rate is \(-\tfrac{1}{a}\tfrac{d[A]}{dt} = -\tfrac{1}{b}\tfrac{d[B]}{dt} = +\tfrac{1}{c}\tfrac{d[C]}{dt} = +\tfrac{1}{d}\tfrac{d[D]}{dt}\). The rate law, \(\text{rate} = k[A]^m[B]^n\), is determined experimentally. The exponents \(m\) and \(n\) are the orders with respect to each reactant; their sum is the overall order. \(k\) is the rate constant with units depending on the overall order.

Collision theory states that for a reaction to occur, particles must collide with proper orientation and with kinetic energy ≥ the activation energy \(E_a\). The Arrhenius equation \(k = Ae^{-E_a/RT}\), or in linear form \(\ln k = \ln A - E_a/(RT)\), quantifies how rate constants depend on temperature: a 10 °C rise typically doubles or triples \(k\). A catalyst provides an alternative mechanism with a lower \(E_a\), increasing rate without being consumed. Reaction mechanisms are step-by-step molecular descriptions; the overall rate law is determined by the slowest step, the rate-determining step (RDS). Reaction intermediates appear in the mechanism but cancel in the overall equation; catalysts appear in early steps and are regenerated.

Integrated rate laws relate concentration to time: zero order, \([A] = [A]_0 - kt\); first order, \(\ln[A] = \ln[A]_0 - kt\), half-life \(t_{1/2} = \ln 2 / k\); second order, \(1/[A] = 1/[A]_0 + kt\).

  • Rate constant (\(k\)) — temperature-dependent proportionality.
  • Activation energy (\(E_a\)) — minimum kinetic energy required.
  • Transition state / activated complex — high-energy arrangement at the peak.
  • Intermediate — species formed and consumed during the mechanism.
  • Catalyst — lowers \(E_a\); homogeneous (same phase) or heterogeneous.
  • Elementary step — single-collision event; molecularity = unimolecular, bimolecular, termolecular.

Worked Example: Determining a rate law from initial rates

For \(2NO(g) + O_2(g) \rightarrow 2NO_2(g)\), data show: doubling [NO] (with [O₂] fixed) quadruples the rate; doubling [O₂] (with [NO] fixed) doubles the rate. Find the rate law.

Order in NO: \(2^m = 4 \Rightarrow m = 2\). Order in O₂: \(2^n = 2 \Rightarrow n = 1\). Rate law: \(\text{rate} = k[NO]^2[O_2]\); overall order 3. Units of \(k\): mol⁻²·L²·s⁻¹ (i.e., M⁻²s⁻¹).

Common mistakes:

  • Using stoichiometric coefficients as rate-law exponents — they are only equal for elementary steps.
  • Confusing the rate-determining (slowest) step with the most thermodynamically favourable step; ΔG and \(E_a\) are independent.
  • Forgetting to include the temperature in Arrhenius problems (T in kelvin).
  • Mistaking a catalyst for an intermediate; the catalyst appears in the first step and reappears in a later step.

Curriculum expectation: Strand D, Expectation D3.4 — "explain, with reference to collision theory and potential energy diagrams, how factors such as concentration, surface area, pressure, temperature, and the presence of a catalyst affect the rate of a chemical reaction."

♻️ Entropy and Gibbs Free Energy
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Entropy (\(S\)) is a measure of the dispersal of energy and matter among accessible microstates; the second law of thermodynamics states that the entropy of the universe increases in every spontaneous process: \(\Delta S_{\text{univ}} = \Delta S_{\text{sys}} + \Delta S_{\text{surr}} \gt 0\). Entropy increases with temperature (more populated states), with the conversion solid → liquid → gas (greater positional disorder), and with the production of more moles of gas. Standard molar entropies \(S^\circ\) (J/(mol·K)) are absolute (the third law of thermodynamics sets \(S = 0\) for a perfect crystal at 0 K).

For a process at constant temperature and pressure, the two driving forces — enthalpy decrease (system stabilization) and entropy increase — are combined into the Gibbs free energy: \(\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S\). The sign of ΔG predicts spontaneity: ΔG < 0, spontaneous (product-favoured); ΔG = 0, at equilibrium; ΔG > 0, non-spontaneous in the forward direction. Four cases follow from the signs of ΔH and ΔS: (ΔH<0, ΔS>0) always spontaneous; (ΔH>0, ΔS<0) never spontaneous; (ΔH<0, ΔS<0) spontaneous below a crossover \(T = \Delta H / \Delta S\); (ΔH>0, ΔS>0) spontaneous above the crossover.

At equilibrium, \(\Delta G^\circ = -RT\ln K\), linking thermodynamics to the equilibrium constant. Under non-standard conditions, \(\Delta G = \Delta G^\circ + RT\ln Q\), with \(Q\) the reaction quotient.

  • Entropy (\(S\)) — J/(mol·K); always \(\geq\) 0 for substances.
  • Gibbs free energy (\(G\)) — \(\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S\); kJ/mol.
  • Spontaneous — proceeds without continuous external input; says nothing about rate.
  • Standard conditions — 1 bar (or 1 atm), 1 M, stated T (usually 298.15 K).
  • Crossover temperature — \(T = \Delta H / \Delta S\) where ΔG = 0.
  • Coupling reactions — pairing a non-spontaneous reaction with a strongly spontaneous one (basis of ATP-driven biochemistry).

Worked Example: ΔG° = −RT ln K

For the reaction \(N_2O_4(g) \rightleftharpoons 2NO_2(g)\) at 298 K, ΔH° = +57.2 kJ/mol and ΔS° = +175.8 J/(mol·K). Find ΔG° and K.

ΔG° = ΔH° − TΔS° = 57.2 − (298)(0.1758) = 57.2 − 52.4 = +4.8 kJ/mol.

ln K = −ΔG°/(RT) = −4800 / (8.314 × 298) = −1.937, so K = e^(−1.937) = 0.144. Reactant-favoured, as expected for a small positive ΔG°.

Common mistakes:

  • Mixing units: ΔH in kJ/mol and ΔS in J/(mol·K) — convert ΔS to kJ before combining.
  • Concluding a "spontaneous" reaction is necessarily fast — thermodynamics says nothing about rate (diamond → graphite is spontaneous but glacially slow).
  • Forgetting that ΔS_surr also depends on temperature: \(\Delta S_{\text{surr}} = -\Delta H_{\text{sys}}/T\).
  • Treating ΔG° as the same as ΔG — only equilibrium between standard states uses the standard value.

Curriculum expectation: Strand D, Expectation D3.7 — "explain, in terms of entropy and enthalpy, the energy changes involved in a chemical reaction, and explain how these factors determine whether the reaction is spontaneous."
