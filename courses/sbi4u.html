📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Strand A is the cross-cutting "skills" strand of every Ontario Grade 11–12 science course. In SBI4U it is never taught as a stand-alone unit; instead, every biochemistry lab, every photosynthesis simulation, and every population study you complete must demonstrate one or more of the four skill clusters: initiating and planning, performing and recording, analysing and interpreting, and communicating. Because Grade 12 marks the transition into university-level science, the bar is higher than in SBI3U: you are expected to design controlled experiments, justify the choice of independent and dependent variables, apply WHMIS hazard symbols, propagate uncertainty through calculations, and present data with appropriate significant figures.

Safe practice and ethical conduct sit at the centre of this strand. You must recognise WHMIS pictograms (flame, exclamation, biohazard, corrosive, etc.), follow MSDS/SDS guidance for any reagent used in enzyme or chromatography labs, and apply the precautionary principle when working with live cultures or DNA. Equally important is the analysis side: a linearised graph (e.g., transforming exponential growth data with a log axis) often reveals a constant that raw data hides, and a properly written lab report communicates uncertainty so that another scientist can reproduce your work.

Career exploration is built into the strand. Ontario expects students to investigate biotechnology, biomedical research, ecology, pharmacology, forensic science, and conservation as possible pathways, and to weigh the social, technological, and ethical implications (STSE) of biological knowledge — for example, the implications of CRISPR gene editing or pesticide-resistant crops.

  • Controlled variable — a factor kept constant across all trials so that changes in the dependent variable can be attributed to the independent variable.
  • WHMIS 2015 — Workplace Hazardous Materials Information System; pictograms communicate hazard class.
  • Significant figures — digits that carry meaning contributing to a measurement's precision.
  • Percent error — \(\\%\\,\\text{error} = \\left|\\frac{\\text{experimental} - \\text{accepted}}{\\text{accepted}}\\right| \\times 100\\%\).
  • STSE — Science, Technology, Society and the Environment connections.

Worked example — Significant figures and percent error

A student measures the mass of glucose produced in a photosynthesis lab as 0.0428 g; the theoretical mass from stoichiometry is 0.0400 g. How many significant figures should the answer carry, and what is the percent error?

The measurement 0.0428 g has three significant figures (leading zeros do not count). Percent error: \(\\left|\\frac{0.0428 - 0.0400}{0.0400}\\right| \\times 100\\% = 7.00\\%\). Report as 7.00% (three sig figs to match the data).

  • Confusing precision (repeatability) with accuracy (closeness to the true value).
  • Writing "0.0400 g" with only one sig fig — trailing zeros after a decimal point ARE significant.
  • Listing variables but failing to identify which is independent vs dependent.
  • Skipping the controls section of a lab report or omitting WHMIS symbols.

🧪 Biological Molecules
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

All life is built from four families of biological macromolecules: carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids. Three of the four (carbohydrates, proteins, nucleic acids) are true polymers built from monomers by condensation (dehydration) synthesis, in which a covalent bond forms with the release of one water molecule. The reverse reaction, hydrolysis, splits a polymer by adding water. Lipids are not polymers in the strict sense, but triglycerides and phospholipids are still assembled by condensation reactions between glycerol and fatty acids (ester bonds).

Carbohydrates obey the empirical formula \((CH_2O)_n\). Monosaccharides like glucose and fructose join through glycosidic linkages to form disaccharides (sucrose = glucose + fructose) and polysaccharides (starch, glycogen, cellulose, chitin). The same monomer (α-glucose vs β-glucose) yields radically different polymers — branched starch for energy storage in plants versus straight, hydrogen-bonded cellulose for structural support. Understanding why matters: cellulose's β-1,4 bonds resist mammalian digestive enzymes, which is why we need fibre but cannot extract glucose from it.

Lipids are defined by hydrophobicity rather than by a shared monomer. Triglycerides store energy at more than twice the caloric density of carbohydrates because their C–H bonds are highly reduced. Phospholipids — amphipathic molecules with a polar phosphate head and two nonpolar fatty-acid tails — spontaneously form the lipid bilayer of every cell membrane. Steroids (cholesterol, testosterone, oestrogen) share a four-ring carbon skeleton and act as signalling molecules and membrane fluidity modulators.

  • Monomer / polymer — single repeating unit / chain built from monomers.
  • Condensation synthesis — bond formation releasing \(H_2O\).
  • Hydrolysis — bond cleavage consuming \(H_2O\).
  • Glycosidic bond — covalent C–O–C bond between monosaccharides.
  • Ester bond — bond linking glycerol to fatty acids in triglycerides.
  • Saturated vs unsaturated — no C=C double bonds vs one or more C=C double bonds in fatty-acid tails.
  • Amphipathic — having both hydrophilic and hydrophobic regions (e.g., phospholipids).

Worked example — Counting water molecules in polymer synthesis

How many water molecules are released when 8 glucose monomers polymerise into a single chain of glycogen?

Each glycosidic bond release one \(H_2O\). Linking n monomers in a single unbranched chain requires \(n - 1\) bonds. For 8 monomers: \(8 - 1 = 7\) water molecules released. (If branching occurs, count one bond per branch point in addition to the linear bonds.)

  • Confusing condensation (releases water) with hydrolysis (consumes water).
  • Calling lipids "polymers" — only carbohydrates, proteins, and nucleic acids are true polymers.
  • Forgetting that α-glucose vs β-glucose changes whether a polysaccharide is digestible.
  • Drawing saturated fats with kinked tails — saturated tails are straight; unsaturated tails kink at C=C.

⚗️ Enzymes
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Enzymes are biological catalysts — almost always globular proteins (a few are ribozymes made of RNA) that accelerate reactions by lowering activation energy \(E_a\). They achieve this by binding the substrate at the active site, stabilising the transition state, and orienting reactants in a favourable geometry. The induced-fit model (Koshland) refines the older lock-and-key model: the active site flexes slightly when the substrate binds, optimising the catalytic contacts. Enzymes are not consumed in the reaction, so a single molecule may turn over thousands of substrate molecules per second (catalase converts ~40 million \(H_2O_2\) per second).

Enzyme activity depends on conditions. Temperature, pH, substrate concentration, enzyme concentration, and the presence of inhibitors all matter. Plotting reaction rate against substrate concentration produces the hyperbolic Michaelis–Menten curve: rate climbs steeply at low [S], then plateaus at \(V_{max}\) when all active sites are saturated. The substrate concentration at half-\(V_{max}\) is \(K_m\) — a measure of binding affinity. Most human enzymes peak near 37 °C and pH 7.4, but pepsin in the stomach is optimal at pH 2, while trypsin in the small intestine prefers pH 8. Beyond an optimum, the enzyme denatures: hydrogen bonds and disulfide bridges break, the tertiary structure unfolds, and the active site loses its shape.

Regulation occurs through inhibitors and activators. Competitive inhibitors resemble the substrate and bind at the active site (raising apparent \(K_m\) without changing \(V_{max}\)); non-competitive (allosteric) inhibitors bind elsewhere, altering enzyme shape (lowering \(V_{max}\)). Feedback inhibition uses the end product of a metabolic pathway to switch off an earlier enzyme — a key example is isoleucine inhibiting threonine deaminase.

  • Active site — region where substrate binds and catalysis occurs.
  • Activation energy (\(E_a\)) — energy barrier to reach the transition state.
  • Cofactor / coenzyme — inorganic ion / organic helper molecule required by some enzymes.
  • Denaturation — loss of 3-D shape (and function) from disruption of bonds maintaining structure.
  • \(K_m\) and \(V_{max}\) — substrate concentration at half-maximal rate; maximum reaction velocity.
  • Allosteric site — regulatory binding site distinct from the active site.

Worked example — Distinguishing competitive vs non-competitive inhibition

Adding inhibitor X to an enzyme assay shifts \(K_m\) from 2.0 mM to 4.0 mM but leaves \(V_{max}\) unchanged. Inhibitor Y lowers \(V_{max}\) from 60 µmol/min to 40 µmol/min but leaves \(K_m\) unchanged. Classify each inhibitor and explain the reasoning.

X is a competitive inhibitor: increasing [S] outcompetes the inhibitor, so \(V_{max}\) is reached eventually, but more substrate is needed to do so (higher apparent \(K_m\)). Y is a non-competitive (allosteric) inhibitor: it binds away from the active site and decreases the proportion of functional enzymes, so \(V_{max}\) falls; binding affinity at the active site is unaffected, so \(K_m\) is constant.

  • Saying enzymes "are used up" — they are regenerated after each catalytic cycle.
  • Claiming higher temperature always means faster reaction — true only up to the optimum; beyond it, denaturation collapses activity.
  • Mixing up competitive (changes \(K_m\)) and non-competitive (changes \(V_{max}\)) effects.
  • Forgetting that enzymes lower \(E_a\) but do not change \(\\Delta G\) of the overall reaction.

🔬 Cell Membrane Structure & Transport
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The plasma membrane is best described by the fluid mosaic model (Singer and Nicolson, 1972): a two-dimensional fluid phospholipid bilayer studded with proteins, cholesterol, and glycolipids/glycoproteins. The bilayer's hydrophobic core (fatty-acid tails) forms a selective barrier to polar and charged solutes, while embedded proteins create regulated entry and exit points. Cholesterol intercalates between phospholipids, modulating fluidity: it prevents the membrane from becoming too fluid at high temperatures and from solidifying at low temperatures. Glycoproteins and glycolipids on the outer leaflet serve as cell-identity markers, essential for tissue recognition, immune function, and blood typing.

Transport across the membrane is classified by whether ATP is required. Passive transport (no ATP) follows the concentration gradient: simple diffusion of small nonpolar molecules (\(O_2\), \(CO_2\)) directly through the bilayer; facilitated diffusion of larger polar molecules and ions through channel and carrier proteins; and osmosis, the diffusion of water across a semipermeable membrane (often through aquaporins). Active transport requires energy: the sodium–potassium pump uses ATP to move 3 \(Na^+\) out and 2 \(K^+\) in against their gradients, maintaining the resting membrane potential of neurons. Bulk transport moves large quantities: endocytosis (phagocytosis, pinocytosis, receptor-mediated) brings material in, exocytosis releases it.

Tonicity describes how the extracellular solution affects a cell. In a hypotonic solution, water enters and animal cells may lyse (plant cells become turgid). In a hypertonic solution, water exits, animal cells crenate and plant cells plasmolyse. Isotonic solutions produce no net water movement and are used for IV fluids.

  • Phospholipid bilayer — amphipathic lipid sheet forming the membrane core.
  • Integral / peripheral protein — proteins embedded in / loosely attached to the membrane.
  • Aquaporin — channel protein that allows rapid water movement.
  • Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase — antiporter pumping 3 \(Na^+\) out and 2 \(K^+\) in per ATP hydrolysed.
  • Tonicity — relative solute concentration of two solutions separated by a membrane.
  • Endocytosis / exocytosis — vesicle-mediated uptake / release of bulk material.

Worked example — Predicting cell volume in different solutions

A red blood cell with internal solute concentration of 0.9% NaCl is placed sequentially in three beakers: (a) 0.45% NaCl, (b) 0.9% NaCl, (c) 1.8% NaCl. Predict whether the cell shrinks, stays the same, or swells in each case.

(a) The cell's interior is hypertonic to the beaker, so water enters by osmosis and the cell swells (and may lyse). (b) The solutions are isotonic; no net water movement, cell volume unchanged. (c) The beaker is hypertonic to the cell, water exits and the cell crenates (shrivels).

  • Confusing active transport (uses ATP, against gradient) with facilitated diffusion (no ATP, with gradient).
  • Saying "the bilayer is impermeable to everything" — small nonpolar molecules cross freely.
  • Mixing up hypertonic and hypotonic (remember: water flows toward the higher solute concentration).
  • Describing osmosis as "movement of solutes" — osmosis specifically refers to the movement of water.

Curriculum expectation: Strand B, Expectation B3.5 — "explain the chemical structure and biological importance of the molecular components of cell membranes (e.g., the lipid bilayer; the role of cholesterol, glycolipids, and proteins) in maintaining cellular function."

🧫 Proteins & Amino Acids
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Proteins are polymers of amino acids, of which 20 are encoded by the standard genetic code in humans. Every amino acid shares the same backbone (an α-carbon bonded to an amino group, a carboxyl group, a hydrogen, and a variable R-group) but differs in the side chain. The R-group dictates whether the amino acid is nonpolar (e.g., valine, leucine), polar (e.g., serine), acidic (aspartate), basic (lysine), or contains sulfur (cysteine — important for disulfide bridges). Amino acids polymerise via peptide bonds, a condensation reaction between the carboxyl of one amino acid and the amino group of the next, releasing water.

Protein structure is hierarchical and shape determines function. Primary structure is the linear sequence of amino acids, dictated by the gene's mRNA. Secondary structure arises from hydrogen bonding between backbone atoms, forming α-helices and β-pleated sheets. Tertiary structure is the overall three-dimensional fold, stabilised by hydrogen bonds, ionic bonds, hydrophobic interactions, van der Waals forces, and disulfide bridges between cysteine residues. Quaternary structure exists only when two or more polypeptide chains assemble (haemoglobin has four subunits — two α and two β chains, each with a heme cofactor).

A protein's job depends on its shape. Enzymes catalyse reactions, antibodies bind antigens, haemoglobin transports \(O_2\), keratin and collagen provide structure, insulin signals between cells, and membrane transport proteins move solutes. A single amino-acid substitution can be catastrophic: in sickle-cell anaemia, glutamate is replaced by valine at position 6 of the β-globin chain, creating a hydrophobic patch that makes haemoglobin polymerise under low \(O_2\) — the molecular root of a debilitating disease.

  • α-carbon — central carbon of an amino acid, bonded to four different groups.
  • Peptide bond — covalent C–N bond between two amino acids, formed by condensation.
  • R-group / side chain — variable portion of an amino acid that determines its chemical character.
  • Disulfide bridge — covalent S–S bond between two cysteine residues, stabilising tertiary structure.
  • Denaturation — loss of higher-order structure (secondary, tertiary, quaternary) without breaking peptide bonds.
  • Chaperone protein — assists correct folding of nascent polypeptides.

Worked example — Predicting the consequence of a point mutation

A gene encodes a transmembrane receptor with a hydrophobic α-helical region embedded in the lipid bilayer. A point mutation changes a valine (nonpolar) in the helix to a glutamate (acidic, charged). Predict the effect on the protein.

Charged residues destabilise hydrophobic environments. Introducing glutamate into the lipid-buried helix forces the helix out of the membrane or distorts the local fold, very likely disrupting tertiary structure and membrane anchoring. The receptor may misfold, be tagged for degradation, or fail to transduce signals.

  • Saying "primary structure determines function directly" — primary structure determines folding, which determines function.
  • Confusing peptide bonds (between amino acids) with hydrogen bonds (within and between proteins).
  • Forgetting that denaturation breaks higher-order bonds but leaves peptide bonds intact.
  • Drawing only one chain for haemoglobin — it is a tetramer (quaternary structure).

Curriculum expectation: Strand B, Expectation B3.4 — "describe, on the basis of research, the structural and functional diversity of proteins (e.g., enzymes, hormones, antibodies) in cells and tissues."

🧬 Nucleic Acids — DNA & RNA
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Nucleic acids — DNA and RNA — are polymers of nucleotides. Each nucleotide consists of three parts: a pentose sugar (deoxyribose in DNA, ribose in RNA), a phosphate group, and one of five nitrogenous bases. Purines (adenine, guanine) have a double-ring structure; pyrimidines (cytosine, thymine in DNA, uracil in RNA) have a single ring. Nucleotides link via phosphodiester bonds between the 3′ hydroxyl of one sugar and the 5′ phosphate of the next, giving every strand a directional 5′ → 3′ polarity.

DNA is a right-handed double helix (Watson, Crick, Franklin, Wilkins, 1953). The two strands are antiparallel: one runs 5′ → 3′, the other 3′ → 5′. Bases pair through hydrogen bonds — A with T (two H-bonds), G with C (three H-bonds), which is why GC-rich regions are more thermally stable. Chargaff's rules follow: %A = %T and %G = %C in any double-stranded DNA. The sugar-phosphate backbone forms the outside; the bases stack on the inside, shielded from water — an elegant arrangement that simultaneously protects the genetic information and allows it to be copied.

RNA differs from DNA in three ways: it uses ribose (with a 2′-OH that makes RNA more reactive but less stable than DNA), uracil instead of thymine, and is usually single-stranded. There are three principal RNA species in protein synthesis: messenger RNA (mRNA) carries the gene's code from the nucleus to the ribosome; transfer RNA (tRNA) brings amino acids that match each mRNA codon via a complementary anticodon; ribosomal RNA (rRNA) is the catalytic core of the ribosome. Other RNAs (microRNAs, siRNAs, lncRNAs) regulate gene expression.

  • Nucleotide — phosphate + pentose sugar + nitrogenous base.
  • Phosphodiester bond — covalent linkage in the nucleic-acid backbone.
  • Antiparallel — strands run in opposite 5′–3′ directions.
  • Purine vs pyrimidine — double-ring (A, G) vs single-ring (C, T, U) bases.
  • Chargaff's rules — %A = %T and %G = %C in double-stranded DNA.
  • Complementary base pairing — A–T and G–C via hydrogen bonds.

Worked example — Applying Chargaff's rules

Analysis of a DNA sample shows it contains 22% adenine. What are the percentages of T, G, and C?

Chargaff: %A = %T, so %T = 22%. The four bases sum to 100%, so %G + %C = 100 − 22 − 22 = 56%. Because %G = %C, each is 28%. Final answer: T = 22%, G = 28%, C = 28%.

  • Drawing DNA with parallel strands — the two strands are antiparallel.
  • Pairing A with G or C with T — only A–T (or A–U in RNA) and G–C pair.
  • Saying RNA contains thymine — RNA replaces thymine with uracil.
  • Forgetting the directionality: DNA polymerase can only add nucleotides to the 3′ end.

Curriculum expectation: Strand B, Expectation B3.2 — "explain the structure and function of cell components (e.g., DNA, RNA, ribosomes) at the molecular level."

🌿 Photosynthesis
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Photosynthesis is the process by which autotrophs convert light energy into chemical energy stored in glucose. The overall equation \(6CO_2 + 6H_2O + \\text{light} \\rightarrow C_6H_{12}O_6 + 6O_2\) hides considerable complexity. In plants, photosynthesis takes place in the chloroplast, an organelle with a double membrane and an internal system of thylakoid sacs stacked into grana. The light-dependent reactions happen on the thylakoid membrane; the Calvin cycle (light-independent) happens in the surrounding fluid called the stroma. This compartmentalisation is essential — it lets the cell maintain a proton gradient across the thylakoid membrane that drives ATP synthesis.

Light-dependent reactions begin when chlorophyll a in photosystem II (PSII, P680) absorbs a photon and is oxidised. Water is split by the oxygen-evolving complex (\(2H_2O \\rightarrow 4H^+ + 4e^- + O_2\)) — the source of all atmospheric oxygen. Excited electrons travel through plastoquinone, the cytochrome b₆f complex, plastocyanin, then to photosystem I (PSI, P700), which re-energises them. The final acceptor is NADP⁺, reduced to NADPH by ferredoxin and NADP⁺ reductase. Along the way, protons are pumped into the thylakoid lumen, building the gradient that ATP synthase uses to make ATP from ADP + Pᵢ (chemiosmosis). The net products are ATP, NADPH, and \(O_2\) — the first two power the Calvin cycle, the third is released as a "waste" product.

Wavelength matters: chlorophyll a absorbs maximally in the blue (~430 nm) and red (~660 nm) regions and reflects green, which is why leaves appear green. Accessory pigments (chlorophyll b, carotenoids, xanthophylls) broaden the absorption spectrum and protect chlorophyll from oxidative damage. An action spectrum overlaid on an absorption spectrum shows that photosynthetic rate closely tracks pigment absorption — strong historical evidence (Engelmann, 1882) that pigments drive photosynthesis.

  • Thylakoid / granum / stroma — flattened sac / stack of thylakoids / fluid matrix of the chloroplast.
  • Photosystem II (P680) / Photosystem I (P700) — light-harvesting complexes.
  • Electron transport chain (ETC) — series of carriers (plastoquinone, cyt b₆f, plastocyanin, ferredoxin) that move electrons and pump \(H^+\).
  • Chemiosmosis — ATP production driven by \(H^+\) flowing through ATP synthase down its gradient.
  • NADPH — electron carrier supplying reducing power to the Calvin cycle.
  • Absorption vs action spectrum — wavelengths absorbed vs wavelengths driving photosynthesis.

Worked example — Tracing oxygen isotopes

An experiment uses \(H_2{}^{18}O\) and \(C{}^{16}O_2\) to feed a plant. Predict whether the released \(O_2\) carries the \({}^{18}O\) or the \({}^{16}O\) label, and explain.

The oxygen released comes from the splitting of water at PSII, not from \(CO_2\). Therefore the released \(O_2\) is labelled \({}^{18}O_2\). This Ruben and Kamen experiment (1941) established that water is the source of photosynthetic oxygen.

  • Saying "\(O_2\) comes from \(CO_2\)" — \(O_2\) comes from the splitting of \(H_2O\) at PSII.
  • Confusing the order: PSII fires first (despite the number), then PSI.
  • Locating the Calvin cycle in the thylakoid membrane — it occurs in the stroma.
  • Forgetting that ATP synthase is powered by the \(H^+\) gradient (chemiosmosis), not directly by electrons.

🌱 Calvin Cycle (Light-Independent Reactions)
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The Calvin cycle (also called the C₃ cycle or carbon fixation pathway) takes place in the stroma of the chloroplast and converts the ATP and NADPH generated by the light reactions into a stable carbohydrate. It is called "light-independent" because it does not directly require photons, but it is wholly dependent on the products of the light reactions and effectively stops in the dark. The cycle has three phases — carbon fixation, reduction, and regeneration of RuBP — and turns three times to produce one molecule of glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate (G3P), the immediate sugar product. Six turns are required to make a hexose like glucose.

In the carbon fixation phase, the enzyme RuBisCO (ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate carboxylase/oxygenase) attaches \(CO_2\) to the 5-carbon sugar RuBP. The unstable 6-carbon intermediate splits immediately into two molecules of 3-phosphoglycerate (3-PGA). RuBisCO is the most abundant protein on Earth, but it is also slow (3 reactions per second) and prone to grab \(O_2\) instead of \(CO_2\) — a wasteful side reaction called photorespiration. In the reduction phase, each 3-PGA is phosphorylated by ATP (giving 1,3-BPG) and reduced by NADPH to G3P. Some G3P leaves the cycle to make glucose; most re-enters the cycle. In the regeneration phase, ATP-driven reshuffling rebuilds RuBP from five G3P molecules so the cycle can continue.

The energy bookkeeping for one G3P is precise: 3 \(CO_2\) in, 9 ATP and 6 NADPH consumed, 1 G3P out (plus regenerated RuBP). For one glucose (2 G3P), that is 18 ATP and 12 NADPH. \(C_4\) plants (corn, sugarcane) and CAM plants (cacti, pineapples) have evolved mechanisms to concentrate \(CO_2\) around RuBisCO and suppress photorespiration in hot, dry climates.

  • RuBP (ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate) — 5-carbon \(CO_2\) acceptor.
  • RuBisCO — enzyme that fixes \(CO_2\) to RuBP.
  • 3-PGA / G3P — 3-carbon intermediates of the cycle; G3P is the net product.
  • Photorespiration — wasteful oxygenation by RuBisCO when \(O_2\) outcompetes \(CO_2\).
  • C₄ pathway / CAM — adaptations that concentrate \(CO_2\) before delivering it to the Calvin cycle.

Worked example — ATP and NADPH accounting

How many ATP and NADPH molecules are required to synthesise one molecule of glucose (\(C_6H_{12}O_6\)) via the Calvin cycle?

Glucose has 6 carbons, so 6 turns of the cycle (one \(CO_2\) fixed per turn) are needed, yielding 2 G3P. Each turn uses 3 ATP (2 in reduction, 1 in regeneration) and 2 NADPH (in reduction). Total: \(6 \\times 3 = 18\) ATP and \(6 \\times 2 = 12\) NADPH. The cycle also regenerates 6 RuBP, so RuBP is catalytic, not net consumed.

  • Calling the Calvin cycle the "dark reaction" — it can occur in light; it simply does not require photons directly.
  • Saying RuBisCO fixes \(O_2\) only — it fixes \(CO_2\) preferentially, but oxygenation occurs and is wasteful (photorespiration).
  • Reporting glucose as the direct product — G3P is the immediate product; glucose is built from two G3P downstream.
  • Forgetting that ATP is used in both reduction and regeneration phases.

🔋 Cellular Respiration
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Cellular respiration is the catabolic process that extracts chemical energy from glucose and stores it in ATP. The overall equation \(C_6H_{12}O_6 + 6O_2 \\rightarrow 6CO_2 + 6H_2O + \\text{ATP} + \\text{heat}\) sums four stages: glycolysis, pyruvate oxidation, the Krebs cycle, and oxidative phosphorylation. Glycolysis is the only stage that occurs in the cytoplasm — it is universal across life and evolutionarily ancient, predating oxygenic conditions on Earth. The remaining three stages take place in the mitochondrion: pyruvate oxidation and the Krebs cycle in the mitochondrial matrix; the electron transport chain and ATP synthase on the inner mitochondrial membrane (folded into cristae to maximise surface area).

In glycolysis, one 6-carbon glucose is split into two 3-carbon pyruvates, generating 2 ATP net (4 made, 2 invested) and 2 NADH. Pyruvate oxidation (in the matrix) converts each pyruvate to acetyl-CoA, releasing \(CO_2\) and producing NADH. The two-carbon acetyl group then enters the Krebs cycle, which spins twice per glucose and yields, per turn, 3 NADH, 1 FADH₂, 1 ATP (or GTP), and 2 \(CO_2\). The real energy harvest happens in oxidative phosphorylation: NADH and FADH₂ deliver electrons to Complex I and Complex II of the inner-membrane electron transport chain. As electrons fall energetically through Complexes I → III → IV (via mobile carriers ubiquinone and cytochrome c), \(H^+\) ions are pumped from matrix to intermembrane space. The proton gradient drives ATP synthase to produce roughly 26 ATP. Oxygen is the terminal electron acceptor, combining with \(H^+\) to form water; without it, the chain backs up and the cycle stops.

If oxygen is absent, cells run fermentation to regenerate NAD⁺ so glycolysis can continue. Lactate fermentation (muscle, lactobacillus) reduces pyruvate to lactate; alcohol fermentation (yeast) releases \(CO_2\) and produces ethanol. Fermentation makes no additional ATP beyond glycolysis's 2 — a steep penalty for anaerobic life.

  • Substrate-level phosphorylation — direct ATP synthesis by transfer of a phosphate from a substrate.
  • Oxidative phosphorylation — ATP synthesis driven by the proton gradient established by the ETC.
  • NADH / FADH₂ — reduced electron carriers feeding the ETC.
  • Cristae / matrix / intermembrane space — mitochondrial compartments separated by inner and outer membranes.
  • Chemiosmosis — coupling of \(H^+\) flow through ATP synthase to ATP synthesis.
  • Fermentation — anaerobic regeneration of NAD⁺ from NADH.

Worked example — ATP yield per glucose

Using the conventional yields (2.5 ATP per NADH, 1.5 ATP per FADH₂), calculate the total ATP per glucose under aerobic conditions.

Glycolysis: 2 ATP + 2 NADH (≈ 5 ATP) = 7 ATP. Pyruvate oxidation: 2 NADH (≈ 5 ATP). Krebs cycle: 2 ATP + 6 NADH (≈ 15 ATP) + 2 FADH₂ (≈ 3 ATP) = 20 ATP. Grand total ≈ 7 + 5 + 20 = 32 ATP per glucose. (Older texts quote 36–38 if they assume 3 ATP/NADH and 2 ATP/FADH₂.)

  • Locating glycolysis in the mitochondrion — it occurs in the cytoplasm.
  • Forgetting that the Krebs cycle turns twice per glucose (one turn per pyruvate-derived acetyl-CoA).
  • Confusing oxidative phosphorylation (uses ETC and \(H^+\) gradient) with substrate-level phosphorylation (direct transfer).
  • Saying \(O_2\) is "used in the Krebs cycle" — \(O_2\) is consumed only at Complex IV as the final electron acceptor.

🧬 DNA Structure & Replication
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

DNA replication is semiconservative: each parental strand serves as a template for a new daughter strand, so every daughter molecule contains one old and one new strand. Meselson and Stahl (1958) proved this with density-gradient centrifugation of bacteria grown in ¹⁵N then shifted to ¹⁴N. Replication begins at sequences called origins of replication. In E. coli, the single circular chromosome has one origin; eukaryotic chromosomes have hundreds, allowing the entire human genome to be copied in roughly six hours.

The replication machinery is a coordinated complex. Helicase unwinds the parental double helix; topoisomerase (gyrase) relieves the supercoiling ahead of the fork; single-strand binding proteins keep the unwound strands apart. Primase synthesises a short RNA primer (~10 nucleotides) because DNA polymerase III cannot initiate new strands — it can only extend an existing 3′-OH. DNA polymerase III then adds nucleotides 5′ → 3′, complementary to the template. Because the two parental strands are antiparallel, only the leading strand is synthesised continuously toward the fork. The lagging strand is built in short Okazaki fragments away from the fork; each requires its own primer, and the fragments are later joined by DNA ligase after DNA polymerase I replaces RNA primers with DNA.

Fidelity is extraordinary — roughly one error per 10⁹–10¹⁰ nucleotides — because DNA polymerase has a 3′ → 5′ exonuclease proofreading activity and is followed by mismatch repair. At chromosome ends, telomeres (repetitive TTAGGG sequences in vertebrates) shorten with each division; telomerase, an RNA-templated reverse transcriptase, extends them in germline and stem cells. Loss of telomere maintenance limits replicative lifespan and is linked to senescence and cancer.

  • Semiconservative replication — each daughter helix has one parental and one new strand.
  • Helicase, topoisomerase, primase, ligase — enzymes that unwind, relax, prime, and seal.
  • DNA polymerase III — main replication enzyme; extends 5′ → 3′ only.
  • Leading / lagging strand — continuously synthesised toward / discontinuously synthesised away from the fork.
  • Okazaki fragment — short DNA segment on the lagging strand.
  • Telomere / telomerase — protective end repeats / enzyme that extends them.

Worked example — Predicting an Okazaki count

A replication bubble travels 5 000 nucleotides along a eukaryotic chromosome with Okazaki fragments of 200 nucleotides each. Approximately how many primers does the lagging strand require?

Lagging-strand length ≈ 5 000 nucleotides; each Okazaki fragment is ~200 nt and requires one primer. Number of primers ≈ 5 000 / 200 = 25 primers. Each primer is later replaced by DNA polymerase I and the fragments are sealed by ligase. (The leading strand requires only one primer.)

  • Saying DNA polymerase can start synthesis from scratch — it requires an RNA primer with a 3′-OH.
  • Drawing both strands as continuous — the lagging strand is discontinuous.
  • Forgetting that helicase, primase, and polymerase function together at the replication fork.
  • Confusing semiconservative with conservative or dispersive models (both ruled out by Meselson–Stahl).

🔬 Transcription & Translation
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The central dogma of molecular biology (Crick, 1958) states that genetic information flows DNA → RNA → protein. Transcription copies a gene from DNA into messenger RNA. In eukaryotes it happens in the nucleus and proceeds in three phases. Initiation: RNA polymerase II, with general transcription factors, binds the gene's promoter (often containing a TATA box ~25 bp upstream). Elongation: the polymerase unwinds DNA and synthesises pre-mRNA 5′ → 3′, reading the template strand 3′ → 5′; the coding strand has the same sequence as the mRNA (except U replaces T). Termination releases the polymerase at a termination signal. The pre-mRNA is then processed: a 5′ 7-methylguanosine cap is added, a poly-A tail of ~200 adenines is appended at the 3′ end, and introns are removed while exons are spliced together by the spliceosome. Alternative splicing of the same pre-mRNA produces multiple protein isoforms, which is one reason humans have only ~20 000 genes but hundreds of thousands of proteins.

Mature mRNA exits the nucleus through a nuclear pore and meets a ribosome in the cytoplasm. Translation decodes the mRNA into a polypeptide using codons — three-nucleotide units, 64 in total, encoding 20 amino acids plus start and stop signals. The code is redundant (most amino acids have several codons), nearly universal across life, and read in a fixed reading frame from the start codon AUG. Translation has three phases. Initiation: the small ribosomal subunit binds the mRNA at the 5′ cap and scans to AUG; the initiator tRNA (carrying methionine) base-pairs there, and the large subunit joins. Elongation: each new tRNA enters the A site with its amino acid; a peptide bond forms (catalysed by the rRNA in the large subunit — a ribozyme); the ribosome translocates by one codon, ejecting the empty tRNA from the E site. Termination: when a stop codon (UAA, UAG, UGA) enters the A site, release factors trigger polypeptide release.

Polypeptides leaving the ribosome then fold, often with chaperones, undergo post-translational modifications (phosphorylation, glycosylation, cleavage), and are targeted to their cellular destinations.

  • Promoter / TATA box — DNA region where RNA polymerase binds to start transcription.
  • Template vs coding strand — read by RNA polymerase vs identical in sequence to the mRNA.
  • Exon / intron — coding region kept / non-coding region removed during splicing.
  • 5′ cap / poly-A tail — mRNA modifications that protect from degradation and aid export.
  • Codon / anticodon — 3-nucleotide mRNA unit / complementary tRNA 3-nucleotide region.
  • Ribosome A/P/E sites — aminoacyl, peptidyl, exit sites where tRNAs occupy the ribosome.

Worked example — From DNA to polypeptide

Given the DNA template strand 3′–TAC GGA TTC ACG ATT–5′, write the mRNA sequence (5′ → 3′) and the resulting amino acid sequence (use the start codon and read until a stop codon).

Transcription reads the template 3′ → 5′ and builds mRNA 5′ → 3′ with complementary bases (A pairs with U). mRNA: 5′–AUG CCU AAG UGC UAA–3′. Codons: AUG (Met / start) – CCU (Pro) – AAG (Lys) – UGC (Cys) – UAA (stop). The polypeptide is Met–Pro–Lys–Cys.

  • Confusing the directions: transcription reads the template 3′ → 5′ and synthesises mRNA 5′ → 3′.
  • Mistaking the coding strand for the template — the coding strand looks like the mRNA (T for U).
  • Forgetting that introns are removed before mRNA leaves the nucleus.
  • Reading a codon table without setting the reading frame from AUG.

🔧 Gene Regulation
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Every cell in your body contains the same DNA, yet a neuron, an erythrocyte, and a hepatocyte look and behave nothing alike. The difference lies in gene regulation: which genes are switched on, in which cells, at what intensity, and at what time. In prokaryotes, gene regulation is fast and largely transcriptional. The classic example is the lac operon in E. coli. When lactose is absent, a repressor protein binds the operator and blocks transcription. When lactose is present, allolactose binds the repressor, changes its shape, and releases it from the operator — so the lacZ, lacY, and lacA genes (β-galactosidase, permease, transacetylase) are transcribed and lactose can be used. The trp operon uses the opposite logic: tryptophan acts as a corepressor; when it is abundant, the operon is repressed.

Eukaryotic regulation is multilayered. Chromatin remodelling: tightly packed heterochromatin is inaccessible; histone acetylation (loosening) and methylation (variable) modulate openness. DNA methylation at CpG islands generally silences genes and is the basis of epigenetic inheritance. Transcriptional regulation: enhancers and silencers, sometimes thousands of base pairs away, are brought to the promoter by activator and repressor transcription factors that bind specific DNA motifs. Post-transcriptional: alternative splicing produces different protein isoforms from one gene; microRNAs and siRNAs bind complementary mRNAs and trigger degradation or block translation (RNA interference). Translational and post-translational: mRNA stability, ribosome availability, phosphorylation, ubiquitination, and degradation all tune protein levels.

Why does this matter? Misregulation causes disease. Mutations in transcription factors (e.g., p53) or in tumour-suppressor microRNAs are central to cancer. Imprinted genes and X-inactivation are gene-regulation phenomena with developmental consequences. Stem-cell pluripotency relies on a master-regulator network (Oct4, Sox2, Nanog) that keeps differentiation genes silenced.

  • Operon — cluster of co-regulated genes under one promoter (prokaryotes).
  • Inducible vs repressible operon — turned ON by a signal (lac) vs turned OFF by a signal (trp).
  • Transcription factor — protein that binds DNA to activate or repress transcription.
  • Enhancer / silencer — distal DNA elements that increase / decrease transcription.
  • Epigenetics — heritable changes in gene expression without altering DNA sequence (methylation, histone modification).
  • RNA interference (RNAi) — gene silencing by microRNAs or siRNAs.

Worked example — Predicting lac-operon expression

Predict whether lacZ is transcribed in each scenario: (i) glucose absent, lactose present; (ii) glucose present, lactose present; (iii) glucose absent, lactose absent.

(i) Lactose binds the repressor (operator free) AND low glucose means high cAMP, which binds CAP/CRP to enhance promoter activity — STRONG transcription. (ii) Lactose removes the repressor BUT high glucose means low cAMP, so CAP is inactive — only weak basal transcription. (iii) No lactose means the repressor sits on the operator — NO transcription. The operon integrates two signals: lactose (induction) and glucose absence (catabolite activation).

  • Confusing inducible (lac, normally OFF) with repressible (trp, normally ON) operons.
  • Saying gene regulation occurs "only at transcription" — it operates at every step from chromatin to protein degradation.
  • Treating epigenetic changes as DNA mutations — DNA sequence is unchanged.
  • Forgetting that all body cells share the same genome; differences come from differential expression.

⚠️ Mutations
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

A mutation is any heritable change in the DNA sequence. Mutations are the ultimate source of genetic variation — without them, there would be no evolution — but a single misplaced nucleotide can also cause severe disease. Mutations are categorised by scale and effect. Point mutations change a single base. A silent mutation alters a codon but produces the same amino acid (a consequence of the genetic code's redundancy). A missense mutation changes one amino acid; sickle-cell anaemia (Glu → Val at position 6 of β-globin) is the textbook case. A nonsense mutation creates a premature stop codon, truncating the protein. Insertions and deletions that are not multiples of three nucleotides cause a frameshift, which scrambles every downstream codon and almost always disables the protein.

Chromosomal mutations are larger in scale. A deletion removes part of a chromosome (e.g., cri-du-chat from a 5p deletion). A duplication copies a region (often a source of evolutionary novelty). An inversion reverses a segment. A translocation moves a segment to a non-homologous chromosome — the Philadelphia chromosome (t(9;22)) creates the BCR-ABL fusion in chronic myeloid leukaemia. Nondisjunction during meiosis can yield aneuploidies, such as trisomy 21 (Down syndrome).

Causes are split between spontaneous (replication errors, tautomeric shifts, depurination) and induced (mutagens). Physical mutagens include UV light (forms thymine dimers) and ionising radiation (double-strand breaks). Chemical mutagens include base analogues (5-bromouracil), alkylating agents (mustard gas), and intercalating agents (ethidium bromide) that cause insertions or deletions. Cells defend themselves with multiple DNA repair pathways: mismatch repair, base excision repair, nucleotide excision repair, and homologous recombination. Failures of repair, such as in xeroderma pigmentosum (UV repair defect), produce high cancer risk.

  • Point mutation — single-base substitution.
  • Silent / missense / nonsense — same / different / stop amino-acid outcome.
  • Frameshift — indel that shifts the reading frame.
  • Aneuploidy — abnormal chromosome number (trisomy, monosomy) from nondisjunction.
  • Mutagen — agent that increases mutation rate (UV, X-rays, chemicals).
  • Germline vs somatic mutation — passed to offspring vs confined to the individual.

Worked example — Classifying mutations

The wild-type mRNA reads 5′–AUG GCA UUC UGG UAA–3′ encoding Met-Ala-Phe-Trp-(stop). Classify each mutant: (a) 5′–AUG GCC UUC UGG UAA–3′; (b) 5′–AUG GCA UAC UGG UAA–3′; (c) 5′–AUG GCA UUC UGA UAA–3′; (d) 5′–AUG GAU UCU GGU AA–3′.

(a) GCA → GCC, both Ala — silent. (b) UUC → UAC, Phe → Tyr — missense. (c) UGG → UGA, Trp → stop — nonsense, truncating the protein. (d) An A was deleted, shifting the reading frame — frameshift, scrambling all downstream codons.

  • Assuming every mutation is harmful — many are silent or neutral; a few are beneficial.
  • Treating frameshift and point mutations as equivalent — frameshifts usually have far larger effects.
  • Confusing germline (heritable) with somatic (not heritable) mutations.
  • Equating "mutation" with "cancer" — most cancers require multiple driver mutations in oncogenes and tumour suppressors.

🔬 Biotechnology & Genetic Engineering
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Biotechnology uses biological systems and molecules to make products or solve problems. Modern molecular biotechnology rests on a small set of foundational tools. Restriction enzymes (restriction endonucleases) cut DNA at specific palindromic recognition sequences — for example, EcoRI cleaves between G and A in GAATTC, leaving complementary "sticky ends." A second enzyme, DNA ligase, seals compatible ends. Together they enable recombinant DNA: a gene of interest from one organism is inserted into a vector (commonly a bacterial plasmid) and introduced into a host (transformation). If the vector carries an antibiotic-resistance gene, transformed bacteria can be selected on antibiotic plates.

The polymerase chain reaction (PCR), invented by Mullis in 1983, exponentially amplifies a defined DNA segment. Each cycle has three temperature steps: denaturation at ~95 °C (strands separate), annealing at ~55 °C (forward and reverse primers bind), and extension at ~72 °C (heat-stable Taq polymerase, from Thermus aquaticus, synthesises new strands). After n cycles, copies grow roughly as \(2^n\). PCR underpins forensic identification, paternity testing, disease diagnostics, and ancient-DNA studies. Gel electrophoresis sorts DNA fragments by size: negatively charged DNA migrates through an agarose gel toward the positive electrode, with shorter fragments travelling faster. DNA sequencing (Sanger dideoxy method or next-generation methods) reads the base order.

Genetic engineering applications include transgenic crops (Bt corn, golden rice with β-carotene), pharmaceutical production (human insulin in E. coli, factor VIII for haemophilia), gene therapy, and genome editing with CRISPR-Cas9, a guide-RNA-directed nuclease system that allows targeted edits in living cells. Each application carries STSE implications: ecological risk from transgene escape, equitable access to gene therapies, animal welfare in transgenic livestock, and ethical limits on human germline editing.

  • Restriction enzyme — sequence-specific DNA endonuclease (e.g., EcoRI, HindIII, BamHI).
  • Plasmid vector — small circular bacterial DNA used to carry recombinant inserts.
  • Sticky ends / blunt ends — complementary single-stranded overhangs / flush double-stranded ends.
  • Taq polymerase — thermostable DNA polymerase enabling PCR.
  • Gel electrophoresis — size-based separation of nucleic acids in an electric field.
  • CRISPR-Cas9 — RNA-guided endonuclease system for targeted genome editing.

Worked example — PCR amplification factor

You start a PCR with 100 copies of a target sequence and run 25 cycles at perfect efficiency. How many copies of the target are produced?

PCR doubles the number of target-defined copies each cycle: final copies \(\\approx N_0 \\times 2^n = 100 \\times 2^{25}\). \(2^{25} = 33\\,554\\,432\), so the yield is roughly \(3.36 \\times 10^9\) copies. In practice, efficiency is below 100% in later cycles as primers and nucleotides become limiting, so the real yield is somewhat less.

  • Saying restriction enzymes "cut DNA at random" — they cut only at their specific recognition site.
  • Forgetting primer design in PCR — primers must flank the target and be complementary to opposite strands.
  • Loading the gel at the positive electrode — DNA loads near the negative electrode and migrates toward the positive.
  • Treating CRISPR as "always safe" — off-target edits and ethical concerns must be evaluated.

⚖️ Homeostasis & Feedback
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Homeostasis (Cannon, 1929) is the maintenance of a relatively constant internal environment — temperature, pH, osmolarity, blood glucose, ion balance — despite external fluctuation. Cells function only within narrow physiological ranges, so homeostasis is not a luxury; it is the cost of being alive. Every homeostatic system has four parts. A set point defines the target value. Sensors (receptors) detect deviations. A control centre (often the hypothalamus or brainstem) compares input to the set point and issues a signal. Effectors (muscles, glands) carry out the correction. Information flows in loops, not lines.

Most physiological control uses negative feedback: an effector action opposes the original change. When core temperature rises, sweat glands secrete and skin vessels dilate, dumping heat; when it falls, shivering and vasoconstriction generate and retain heat. Negative feedback produces oscillation around the set point, never perfect constancy — your body temperature swings about 0.5 °C through the day. Positive feedback is rarer because it amplifies a change away from the set point. Useful examples are limited and self-terminating: oxytocin during childbirth (each contraction stretches the cervix, triggering more oxytocin until birth), the clotting cascade (each clotting factor activates the next until a stable clot forms), and the action-potential upstroke (sodium influx triggers more sodium channels to open).

Homeostasis can fail. A loss of glucose regulation produces diabetes mellitus; failure of body-temperature regulation in extreme cold causes hypothermia; renal failure compromises ion and water balance. Many disease states are best described as homeostatic failures in specific control loops.

  • Set point — physiological target value (e.g., 37 °C, pH 7.35–7.45).
  • Negative feedback — corrective response opposing the deviation; stabilising.
  • Positive feedback — amplifying response; self-terminating after a specific event.
  • Receptor / effector — sensor / structure that carries out the response.
  • Hypothalamus — central control hub for temperature, hunger, thirst, hormones.
  • Acclimation — physiological adjustment to chronic environmental change.

Worked example — Mapping a feedback loop

You step outside on a –10 °C day. Identify the stimulus, receptor, control centre, effectors, and response in the feedback loop, and classify it as positive or negative.

Stimulus: cold air drops skin and core temperature. Receptors: peripheral thermoreceptors in skin and central thermoreceptors in the hypothalamus. Control centre: anterior/posterior hypothalamus. Effectors: skeletal muscles (shivering thermogenesis), arterioles (vasoconstriction), arrector pili (piloerection), and over time the thyroid (raises metabolic rate). Response: heat production rises and heat loss falls, returning core temperature toward 37 °C. This is negative feedback — the response opposes the original deviation.

  • Calling negative feedback "bad" — negative refers to direction (opposing), not value.
  • Treating childbirth oxytocin as ongoing positive feedback — it ends when the baby is delivered.
  • Forgetting that homeostasis oscillates around a set point rather than holding it perfectly.
  • Mixing up receptor (detector) and effector (responder).

💧 Osmoregulation
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Osmoregulation is the control of body-fluid composition — water content, solute concentration, and ion balance — within tolerable limits. In humans the principal osmoregulatory organ is the kidney. Each kidney contains roughly one million nephrons, the functional units. A nephron's filtration unit, the glomerulus, sits inside Bowman's capsule; blood pressure forces water and small solutes (ions, glucose, amino acids, urea) out of the glomerular capillaries into the capsule, producing a protein-free filtrate at roughly 180 L/day. From there the filtrate passes through the proximal convoluted tubule, the loop of Henle, the distal convoluted tubule, and the collecting duct, with selective reabsorption and secretion all along the way.

The proximal tubule reabsorbs about 65% of filtered water and all of the glucose and amino acids through active transport coupled to \(Na^+\). The loop of Henle creates a steep osmotic gradient in the medulla via countercurrent multiplication: the descending limb is water-permeable but solute-impermeable (water leaves into the salty medulla), the ascending limb is the reverse (\(Na^+\) and \(Cl^-\) actively leave but water does not). The distal tubule fine-tunes ion balance under the control of aldosterone (adrenal cortex), which increases \(Na^+\) reabsorption and \(K^+\) excretion. The final water adjustment happens in the collecting duct under the control of antidiuretic hormone (ADH / vasopressin), released from the posterior pituitary in response to high blood osmolarity. ADH inserts aquaporin-2 channels into the duct membrane, allowing water to be reabsorbed and producing concentrated urine. When blood is dilute, ADH falls, aquaporins are withdrawn, and dilute urine is produced.

The renin–angiotensin–aldosterone system (RAAS) handles low blood pressure or volume: renin from the kidney converts angiotensinogen to angiotensin I, which is then converted to angiotensin II by ACE in the lungs. Angiotensin II vasoconstricts and stimulates aldosterone, restoring pressure.

  • Nephron — functional unit of the kidney; ~1 million per organ.
  • Glomerulus / Bowman's capsule — capillary tuft / filtration cup of the nephron.
  • Loop of Henle — creates medullary osmotic gradient through countercurrent multiplication.
  • ADH / aldosterone — water-retaining / sodium-retaining hormones.
  • Aquaporin — water channel inserted by ADH into the collecting duct.
  • RAAS — renin–angiotensin–aldosterone system regulating blood pressure and volume.

Worked example — Predicting urine concentration after dehydration

A hiker has been sweating for hours and has not drunk water. Predict the changes in plasma osmolarity, ADH release, aquaporin insertion, and urine concentration.

Sweating loses water faster than solutes, so plasma osmolarity rises. Osmoreceptors in the hypothalamus detect this and trigger ADH release from the posterior pituitary. ADH binds collecting-duct cells, inserting aquaporin-2 channels. Water is reabsorbed into the medullary interstitium, leaving small volumes of highly concentrated urine. Plasma osmolarity falls back toward 290 mOsm/L — classic negative feedback.

  • Saying ADH "adds water to the blood" directly — ADH increases water reabsorption from filtrate by inserting aquaporins.
  • Mixing up aldosterone (\(Na^+\)) and ADH (water).
  • Forgetting that glucose appears in urine (glucosuria) only when filtered glucose exceeds the proximal tubule's reabsorption capacity (diabetes).
  • Reversing the loop of Henle: descending limb is water-permeable; ascending limb is solute-permeable.

🧬 Endocrine System & Hormones
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The endocrine system coordinates long-distance, slow-onset, long-lasting control of body function through hormones — chemical messengers secreted into the bloodstream by ductless glands. Compared to the nervous system, endocrine signalling is broader, slower, and persists for minutes to days. Major endocrine glands include the hypothalamus, anterior and posterior pituitary, thyroid, parathyroids, adrenal cortex and medulla, pancreas (islets of Langerhans), ovaries, and testes. Each gland secretes specific hormones that act only on cells bearing the matching receptor; specificity arises from the receptor, not from the bloodstream.

Hormones come in two structural classes that dictate how they signal. Steroid hormones (cortisol, aldosterone, oestrogen, testosterone) are lipid-soluble, diffuse through the plasma membrane, and bind intracellular receptors that act as transcription factors — slow but lasting effects on gene expression. Peptide and amine hormones (insulin, glucagon, ADH, adrenaline) are water-soluble and bind cell-surface receptors, triggering second-messenger cascades (cAMP, IP₃, \(Ca^{2+}\)) — rapid but transient effects on existing proteins. Adrenaline binding to a β-adrenergic receptor activates a G-protein, which activates adenylyl cyclase, which raises cAMP, which activates protein kinase A — a signal-amplifying cascade in which one hormone molecule can change the activity of thousands of enzymes.

The hypothalamic–pituitary axis is the master command structure. The hypothalamus releases tropic hormones (TRH, CRH, GnRH) that signal the anterior pituitary, which in turn releases its own tropic hormones (TSH, ACTH, FSH/LH, GH, prolactin) targeting peripheral glands. Negative feedback closes most loops: high circulating thyroid hormone (\(T_3, T_4\)) inhibits both TRH and TSH, preventing runaway secretion.

  • Hormone — blood-borne chemical signal acting on distant target cells.
  • Steroid vs peptide hormone — lipid-soluble, intracellular receptor / water-soluble, cell-surface receptor.
  • Second messenger — intracellular signal (cAMP, IP₃, \(Ca^{2+}\)) propagating a peptide-hormone signal.
  • Tropic hormone — hormone whose target is another endocrine gland (e.g., TSH targets thyroid).
  • Hypothalamic-pituitary axis — central control network for most endocrine glands.
  • Negative feedback regulation — circulating hormone level inhibits its own release.

Worked example — Tracing the thyroid axis

Trace the hormonal cascade that raises basal metabolic rate when body temperature falls, naming each gland, hormone, and the final feedback step.

Cold detected by hypothalamus → hypothalamus secretes TRH (thyrotropin-releasing hormone) → anterior pituitary secretes TSH (thyroid-stimulating hormone) → thyroid secretes \(T_3\) and \(T_4\) → target cells increase metabolic rate (more mitochondria, more thermogenesis). Negative feedback: rising \(T_3/T_4\) inhibits both TRH (hypothalamus) and TSH (pituitary), preventing overshoot. This is a textbook three-tier endocrine axis with negative feedback at two levels.

  • Confusing exocrine (ducted, e.g., sweat) with endocrine (ductless) glands.
  • Saying all hormones act fast — steroid hormones change gene expression and act over hours.
  • Forgetting that target specificity depends on receptors, not on hormone routing.
  • Mixing up the anterior pituitary (synthesises its own hormones) with the posterior pituitary (stores ADH and oxytocin made in the hypothalamus).

⚡ Nervous System
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The nervous system provides fast, targeted, short-acting control through electrical and chemical signalling in neurons. The two branches are the central nervous system (CNS) — brain and spinal cord — and the peripheral nervous system (PNS), which carries sensory information into the CNS and motor commands out. The PNS subdivides into somatic (voluntary skeletal muscle) and autonomic (involuntary smooth muscle, cardiac muscle, glands) divisions; the autonomic system further splits into sympathetic (fight-or-flight) and parasympathetic (rest-and-digest) branches that usually act antagonistically.

A neuron has three functional regions. Dendrites receive signals; the cell body (soma) integrates them; the axon conducts action potentials to the synaptic terminals. At rest the inside of the axon is about −70 mV relative to outside, maintained by the \(Na^+/K^+\) pump (3 \(Na^+\) out, 2 \(K^+\) in per ATP) and by potassium-selective leak channels. When a stimulus depolarises the membrane past threshold (~−55 mV), voltage-gated \(Na^+\) channels open: \(Na^+\) rushes in, the membrane swings to +30 mV — this is the rising phase of the action potential. \(Na^+\) channels then inactivate and voltage-gated \(K^+\) channels open, \(K^+\) leaves, and the membrane repolarises (and briefly hyperpolarises) before settling back. Action potentials are all-or-nothing and propagate unidirectionally; in myelinated axons they jump between nodes of Ranvier in saltatory conduction, dramatically increasing speed.

At the synapse, the arriving action potential opens voltage-gated \(Ca^{2+}\) channels in the presynaptic terminal; \(Ca^{2+}\) triggers vesicle fusion and release of neurotransmitter (acetylcholine, glutamate, GABA, dopamine, serotonin, norepinephrine) into the synaptic cleft. The neurotransmitter binds postsynaptic receptors and causes either depolarisation (excitatory, EPSP) or hyperpolarisation (inhibitory, IPSP). The signal is terminated by enzymatic breakdown (e.g., acetylcholinesterase) or reuptake (e.g., SSRIs block serotonin reuptake).

  • Resting potential — ~−70 mV, maintained by \(Na^+/K^+\) pump and \(K^+\) leak.
  • Action potential — all-or-nothing depolarisation triggered above threshold.
  • Myelin sheath / nodes of Ranvier — insulating wrap / gaps where APs regenerate.
  • Saltatory conduction — jumping conduction along myelinated axons.
  • Synaptic vesicle / neurotransmitter — vesicle storing chemical messenger / messenger released at synapses.
  • EPSP / IPSP — excitatory / inhibitory postsynaptic potential.

Worked example — Why myelination matters

Compare the conduction speed of an unmyelinated 1 µm-diameter axon (~1 m/s) with a myelinated 1 µm axon (~50–100 m/s) and explain the underlying mechanism.

In an unmyelinated axon every patch of membrane must regenerate the action potential, which is slow. Myelin (made by oligodendrocytes in the CNS and Schwann cells in the PNS) electrically insulates the axon. Voltage-gated \(Na^+\) channels cluster at nodes of Ranvier, so the action potential effectively "jumps" from node to node — saltatory conduction. The same diameter axon conducts ~50–100× faster when myelinated. This is why demyelinating disease (multiple sclerosis) causes weakness and conduction delays.

  • Saying \(Na^+\) channels open during repolarisation — they inactivate; \(K^+\) channels open in repolarisation.
  • Treating action potentials as graded — they are all-or-nothing once threshold is reached.
  • Confusing synaptic transmission direction — neurotransmitter flows pre → post.
  • Forgetting the absolute refractory period, which prevents backward propagation.

🩸 Blood Glucose Regulation
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Blood glucose is one of the most tightly regulated physiological variables in the body. The normal fasting range is 4–6 mmol/L (about 70–110 mg/dL). Falling below it (hypoglycaemia) starves the brain — the only organ that under normal conditions cannot use fatty acids for fuel — and risks coma. Sustained elevation (hyperglycaemia) damages blood vessels, nerves, and kidneys. The pancreas is the central regulator, with two cell populations in the islets of Langerhans secreting hormones with opposing effects: α-cells release glucagon, and β-cells release insulin.

After a meal, blood glucose rises. β-cells sense the rise and release insulin. Insulin binds receptor tyrosine kinases on most body cells; in muscle and adipose tissue, this triggers translocation of GLUT4 glucose transporters from intracellular vesicles to the plasma membrane, allowing glucose uptake. In the liver, insulin stimulates glycogenesis (glucose polymerised to glycogen for storage) and lipogenesis. Blood glucose falls back to baseline — a classic negative-feedback loop. Between meals or during exercise, glucose falls. α-cells sense the drop and release glucagon. Glucagon acts mainly on the liver, stimulating glycogenolysis (breakdown of glycogen) and gluconeogenesis (synthesis of new glucose from amino acids, lactate, and glycerol) — restoring blood glucose. Adrenaline and cortisol reinforce glucagon's effect during stress.

Failures of this loop produce diabetes mellitus. Type 1 diabetes is autoimmune destruction of β-cells, so insulin production fails; treatment requires injected insulin. Type 2 diabetes is insulin resistance — receptors and downstream signalling are blunted, so even normal or elevated insulin cannot move glucose into cells. It is closely linked to obesity, inactivity, and genetics, and is managed by lifestyle change, oral hypoglycaemics, and sometimes insulin.

  • Insulin — β-cell hormone that lowers blood glucose.
  • Glucagon — α-cell hormone that raises blood glucose.
  • Glycogenesis / glycogenolysis — synthesis / breakdown of glycogen.
  • Gluconeogenesis — synthesis of new glucose from non-carbohydrate precursors.
  • GLUT4 — insulin-regulated glucose transporter in muscle and fat.
  • Type 1 vs Type 2 diabetes — autoimmune insulin deficiency vs insulin resistance.

Worked example — Tracing the post-meal response

You eat a bowl of pasta. Trace the hormonal and cellular response that returns blood glucose to baseline.

Carbohydrate is digested to glucose and absorbed across the small-intestinal epithelium into blood. Blood glucose rises to ~8–10 mmol/L. β-cells of the pancreatic islets detect the rise and release insulin into the bloodstream. Insulin binds receptors on muscle and adipose cells, triggering GLUT4 vesicle fusion at the membrane — glucose is taken up. In the liver, insulin stimulates glycogen synthase, storing glucose as glycogen. As blood glucose returns to ~5 mmol/L, β-cells reduce insulin release and α-cells increase glucagon release slightly to prevent overshoot — negative feedback.

  • Saying insulin "produces glucose" — insulin lowers blood glucose by promoting uptake and storage.
  • Confusing glycogenesis (storage) with gluconeogenesis (making new glucose from non-sugars).
  • Treating Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes as the same disease — mechanisms and treatments differ.
  • Forgetting that α-cells produce glucagon and β-cells produce insulin (β raises ↓, α raises ↑).

📈 Exponential & Logistic Population Growth
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

A population is a group of organisms of the same species living in a defined area and capable of interbreeding. Population ecology asks how and why populations change over time. Four processes drive change: births (natality, B), deaths (mortality, D), immigration (I), and emigration (E). The change in population size is \(\\Delta N = (B + I) - (D + E)\). When resources are unlimited, populations grow exponentially: \(\\frac{dN}{dt} = rN\), where \(r\) is the per-capita rate of increase. Integrated, this gives \(N_t = N_0 e^{rt}\) — a J-shaped curve that looks straight on a semi-log plot. Bacteria splitting every 20 minutes in a fresh nutrient broth, the early-stage colonisation of cleared land, and the first few decades of an invasive-species outbreak all approximate exponential growth.

Real environments have limits. Food, water, nesting sites, sunlight, predators, and disease impose a carrying capacity \(K\) — the maximum population the environment can sustain indefinitely. The logistic growth model adds this constraint: \(\\frac{dN}{dt} = rN\\left(\\frac{K - N}{K}\\right)\). When \(N\) is much smaller than \(K\), the term in brackets is near 1 and growth is nearly exponential. As \(N\) approaches \(K\), the brackets shrink toward zero and growth slows. If \(N\) exceeds \(K\), the term goes negative and the population declines. The resulting curve is S-shaped (sigmoidal).

Limiting factors are density-dependent (intensify as the population grows — competition for food, predation, infectious disease) or density-independent (act regardless of size — droughts, fires, hurricanes, cold snaps). Species also fall along an r-selected / K-selected continuum: r-strategists (mosquitoes, dandelions) produce many small offspring with little parental care and thrive in disturbed habitats; K-strategists (elephants, oak trees, humans) produce few large offspring with heavy investment and dominate stable habitats near \(K\). Population survivorship curves capture mortality patterns: Type I (low juvenile mortality, late-life decline, e.g., humans), Type II (constant mortality, e.g., many birds), Type III (high juvenile mortality, e.g., oysters, frogs).

  • Carrying capacity (\(K\)) — maximum sustainable population size.
  • Intrinsic rate of increase (\(r\)) — per-capita growth under ideal conditions.
  • Exponential vs logistic growth — unlimited (J-curve) vs limited (S-curve).
  • Density-dependent vs independent — strength scales with population size or not.
  • r-strategist / K-strategist — many cheap offspring vs few expensive offspring.
  • Survivorship curve — graphical depiction of cohort survival over age.

Worked example — Calculating exponential and logistic growth rates

A population of 1 000 deer has an intrinsic rate of increase \(r = 0.15\) yr⁻¹ and a carrying capacity \(K = 5\\,000\). Compute (a) the instantaneous growth rate under exponential conditions and (b) under logistic conditions at the current size.

(a) \(\\frac{dN}{dt} = rN = 0.15 \\times 1000 = 150\) deer/yr. (b) \(\\frac{dN}{dt} = rN\\,\\frac{K - N}{K} = 0.15 \\times 1000 \\times \\frac{5000 - 1000}{5000} = 150 \\times 0.8 = 120\) deer/yr. As \(N\) approaches \(K\), the logistic rate slows toward zero; at \(N = K\) the population is stable.

  • Reading a J-curve on a semi-log plot as linear growth — exponential growth is linear ONLY after a log transformation.
  • Confusing carrying capacity with the maximum ever observed value — \(K\) is the sustainable level.
  • Treating "r-selected" as inferior — both strategies are evolutionarily successful in their respective environments.
  • Forgetting that \(\\frac{dN}{dt}\) can be negative if \(N > K\).

🦁 Community Ecology & Predator-Prey
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

A community is the assemblage of all populations that interact in a given area. Community ecology asks how these populations are connected and how their interactions shape diversity, distribution, and stability. The major interactions are competition (both species are harmed when they share a limiting resource), predation (one benefits, one is killed), herbivory (a special case of predation on plants), parasitism (one benefits, the host is harmed but usually not killed), mutualism (both benefit, e.g., bees and flowers, mycorrhizae and tree roots), and commensalism (one benefits, the other is unaffected).

Predator-prey dynamics often produce coupled oscillations. As prey numbers rise, predators have abundant food and reproduce; growing predator numbers depress prey; reduced prey leads to predator decline; predator decline allows prey to recover — and the cycle repeats. The Lotka–Volterra equations \(\\frac{dN_{\\text{prey}}}{dt} = rN_{\\text{prey}} - aN_{\\text{prey}}N_{\\text{pred}}\) and \(\\frac{dN_{\\text{pred}}}{dt} = baN_{\\text{prey}}N_{\\text{pred}} - mN_{\\text{pred}}\) model this with characteristic out-of-phase cycles. The classic Hudson's Bay Company fur-record data for snowshoe hare and Canada lynx, spanning over a century, shows ~10-year oscillations where lynx peaks lag hare peaks by roughly 1–2 years.

Competition has two flavours: interspecific (between species) and intraspecific (within a species, usually more intense because needs are identical). The competitive exclusion principle (Gause) states that two species cannot coexist indefinitely if they occupy identical ecological niches; one will out-compete the other. Coexistence is possible through resource partitioning, in which species subdivide the niche (different feeding times, depths, or prey sizes). MacArthur's classic study of five warbler species in spruce trees showed each species feeding in a different vertical zone, an example of partitioning. Keystone species exert disproportionately large effects on community structure relative to their abundance (sea otters maintain kelp forests by suppressing urchins); invasive species can collapse community structure by lacking natural enemies in their new range.

  • Ecological niche — total set of biotic and abiotic conditions a species uses.
  • Competitive exclusion — two species with identical niches cannot stably coexist.
  • Resource partitioning — niche subdivision allowing coexistence.
  • Predator-prey cycle — coupled oscillations with predator peaks lagging prey peaks.
  • Symbiosis types — mutualism (+/+), commensalism (+/0), parasitism (+/−).
  • Keystone species — species whose impact greatly exceeds its abundance.

Worked example — Interpreting a predator-prey graph

A graph shows snowshoe hare populations peaking in 1865, 1875, 1885, and 1895. Lynx populations peak in 1866, 1876, 1886, and 1896. Explain the phase relationship and predict what happens when the hare population crashes.

Lynx peaks lag hare peaks by ~1 year — classic predator-prey coupling. The lag arises because predator reproduction depends on abundant prey, and predator decline follows prey decline. When hares crash, lynx food supply collapses; some lynx starve, others fail to reproduce, and lynx numbers fall sharply within 1–2 years. Reduced predation then lets hares recover, restarting the cycle.

  • Saying predator-prey graphs are "in phase" — predator peaks lag prey peaks.
  • Confusing commensalism (+/0) with mutualism (+/+).
  • Forgetting that intraspecific competition is usually stronger than interspecific.
  • Treating every conspicuous predator as a keystone species — keystone status depends on disproportionate impact.

🌳 Ecological Succession
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Ecological succession is the directional, often predictable, change in community composition over time following disturbance or colonisation of new ground. Two main types are recognised. Primary succession begins on bare substrate where no soil exists — newly cooled lava flows, glacial moraines exposed by retreating ice, or bare rock revealed by landslides. The first colonisers, called pioneer species, are usually lichens and cyanobacteria that break down rock and add organic matter; mosses and small herbs follow as a thin soil forms; grasses and shrubs come next; finally, trees of increasing size establish until a mature climax community is reached. The timescale is centuries to millennia. Secondary succession begins on disturbed ground where soil already exists — after fire, logging, abandoned farmland, or flooding. Because soil and seed banks are present, recovery is much faster (decades to a few centuries) and starts with grasses and herbs rather than lichens.

Two contrasting views explain succession's directionality. The classic Clementsian view treats the community as a "superorganism" with a predictable, deterministic climax. The modern Gleasonian view emphasises individual species' responses to local conditions and stochastic processes, so multiple climax outcomes are possible. Mechanisms of replacement include facilitation (early species modify the environment in ways that favour later species — nitrogen-fixing alder enriches soil for spruce), tolerance (later species can establish under early ones without help or harm), and inhibition (early species block later ones until disturbance reopens the system).

Disturbance is not always negative; intermediate disturbance hypothesis (Connell) predicts maximum diversity at moderate disturbance frequencies — frequent enough to prevent competitive exclusion by dominant species, rare enough to allow late-successional species to mature. Fire-adapted ecosystems (jack pine forests, prairies) depend on periodic burns for regeneration; suppressing fire entirely allows fuel to build up and eventually produces catastrophic crown fires.

  • Primary succession — starts on bare rock or new substrate without soil.
  • Secondary succession — starts on disturbed ground where soil persists.
  • Pioneer species — first colonisers (lichens, mosses, hardy weeds).
  • Climax community — relatively stable end-stage assemblage.
  • Facilitation / tolerance / inhibition — mechanisms of species replacement.
  • Intermediate disturbance hypothesis — diversity peaks at moderate disturbance.

Worked example — Classifying a succession

A forest in Algonquin Park burns in a lightning fire. Over the next 80 years, fireweed and grasses dominate, then aspen and birch, then white pine and maple. Identify the succession type and the mechanism driving the transition from fireweed to trees.

Soil and root systems survived the fire, so this is secondary succession. Fireweed and grasses are pioneer-like species that stabilise soil and contribute organic matter — they facilitate aspen and birch by modifying nutrient and light conditions. As aspen/birch grow they shade the herbaceous layer, suppressing fireweed (an example of inhibition mixed with tolerance), and create conditions in which shade-tolerant pine and maple can establish. The white pine–maple stage approximates a climax community for the boreal-mixed-wood region.

  • Confusing primary with secondary succession — the key difference is the presence of soil.
  • Treating the climax community as eternal — disturbance resets succession repeatedly.
  • Forgetting that pioneer species are usually r-strategists and climax species are usually K-strategists.
  • Assuming succession always increases diversity linearly — diversity often peaks mid-succession.

🌍 Human Population Growth & Environmental Impact
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Human population has grown from roughly 1 billion in 1800 to over 8 billion in 2023 — a 700% increase in roughly two centuries. Three factors drove this expansion: the agricultural Green Revolution (synthetic fertilisers, irrigation, high-yield crops, mechanisation) boosted food supply; advances in public health (sanitation, vaccination, antibiotics) cut mortality, especially child mortality; and the industrial revolution provided energy and economic capacity to support larger populations. Growth has not been spatially or temporally even. Most developed countries have completed the demographic transition, in which high birth and death rates (Stage 1) drop sequentially through declining death rates (Stage 2, rapid growth), declining birth rates (Stage 3, slowing growth), and finally low birth and death rates (Stage 4, near-stable population). Many lower-income countries are in Stage 2 or 3, contributing most of current global growth.

Two measures matter for environmental impact: total population and per-capita resource use. The ecological footprint estimates the productive land and water area required to supply an individual or population's consumption and absorb its waste. Canadian per-capita ecological footprints (around 8 global hectares) are roughly 4× the global average and 30× a typical low-income-country footprint. The IPAT identity \(I = P \\times A \\times T\) (Impact = Population × Affluence × Technology) emphasises that environmental impact depends on consumption and technology, not population alone. A small wealthy population can have a larger total footprint than a large poor one.

Human activity is now a dominant geological force — the proposed Anthropocene. Land conversion for agriculture and cities has reduced wild habitat; CO₂ emissions from fossil fuels are driving climate change; nitrogen and phosphorus overload from fertiliser runoff produces eutrophic dead zones; plastic pollution permeates oceans; the current extinction rate runs 100–1000× background. Sustainability requires reducing per-capita impact (efficiency, renewable energy, dietary shifts) alongside policies that voluntarily slow population growth through education and reproductive health access.

  • Demographic transition — four-stage shift from high birth/death to low birth/death rates.
  • Age structure pyramid — graphical age-and-sex distribution predicting growth or decline.
  • Doubling time — \(t_d \\approx \\frac{70}{r\\,(\\%)}\) years for an exponentially growing population.
  • Ecological footprint — productive area required to sustain consumption levels.
  • IPAT — Impact = Population × Affluence × Technology framework.
  • Carrying capacity for humans — contested estimate (8–16 billion) depending on diet, technology, and equity.

Worked example — Doubling time calculation

A country has an annual population growth rate of 2.0%. Estimate how long it would take the population to double, and compare with a country growing at 0.5%/yr.

The rule of 70 (or "rule of 72") gives \(t_d \\approx 70 / r\\,(\\%)\). At 2.0% per year: \(t_d \\approx 70 / 2.0 = 35\) years. At 0.5% per year: \(t_d \\approx 70 / 0.5 = 140\) years. A four-fold difference in growth rate produces a four-fold difference in doubling time, illustrating how small changes in \(r\) compound enormously over decades.

  • Reading log-scale population graphs as linear — exponential curves appear linear ONLY after log transformation.
  • Equating population growth with environmental impact — affluence and technology are part of the IPAT equation.
  • Confusing the demographic transition's stages — Stage 2 is rapid growth from falling death rates; Stage 3 is slowing growth from falling birth rates.
  • Assuming Earth has a single, fixed human carrying capacity — \(K\) depends on diet, technology, equity, and what quality of life is accepted.
