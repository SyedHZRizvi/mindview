5.1 — Special Relativity
Einstein's postulates, time dilation, length contraction, mass-energy equivalence E=mc²
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

In 1905 Einstein proposed two postulates that overturned 200 years of Newtonian thinking. Postulate 1 (principle of relativity): the laws of physics are identical in all inertial reference frames. Postulate 2 (invariance of $c$): the speed of light in vacuum, $c$, is the same in every inertial frame, regardless of the motion of the source or observer. The second postulate flatly contradicts Galilean velocity addition; preserving both postulates forces us to abandon absolute simultaneity and absolute time. Space and time become coupled coordinates of a single 4-dimensional spacetime.

The dramatic consequences: time dilation — a clock moving at speed $v$ relative to an observer ticks slowly: $\Delta t=\gamma\Delta t_0$, where $\Delta t_0$ is the proper time (in the clock's rest frame) and the Lorentz factor $\gamma=1/\sqrt{1-v^2/c^2}\ge 1$. Length contraction — an object's length along its direction of motion appears shortened: $L=L_0/\gamma$, where $L_0$ is the proper length. Mass-energy equivalence — the total energy of a particle of rest mass $m$ moving at speed $v$ is $E=\gamma mc^2$, comprising rest energy $E_0=mc^2$ and relativistic kinetic energy $E_k=(\gamma-1)mc^2$. At low speeds ($v\ll c$, $\gamma\approx 1+v^2/(2c^2)$) this reduces to Newtonian $E_k=\tfrac12 mv^2$ — the bridge between classical and relativistic mechanics.

  • Lorentz factor $\gamma=1/\sqrt{1-v^2/c^2}$ (dimensionless); $\gamma=1$ at rest, $\to\infty$ as $v\to c$.
  • Proper time $\Delta t_0$ — measured in the frame where two events occur at the same place.
  • Proper length $L_0$ — measured in the rest frame of the object.
  • $E=mc^2$ — 1 kg ↔ 9×10¹⁶ J; basis of nuclear energy and particle physics.

Worked example — muon decay

A muon has a proper lifetime $\tau_0=2.2\,\mu$s. Cosmic-ray muons created at 15 km altitude travel at $v=0.995c$. Do they reach the ground before decaying?

Step 1 — Lorentz factor: $\gamma=1/\sqrt{1-0.995^2}=1/\sqrt{0.00998}=10.0$.

Step 2 — dilated lifetime as measured from the ground: $\tau=\gamma\tau_0=22\,\mu$s.

Step 3 — distance travelled before decay: $d=v\tau=(0.995)(3\times 10^8)(22\times 10^{-6})=6.6\times 10^3$ m, but exponential decay extends this; the experimental flux at sea level matches relativistic prediction, not the 660 m of classical physics.

Step 4 — alternative: in muon's frame, the 15 km mountain is contracted to $L_0/\gamma=1500$ m, easily covered in 5 μs < lifetime. Same physics, two viewpoints. ✓

  • Applying $\gamma$ formula at non-relativistic speeds — pointless; gives 1.000…; classical formulas suffice.
  • Saying "moving objects gain mass" — modern convention treats $m$ as invariant rest mass; what increases is $\gamma m$ (relativistic momentum and energy).
  • Mixing proper and improper time/length — always identify which observer measures the "proper" quantity.
  • Using Galilean velocity addition — must use $u'=(u-v)/(1-uv/c^2)$ at high speeds.

Curriculum expectation: Strand F, Expectation F2.3 — "solve problems related to Einstein's theory of special relativity (e.g., problems involving time dilation, length contraction, and mass-energy equivalence)."

5.2 — Photoelectric Effect & Quantum Mechanics
Photons, work function, threshold frequency, de Broglie wavelength, wave-particle duality
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Despite the success of Maxwell's wave theory of light, three early-1900s experiments forced the introduction of quanta. The most decisive was the photoelectric effect: light incident on a metal ejects electrons, but only if the frequency $f$ exceeds a metal-specific threshold $f_0$, regardless of intensity. Einstein (1905) explained this by proposing that light comes in discrete bundles called photons, each carrying energy $E=hf$, where $h=6.626\times 10^{-34}$ J·s is Planck's constant. The maximum kinetic energy of an ejected electron is $E_{k,max}=hf-\phi$, where $\phi$ is the work function (the minimum energy needed to liberate an electron from that metal). Below threshold, $hf<\phi$, no electrons emerge no matter how bright the light. This won Einstein the 1921 Nobel Prize.

If light has particle properties, by symmetry matter should have wave properties. De Broglie (1924) postulated that every particle has a wavelength $\lambda=h/p$, where $p$ is its momentum. For electrons accelerated through a potential difference $V$, $p=\sqrt{2meV}$, giving $\lambda$ in the angstrom range — confirmed in 1927 when Davisson and Germer observed electron diffraction from a crystal lattice. Modern electron microscopes exploit this. Wave-particle duality is now a foundational principle: every quantum object is described by a wave function whose intensity gives the probability of finding the particle.

  • Planck's constant $h=6.626\times 10^{-34}$ J·s; reduced $\hbar=h/(2\pi)$.
  • Photon energy: $E=hf=hc/\lambda$ (J); often quoted in eV: $E_\text{eV}=1240/\lambda_\text{nm}$.
  • Photoelectric equation: $E_{k,max}=hf-\phi=h(f-f_0)$, with $\phi=hf_0$.
  • de Broglie wavelength: $\lambda=h/p$ — applies to any particle.

Worked example — photoelectrons from sodium

Sodium has $\phi=2.28$ eV. Light of $\lambda=400$ nm shines on it. Find the maximum KE of ejected photoelectrons and the cutoff frequency.

Step 1 — photon energy: $E=hc/\lambda=(6.626\times 10^{-34})(3\times 10^8)/(400\times 10^{-9})=4.97\times 10^{-19}$ J = 3.10 eV.

Step 2 — max KE: $E_{k,max}=E-\phi=3.10-2.28=0.82$ eV = $1.31\times 10^{-19}$ J.

Step 3 — max electron speed: $v=\sqrt{2E_k/m_e}=\sqrt{2(1.31\times 10^{-19})/(9.11\times 10^{-31})}=5.37\times 10^5$ m/s.

Step 4 — cutoff frequency: $f_0=\phi/h=(2.28\times 1.602\times 10^{-19})/(6.626\times 10^{-34})=5.51\times 10^{14}$ Hz (cutoff $\lambda_0=544$ nm, green-yellow). ✓

  • Confusing photon energy with frequency — $E$ has units of J or eV; $f$ has units of Hz. They differ by a factor of $h$.
  • Thinking brighter light gives more energetic electrons — intensity gives more electrons (more photons), but $E_{k,max}$ depends only on $f$.
  • Using $E=hf$ for material particles — for particles, energy is $\tfrac12 mv^2$ (or relativistic), not $hf$ directly; their wavelength is $h/p$.
  • Forgetting $\phi$ varies with metal — Cs ≈ 2.1 eV, Na ≈ 2.3 eV, Cu ≈ 4.7 eV; affects threshold.

Curriculum expectation: Strand F, Expectation F2.4 — "analyse data related to the photoelectric effect, and use the data to determine Planck's constant and the work function of a metal."

5.3 — Nuclear Physics & Radioactivity
Atomic models, nuclear reactions, alpha/beta/gamma decay, half-life, fission and fusion
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The atomic nucleus contains protons (charge $+e$, mass ≈ 1.673×10⁻²⁷ kg) and neutrons (no charge, mass ≈ 1.675×10⁻²⁷ kg), collectively called nucleons, bound together by the short-range strong nuclear force. A nuclide is labeled $^A_Z X$ where $Z$ is the atomic number (number of protons), $A$ is the mass number (total nucleons), and $N=A-Z$ is the number of neutrons. Isotopes of an element share $Z$ but differ in $N$. The mass of a nucleus is less than the sum of its constituents' masses; this mass defect $\Delta m$ corresponds (via $E=\Delta mc^2$) to the binding energy holding the nucleus together. Iron-56 has the highest binding energy per nucleon (~8.8 MeV), explaining why fusion of light nuclei and fission of heavy ones both release energy.

Unstable nuclei decay by three main modes. Alpha decay: emission of an $\alpha$ particle ($^4_2\text{He}$ nucleus); $A$ decreases by 4, $Z$ by 2. Beta-minus decay: a neutron converts to a proton plus an electron (the $\beta^-$ particle) and an antineutrino; $A$ unchanged, $Z$ increases by 1. Gamma decay: high-energy photon emission from an excited nucleus; $A$ and $Z$ unchanged. Each decay obeys conservation of charge, of nucleon number, and of energy-momentum. Decay is a statistical process: the number of undecayed nuclei follows $N(t)=N_0(1/2)^{t/T_{1/2}}$, where $T_{1/2}$ is the half-life — the time for half of a population to decay. Half-lives span from microseconds to billions of years.

  • Atomic mass unit: 1 u = 1.661×10⁻²⁷ kg ↔ 931.5 MeV/c² of energy.
  • Decay law: $N(t)=N_0 e^{-\lambda t}=N_0(1/2)^{t/T_{1/2}}$; decay constant $\lambda=\ln 2/T_{1/2}$.
  • Activity $A=\lambda N$ (Bq = decays/s); 1 Ci = 3.7×10¹⁰ Bq.
  • Fission — heavy nucleus splits (e.g. U-235); fusion — light nuclei combine (e.g. D + T → He-4 + n).

Worked example — radiocarbon dating

$^{14}\text{C}$ has $T_{1/2}=5730$ y. A wooden artifact contains $^{14}\text{C}$ at 25% of the ratio in living wood. Estimate its age.

Step 1 — fraction remaining: $N/N_0=0.25=(1/2)^2$, so 2 half-lives have passed.

Step 2 — age: $t=2\times 5730=11{,}460$ y.

Step 3 — energy-defect cross-check: $^{14}\text{C}\to {}^{14}\text{N}+\beta^-+\bar\nu_e$. Mass excess: $\Delta m c^2=0.156$ MeV released per decay.

Step 4 — uncertainty: dating reliable to ~50,000 y because $^{14}\text{C}$ becomes too sparse beyond ~9 half-lives.

  • Forgetting to conserve $A$ and $Z$ in a decay equation — both must balance on each side.
  • Confusing $\beta^-$ (electron) and $\beta^+$ (positron) decay — they change $Z$ in opposite directions.
  • Linear scaling of decay — radioactive decay is exponential, not linear; "half-life" times two does NOT give zero remaining.
  • Mixing fission and fusion — both release energy because both move toward Fe-56 on the binding-energy curve, but from opposite ends.

Curriculum expectation: Strand F, Expectation F3.4 — "describe the principal types of nuclear reactions (e.g., alpha, beta, and gamma decay; fission; fusion), and represent them using nuclear equations."
