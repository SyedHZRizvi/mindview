2.1 — Work and the Work-Energy Theorem
Work done by constant forces, kinetic energy, work-energy theorem
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Energy is a scalar bookkeeping quantity that — uniquely among physical concepts — is universally conserved. Work is the mechanism by which energy is transferred to or from an object by a force. For a constant force $\vec{F}$ acting through a displacement $\vec{d}$, work is the dot product $W=\vec{F}\cdot\vec{d}=Fd\cos\theta$. The $\cos\theta$ matters: only the component of force along the displacement does work. A force perpendicular to motion (like the normal force on a horizontally sliding block, or the centripetal force on circular motion) does zero work.

The work-energy theorem states that the net work done on an object equals the change in its kinetic energy: $W_{net}=\Delta E_k=\tfrac12 m v^2-\tfrac12 m v_0^2$. This is essentially Newton II integrated over distance, and it converts a complicated force-vs-time problem into a simpler force-vs-distance scalar one. For variable forces, work is the area under an $F$ vs. $x$ graph — that's why springs and other non-constant forces are handled through integration in calculus-based physics.

  • Work $W$ — SI unit joule (J); 1 J = 1 N·m = 1 kg·m²/s².
  • Kinetic energy $E_k=\tfrac12 mv^2$ (J); always non-negative; depends on $v^2$ so doubling speed quadruples KE.
  • Power $P=W/t=\vec{F}\cdot\vec{v}$ (W); 1 W = 1 J/s.
  • Dimensional check: $[E_k]=[\text{kg}][\text{m/s}]^2=[\text{kg·m}^2/\text{s}^2]=[\text{J}]$. ✓

Worked example — work and speed

A 1500 kg car accelerates from 10 m/s to 25 m/s. Find the net work done.

Step 1 — initial KE: $E_{k0}=\tfrac12(1500)(10)^2=75{,}000$ J.

Step 2 — final KE: $E_k=\tfrac12(1500)(25)^2=468{,}750$ J.

Step 3 — work-energy theorem: $W_{net}=\Delta E_k=468{,}750-75{,}000=3.94\times 10^5$ J.

Step 4 — if this happens in 8.0 s, average power $P=W/t=3.94\times 10^5/8.0=49{,}200$ W = 49.2 kW (about 66 hp).

  • Forgetting the $\cos\theta$ factor — only the parallel component does work.
  • Treating work as a vector — work is a scalar; it can be positive (energy in) or negative (energy out).
  • Saying "the centripetal force does work" — it is perpendicular to $\vec{v}$, so $W=0$.
  • Confusing average force × distance with $\int F\,dx$ — only equal for constant force.

Curriculum expectation: Strand C, Expectation C2.2 — "analyse, and solve problems involving, the relationships between work, kinetic energy, and potential energy."

2.2 — Potential Energy and Conservation of Energy
Gravitational potential energy, law of conservation of energy, energy transformations
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

A conservative force (gravity, spring, electrostatics) has the special property that the work it does between two points is independent of path. This lets us define a potential energy function $U$ such that $W_{cons}=-\Delta U$. The work done by gravity in lowering an object equals the loss of gravitational PE. Near Earth's surface where $g$ is effectively constant, $U_g=mgh$ with $h$ measured from any chosen reference; only changes in PE matter physically.

Combining the work-energy theorem with PE gives the law of conservation of mechanical energy: when only conservative forces act, $E_k+U=$ constant. If non-conservative forces (friction, applied force, air drag) act, the energy equation becomes $E_{k,0}+U_0+W_{nc}=E_k+U$, where $W_{nc}$ is positive if non-conservative forces add energy (e.g. a push) and negative if they remove it (friction). The broader law of conservation of energy says the total energy of a closed system — including thermal, chemical, electromagnetic, nuclear, etc. — is constant. Friction does not "destroy" energy; it converts mechanical energy into thermal energy.

  • Gravitational PE (near Earth): $U_g=mgh$ (J); reference height is arbitrary.
  • Mechanical energy $E_{mech}=E_k+U$ (J) — conserved iff only conservative forces act.
  • Thermal energy $Q$ — random KE of molecules; the "destination" of work done against friction.
  • Efficiency $\eta=E_{useful}/E_{input}\times 100\%$ — never exceeds 100% by the first law.

Worked example — roller coaster with friction

A 500 kg coaster car starts at rest at height 30 m, reaches the bottom with speed 20 m/s. How much energy was lost to friction?

Step 1 — initial energy: $E_0=mgh=500(9.81)(30)=1.47\times 10^5$ J (all PE).

Step 2 — final energy: $E_f=\tfrac12 mv^2=\tfrac12(500)(20)^2=1.00\times 10^5$ J (all KE).

Step 3 — energy "lost" (now thermal): $|W_{nc}|=E_0-E_f=4.7\times 10^4$ J.

Step 4 — efficiency: $\eta=E_f/E_0=68\%$. The remaining 32% became heat and sound.

  • Choosing two different reference heights for $U_0$ and $U_f$ — pick one and stick with it.
  • Saying "energy was lost" — it was transformed, never destroyed (first law).
  • Applying $E_k+U=$ const when friction is present — must include $W_{nc}$ term.
  • Confusing kinetic and potential energy signs — KE is always non-negative; gravitational PE near Earth can be negative if reference is above the object.

Curriculum expectation: Strand C, Expectation C2.3 — "use the law of conservation of energy to solve problems in a variety of contexts, including those involving efficiency."

2.3 — Elastic Potential Energy and Simple Harmonic Motion
Springs, Hooke's law, elastic PE, SHM, conservation with springs
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

An ideal spring obeys Hooke's law $F_s=-kx$, where $x$ is the displacement from the natural (unstretched) length and $k$ (N/m) is the spring constant — a stiffness rating. The minus sign is essential: the spring force is always a restoring force, pointing back toward equilibrium. Because $F$ varies linearly with $x$, the work done in stretching from 0 to $x$ is the triangular area under the $F$ vs $x$ graph: $W=\tfrac12 kx^2$, which we call the elastic potential energy $U_s=\tfrac12 kx^2$.

When a mass on a spring is displaced and released, the system undergoes simple harmonic motion (SHM): $x(t)=A\cos(\omega t+\phi)$, with angular frequency $\omega=\sqrt{k/m}$ and period $T=2\pi\sqrt{m/k}$. SHM is the prototype of every small-amplitude oscillation in physics — pendulums, atomic vibrations in solids, LC circuits — because near any stable equilibrium the restoring force is approximately linear (Taylor expansion). Energy oscillates between $E_k=\tfrac12 mv^2$ and $U_s=\tfrac12 kx^2$, while the total $E=\tfrac12 kA^2$ stays constant.

  • Spring constant $k$ (N/m); larger $k$ = stiffer spring.
  • Elastic PE $U_s=\tfrac12 kx^2$ (J) — quadratic in displacement.
  • Angular frequency $\omega=\sqrt{k/m}$ (rad/s); period $T=2\pi/\omega$ (s) — independent of amplitude.
  • Max speed in SHM: $v_{max}=\omega A$, at $x=0$.

Worked example — spring launches a block

A spring with $k=300$ N/m is compressed 0.15 m and released to launch a 0.50 kg block on a frictionless surface. Find its speed when the spring returns to natural length, and the period if it were attached.

Step 1 — energy conservation: $\tfrac12 k x^2=\tfrac12 m v^2$.

Step 2 — solve for $v$: $v=x\sqrt{k/m}=0.15\sqrt{300/0.50}=0.15(24.5)=3.67$ m/s.

Step 3 — if attached, period $T=2\pi\sqrt{m/k}=2\pi\sqrt{0.50/300}=0.256$ s.

Step 4 — frequency $f=1/T=3.91$ Hz; angular $\omega=24.5$ rad/s, matching the $\sqrt{k/m}$ above. ✓

  • Dropping the minus in Hooke's law in vector form — it makes the force a restoring force.
  • Forgetting the $\tfrac12$ in $U_s$ — area of a triangle, not a rectangle.
  • Thinking period depends on amplitude — for an ideal Hookean spring it does not.
  • Mixing $f$ (Hz) with $\omega$ (rad/s) — $\omega=2\pi f$.

Curriculum expectation: Strand C, Expectation C2.5 — "analyse, in qualitative and quantitative terms, the relationships among the kinetic and potential energies of a mass undergoing simple harmonic motion."

2.4 — Momentum and Impulse
Linear momentum, impulse-momentum theorem, conservation of momentum in 1D
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Linear momentum $\vec{p}=m\vec{v}$ is the proper way to express Newton's "quantity of motion." In fact Newton II is most precisely stated as $\vec{F}_{net}=d\vec{p}/dt$, which reduces to $m\vec{a}$ only when mass is constant (rockets, where fuel is expelled, are the famous counter-example). The integral of force over time is the impulse $\vec{J}=\int \vec{F}\,dt$, and the impulse-momentum theorem says $\vec{J}=\Delta\vec{p}$. This is why airbags work: spreading the same $\Delta p$ over a longer $\Delta t$ reduces the peak $\vec{F}$.

If no external force acts on a system, total momentum is conserved: $\sum\vec{p}_i = \sum\vec{p}_f$. This is independent of, and more general than, energy conservation — momentum is conserved in every collision (elastic, inelastic, explosive); kinetic energy is not. Because momentum is a vector, conservation applies component-by-component: $\sum p_x$ before = $\sum p_x$ after, and similarly for $y$.

  • Momentum $\vec{p}=m\vec{v}$ — SI unit kg·m/s, equivalent to N·s; vector quantity.
  • Impulse $\vec{J}=\vec{F}_{avg}\Delta t$ (N·s) — equals $\Delta\vec{p}$.
  • System — collection of objects you've chosen; "internal" forces between them cancel by Newton III.
  • Dimensional check: $[J]=[\text{N}][\text{s}]=[\text{kg·m/s}^2][\text{s}]=[\text{kg·m/s}]=[p]$. ✓

Worked example — recoil of a rifle

A 4.0 kg rifle fires a 0.020 kg bullet at 400 m/s. Find the recoil speed of the rifle.

Step 1 — system: rifle + bullet, both initially at rest, so $p_{total,i}=0$.

Step 2 — conservation: $0=m_b v_b+m_r v_r\Rightarrow v_r=-m_b v_b/m_r$.

Step 3 — compute: $v_r=-(0.020)(400)/4.0=-2.0$ m/s. Minus = opposite direction to bullet.

Step 4 — energy check: $E_{bullet}=1600$ J vs $E_{rifle}=8$ J — KE is NOT conserved (this is an explosion, gas pressure adds KE).

  • Treating momentum as a scalar — always a vector; direction matters.
  • Confusing momentum and kinetic energy — $p$ linear in $v$, $E_k$ quadratic in $v$.
  • Forgetting external forces — gravity, friction, etc. can change a system's total momentum.
  • Using $\vec{F}=m\vec{a}$ for variable-mass systems — must use $d\vec{p}/dt$ form.

Curriculum expectation: Strand C, Expectation C2.6 — "analyse, and solve problems involving, the law of conservation of momentum in one and two dimensions."

2.5 — Collisions in One and Two Dimensions
Elastic vs inelastic collisions, perfectly inelastic, 2D glancing collisions
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Collisions are classified by what happens to kinetic energy. In an elastic collision both momentum AND kinetic energy are conserved — billiard balls and ideal hard-sphere scattering are good approximations. In an inelastic collision only momentum is conserved; some KE is converted to heat, sound, or deformation. The extreme case is a perfectly inelastic collision where the objects stick together and move with a common final velocity; this loses the maximum possible KE consistent with momentum conservation.

For a 1-D elastic collision between $m_1$ (initial velocity $v_1$) and a stationary $m_2$, the conservation equations solve to $v_1'=(m_1-m_2)v_1/(m_1+m_2)$ and $v_2'=2m_1 v_1/(m_1+m_2)$. Key limits: if $m_1=m_2$, the projectile stops and the target takes its full velocity. If $m_1\ll m_2$ (ball off a wall), $v_1'\to -v_1$ (perfect bounce). In 2-D collisions, momentum conservation gives two scalar equations ($p_x$ and $p_y$); if the collision is also elastic, kinetic energy gives a third — that's three equations for the four unknowns of two final velocity vectors, so you need additional info (like a scattering angle).

  • Elastic: $\sum p$ conserved, $\sum E_k$ conserved.
  • Inelastic: $\sum p$ conserved, $\sum E_k$ decreases.
  • Perfectly inelastic: objects coalesce, common final $\vec{v}$; maximum KE loss.
  • Coefficient of restitution $e=|v_{2}'-v_1'|/|v_1-v_2|$ — 1 for elastic, 0 for perfectly inelastic.

Worked example — 2-D inelastic collision

A 1500 kg car moving east at 20 m/s collides and locks with a 1000 kg car moving north at 15 m/s. Find their common velocity.

Step 1 — $p_x$: $(1500)(20)+0=(2500)v_x' \Rightarrow v_x'=12.0$ m/s east.

Step 2 — $p_y$: $0+(1000)(15)=(2500)v_y' \Rightarrow v_y'=6.0$ m/s north.

Step 3 — magnitude: $v'=\sqrt{12^2+6^2}=13.4$ m/s.

Step 4 — direction: $\theta=\tan^{-1}(6/12)=26.6°$ N of E. Check KE loss: $E_{k,i}=3.00\times 10^5+1.13\times 10^5=4.13\times 10^5$ J; $E_{k,f}=\tfrac12(2500)(13.4)^2=2.24\times 10^5$ J. Lost $\approx$ 46% to deformation/heat. ✓

  • Assuming KE is conserved in every collision — only elastic ones.
  • Adding momenta as scalars in 2-D — they are vectors; do $x$ and $y$ separately.
  • Forgetting sign conventions — a head-on collision in 1-D needs opposite signs.
  • Confusing elastic with perfectly inelastic — opposite ends of the spectrum.

Curriculum expectation: Strand C, Expectation C2.7 — "analyse, with the aid of vector diagrams, the linear momentum of a collection of objects, and apply quantitatively the law of conservation of linear momentum."
