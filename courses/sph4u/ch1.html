1.1 — Kinematics — Motion and Motion Graphs
Review of 1D kinematics, position-time and velocity-time graphs, equations of motion
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Kinematics is the geometric description of motion: where an object is, how fast it is moving, and how quickly its velocity is changing — without yet asking why. In SPH4U we sharpen the SPH3U treatment by being strict about three vector ideas: position $\vec{x}$, displacement $\Delta\vec{x}$, and velocity $\vec{v}=d\vec{x}/dt$. The "why" of kinematics graphs is that calculus is hidden inside them: the slope of a position-time graph is instantaneous velocity, and the area under a velocity-time graph is displacement. Recognising those geometric meanings lets you solve problems even when you have data but no algebraic formula.

The five "BIG-5" kinematics equations apply only when acceleration is constant. Each equation eliminates one variable, so the trick is to list your knowns ($\vec{x}_0$, $\vec{v}_0$, $\vec{v}$, $\vec{a}$, $t$) and pick the equation missing the unknown you don't care about. Signs encode direction along a chosen axis; an object can have negative velocity while still speeding up if its acceleration is also negative. Free fall near Earth's surface is the canonical constant-acceleration problem with $\vec{a}=\vec{g}=9.81\,\text{m/s}^2$ downward.

  • Displacement $\Delta\vec{x}$ (m) — vector from initial to final position; not the same as path length.
  • Average velocity $\vec{v}_{avg}=\Delta\vec{x}/\Delta t$ (m/s); instantaneous velocity = limit as $\Delta t\to 0$.
  • Acceleration $\vec{a}=d\vec{v}/dt$ (m/s²); dimensional check: $[\text{m/s}]/[\text{s}]=[\text{m/s}^2]$.
  • BIG-5: $v=v_0+at$; $\Delta x=v_0 t+\tfrac12 at^2$; $v^2=v_0^2+2a\Delta x$; $\Delta x=\tfrac12(v_0+v)t$; $\Delta x=vt-\tfrac12 at^2$.

Worked example — a stone dropped from rest

A stone is dropped from a bridge and hits the water 2.4 s later. How far did it fall, and what speed does it strike the water with? Take down as positive, $g=9.81\,\text{m/s}^2$.

Step 1 — list knowns: $v_0=0$, $a=+9.81\,\text{m/s}^2$, $t=2.4\,\text{s}$, $\Delta x=?$, $v=?$.

Step 2 — choose $\Delta x=v_0 t+\tfrac12 at^2 = 0 + \tfrac12(9.81)(2.4)^2 = 28.2\,\text{m}$.

Step 3 — choose $v=v_0+at = 0+(9.81)(2.4)=23.5\,\text{m/s}$ downward.

Step 4 — sanity check with $v^2=v_0^2+2a\Delta x$: $\sqrt{2(9.81)(28.2)}=23.5$ m/s. ✓

  • Confusing speed and velocity — speed is a scalar; velocity carries a sign or direction.
  • Mis-reading a v-t graph — its slope is acceleration, its area is displacement, not the value on the y-axis.
  • Using BIG-5 with non-constant acceleration — illegal; you must use calculus or piecewise constant intervals.
  • Forgetting sign conventions — once you choose "up = +", every quantity must follow that convention.

Curriculum expectation: Strand B, Expectation B2.2 — "solve problems involving the motion of objects in horizontal, vertical, and inclined planes, using vectors, graphs, and free-body diagrams."

1.2 — Two-Dimensional Motion
Displacement, velocity, and acceleration vectors in 2D
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

In two dimensions, motion is described by independent perpendicular components — typically $x$ (horizontal) and $y$ (vertical). The deep reason this works is that Newton's second law is a vector equation: a force component along $x$ produces acceleration only along $x$. So a 2-D problem is really two simultaneous 1-D problems sharing a single clock $t$.

To resolve a vector $\vec{A}$ of magnitude $A$ at angle $\theta$ above the $+x$-axis: $A_x=A\cos\theta$, $A_y=A\sin\theta$. To recombine, $A=\sqrt{A_x^2+A_y^2}$ and $\theta=\tan^{-1}(A_y/A_x)$ (with attention to the quadrant). Adding vectors head-to-tail is geometrically intuitive but algebraically we add components: $\vec{C}=\vec{A}+\vec{B}\Rightarrow C_x=A_x+B_x,\ C_y=A_y+B_y$. Average velocity in 2-D is $\vec{v}_{avg}=\Delta\vec{r}/\Delta t$, where $\Delta\vec{r}$ is the straight-line vector between two positions, regardless of the actual curved path.

  • Position vector $\vec{r}=(x,y)$ measured in metres from a chosen origin.
  • Unit vectors $\hat{i},\hat{j}$ — dimensionless, point along $+x$ and $+y$.
  • Magnitude/direction form ↔ component form — equivalent representations of the same vector.
  • Right-triangle SI check: $[A_x]=[A_y]=[A]$; angles are dimensionless.

Worked example — resultant displacement

A hiker walks 4.0 km east, then 3.0 km at 60° north of east. Find the resultant displacement.

Step 1 — resolve leg 2: $x_2=3.0\cos 60°=1.5$ km; $y_2=3.0\sin 60°=2.60$ km.

Step 2 — sum components: $x_{tot}=4.0+1.5=5.5$ km; $y_{tot}=0+2.60=2.60$ km.

Step 3 — magnitude: $|\vec{R}|=\sqrt{5.5^2+2.60^2}=6.08$ km.

Step 4 — direction: $\theta=\tan^{-1}(2.60/5.5)=25.3°$ N of E.

  • Adding magnitudes instead of vectors — only valid if the vectors are parallel.
  • Forgetting the quadrant — $\tan^{-1}$ on a calculator returns angles in $(-90°,+90°)$ only.
  • Mixing reference frames — components must be measured from the same axes throughout the problem.
  • Conflating displacement and distance — distance is the path length (scalar); displacement is the net vector.

Curriculum expectation: Strand B, Expectation B3.1 — "analyse, with the aid of vector diagrams, the relative motion of objects moving with uniform velocity in two dimensions."

1.3 — Projectile Motion and Relative Motion
Projectile motion analysis, relative velocity in 2D
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

A projectile is any object launched into the air with no propulsion, subject only to gravity (air resistance neglected at this level). The key physical insight — first articulated by Galileo — is the independence of horizontal and vertical motion: gravity acts only vertically, so $a_x=0$ and $a_y=-g$. Horizontally the projectile undergoes uniform velocity; vertically it accelerates downward. Both motions share the same time of flight, which is the variable that "stitches" the two 1-D problems together.

For a launch from ground level at speed $v_0$ and angle $\theta$: $v_{0x}=v_0\cos\theta$, $v_{0y}=v_0\sin\theta$. Time to apex: $t_{up}=v_{0y}/g$. Maximum height: $H=v_{0y}^2/(2g)$. Range on level ground: $R=v_0^2\sin(2\theta)/g$ — maximised at $\theta=45°$ in vacuum. Relative motion follows the Galilean transformation: if frame $B$ moves with velocity $\vec{v}_{BA}$ relative to frame $A$, then an object's velocity in $A$ equals its velocity in $B$ plus $\vec{v}_{BA}$ — i.e. $\vec{v}_{PA}=\vec{v}_{PB}+\vec{v}_{BA}$. This is essential for boats crossing rivers and planes in crosswinds.

  • $g$ = 9.81 m/s² downward near Earth's surface; always positive in magnitude.
  • Range equation $R=v_0^2\sin 2\theta/g$ — valid only for equal launch and landing heights.
  • Trajectory — parabolic in vacuum; shape derived by eliminating $t$ between $x(t)$ and $y(t)$.
  • Relative-velocity subscript rule: $\vec{v}_{AC}=\vec{v}_{AB}+\vec{v}_{BC}$ — inner subscripts cancel.

Worked example — projectile from a cliff

A ball is kicked horizontally at 15 m/s off a 20 m cliff. Where does it land? Take down as positive $y$.

Step 1 — vertical: $\Delta y=\tfrac12 g t^2 \Rightarrow 20=\tfrac12(9.81)t^2 \Rightarrow t=\sqrt{40/9.81}=2.02$ s.

Step 2 — horizontal: $x=v_{0x}t=(15)(2.02)=30.3$ m.

Step 3 — landing velocity components: $v_x=15$ m/s, $v_y=gt=19.8$ m/s; speed $=\sqrt{15^2+19.8^2}=24.8$ m/s at $\tan^{-1}(19.8/15)=52.9°$ below horizontal.

  • Adding $g$ to horizontal motion — gravity has no horizontal component; $v_x$ is constant.
  • Using the range formula on unequal heights — it is invalid; you must do the full $y(t)$ analysis.
  • Forgetting that velocity at apex is not zero — only $v_y=0$ at peak; $v_x$ persists.
  • Mis-applying relative-velocity addition — order matters: $\vec{v}_{PA}\ne\vec{v}_{AP}$; they are opposite.

Curriculum expectation: Strand B, Expectation B2.2 — "solve problems involving the motion of objects in horizontal, vertical, and inclined planes, using vectors, graphs, and free-body diagrams."

1.4 — Forces and Newton's Laws
Free body diagrams, Newton's three laws, net force and acceleration
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Newton's three laws are the foundation of classical mechanics. The first law (inertia) defines what an inertial reference frame is: one in which an object free of net force keeps a constant velocity. The second law $\sum\vec{F}=m\vec{a}$ is the operational definition of force — it links the kinematic quantity $\vec{a}$ to the dynamical cause. The third law states that forces come in equal-and-opposite action-reaction pairs acting on different bodies. Mistaking the third law as "two forces on the same body" causes endless confusion; the two paired forces never cancel because they are not on the same object.

A free-body diagram (FBD) is the single most important tool. You isolate one object, represent it as a dot or box, then draw every force acting on it with arrows. Common forces: gravity $\vec{F}_g=m\vec{g}$, normal $\vec{F}_N$ (perpendicular to surface), tension $\vec{T}$, applied force, friction $\vec{f}$, and spring $\vec{F}_s=-k\vec{x}$. Once axes are chosen (often aligned with motion), Newton's second law is written separately for each axis: $\sum F_x=ma_x$ and $\sum F_y=ma_y$.

  • Force $\vec{F}$ — SI unit newton (N); 1 N = 1 kg·m/s².
  • Mass $m$ (kg) — measure of inertia, frame-invariant in classical mechanics.
  • Weight $W=mg$ (N) — gravitational force, not the same as mass.
  • Normal force $F_N$ — a constraint force; adjusts itself to prevent interpenetration.

Worked example — elevator problem

A 70.0 kg person stands on a scale in an elevator that accelerates upward at 1.20 m/s². What does the scale read?

Step 1 — FBD: weight $mg$ down, normal $F_N$ up (scale reading).

Step 2 — Newton II (up = +): $F_N-mg=ma \Rightarrow F_N=m(g+a)$.

Step 3 — substitute: $F_N=70.0(9.81+1.20)=770.7$ N. Apparent weight ≈ 78.6 kg of force.

Step 4 — interpret: scale reads more than rest weight because elevator accelerates upward.

  • Pairing forces incorrectly under Newton III — gravity on you and normal on you are NOT an action-reaction pair (same body); gravity on you and gravity on Earth IS.
  • Confusing mass and weight — mass is in kg; weight is a force in N and changes with $g$.
  • Drawing forces from objects, not on objects — only forces acting on the chosen body belong on its FBD.
  • Forgetting the normal force is not always $mg$ — on an incline or in an accelerating elevator it differs.

Curriculum expectation: Strand B, Expectation B3.2 — "analyse and describe, using Newton's laws, the forces acting on an object, and use free-body diagrams to determine net force and acceleration."

1.5 — Applying Newton's Laws and Friction
Multi-body problems, static and kinetic friction, inclined planes
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Real Newton's-law problems usually have multiple bodies connected by strings, pushed up inclines, or sliding against rough surfaces. The strategy is unchanged but disciplined: choose your system, draw an FBD for each body separately, pick axes (on an incline, tilt them along the slope), and write Newton II for each axis. For connected bodies on a single inextensible string, the magnitude of acceleration is the same for both — that constraint plus the two Newton-II equations gives you a closed system of equations.

Friction opposes relative motion (or attempted motion) between surfaces. Static friction $f_s\le\mu_s F_N$ adjusts itself up to a maximum to prevent slipping; once $f_s$ would exceed $\mu_s F_N$, slipping begins and kinetic friction $f_k=\mu_k F_N$ takes over (typically $\mu_k<\mu_s$). On an incline of angle $\theta$ with no friction, the component of gravity along the slope is $mg\sin\theta$, and the normal force is $mg\cos\theta$. The acceleration of a block sliding freely down is $a=g\sin\theta$; with kinetic friction included it becomes $a=g(\sin\theta-\mu_k\cos\theta)$.

  • Coefficient of friction $\mu$ — dimensionless; depends on surface pair, not area.
  • Tension $T$ — internal force in an ideal massless string, same throughout.
  • Normal force on incline: $F_N=mg\cos\theta$ (only if no extra perpendicular forces).
  • Atwood constraint: $|a_1|=|a_2|$, $T_1=T_2$ for ideal pulley.

Worked example — block on rough incline

A 5.0 kg block slides down a 30° incline with $\mu_k=0.20$. Find the acceleration.

Step 1 — tilt axes along slope (+ down-slope). FBD: $mg\sin\theta$ down-slope, $\mu_k F_N$ up-slope, $F_N=mg\cos\theta$ ⊥.

Step 2 — Newton II along slope: $ma=mg\sin\theta-\mu_k mg\cos\theta$.

Step 3 — divide by $m$: $a=g(\sin 30°-0.20\cos 30°)=9.81(0.500-0.173)=3.21\,\text{m/s}^2$.

Step 4 — direction: positive means down the slope. Check: if $\mu_k\ge\tan\theta$, the block would not accelerate — here $0.20<\tan 30°=0.577$, so it slides. ✓

  • Using $\mu_s$ when the block is already moving — must switch to $\mu_k$ once sliding.
  • Forgetting friction is parallel to surface, not horizontal.
  • Wrong normal force on incline — it is $mg\cos\theta$, not $mg$.
  • Treating tension as different on each side of an ideal pulley — for a massless, frictionless pulley it is the same.

Curriculum expectation: Strand B, Expectation B3.3 — "predict, in qualitative and quantitative terms, the forces acting on systems of objects (e.g., on an inclined plane, on a pulley system), and verify the predictions experimentally."

1.6 — Circular Motion
Centripetal acceleration and force, banked curves, vertical loops
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

An object moving in a circle at constant speed is still accelerating because its velocity vector is constantly changing direction. The direction of this centripetal ("centre-seeking") acceleration is always toward the centre of the circle, and its magnitude is $a_c=v^2/r=\omega^2 r$, where $\omega=2\pi/T=2\pi f$ is the angular speed. The deep why: in time $\Delta t$, the velocity vector rotates through angle $\Delta\theta=v\Delta t/r$, so $|\Delta\vec{v}|\approx v\Delta\theta$ and $a=|\Delta\vec{v}|/\Delta t=v^2/r$.

Critically, "centripetal force" is not a new kind of force — it is the net radially-inward force, whatever its physical source: tension for a ball on a string, gravity for a satellite, friction for a car turning on a flat road, the horizontal component of normal force on a banked curve. On a properly banked frictionless curve of angle $\theta$: $\tan\theta=v^2/(rg)$. At the top of a vertical loop, gravity helps supply centripetal force; the minimum speed at which the track exerts zero normal force satisfies $v_{min}=\sqrt{rg}$.

  • Period $T$ (s); frequency $f=1/T$ (Hz); angular speed $\omega=2\pi f$ (rad/s).
  • Centripetal acceleration $a_c=v^2/r$ (m/s²); always perpendicular to $\vec{v}$.
  • Centripetal force $F_c=mv^2/r$ — the net radial force, not a separate force on the FBD.
  • Tangential vs radial: tangential force changes speed; radial force changes direction only.

Worked example — car on a flat curve

A 1200 kg car rounds a flat curve of radius 50 m at 20 m/s. What minimum coefficient of static friction is needed?

Step 1 — required centripetal force: $F_c=mv^2/r=1200(20)^2/50=9600$ N inward.

Step 2 — friction provides it: $f_s\ge F_c$, with $f_s\le\mu_s F_N=\mu_s mg$.

Step 3 — solve $\mu_s\ge v^2/(rg)=400/(50\cdot 9.81)=0.816$.

Step 4 — interpret: on wet asphalt ($\mu_s\approx 0.4$), the car would skid; needs a banked curve or slower speed.

  • Drawing a "centripetal force" as an extra arrow — it is the resultant of real forces, not a separate one.
  • Including a fictitious "centrifugal" outward force in an inertial frame — there isn't one.
  • Confusing $v$ (linear) and $\omega$ (angular) — related by $v=\omega r$, units differ (m/s vs rad/s).
  • Forgetting direction of $\vec{a}_c$ — it points toward the centre at every instant, not along $\vec{v}$.

Curriculum expectation: Strand B, Expectation B3.4 — "analyse, in qualitative and quantitative terms, the forces acting on an object in uniform circular motion."
