📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

A line in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) is uniquely determined by a point \(P_0(x_0,y_0)\) on it together with a direction vector \(\vec{d} = (d_1, d_2)\) parallel to the line. The vector equation is \(\vec{r} = \vec{r}_0 + t\vec{d}\), where \(t\) is a real parameter sweeping out every point on the line. Reading off components yields the parametric equations \(x = x_0 + t d_1\) and \(y = y_0 + t d_2\).

Any non-zero scalar multiple of \(\vec{d}\) is an equally valid direction vector for the same line; the choice of \(P_0\) is also non-unique. As \(t\) varies, the parametric form generates every point on the line — values of \(t > 0\) trace one direction from \(P_0\), values \(t < 0\) trace the opposite direction, and \(t = 0\) recovers \(P_0\).

  • Direction vector — a non-zero vector \(\vec{d}\) parallel to a line; any non-zero scalar multiple is also a direction vector.
  • Vector equation of a line (\(\mathbb{R}^2\)) — \(\vec{r} = \vec{r}_0 + t\vec{d}\), \(t \in \mathbb{R}\).
  • Parametric equations (\(\mathbb{R}^2\)) — \(x = x_0 + t d_1\), \(y = y_0 + t d_2\).
  • Parameter — the scalar \(t\) that traces all points on the line.
  • Non-uniqueness — many \((P_0, \vec{d})\) pairs describe the same line.

Worked Example: Vector and parametric form of a line in \(\mathbb{R}^2\)

Find vector and parametric equations of the line through \(P(1, -2)\) with direction \(\vec{d} = (3, 5)\).

Vector form: \(\vec{r} = (1, -2) + t(3, 5)\).

Parametric form: \(x = 1 + 3t\), \(y = -2 + 5t\). At \(t = 2\) we get \((7, 8)\), confirming the line continues into the first quadrant.

  • Picking a direction vector that is the zero vector — \(\vec{d}\) must be non-zero or there is no direction.
  • Believing only one parametrization is correct; both \((1,-2)+t(3,5)\) and \((4,3)+t(6,10)\) describe the same line.
  • Confusing the parameter \(t\) with the \(x\)-coordinate.

Curriculum expectation: Strand C, Expectation C4.2 — "determine, through investigation with technology … and without technology, the equation of a line in two-space, given the slope and a point on the line, two points on the line, the equation of a line parallel to the desired line and a point on the desired line, or the equation of a line perpendicular to the desired line and a point on the desired line."

In Grade 10 we wrote lines as \(y = mx + b\). The Cartesian (scalar) equation generalizes this: in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) every line satisfies \(ax + by + c = 0\), where the normal vector to the line is \(\vec{n} = (a, b)\). The relation \(\vec{n}\cdot\vec{d} = 0\) ties the normal to the direction: if \(\vec{d} = (d_1, d_2)\), one valid normal is \((d_2, -d_1)\). The slope of the line is \(-\tfrac{a}{b}\) (when \(b \ne 0\)).

To convert from parametric to Cartesian form, eliminate the parameter \(t\) from the two component equations. To go the other way, choose a point satisfying the Cartesian equation and read off any direction vector perpendicular to \(\vec{n}\). The Cartesian form is convenient for testing whether a given point lies on the line (substitute and check) and for finding intersections (solve a system).

  • Cartesian (scalar) equation (\(\mathbb{R}^2\)) — \(ax + by + c = 0\), with \((a,b)\) the normal vector.
  • Normal to a line — vector perpendicular to the direction; rotates \((d_1,d_2)\) by \(90^\circ\) to \((d_2, -d_1)\) or \((-d_2, d_1)\).
  • Slope from Cartesian — \(m = -\tfrac{a}{b}\) when \(b \ne 0\).
  • Point test — substitute coordinates into \(ax+by+c\) and check for 0.
  • Conversion — eliminate \(t\) between parametric equations to obtain Cartesian.

Worked Example: Convert parametric to Cartesian

Line: \(x = 1 + 3t\), \(y = -2 + 5t\). From the first, \(t = \tfrac{x-1}{3}\). Sub into the second: \(y = -2 + 5\cdot\tfrac{x-1}{3}\).

Multiply by 3: \(3y = -6 + 5(x-1) = 5x - 11\), so \(5x - 3y - 11 = 0\). Normal: \(\vec{n} = (5, -3)\); slope: \(\tfrac{5}{3}\).

  • Using \(y = mx + b\) form in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) — slope-intercept does not generalize.
  • Forgetting the normal is perpendicular to (not parallel to) the line's direction.
  • Mis-rotating the direction: \((d_1, d_2) \to (d_2, -d_1)\), not \((-d_1, d_2)\).

Curriculum expectation: Strand C, Expectation C4.1 — "recognize that the solution points \((x, y)\) in two-space of a single linear equation in two variables form a line and that the solution points \((x, y)\) of a system of two linear equations in two variables determine the point of intersection of two lines, if the lines are not coincident or parallel."

In \(\mathbb{R}^3\), a single linear equation describes a plane, not a line — so the Cartesian form does not generalize directly. The vector and parametric forms do: a line is given by \(\vec{r} = \vec{r}_0 + t\vec{d}\), with \(\vec{r}_0 = (x_0, y_0, z_0)\) and \(\vec{d} = (d_1, d_2, d_3)\). Eliminating \(t\) component-by-component yields the symmetric equations \(\frac{x-x_0}{d_1} = \frac{y-y_0}{d_2} = \frac{z-z_0}{d_3}\), valid only when all direction components are non-zero.

Two lines in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) can be coincident, parallel and distinct, intersecting, or skew (neither parallel nor intersecting — a configuration impossible in \(\mathbb{R}^2\)). Parallelism is tested algebraically: directions must be scalar multiples.

  • Vector equation (\(\mathbb{R}^3\)) — \(\vec{r} = \vec{r}_0 + t\vec{d}\); \(t\) sweeps the line.
  • Parametric equations — \(x = x_0 + t d_1\), \(y = y_0 + t d_2\), \(z = z_0 + t d_3\).
  • Symmetric equations — \(\frac{x-x_0}{d_1} = \frac{y-y_0}{d_2} = \frac{z-z_0}{d_3}\) when no \(d_i = 0\).
  • Skew lines — lines in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) that are neither parallel nor intersecting.
  • Parallel test — direction vectors are scalar multiples of each other.

Worked Example: All three forms of a line in \(\mathbb{R}^3\)

Find vector, parametric, and symmetric equations for the line through \(P(2, -1, 4)\) and \(Q(5, 3, -2)\).

Direction vector: \(\vec{d} = \vec{PQ} = (5-2,\, 3-(-1),\, -2-4) = (3, 4, -6)\).

Vector form: \(\vec{r} = (2,-1,4) + t(3,4,-6)\).

Parametric form: \(x = 2 + 3t\), \(y = -1 + 4t\), \(z = 4 - 6t\).

Symmetric form: \(\frac{x-2}{3} = \frac{y+1}{4} = \frac{z-4}{-6}\).

Check that \(Q\) lies on the line: \(t = 1\) gives \((2+3, -1+4, 4-6) = (5, 3, -2)\) ✓.

  • Writing a single linear equation in \(x, y, z\) and calling it a line — in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) that is a plane.
  • Writing symmetric equations when a direction component is zero — the corresponding fraction is undefined and that component must be treated separately.
  • Using the same parameter \(t\) for two different lines when looking for intersections; use distinct parameters.

Curriculum expectation: Strand C, Expectation C4.2 — "determine, through investigation … the equation of a line in two-space and three-space, given the slope and a point on the line, two points on the line, the equation of a line parallel to the desired line and a point on the desired line, or the equation of a line perpendicular to the desired line and a point on the desired line."

A plane in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) is uniquely determined by a point \(\vec{r}_0\) lying in it together with two non-collinear direction vectors \(\vec{a}\) and \(\vec{b}\) that lie within the plane. The vector equation is \(\vec{r} = \vec{r}_0 + s\vec{a} + t\vec{b}\), where \(s\) and \(t\) are real parameters. Reading off components gives the parametric equations for the plane: \(x = x_0 + s a_1 + t b_1\), and similarly for \(y\) and \(z\). Two parameters are needed because a plane is two-dimensional.

The same plane can also be determined by three non-collinear points: convert by computing two direction vectors as differences of point position vectors (e.g., \(\vec{a} = \vec{AB}\) and \(\vec{b} = \vec{AC}\)). Any non-zero scalar multiples of these direction vectors work equally well.

  • Vector equation of a plane — \(\vec{r} = \vec{r}_0 + s\vec{a} + t\vec{b}\) for non-collinear \(\vec{a}, \vec{b}\) in the plane.
  • Parametric equations of a plane — three equations of the form \(x = x_0 + s a_1 + t b_1\), etc.
  • Non-collinearity requirement — \(\vec{a}\) and \(\vec{b}\) must not be parallel, or they describe a line, not a plane.
  • From three points — let \(\vec{a} = \vec{AB}\) and \(\vec{b} = \vec{AC}\); use any one of the three as \(\vec{r}_0\).
  • Two parameters — required because a plane has two dimensions.

Worked Example: Vector equation of a plane through three points

Plane through \(A(1,2,3)\), \(B(2,0,1)\), \(C(0,1,4)\).

Direction vectors: \(\vec{AB} = (1,-2,-2)\) and \(\vec{AC} = (-1,-1,1)\). Check non-collinearity: \(\vec{AB}\) is not a scalar multiple of \(\vec{AC}\) (e.g., compare ratios of entries) ✓.

Vector equation: \(\vec{r} = (1,2,3) + s(1,-2,-2) + t(-1,-1,1)\).

  • Picking collinear direction vectors — the result is a line, not a plane.
  • Using only one parameter — describes a line within the plane, not the whole plane.
  • Forgetting that direction vectors and normal vectors are not unique — any non-zero scalar multiple works.

Curriculum expectation: Strand C, Expectation C4.6 — "determine, using properties of a plane, the equation of a plane in three-space, given … three non-collinear points on the plane, two non-parallel lines lying in the plane, [or] two parallel lines lying in the plane."

The scalar (Cartesian) equation of a plane in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) is \(ax + by + cz + d = 0\), where \((a,b,c)\) are the components of the normal vector \(\vec{n}\) — a vector perpendicular to every direction lying in the plane — and \(d = -\vec{n}\cdot\vec{r}_0\) for any point \(\vec{r}_0\) on the plane. A single linear equation in \(x, y, z\) describes a plane; this is the three-dimensional analogue of \(ax + by + c = 0\) for a line in \(\mathbb{R}^2\).

To go from vector form to scalar form, compute the normal as \(\vec{n} = \vec{a}\times\vec{b}\). To go from three points, take cross products of difference vectors. To test whether a point lies on the plane, substitute its coordinates into the scalar equation and check for zero.

  • Scalar equation of a plane — \(ax + by + cz + d = 0\), with \((a,b,c) = \vec{n}\).
  • Normal vector — perpendicular to every direction lying in the plane.
  • Conversion — \(\vec{n} = \vec{a}\times\vec{b}\) for plane spanned by \(\vec{a}\) and \(\vec{b}\).
  • Constant \(d\) — set \(d = -(\vec{n}\cdot\vec{r}_0)\) using any known point \(\vec{r}_0\) on the plane.
  • Point test — substitute and check whether the expression is zero.

Worked Example: Scalar equation of a plane from three points

Find the scalar equation of the plane through \(A(1,2,3)\), \(B(2,0,1)\), \(C(0,1,4)\).

Two direction vectors in the plane: \(\vec{AB} = (1,-2,-2)\) and \(\vec{AC} = (-1,-1,1)\).

Normal vector: \(\vec{n} = \vec{AB}\times\vec{AC}\). Compute the determinant: \[\vec{n} = \begin{vmatrix} \hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k} \\ 1 & -2 & -2 \\ -1 & -1 & 1 \end{vmatrix} = \hat{i}((-2)(1)-(-2)(-1)) - \hat{j}((1)(1)-(-2)(-1)) + \hat{k}((1)(-1)-(-2)(-1)).\]

Evaluate: \(\hat{i}(-2-2) - \hat{j}(1-2) + \hat{k}(-1-2) = -4\hat{i} + \hat{j} - 3\hat{k} = (-4, 1, -3)\).

Use point \(A\): \(-4(x-1) + 1(y-2) - 3(z-3) = 0 \implies -4x + 4 + y - 2 - 3z + 9 = 0 \implies -4x + y - 3z + 11 = 0\).

Verify with \(B\): \(-4(2) + 0 - 3(1) + 11 = -8 + 0 - 3 + 11 = 0\) ✓.

  • Forgetting the constant \(d\) in the scalar equation; it shifts the plane to pass through the chosen point.
  • Mis-signing the middle term in the determinant expansion; the \(\hat{j}\) component carries a minus sign.
  • Confusing the normal vector with a direction vector in the plane.

Curriculum expectation: Strand C, Expectation C4.3 — "recognize that the solution points \((x, y, z)\) in three-space of a single linear equation in three variables form a plane and that the solution points \((x, y, z)\) of a system of two linear equations in three variables form the line of intersection of two planes, if the planes are not parallel."

When a plane is specified by a point \(\vec{r}_0\) and a normal vector \(\vec{n}\), the equation falls out of the perpendicularity condition: any point \(\vec{r}\) in the plane satisfies \(\vec{n}\cdot(\vec{r} - \vec{r}_0) = 0\), which expands to the scalar form \(ax + by + cz + d = 0\) with \(d = -\vec{n}\cdot\vec{r}_0\). This point-normal form is the most direct route to the scalar equation when a normal is already known.

Parallelism, perpendicularity, and intersections of lines and planes all reduce to algebra on direction and normal vectors. Two planes are parallel iff their normals are scalar multiples. A line with direction \(\vec{d}\) is parallel to a plane (and either lies in it or misses it entirely) iff \(\vec{d}\cdot\vec{n} = 0\); it is perpendicular to the plane iff \(\vec{d}\) is parallel to \(\vec{n}\). These tests are independent of the specific points chosen.

  • Point-normal form — \(\vec{n}\cdot(\vec{r}-\vec{r}_0) = 0\), expands to \(ax + by + cz + d = 0\).
  • Parallel planes — normals are scalar multiples.
  • Line parallel to plane — \(\vec{d}\cdot\vec{n} = 0\).
  • Line perpendicular to plane — \(\vec{d}\) is a scalar multiple of \(\vec{n}\).
  • Non-uniqueness of normal — any non-zero scalar multiple of \(\vec{n}\) works.

Worked Example: Plane from a point and a normal

Find the scalar equation of the plane through \(P_0(2, -1, 3)\) with normal \(\vec{n} = (4, 1, -2)\).

Point-normal: \(4(x-2) + 1(y+1) - 2(z-3) = 0\).

Expand: \(4x - 8 + y + 1 - 2z + 6 = 0 \implies 4x + y - 2z - 1 = 0\).

  • Mixing up "parallel to the plane" (\(\vec{d}\cdot\vec{n} = 0\)) with "perpendicular to the plane" (\(\vec{d}\) parallel to \(\vec{n}\)).
  • Computing \(d\) with the wrong sign — remember \(d = -\vec{n}\cdot\vec{r}_0\), with the minus.
  • Treating a different scalar multiple of \(\vec{n}\) as defining a different plane — it does not.

Curriculum expectation: Strand C, Expectation C4.4 — "determine, through investigation with technology … and without technology, the equation of a plane in three-space, given the scalar equation of a plane parallel to the desired plane and a point on the desired plane, or the equation of a plane perpendicular to the desired plane and a point on the desired plane."
