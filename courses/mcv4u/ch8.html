Unit 8: Lines and Planes

Equations of lines in 2D/3D, equations of planes

📚 Strand C: Vectors 📹 7 lessons
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

In Grade 10 we wrote lines as \(y = mx + b\). That representation works in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) but breaks down in \(\mathbb{R}^3\), where a single linear equation in \(x, y, z\) describes a plane, not a line. Vector geometry solves this. A line is uniquely determined by a point \(P_0\) on it and a direction vector \(\vec{d}\); its vector equation is \(\vec{r} = \vec{r}_0 + t\vec{d}\), where \(t\) is a real parameter. Splitting into components gives the parametric equations, and (in \(\mathbb{R}^3\)) eliminating \(t\) gives the symmetric equations \(\frac{x-x_0}{d_1} = \frac{y-y_0}{d_2} = \frac{z-z_0}{d_3}\), valid only where the direction components are non-zero.

A plane in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) is uniquely determined by a point and a normal vector \(\vec{n}\) (a vector perpendicular to the plane). The scalar (Cartesian) equation of the plane is \(ax + by + cz + d = 0\), where \((a,b,c)\) are the components of \(\vec{n}\) and \(d = -\vec{n}\cdot\vec{r}_0\). Alternatively, a plane can be specified by two direction vectors lying within it, giving the vector equation \(\vec{r} = \vec{r}_0 + s\vec{a} + t\vec{b}\). To convert between the two forms, the normal is \(\vec{n} = \vec{a}\times\vec{b}\).

The power of these representations is that they handle parallelism, perpendicularity, and intersections uniformly. Two lines are parallel iff their direction vectors are scalar multiples; two planes are parallel iff their normals are; a line is parallel to a plane iff its direction is perpendicular to the plane's normal (\(\vec{d}\cdot\vec{n} = 0\)).

  • Direction vector — a non-zero vector \(\vec{d}\) parallel to a line; any non-zero scalar multiple is also a direction vector.
  • Vector equation of a line — \(\vec{r} = \vec{r}_0 + t\vec{d}\), \(t \in \mathbb{R}\).
  • Parametric equations — \(x = x_0 + t d_1\), \(y = y_0 + t d_2\), \(z = z_0 + t d_3\).
  • Symmetric equations (\(\mathbb{R}^3\), nonzero direction components) — \(\frac{x-x_0}{d_1} = \frac{y-y_0}{d_2} = \frac{z-z_0}{d_3}\).
  • Normal vector to a plane — vector perpendicular to every direction in the plane.
  • Scalar equation of a plane — \(ax + by + cz + d = 0\), where \(\vec{n} = (a,b,c)\) is normal.
  • Vector equation of a plane — \(\vec{r} = \vec{r}_0 + s\vec{a} + t\vec{b}\) for non-collinear \(\vec{a}, \vec{b}\) in the plane.
  • Skew lines — lines in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) that are neither parallel nor intersecting.

Worked Example: All three forms of a line in \(\mathbb{R}^3\)

Find vector, parametric, and symmetric equations for the line through \(P(2, -1, 4)\) and \(Q(5, 3, -2)\).

Direction vector: \(\vec{d} = \vec{PQ} = (5-2,\, 3-(-1),\, -2-4) = (3, 4, -6)\).

Vector form: \(\vec{r} = (2,-1,4) + t(3,4,-6)\).

Parametric form: \(x = 2 + 3t\), \(y = -1 + 4t\), \(z = 4 - 6t\).

Symmetric form: \(\frac{x-2}{3} = \frac{y+1}{4} = \frac{z-4}{-6}\).

Check that \(Q\) lies on the line: \(t = 1\) gives \((2+3, -1+4, 4-6) = (5, 3, -2)\) ✓.

Worked Example: Scalar equation of a plane from three points

Find the scalar equation of the plane through \(A(1,2,3)\), \(B(2,0,1)\), \(C(0,1,4)\).

Two direction vectors in the plane: \(\vec{AB} = (1,-2,-2)\) and \(\vec{AC} = (-1,-1,1)\).

Normal vector: \(\vec{n} = \vec{AB}\times\vec{AC}\). Compute the determinant: \[\vec{n} = \begin{vmatrix} \hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k} \\ 1 & -2 & -2 \\ -1 & -1 & 1 \end{vmatrix} = \hat{i}((-2)(1)-(-2)(-1)) - \hat{j}((1)(1)-(-2)(-1)) + \hat{k}((1)(-1)-(-2)(-1)).\]

Evaluate: \(\hat{i}(-2-2) - \hat{j}(1-2) + \hat{k}(-1-2) = -4\hat{i} + \hat{j} - 3\hat{k} = (-4, 1, -3)\).

Use point \(A\): \(-4(x-1) + 1(y-2) - 3(z-3) = 0 \implies -4x + 4 + y - 2 - 3z + 9 = 0 \implies -4x + y - 3z + 11 = 0\).

Verify with \(B\): \(-4(2) + 0 - 3(1) + 11 = -8 + 0 - 3 + 11 = 0\) ✓.

  • Writing a single linear equation in \(x, y, z\) and calling it a line — in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) that is a plane.
  • Forgetting that direction vectors and normal vectors are not unique — any non-zero scalar multiple works.
  • Using \(y = mx + b\) form in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) — slope-intercept does not generalize.
  • Writing symmetric equations when a direction component is zero — the corresponding fraction is undefined and that component must be treated separately.
  • Mixing up "parallel to the plane" (\(\vec{d}\cdot\vec{n} = 0\)) with "perpendicular to the plane" (\(\vec{d}\) parallel to \(\vec{n}\)).

Curriculum expectation: Strand C, Expectation C4.1 — "recognize that the solution points \((x, y)\) in two-space of a single linear equation in two variables form a line and that the solution points \((x, y)\) of a system of two linear equations in two variables determine the point of intersection of two lines, if the lines are not coincident or parallel." Also C4.2 — "determine, through investigation with technology … and without technology, the equation of a line in two-space, given the slope and a point on the line, two points on the line, the equation of a line parallel to the desired line and a point on the desired line, or the equation of a line perpendicular to the desired line and a point on the desired line." And C4.3 — "recognize that the solution points \((x, y, z)\) in three-space of a single linear equation in three variables form a plane and that the solution points \((x, y, z)\) of a system of two linear equations in three variables form the line of intersection of two planes, if the planes are not parallel."
