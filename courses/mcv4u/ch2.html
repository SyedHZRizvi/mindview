Unit 2: Derivatives

Derivative rules: power, product, quotient, chain rule

📚 Strand A: Rate of Change 📹 6 lessons
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The derivative \(f'(x) = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\) is the engine of differential calculus. Computing it from first principles every time would be tedious, so this unit establishes a toolkit of rules that let us differentiate by inspection. The power rule, product rule, quotient rule and chain rule are not arbitrary recipes — each is provable from the limit definition, and each addresses a specific way functions combine.

Why so many rules? Because functions combine in distinct ways: addition (handled by the linearity of limits), multiplication (product rule, because \((fg)' \ne f'g'\)), division (quotient rule, derived from the product rule), and composition (chain rule, the most consequential of all). The chain rule expresses how rates of change cascade through nested processes: if \(y\) depends on \(u\) and \(u\) depends on \(x\), then \(\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du}\cdot\frac{du}{dx}\). This is precisely how related rates and implicit differentiation become tractable.

A subtle point: \(\frac{d}{dx}[f(x)g(x)] = f'(x)g(x) + f(x)g'(x)\) — both terms are required, because at any instant both factors are changing. Treating it like \(\frac{d}{dx}(c\cdot f) = c\cdot f'\) (which is correct only when one factor is a constant) is the single most common error in this unit.

  • Derivative — \(f'(x) = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\); also written \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) or \(Df(x)\).
  • Power rule — for any real \(n\), \(\frac{d}{dx}[x^n] = n x^{n-1}\).
  • Product rule — \((fg)' = f'g + fg'\).
  • Quotient rule — \(\left(\frac{f}{g}\right)' = \frac{f'g - fg'}{g^2}\), valid where \(g \ne 0\).
  • Chain rule — \(\frac{d}{dx}f(g(x)) = f'(g(x))\cdot g'(x)\).
  • Differentiability — \(f\) is differentiable at \(x=a\) if \(f'(a)\) exists; differentiability implies continuity, but not vice versa.
  • Implicit differentiation — applying \(\frac{d}{dx}\) to both sides of an equation in \(x\) and \(y\), treating \(y\) as an implicit function of \(x\) and applying the chain rule whenever \(y\) is differentiated.

Worked Example: Differentiate \(y = (3x^2+1)(x^3-4x)\)

Apply the product rule with \(f = 3x^2+1\) and \(g = x^3-4x\): \[y' = f'g + fg' = (6x)(x^3-4x) + (3x^2+1)(3x^2-4).\]

Expand each piece: \((6x)(x^3-4x) = 6x^4 - 24x^2\); \((3x^2+1)(3x^2-4) = 9x^4 - 12x^2 + 3x^2 - 4 = 9x^4 - 9x^2 - 4\).

Combine: \(y' = 6x^4 - 24x^2 + 9x^4 - 9x^2 - 4 = 15x^4 - 33x^2 - 4\).

Worked Example: Chain rule on \(y = (2x^3 - 5)^4\)

Identify outer function \(f(u) = u^4\) and inner function \(u = 2x^3 - 5\). Then \(\frac{dy}{du} = 4u^3\) and \(\frac{du}{dx} = 6x^2\).

Apply the chain rule: \[\frac{dy}{dx} = 4u^3 \cdot 6x^2 = 4(2x^3-5)^3 \cdot 6x^2 = 24x^2(2x^3-5)^3.\]

Notice the inner derivative \(6x^2\) does not get absorbed into the exponent — it multiplies the whole expression.

  • Writing \((fg)' = f'g'\) — this is wrong; the product rule requires both cross terms.
  • Forgetting the inner derivative in the chain rule — \(\frac{d}{dx}(\sin(3x))=3\cos(3x)\), not \(\cos(3x)\).
  • Mis-signing the quotient rule numerator: it is \(f'g - fg'\), not \(fg' - f'g\).
  • Applying the power rule to non-power expressions (e.g., \(\frac{d}{dx}[2^x] \ne x\cdot 2^{x-1}\); that requires the exponential rule in Unit 5).
  • Forgetting the \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) factor when implicitly differentiating a \(y\)-term.

Also A3.4 — "verify, through investigation, the constant, constant multiple, sum, and difference rules graphically and numerically … and read and interpret proofs involving \(\lim_{h\to 0} \frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\) of the constant, constant multiple, sum, and difference rules."

Sketching the derivative function \(f'\) from a graph of \(f\) — without computing any algebra — is a powerful diagnostic of conceptual understanding. The graph of \(f'\) records the slope of \(f\) at each \(x\)-value as a height. Where \(f\) is rising, \(f'\) is positive (above the \(x\)-axis); where \(f\) is falling, \(f'\) is negative; where \(f\) has a horizontal tangent (local max, local min, or horizontal inflection), \(f'\) crosses the \(x\)-axis.

Steepness translates to magnitude: a steep portion of \(f\) corresponds to a \(y\)-value of \(f'\) that is far from zero, while a nearly-flat portion corresponds to \(f'\) close to zero. A linear segment of \(f\) gives a horizontal segment in \(f'\) (constant slope). A cusp or corner in \(f\) — where the slope jumps or becomes undefined — produces a discontinuity in \(f'\).

Reverse engineering is just as useful: given \(f'\), what does \(f\) look like? Wherever \(f' > 0\), \(f\) ascends; wherever \(f' < 0\), \(f\) descends; the zeros of \(f'\) are the critical points of \(f\). The vertical position of \(f\) is one degree of freedom we cannot recover (an arbitrary constant), but the shape is determined.

  • Slope-to-height correspondence — slope of \(f\) at \(x=a\) equals height of \(f'\) at \(x=a\).
  • Critical \(x\)-value — where \(f'(x)=0\) or is undefined; appears as an \(x\)-intercept or hole in the graph of \(f'\).
  • Sign chart — divide the \(x\)-axis at critical points; on each region record the sign of \(f'\); use to predict where \(f\) rises or falls.
  • Cusp/corner — sharp change of direction in \(f\); the derivative has a jump discontinuity or one-sided limits that disagree.
  • Antidifference — informally, "reading backward" from \(f'\) to \(f\) is the reverse of differentiation.

Worked Example: \(f\) is a parabola opening up with vertex at \((2,-3)\). Sketch \(f'\).

At \(x=2\), \(f\) has a horizontal tangent, so \(f'(2)=0\); the graph of \(f'\) crosses the \(x\)-axis at \(x=2\).

For \(x<2\), \(f\) is decreasing — \(f'\) is negative there. For \(x>2\), \(f\) is increasing — \(f'\) is positive.

The further from \(x=2\) we go, the steeper \(f\) becomes (in either direction), so \(|f'|\) grows linearly. The result: \(f'\) is a straight line through \((2,0)\) with positive slope — consistent with the fact that \(f(x) = a(x-2)^2 - 3\) gives \(f'(x) = 2a(x-2)\), a linear function.

  • Plotting \(f'\) as a shifted copy of \(f\) — the relationship is between slope and height, not between heights.
  • Forgetting that \(f'=0\) appears at MAX, MIN, AND horizontal inflection points; you can't tell which from \(f'\) alone without checking sign changes.
  • Drawing a continuous \(f'\) through a corner of \(f\) — corners make \(f'\) discontinuous.
  • Reading "above the axis" as "function is positive" — the graph being plotted is \(f'\), so above the axis means \(f' > 0\), i.e., \(f\) is increasing.

Curriculum expectation: Strand A, Expectation A2.4 — "sketch the graph of the derivative function, given the graph of the function, and vice versa, by recognizing connections between the slope of the tangent line at a point and the value of the derivative."

The derivative function \(f'\) is built point-by-point from the limit definition: \(f'(x) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\). Rather than computing a slope at a specific number \(x=a\), we treat \(x\) as a variable and produce a new function whose value at any \(x\) is the slope of \(f\) at that location. This is the function version of the tangent-slope idea.

Computing derivatives from first principles means executing this limit explicitly, without invoking the power rule or other shortcuts. The procedure is mechanical but fragile: (1) write the difference quotient \(\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\); (2) expand and simplify the numerator until \(h\) factors out; (3) cancel the \(h\); (4) take the limit by substituting \(h=0\). Algebra errors at step (2) are the usual failure mode.

Why bother when the power rule gives the answer in one line? Because first principles is the only proof that the power rule works, and because some functions (with absolute values, piecewise definitions, or non-standard bases) yield to first principles before any shortcut applies. The technique is also the bridge to the formal definition of the derivative used in university analysis.

  • Derivative function — \(f'(x) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\); domain consists of \(x\) where this limit exists.
  • First principles — computing \(f'\) directly from the limit, not via shortcut rules.
  • Difference quotient — \(\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\); the algebraic object on which the limit is taken.
  • Differentiable at \(a\) — \(f'(a)\) exists as a finite real number.
  • Leibniz notation — \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) as an alternative to \(f'(x)\), emphasizing the variable of differentiation.

Worked Example: Compute \(f'(x)\) from first principles for \(f(x) = 3x^2 - 2x\).

Difference quotient: \(\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h} = \frac{3(x+h)^2 - 2(x+h) - (3x^2 - 2x)}{h}\).

Expand: \(3(x+h)^2 = 3x^2 + 6xh + 3h^2\), so the numerator is \(3x^2 + 6xh + 3h^2 - 2x - 2h - 3x^2 + 2x = 6xh + 3h^2 - 2h\).

Factor \(h\): \(\frac{h(6x + 3h - 2)}{h} = 6x + 3h - 2\).

Take the limit as \(h\to 0\): \(f'(x) = 6x - 2\). (Confirm with the power rule: \(f'(x) = 6x - 2\). Agreement.)

Worked Example: Compute \(f'(x)\) from first principles for \(f(x) = \sqrt{x}\).

Difference quotient: \(\frac{\sqrt{x+h} - \sqrt{x}}{h}\). Multiply top and bottom by the conjugate \(\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x}\): \[\frac{(x+h)-x}{h(\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x})} = \frac{h}{h(\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x})} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x}}.\]

Limit as \(h\to 0\): \(f'(x) = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}\) — confirming the power rule for \(n=\tfrac{1}{2}\).

  • Forgetting to include the \(-f(x)\) (with both terms!) in the numerator; many students lose a minus sign here.
  • Treating \(h\) as a constant after substitution: it isn't — it is the variable of the limit.
  • Cancelling \(h\) before factoring it out — the cancellation requires it to be a common factor.
  • Forgetting that the result is a function of \(x\), not a number — students sometimes substitute the original \(x\) value before taking the limit, defeating the purpose.

Curriculum expectation: Strand A, Expectation A2.3 — "definition of the derivative function of a function \(f\) at \(x\), denoted \(f'(x)\) … and the connection to instantaneous rates of change; determine, through investigation, … \(f'(x)\) from first principles, using the formal definition."

Topic 2.2 promotes the power rule from a single-term shortcut to a tool for differentiating any polynomial. Three building blocks combine: the power rule \(\frac{d}{dx}[x^n] = n x^{n-1}\) for any real \(n\); the constant multiple rule \(\frac{d}{dx}[c\cdot f(x)] = c\cdot f'(x)\); and the sum/difference rule \(\frac{d}{dx}[f(x)\pm g(x)] = f'(x)\pm g'(x)\). Together they allow term-by-term differentiation: differentiate each monomial separately, attach its coefficient, then sum.

The derivative of a constant is zero — \(\frac{d}{dx}[c] = 0\) — because the graph of a constant is a horizontal line with slope zero. Equivalently, the power rule applied to \(c = c\cdot x^0\) gives \(0\cdot c\cdot x^{-1} = 0\). Knowing this saves time: any constant term in a polynomial vanishes under differentiation.

The power rule also handles fractional and negative exponents, which is essential once we rewrite radicals (\(\sqrt{x} = x^{1/2}\)) and reciprocals (\(\tfrac{1}{x^2} = x^{-2}\)) in exponent form. So before differentiating, simplify each term to a single power of \(x\) — this prevents the temptation to apply the quotient rule when it isn't needed.

  • Power rule — \(\frac{d}{dx}[x^n] = n x^{n-1}\), valid for any real \(n\).
  • Constant multiple rule — \(\frac{d}{dx}[c f(x)] = c f'(x)\).
  • Sum/difference rule — derivative of a sum is the sum of derivatives.
  • Derivative of a constant — \(\frac{d}{dx}[c] = 0\).
  • Term-by-term differentiation — combine the three rules to differentiate any polynomial.

Worked Example: Differentiate \(p(x) = 4x^5 - 7x^3 + \tfrac{2}{x} - 6\sqrt{x} + 11\).

Rewrite \(\tfrac{2}{x} = 2x^{-1}\) and \(6\sqrt{x} = 6 x^{1/2}\). The polynomial becomes \(4x^5 - 7x^3 + 2x^{-1} - 6x^{1/2} + 11\).

Apply the power rule term-by-term: \[p'(x) = 20x^4 - 21x^2 - 2x^{-2} - 3x^{-1/2} + 0.\]

Rewrite in original form if desired: \(p'(x) = 20x^4 - 21x^2 - \tfrac{2}{x^2} - \tfrac{3}{\sqrt{x}}\).

Worked Example: Find the slope of \(y = x^4 - 3x^2 + 1\) at \(x=-1\).

Differentiate: \(y' = 4x^3 - 6x\). Substitute \(x=-1\): \(y'(-1) = 4(-1)^3 - 6(-1) = -4 + 6 = 2\). The tangent slope at \(x=-1\) is \(2\).

  • Forgetting to reduce the exponent by one: \(\frac{d}{dx}[x^5] = 5x^4\), not \(5x^5\).
  • Mishandling \(\tfrac{1}{x}\): it is \(x^{-1}\), so its derivative is \(-x^{-2} = -\tfrac{1}{x^2}\), not \(\ln x\) or anything else.
  • Forgetting that the derivative of a constant is zero — students sometimes carry the constant unchanged.
  • Multiplying instead of subtracting the exponent's coefficient: from \(7x^3\) the derivative is \(21x^2\), not \(7\cdot 3 \cdot x^3\).

Curriculum expectation: Strand A, Expectation A3.4 — "verify, through investigation, the constant, constant multiple, sum, and difference rules graphically and numerically (e.g., using technology), and read and interpret proofs involving \(\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\) of the constant, constant multiple, sum, and difference rules."

When a function is a product \(y = f(x)g(x)\), the temptation is to differentiate each factor and multiply, but this is incorrect. The correct formula, the product rule, is \[(fg)' = f'g + fg'.\] Both factors are changing simultaneously, and the total instantaneous change picks up contributions from each — \(f'g\) (the rate at which \(f\) changes weighted by current \(g\)) plus \(fg'\) (rate of \(g\) weighted by current \(f\)). The derivation from first principles \(\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x+h)g(x+h) - f(x)g(x)}{h}\) requires a clever "add-and-subtract" trick \(f(x+h)g(x+h) - f(x+h)g(x) + f(x+h)g(x) - f(x)g(x)\), which factors into the two product-rule terms.

The product rule applies whenever you can identify two non-trivially-variable factors. If one factor is a constant, the constant-multiple rule already handles it. If a factor is itself a composition, you'll need to layer the chain rule on top.

A common strategic question: should you expand first, then differentiate, or apply the product rule directly? For small polynomial products either approach works; expanding is sometimes faster. For factors that don't expand cleanly (radicals, transcendentals later in Unit 5, or compositions), the product rule is essential.

  • Product rule — \((fg)' = f'g + fg'\); symmetric in \(f\) and \(g\).
  • Identify factors — choose \(f\) and \(g\) such that each is easy to differentiate.
  • Both terms required — \((fg)' \ne f' g'\); leaving out the second cross term is the canonical error.
  • Generalizes to three factors — \((fgh)' = f'gh + fg'h + fgh'\); each factor takes its turn being differentiated.
  • Strategic expansion — sometimes faster to multiply out, then use the power rule.

Worked Example: Differentiate \(y = (x^2 + 3)(2x - 5)\) using the product rule.

Let \(f = x^2 + 3\), \(g = 2x - 5\). Then \(f' = 2x\) and \(g' = 2\).

Apply the rule: \(y' = f'g + fg' = (2x)(2x-5) + (x^2+3)(2)\).

Expand: \((2x)(2x-5) = 4x^2 - 10x\); \((x^2+3)(2) = 2x^2 + 6\). Sum: \(6x^2 - 10x + 6\).

Cross-check by expanding first: \(y = (x^2+3)(2x-5) = 2x^3 - 5x^2 + 6x - 15\). Differentiate: \(y' = 6x^2 - 10x + 6\). Agreement.

Worked Example: Differentiate \(y = x^2\sqrt{x+1}\).

Let \(f = x^2\), \(g = (x+1)^{1/2}\). Then \(f' = 2x\), and (using chain rule on \(g\)) \(g' = \tfrac{1}{2}(x+1)^{-1/2} \cdot 1 = \tfrac{1}{2\sqrt{x+1}}\).

Product rule: \(y' = 2x\sqrt{x+1} + x^2 \cdot \tfrac{1}{2\sqrt{x+1}} = \tfrac{4x(x+1) + x^2}{2\sqrt{x+1}} = \tfrac{5x^2 + 4x}{2\sqrt{x+1}}\).

  • Writing \((fg)' = f'g'\) — this is the most common error in this unit.
  • Forgetting one of the two terms; the rule is symmetric and you need both.
  • Computing \(f'\) and then carrying \(g\) untouched into the second term too — re-read the formula.
  • Forgetting the chain rule on factors that are compositions (e.g., \((x+1)^{1/2}\)).

Curriculum expectation: Strand A, Expectation A3.5 — "verify, through investigation, using technology, the chain rule and the product rule (e.g., compare … the graph of \(f(x)=g(x)h(x)\) and the graph of the derivative \(f'(x)\), where \(f(x)\) is determined by applying the product rule … to the graphs of \(g(x)\) and \(h(x)\))."

The quotient rule handles derivatives of fractions: \[\left(\frac{f}{g}\right)' = \frac{f'g - fg'}{g^2}, \qquad g(x) \ne 0.\] The sign and order matter: it is \(f' g\) minus \(f g'\), not the other way around. A standard mnemonic — "low d-high minus high d-low, over the square of what's below" — encodes the formula in a memorable rhythm.

The quotient rule is a consequence of the product rule plus the chain rule. Writing \(\tfrac{f}{g} = f \cdot g^{-1}\) and applying the product rule gives \(f' g^{-1} + f \cdot (-g^{-2}) g'\), which simplifies to \(\tfrac{f' g - f g'}{g^2}\). Knowing this derivation explains why the \(g^2\) appears in the denominator and locks in the sign convention.

When should you use it? Only when the denominator genuinely involves \(x\). If the denominator is a constant, factor it out and use the constant-multiple rule. If the numerator is a constant, rewrite as \(c\cdot g(x)^{-1}\) and use the chain rule. The quotient rule is reserved for honest \(x\)-in-both-places fractions.

  • Quotient rule — \(\left(\tfrac{f}{g}\right)' = \tfrac{f'g - fg'}{g^2}\), \(g\ne 0\).
  • Sign convention — \(f'g\) is positive, \(fg'\) is subtracted; the order matters and reversing it is a common mistake.
  • Square the denominator — the \(g^2\) is required; forgetting it produces the wrong rate.
  • Equivalent product form — \(\tfrac{f}{g} = f g^{-1}\); useful when chain rule is more familiar.
  • Domain restriction — \(g(x)=0\) is excluded; the rule fails (and so does the function) there.

Worked Example: Differentiate \(y = \dfrac{2x+1}{x^2-3}\).

Let \(f = 2x+1\), \(g = x^2-3\). Then \(f' = 2\), \(g' = 2x\).

Apply the rule: \(y' = \dfrac{f'g - fg'}{g^2} = \dfrac{2(x^2-3) - (2x+1)(2x)}{(x^2-3)^2}\).

Expand the numerator: \(2x^2 - 6 - (4x^2 + 2x) = 2x^2 - 6 - 4x^2 - 2x = -2x^2 - 2x - 6\).

So \(y' = \dfrac{-2x^2 - 2x - 6}{(x^2-3)^2} = \dfrac{-2(x^2 + x + 3)}{(x^2-3)^2}\).

Worked Example: Find the slope of \(y = \dfrac{x^2}{x+1}\) at \(x=2\).

Let \(f = x^2, g = x+1\). Then \(f' = 2x, g' = 1\). The derivative is \(y' = \dfrac{2x(x+1) - x^2 \cdot 1}{(x+1)^2} = \dfrac{2x^2 + 2x - x^2}{(x+1)^2} = \dfrac{x^2 + 2x}{(x+1)^2}\).

At \(x=2\): \(y'(2) = \dfrac{4 + 4}{9} = \dfrac{8}{9}\).

  • Reversing the numerator sign: \(\tfrac{fg' - f'g}{g^2}\) gives the negative of the correct derivative.
  • Forgetting to square the denominator — writing \(g\) instead of \(g^2\) at the bottom.
  • Applying the rule when the denominator is a constant (e.g., \(\tfrac{x^2}{5}\)) — use the constant-multiple rule instead.
  • Skipping simplification of the numerator, then misreading the final expression as zero when it isn't.

Topic 2.5 introduces the chain rule, the most important differentiation rule in calculus. If \(y\) is a function of \(u\) and \(u\) is a function of \(x\), then \[\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du}\cdot\frac{du}{dx}.\] Equivalently, for the composition \(h(x) = f(g(x))\), \(h'(x) = f'(g(x))\cdot g'(x)\) — differentiate the outer function (leaving the inner alone), then multiply by the derivative of the inner.

The intuition is that rates compose: if \(y\) changes 3 units for every unit of \(u\), and \(u\) changes 5 units for every unit of \(x\), then \(y\) changes \(3\cdot 5 = 15\) units per unit of \(x\). The chain rule formalizes this cascade. Almost every function in the wild is a composition once you strip away simple polynomials, so practising chain rule patterns is essential for the rest of the course.

Layer the chain rule with itself for deeper nesting: \(\frac{d}{dx}f(g(h(x))) = f'(g(h(x)))\cdot g'(h(x))\cdot h'(x)\). The pattern is "differentiate from outside in, multiplying by each inner derivative as you peel off a layer."

  • Composition — \(h(x) = f(g(x))\) means apply \(g\) first, then \(f\); \(g\) is "inner", \(f\) is "outer".
  • Chain rule — \(\frac{d}{dx}f(g(x)) = f'(g(x))\cdot g'(x)\).
  • Leibniz form — \(\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du}\frac{du}{dx}\); helpful when the layers have natural names.
  • Inner derivative — never forget the \(g'(x)\) factor; missing it is the single biggest chain-rule error.
  • General power rule — \(\frac{d}{dx}[g(x)]^n = n[g(x)]^{n-1}\cdot g'(x)\); the most-used special case of the chain rule.

Worked Example: Differentiate \(y = (3x^2 - 7)^5\).

Outer: \(u^5\), so \(\frac{dy}{du} = 5u^4\). Inner: \(u = 3x^2 - 7\), so \(\frac{du}{dx} = 6x\).

Chain rule: \(\frac{dy}{dx} = 5u^4 \cdot 6x = 30x(3x^2 - 7)^4\).

Worked Example: Differentiate \(y = \sqrt{1 + x^2}\) (a triple-layer composition once we write \(\sqrt{u} = u^{1/2}\)).

Rewrite: \(y = (1+x^2)^{1/2}\). Apply the general power rule: \(y' = \tfrac{1}{2}(1+x^2)^{-1/2}\cdot 2x = \dfrac{x}{\sqrt{1+x^2}}\).

  • Forgetting the inner derivative — writing \((3x^2-7)^5\)' as \(5(3x^2-7)^4\) without the \(6x\) factor.
  • Differentiating both the inner and the outer at the same time (e.g., trying to "expand" \(u^5\) into \(x\) immediately).
  • Mis-identifying outer and inner for square roots: \(\sqrt{f(x)}\) has outer \(\sqrt{u}\) and inner \(f(x)\); the derivative is \(\tfrac{f'(x)}{2\sqrt{f(x)}}\).
  • Stopping after one layer for a triple composition — keep multiplying derivatives as you unwrap.

Curriculum expectation: Strand A, Expectation A3.5 — "verify, through investigation, using technology, the chain rule and the product rule (e.g., compare … the graph of \(f(x)=g(h(x))\) and the graph of the derivative \(f'(x)\) determined by applying the chain rule)."

Implicit differentiation is the technique for finding \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) when \(y\) is defined implicitly by an equation in \(x\) and \(y\) — for example \(x^2 + y^2 = 25\) or \(x^3 + y^3 = 6xy\) — rather than as an explicit function \(y = f(x)\). The procedure: differentiate both sides of the equation with respect to \(x\), treating \(y\) as a differentiable function of \(x\) and applying the chain rule every time \(y\) is differentiated; then solve algebraically for \(\frac{dy}{dx}\).

The key mechanical move is that \(\frac{d}{dx}[y^n] = n y^{n-1} \cdot \frac{dy}{dx}\), not just \(n y^{n-1}\). The extra \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) factor is the chain rule contribution: \(y\) is itself changing as \(x\) changes, and that change rate must be carried along. The same applies to \(\frac{d}{dx}[\sin y] = \cos y \cdot \frac{dy}{dx}\), \(\frac{d}{dx}[xy] = y + x\frac{dy}{dx}\) (product rule!), and so on.

Why bother? Some curves — circles, ellipses, the folium of Descartes \(x^3 + y^3 = 6xy\) — cannot be expressed as a single function \(y = f(x)\), yet they have well-defined tangent slopes at most points. Implicit differentiation gives those slopes without solving for \(y\). It also dramatically simplifies derivatives that, when written explicitly, would be lengthy.

  • Implicit equation — an equation relating \(x\) and \(y\) that may define multiple branches of \(y\).
  • Implicit differentiation — apply \(\frac{d}{dx}\) to both sides; treat \(y\) as \(y(x)\); attach \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) whenever \(y\) is differentiated.
  • Chain rule on \(y\)-terms — \(\frac{d}{dx}[y^n] = n y^{n-1} y'\); the most common application.
  • Product rule on \(xy\)-terms — \(\frac{d}{dx}[xy] = y + x y'\), not just \(y'\).
  • Solve for \(y'\) — collect all \(y'\) terms on one side; factor; divide.

Worked Example: Find \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) for \(x^2 + y^2 = 25\), then the slope at \((3,4)\).

Differentiate both sides: \(2x + 2y\frac{dy}{dx} = 0\).

Solve for \(\frac{dy}{dx}\): \(2y\frac{dy}{dx} = -2x \Rightarrow \frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{x}{y}\).

At \((3,4)\): \(\frac{dy}{dx} = -\tfrac{3}{4}\). The tangent to the circle at \((3,4)\) has slope \(-\tfrac{3}{4}\), as expected from elementary geometry (perpendicular to the radius).

Worked Example: Find \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) for \(x^2 y + xy^2 = 6\).

Differentiate term by term (each is a product, so use product rule and chain rule): \(\frac{d}{dx}[x^2 y] = 2xy + x^2 y'\); \(\frac{d}{dx}[xy^2] = y^2 + x\cdot 2y y' = y^2 + 2xy y'\).

So \(2xy + x^2 y' + y^2 + 2xy y' = 0\). Group \(y'\) terms: \((x^2 + 2xy)y' = -(2xy + y^2)\).

Solve: \(y' = -\frac{2xy + y^2}{x^2 + 2xy} = -\frac{y(2x + y)}{x(x + 2y)}\).

  • Forgetting the \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) factor when differentiating a \(y\)-term — by far the most common mistake.
  • Treating \(xy\) as a single variable rather than a product; \(\frac{d}{dx}[xy] = y + xy'\).
  • Failing to solve algebraically for \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) at the end — the answer should be in terms of both \(x\) and \(y\).
  • Plugging a point in too early; differentiate first, then substitute.

Curriculum expectation: Strand A, Expectation A3.5 (extension) — application of the chain rule to relations defined implicitly, supporting tangent-line problems on curves that cannot be expressed as \(y=f(x)\).

Higher-order derivatives are derivatives of derivatives. If \(f'(x)\) is the first derivative, then \(f''(x)\) (read "f double-prime") is the derivative of \(f'(x)\) — the second derivative. Continuing, \(f'''(x), f^{(4)}(x), \ldots, f^{(n)}(x)\) record successive instantaneous rates of change. Each takes the previous derivative as input and differentiates again with the usual rules.

Physical meaning: if \(s(t)\) is position, then \(s'(t) = v(t)\) is velocity, \(s''(t) = a(t)\) is acceleration, and \(s'''(t)\) is "jerk" (the rate of change of acceleration). Knowing one of these tells you about the others — for example, an object's velocity is constant iff its acceleration is zero, and its acceleration is positive iff its velocity is increasing.

In curve sketching (Unit 4), \(f''(x)\) determines concavity: where \(f''>0\), the graph of \(f\) is concave up (smiling); where \(f''<0\), concave down (frowning); where \(f''\) changes sign, the graph has an inflection point. So higher-order derivatives are not just abstract: they encode geometric and physical information the first derivative alone cannot.

  • Second derivative — \(f''(x) = \frac{d}{dx}[f'(x)]\); also \(\frac{d^2 y}{dx^2}\).
  • Higher-order derivative — \(f^{(n)}(x)\), iterated differentiation \(n\) times.
  • Position/velocity/acceleration — \(s(t), s'(t)=v(t), s''(t)=a(t)\).
  • Concavity sign — \(f''>0\) means concave up; \(f''<0\) means concave down.
  • Inflection point — where \(f''\) changes sign; concavity reverses.

Worked Example: For \(f(x) = x^4 - 6x^2 + 5\), find \(f'(x), f''(x), f'''(x)\).

\(f'(x) = 4x^3 - 12x\).

\(f''(x) = 12x^2 - 12\).

\(f'''(x) = 24x\). (And \(f^{(4)}(x) = 24\), \(f^{(5)}(x) = 0\) — eventually a polynomial's derivatives become zero.)

Worked Example: A particle has position \(s(t) = t^3 - 6t^2 + 9t\) (in metres, \(t\) in seconds). Find velocity and acceleration at \(t=2\).

Velocity: \(v(t) = s'(t) = 3t^2 - 12t + 9\). At \(t=2\): \(v(2) = 12 - 24 + 9 = -3\) m/s (moving backward).

Acceleration: \(a(t) = s''(t) = 6t - 12\). At \(t=2\): \(a(2) = 0\) m/s². (Velocity is at its minimum and about to start increasing.)

  • Confusing \(f''(x)\) with \([f'(x)]^2\) — they are different; \(f''\) is a second derivative, \([f']^2\) is a square.
  • Forgetting to differentiate each time before substituting numerical values.
  • Stopping after one differentiation when asked for the second derivative.
  • Mistakenly thinking acceleration is the second derivative of velocity — it is the first derivative of velocity, equivalently the second derivative of position.
