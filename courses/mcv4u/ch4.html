Unit 4: Curve Sketching

Increasing/decreasing, critical points, concavity, asymptotes

📚 Strand B: Derivatives & Applications 📹 10 lessons
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The first derivative tells us about the direction of a curve: \(f'(x) > 0\) means \(f\) is increasing, \(f'(x) < 0\) means decreasing. Where \(f'\) changes sign — at a critical number — the function may have a local maximum or minimum. The first derivative test diagnoses these: at a critical number \(c\), if \(f'\) changes from positive to negative, \(c\) is a local max; if negative to positive, a local min; if no sign change, neither.

The second derivative reveals curvature. When \(f''(x) > 0\), the curve is concave up (cup-shaped, holding water); when \(f''(x) < 0\), it is concave down. A point where concavity changes is a point of inflection. The second derivative test at a critical number \(c\) with \(f'(c)=0\): if \(f''(c) > 0\), local min; if \(f''(c) < 0\), local max; if \(f''(c)=0\), the test is inconclusive (fall back to the first derivative test).

Asymptotes describe end behaviour. Vertical asymptotes appear where a rational function's denominator is zero but the numerator is not. Horizontal asymptotes emerge from \(\lim_{x\to\pm\infty} f(x)\); for rational functions, compare degrees of numerator and denominator. Oblique (slant) asymptotes occur when the numerator degree exceeds the denominator's by exactly one — long division reveals the linear asymptote. A complete sketch synthesizes domain, intercepts, asymptotes, intervals of increase/decrease, local extrema, concavity, and inflection points.

  • Increasing — \(f(x_1) < f(x_2)\) whenever \(x_1 < x_2\); equivalent to \(f'(x) \ge 0\) on the interval (positive everywhere except possibly isolated points).
  • Critical number — \(x = c\) where \(f'(c) = 0\) or \(f'(c)\) does not exist (and \(c\) is in the domain of \(f\)).
  • Concave up / down — concave up when \(f''(x) > 0\); concave down when \(f''(x) < 0\).
  • Point of inflection — a point on the graph where concavity changes and the tangent line exists.
  • Vertical asymptote — line \(x=a\) such that \(\lim_{x\to a^\pm} f(x) = \pm\infty\).
  • Horizontal asymptote — line \(y=L\) such that \(\lim_{x\to\pm\infty} f(x) = L\).
  • Oblique asymptote — non-horizontal line \(y = mx + b\) that the curve approaches as \(x \to \pm\infty\).

Worked Example: Full analysis of \(f(x) = x^3 - 6x^2 + 9x\)

Domain: all real \(x\). Intercepts: \(f(0)=0\); \(f(x) = x(x-3)^2 = 0\) at \(x=0\) and \(x=3\) (double root).

First derivative: \(f'(x) = 3x^2 - 12x + 9 = 3(x-1)(x-3)\). Critical numbers: \(x=1\), \(x=3\).

Sign of \(f'\): positive on \((-\infty,1)\), negative on \((1,3)\), positive on \((3,\infty)\). So \(f\) increases on \((-\infty,1)\), decreases on \((1,3)\), increases on \((3,\infty)\). Local max at \(x=1\): \(f(1) = 1-6+9 = 4\). Local min at \(x=3\): \(f(3) = 27 - 54 + 27 = 0\).

Second derivative: \(f''(x) = 6x - 12 = 6(x-2)\). Inflection at \(x=2\); \(f(2) = 8-24+18 = 2\). Concave down on \((-\infty,2)\), concave up on \((2,\infty)\).

Verify max/min with second derivative test: \(f''(1) = -6 < 0\) confirms local max; \(f''(3) = 6 > 0\) confirms local min.

Worked Example: Asymptotes of \(\displaystyle f(x) = \frac{2x^2 + 3x - 1}{x - 2}\)

Vertical: denominator zero at \(x=2\); numerator at \(x=2\) is \(8 + 6 - 1 = 13 \ne 0\), so \(x=2\) is a vertical asymptote.

Degree of numerator (2) exceeds denominator (1) by one, so there is an oblique asymptote (and no horizontal asymptote). Polynomial long division: \[\frac{2x^2 + 3x - 1}{x-2} = 2x + 7 + \frac{13}{x-2}.\]

As \(x\to\pm\infty\), the remainder \(\frac{13}{x-2} \to 0\), so the oblique asymptote is \(y = 2x + 7\).

  • Claiming an inflection point wherever \(f''=0\), without verifying that the sign of \(f''\) actually changes.
  • Treating every point where the denominator is zero as a vertical asymptote — if the numerator also vanishes there and the factor cancels, you have a hole, not an asymptote.
  • Forgetting that \(x=c\) is only a critical number if \(c\) is in the domain of \(f\).
  • Using the second derivative test when \(f''(c) = 0\) — the test gives no information; revert to the first derivative test.
  • Mis-reading a sign chart by testing values outside the interval being studied.

Topic 4.1 formalizes how the first derivative tells us where a function is rising or falling. A function \(f\) is increasing on an interval \(I\) if \(x_1 < x_2 \Rightarrow f(x_1) < f(x_2)\) for all \(x_1, x_2 \in I\); it is decreasing if \(x_1 < x_2 \Rightarrow f(x_1) > f(x_2)\). The first-derivative connection: if \(f'(x) > 0\) throughout \(I\), then \(f\) is increasing on \(I\); if \(f'(x) < 0\), then decreasing. This is a consequence of the Mean Value Theorem.

The practical procedure is a sign chart for \(f'\): (1) compute \(f'(x)\); (2) find critical numbers, where \(f'\) is zero or undefined; (3) determine the sign of \(f'\) on each interval between critical numbers and at the domain boundary, by testing a representative \(x\)-value or factoring; (4) state the intervals of increase (\(f'>0\)) and decrease (\(f'<0\)).

Domain matters: include all numbers where \(f'\) is undefined but \(f\) is defined (cusps, vertical tangents), and exclude any number not in the domain of \(f\) (like the asymptote of a rational function). Critical numbers where \(f\) is undefined are NOT part of the function's intervals; they break the number line into separate analysis regions.

  • Increasing on \(I\) — \(f'(x) > 0\) for all \(x \in I\) (strict version).
  • Decreasing on \(I\) — \(f'(x) < 0\) for all \(x \in I\).
  • Critical number — value where \(f'(x) = 0\) or \(f'(x)\) is undefined (but \(f(x)\) is defined).
  • Sign chart — a number-line diagram showing signs of \(f'\) on each subinterval.
  • Mean Value Theorem — between any two values of \(x\) where \(f'>0\) throughout, \(f\) increases (the underlying theorem).

Worked Example: Find the intervals on which \(f(x) = x^3 - 6x^2 + 9x + 2\) is increasing and decreasing.

\(f'(x) = 3x^2 - 12x + 9 = 3(x^2 - 4x + 3) = 3(x-1)(x-3)\). Critical numbers: \(x = 1, 3\).

Sign chart: for \(x<1\), \(f'>0\) (test \(x=0\): \(3(-1)(-3) = 9 > 0\)). For \(1 < x < 3\), \(f'<0\) (test \(x=2\): \(3(1)(-1) = -3 < 0\)). For \(x>3\), \(f'>0\) (test \(x=4\): \(3(3)(1) = 9 > 0\)).

Increasing on \((-\infty, 1) \cup (3, \infty)\); decreasing on \((1, 3)\).

Worked Example: For \(g(x) = \frac{x}{x^2 + 1}\), find the intervals of increase/decrease.

\(g'(x) = \frac{(x^2+1) - x(2x)}{(x^2+1)^2} = \frac{1 - x^2}{(x^2+1)^2}\). Denominator always positive; sign of \(g'\) matches \(1 - x^2 = (1-x)(1+x)\).

Critical numbers: \(x = \pm 1\). Sign: \(g'<0\) for \(x<-1\); \(g'>0\) for \(-11\).

Decreasing on \((-\infty, -1) \cup (1, \infty)\); increasing on \((-1, 1)\).

  • Reporting "increasing where \(f > 0\)" — that's about the function's sign, not its slope; you want \(f' > 0\).
  • Forgetting to factor \(f'\) before sign-charting; partially factored expressions hide critical numbers.
  • Including critical numbers in BOTH the increasing and decreasing intervals — convention is to use open intervals at critical numbers.
  • Forgetting to remove points outside the domain (e.g., vertical asymptotes of a rational function).

Curriculum expectation: Strand B, Expectation B1.1 — "sketch the graph of a derivative function, given the graph of a function that is continuous over an interval, and recognize points of inflection of the given function," with B2.1 — "make connections between the sign of the first derivative on either side of a critical number and the existence of a local extremum at that critical number."

Topic 4.2 classifies critical points as local maxima, local minima, or neither using the first derivative test. At a critical number \(x=c\) (where \(f'(c)=0\) or \(f'\) is undefined), examine the sign of \(f'\) immediately to the left and right: if \(f'\) goes from \(+\) to \(-\), then \(c\) is a local max; from \(-\) to \(+\), local min; same sign on both sides (e.g., \(+/+\) or \(-/-\)), neither — typically a horizontal point of inflection.

Local extrema are a local property: \(f(c)\) is larger (or smaller) than nearby \(f\) values, but not necessarily than all values on the function's domain. This contrasts with the absolute extrema on a closed interval (Topic 3.2). A function on an unbounded or open domain may have local extrema without absolute ones, or vice versa.

The first-derivative test is a sign-change test, which makes it robust: it works even when \(f''\) is hard to compute, when \(f''(c) = 0\) (the second-derivative test becomes inconclusive), and when \(f'\) is undefined at \(c\) (e.g., a cusp like in \(f(x) = x^{2/3}\), where \(c=0\) is a local minimum despite \(f'(0)\) being undefined).

  • Local maximum at \(c\) — \(f(c) \ge f(x)\) for all \(x\) near \(c\).
  • Local minimum at \(c\) — \(f(c) \le f(x)\) for all \(x\) near \(c\).
  • Critical point — the point \((c, f(c))\) where \(c\) is a critical number.
  • First derivative test — sign change of \(f'\) at \(c\): \(+/-\) → max; \(-/+\) → min; same sign → neither.
  • Local vs. absolute — local extrema concern small neighbourhoods; absolute extrema concern the whole domain or interval.

Worked Example: Locate and classify the local extrema of \(f(x) = 2x^3 - 9x^2 + 12x - 1\).

\(f'(x) = 6x^2 - 18x + 12 = 6(x^2 - 3x + 2) = 6(x-1)(x-2)\). Critical numbers: \(x=1, x=2\).

Sign of \(f'\): at \(x=0\), \(f'(0) = 12 > 0\); at \(x = 1.5\), \(f'(1.5) = 6(0.5)(-0.5) = -1.5 < 0\); at \(x=3\), \(f'(3) = 6(2)(1) = 12 > 0\).

So \(f'\) goes \(+,-,+\). At \(x=1\) (\(+\to -\)): local maximum, \(f(1) = 2 - 9 + 12 - 1 = 4\). At \(x=2\) (\(-\to +\)): local minimum, \(f(2) = 16 - 36 + 24 - 1 = 3\).

Worked Example: Classify the critical point at \(x=0\) for \(f(x) = x^3\).

\(f'(x) = 3x^2\). \(f'(0) = 0\), so \(x=0\) is a critical number.

Sign of \(f'\): for \(x<0\), \(3x^2 > 0\); for \(x>0\), \(3x^2 > 0\). No sign change.

Therefore \(x=0\) is neither a local max nor a local min; it's a horizontal point of inflection.

  • Concluding \(x=0\) is a local extremum for \(f(x)=x^3\) just because \(f'(0)=0\) — sign of \(f'\) doesn't change, so it isn't an extremum.
  • Reporting only \(x\)-values without the corresponding \(f(c)\) value; the "extremum" is the function value.
  • Forgetting critical numbers where \(f'\) is undefined (cusps).
  • Confusing local max (\(+\to -\)) with local min (\(-\to +\)) — the direction matters.

Curriculum expectation: Strand B, Expectation B2.1 — "make connections between the sign of the first derivative on either side of a critical number and the existence of a local extremum at that critical number."

Asymptotes describe how a curve behaves at infinity or near a vertical line of indefinition. A vertical asymptote at \(x=a\) occurs when \(\lim_{x\to a^\pm}f(x) = \pm\infty\). For a rational function \(\frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}\) in lowest terms, this happens precisely when \(Q(a)=0\) and \(P(a)\ne 0\) — the denominator vanishes faster than the numerator. If both vanish, factor and cancel: the cancellation produces a removable discontinuity (a hole) rather than a vertical asymptote.

A horizontal asymptote is a horizontal line \(y=L\) such that \(\lim_{x\to\pm\infty} f(x) = L\). For rationals, compare the degrees of numerator (\(n\)) and denominator (\(d\)): if \(nd\), there is no horizontal asymptote — but an oblique (slant) asymptote exists when \(n = d+1\), obtained by polynomial long division.

The slant asymptote is the polynomial quotient. For \(\frac{x^2+1}{x-1}\), long division gives \(x + 1 + \frac{2}{x-1}\); as \(x\to\pm\infty\), the remainder term \(\frac{2}{x-1}\to 0\), so \(y = x+1\) is the slant asymptote. The curve approaches this line as \(x\) grows large in either direction.

  • Vertical asymptote at \(x=a\) — \(\lim_{x\to a^\pm}f(x)=\pm\infty\); for rationals in lowest terms, \(Q(a)=0, P(a)\ne 0\).
  • Horizontal asymptote — \(\lim_{x\to\pm\infty}f(x)=L\); compare numerator and denominator degrees.
  • Oblique (slant) asymptote — exists for rationals when \(\deg P = \deg Q + 1\); obtained by polynomial division.
  • Removable discontinuity — common factor of \(P\) and \(Q\); a hole, not an asymptote.
  • Behaviour near asymptote — examine one-sided limits to determine \(+\infty\) vs. \(-\infty\).

Worked Example: Find all asymptotes of \(f(x) = \dfrac{2x^2 - 3}{x^2 - 4}\).

Denominator \(x^2 - 4 = (x-2)(x+2) = 0\) at \(x=\pm 2\); numerator at those points: \(2(4)-3 = 5\) and \(2(4)-3=5\), both nonzero. Vertical asymptotes at \(x=-2\) and \(x=2\).

Degrees: numerator 2, denominator 2; horizontal asymptote at \(y = \tfrac{2}{1} = 2\).

Worked Example: Find the oblique asymptote of \(g(x) = \dfrac{x^2 + 2x + 5}{x + 1}\).

Polynomial long division: \(x^2 + 2x + 5 \div (x+1)\). Divide \(x^2 \div x = x\); multiply back: \(x(x+1) = x^2 + x\); subtract: \(x + 5\). Then \(x \div x = 1\); multiply: \(x+1\); subtract: \(4\).

So \(g(x) = x + 1 + \frac{4}{x+1}\). As \(x \to \pm\infty\), \(\frac{4}{x+1} \to 0\); the slant asymptote is \(y = x + 1\).

  • Mistaking a hole for a vertical asymptote — always check whether the numerator also vanishes at the suspect point.
  • Reporting a horizontal asymptote when degrees are unequal in the wrong direction (\(n>d\)): there isn't one.
  • Forgetting that horizontal and oblique asymptotes are mutually exclusive for rationals.
  • Setting up the limit \(\lim_{x\to a}f(x)\) without distinguishing left- and right-hand approaches at a vertical asymptote.

Curriculum expectation: Strand B, Expectation B3.1 — "describe key features of a polynomial function \(\big(\)e.g., domain and range, intercepts, increasing/decreasing intervals, local maxima and minima, points of inflection, intervals of concavity)\(\big)\), and of a rational function \(\big(\)e.g., asymptotes\(\big)\)."

Topic 4.4 uses the second derivative to analyze a curve's concavity — the way it bends. A graph is concave up on an interval if the curve lies above its tangent lines (smiling, holds water); it is concave down if the curve lies below its tangent lines (frowning, sheds water). Algebraically, concavity is governed by the sign of \(f''\): \(f''(x) > 0\) means concave up, \(f''(x) < 0\) means concave down.

A point of inflection is a point on the graph where concavity changes — from up to down or vice versa. To find them: (1) compute \(f''(x)\); (2) find where \(f''=0\) or \(f''\) is undefined; (3) verify that \(f''\) actually changes sign at each candidate (a zero of \(f''\) without sign change is not an inflection point — like \(x=0\) for \(f(x) = x^4\)).

The second derivative test classifies critical points: at a critical number \(c\) with \(f'(c) = 0\), if \(f''(c) > 0\) the graph is concave up at \(c\) so \(c\) is a local min; if \(f''(c) < 0\), local max; if \(f''(c) = 0\), inconclusive — fall back to the first derivative test. The test is fast when \(f''\) is easy to compute, but it can fail at degenerate critical points.

  • Concave up — \(f''(x) > 0\); curve bends upward; tangent lies below the curve.
  • Concave down — \(f''(x) < 0\); curve bends downward; tangent lies above the curve.
  • Point of inflection — a point where concavity changes sign; \(f''\) must change sign there.
  • Second derivative test — at \(f'(c)=0\): \(f''(c)>0\) → local min; \(<0\) → local max; \(=0\) → inconclusive.
  • Sign chart for \(f''\) — analogous to the \(f'\) chart; intervals separated by zeros and discontinuities of \(f''\).

Worked Example: Find the inflection points and intervals of concavity for \(f(x) = x^3 - 6x^2 + 5\).

\(f'(x) = 3x^2 - 12x\); \(f''(x) = 6x - 12\). Set \(f''=0\): \(x=2\).

Sign of \(f''\): for \(x<2\), \(f''<0\) (concave down); for \(x>2\), \(f''>0\) (concave up). Sign changes at \(x=2\), so \((2, f(2)) = (2, -11)\) is an inflection point.

Worked Example: Use the second derivative test on \(f(x) = x^4 - 4x^3\).

\(f'(x) = 4x^3 - 12x^2 = 4x^2(x-3)\). Critical numbers: \(x=0\) and \(x=3\).

\(f''(x) = 12x^2 - 24x\). \(f''(3) = 108 - 72 = 36 > 0\) → \(x=3\) is local min. \(f''(0) = 0\) → inconclusive.

At \(x=0\), fall back to the first derivative test: \(f'(x) = 4x^2(x-3)\), and \(4x^2 \ge 0\) always. So \(f'\) has the same sign as \(x-3\); for \(x\) near 0 (and \(<3\)), \(f' < 0\) on both sides. No sign change — \(x=0\) is neither (a horizontal point of inflection at \((0,0)\)).

  • Concluding that \(f''(c)=0\) automatically means an inflection point — a sign change is required.
  • Forgetting that the second derivative test is inconclusive when \(f''(c) = 0\).
  • Confusing concave up with increasing — they describe different things (bending vs. rising).
  • Reporting only the \(x\)-value of an inflection point; it's a point, so include \(f(x)\).

Curriculum expectation: Strand B, Expectation B1.5 — "verify that the second derivative provides information about concavity and points of inflection, by … connecting the sign of the second derivative to the concavity of the graph of \(f\)."

Topic 4.5 integrates everything from Topics 4.1-4.4 into a single procedure: produce an accurate sketch of \(y = f(x)\) using calculus. The standard curve-sketching algorithm follows seven steps, each contributing a feature to the graph: (1) Domain — exclude points where \(f\) is undefined; (2) Intercepts — set \(y=0\) for \(x\)-intercepts (often hard, sometimes skipped) and evaluate \(f(0)\) for \(y\)-intercept; (3) Symmetry — check \(f(-x)\) against \(f(x)\) for even/odd parity; (4) Asymptotes — vertical (where denominator vanishes), horizontal (limits at \(\pm\infty\)), and oblique (polynomial long division if applicable).

(5) First derivative analysis — compute \(f'(x)\), find critical numbers, build a sign chart, and identify intervals of increase/decrease plus local extrema; (6) Second derivative analysis — compute \(f''(x)\), find candidate inflection points, build a concavity chart; (7) Sketch — plot intercepts, asymptotes, extrema, and inflection points, then draw a smooth curve consistent with the sign charts. Label everything.

The algorithm is rote, but the skill is in interpreting the algebra. A negative second derivative at a critical number tells you both "concave down here" AND "local maximum". A vertical asymptote near a critical number determines whether the curve runs to \(+\infty\) or \(-\infty\). Reading all signals together gives the global shape without plotting a hundred points.

  • Curve-sketching algorithm — domain, intercepts, symmetry, asymptotes, first-derivative analysis, second-derivative analysis, sketch.
  • Even function — \(f(-x) = f(x)\); symmetric about the \(y\)-axis.
  • Odd function — \(f(-x) = -f(x)\); symmetric about the origin.
  • Behaviour near asymptotes — examine one-sided limits to determine \(\pm\infty\).
  • Synthesis — first- and second-derivative charts together encode shape; intercepts and asymptotes pin down position.

Worked Example: Sketch \(f(x) = \dfrac{x^2}{x^2 - 4}\).

Domain: \(x \ne \pm 2\). Y-intercept: \(f(0) = 0\). X-intercept: numerator = 0 at \(x=0\). Symmetry: \(f(-x) = f(x)\) — even.

Vertical asymptotes at \(x = \pm 2\). Horizontal: \(\lim_{x\to\pm\infty}\frac{x^2}{x^2-4} = 1\), so \(y=1\).

\(f'(x) = \frac{2x(x^2-4) - x^2(2x)}{(x^2-4)^2} = \frac{-8x}{(x^2-4)^2}\). Critical numbers: \(x=0\) (and \(\pm 2\) are not critical, they're excluded). \(f' > 0\) for \(x<0\) (except \(x=-2\)); \(f' < 0\) for \(x>0\) (except \(x=2\)). So \(x=0\) is a local max with \(f(0)=0\).

The curve passes through \((0,0)\) as a local max, dives down to \(-\infty\) at the asymptotes, then climbs to \(+\infty\) on the outside of the asymptotes, leveling toward \(y=1\) at the far ends.

  • Skipping the domain step and ending up with extraneous features near excluded \(x\)-values.
  • Forgetting to check symmetry — it cuts work in half for even or odd functions.
  • Mixing up vertical vs. horizontal asymptote conditions; they require different limit setups.
  • Drawing a sketch that contradicts the sign charts (e.g., a "concave up" piece where \(f''<0\)).

Curriculum expectation: Strand B, Expectation B3.2 — "determine, from the equation of a polynomial function or a simple rational function, the key features of the graph of the function (i.e., domain and range, intercepts, increasing/decreasing intervals, local maxima and minima, points of inflection, intervals of concavity, asymptotes), using the techniques of differential calculus, and sketch the graph by hand."
