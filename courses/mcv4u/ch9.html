Unit 9: Intersections

Intersection of lines, planes, distances, matrices

📚 Strand C: Vectors 📹 9 lessons
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Geometric intersection problems become algebraic systems once the objects are expressed in vector or scalar form. Two lines in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) can be coincident (same line), parallel and distinct (no intersection, same direction up to scalar), intersecting (one common point), or skew (no intersection, non-parallel directions). The first three cases also occur for two planes — but planes can never be skew. Three planes give richer possibilities: one common point, a common line, no intersection, or coincidence.

To intersect a line and a plane, substitute the parametric line equations into the scalar plane equation and solve for \(t\). One solution gives a single intersection point; no solution means the line is parallel to (and outside) the plane; infinitely many means the line lies in the plane. To intersect two planes, treat the system as two equations in three unknowns; the solution is one parameter family — a line. To intersect three planes, solve the \(3\times 3\) system, often using row reduction on an augmented matrix.

Distances are computed using projections. The distance from a point \(Q\) to a line with direction \(\vec{d}\) and point \(P_0\) is the magnitude of the component of \(\vec{P_0Q}\) perpendicular to \(\vec{d}\); compactly, \(\frac{|\vec{P_0Q}\times\vec{d}|}{|\vec{d}|}\). The distance from a point \(Q(x_1, y_1, z_1)\) to a plane \(ax + by + cz + d = 0\) is \[\frac{|ax_1 + by_1 + cz_1 + d|}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2+c^2}}.\]These formulas pack the entire chapter's geometry into clean one-line expressions.

  • Coincident lines/planes — same object, infinitely many points in common.
  • Skew lines — lines in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) that are non-parallel and non-intersecting.
  • Consistent system — system with at least one solution.
  • Inconsistent system — system with no solution (contradictory equations).
  • Augmented matrix — matrix representation of a linear system; coefficients on the left, constants on the right.
  • Elementary row operations — swap two rows, multiply a row by a non-zero scalar, add a multiple of one row to another. They preserve the solution set.
  • Row-echelon form — staircase pattern of leading entries; allows solving by back-substitution.
  • Distance point-to-plane — \(\displaystyle d = \frac{|ax_1 + by_1 + cz_1 + d|}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2+c^2}}\).
  • Distance point-to-line (\(\mathbb{R}^3\)) — \(\displaystyle d = \frac{|\vec{P_0Q}\times\vec{d}|}{|\vec{d}|}\).

Worked Example: Intersection of a line and a plane

Line: \(\vec{r} = (1, 0, 2) + t(2, -1, 3)\). Plane: \(x + 2y - z + 4 = 0\). Find the point of intersection.

Parametric line: \(x = 1+2t\), \(y = -t\), \(z = 2 + 3t\).

Substitute into the plane: \((1+2t) + 2(-t) - (2+3t) + 4 = 0\).

Simplify: \(1 + 2t - 2t - 2 - 3t + 4 = 0 \implies 3 - 3t = 0 \implies t = 1\).

Intersection point: \((x,y,z) = (1+2, -1, 2+3) = (3, -1, 5)\). Verify in the plane: \(3 + 2(-1) - 5 + 4 = 0\) ✓.

Worked Example: Distance from a point to a plane

Find the distance from \(Q(2, -1, 3)\) to the plane \(2x - y + 2z - 5 = 0\).

Apply the formula with \((a,b,c,d) = (2,-1,2,-5)\) and \((x_1, y_1, z_1) = (2,-1,3)\):

Numerator: \(|2(2) + (-1)(-1) + 2(3) - 5| = |4 + 1 + 6 - 5| = 6\).

Denominator: \(\sqrt{2^2 + (-1)^2 + 2^2} = \sqrt{9} = 3\).

Distance: \(\frac{6}{3} = 2\) units.

Worked Example: Three-plane system via row reduction

Solve \(\;\;\Pi_1\!:\, x + y + z = 6,\;\; \Pi_2\!:\, 2x - y + z = 3,\;\; \Pi_3\!:\, x + 2y - z = 2\).

Augmented matrix: \[\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 & 6 \\ 2 & -1 & 1 & 3 \\ 1 & 2 & -1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}.\]

\(R_2 \to R_2 - 2R_1\) and \(R_3 \to R_3 - R_1\): \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 & 6 \\ 0 & -3 & -1 & -9 \\ 0 & 1 & -2 & -4 \end{bmatrix}\).

\(R_3 \to 3R_3 + R_2\): \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 & 6 \\ 0 & -3 & -1 & -9 \\ 0 & 0 & -7 & -21 \end{bmatrix}\). Back-substitute: \(-7z = -21 \implies z = 3\); \(-3y - 3 = -9 \implies y = 2\); \(x + 2 + 3 = 6 \implies x = 1\).

Unique solution \((1, 2, 3)\) — the three planes meet at exactly one point.

  • Claiming "no intersection" when the system actually has infinitely many solutions (the line lies in the plane or two planes are coincident).
  • Forgetting absolute value in the point-to-plane distance formula — distance is non-negative.
  • Substituting numerical values for parameters before solving the system, then losing the parameter family description.
  • Using the same parameter \(t\) for two different lines when checking intersection — use \(s\) for one and \(t\) for the other.
  • Confusing "no solution" (parallel/inconsistent) with "infinitely many solutions" (dependent/coincident) when interpreting row-reduced systems.

Curriculum expectation: Strand C, Expectation C4.4 — "determine, through investigation with technology … and without technology, the equation of a plane in three-space, given the scalar equation of a plane parallel to the desired plane and a point on the desired plane, or the equation of a plane perpendicular to the desired plane and a point on the desired plane." Also C4.5 — "solve problems relating to lines and planes in three-space that are represented in a variety of ways (e.g., scalar, vector, parametric equations) and involving distances (e.g., between a point and a plane; between two skew lines) or intersections (e.g., of two lines, of a line and a plane), and interpret the result geometrically." And C4.6 — "determine, using properties of a plane, the equation of a plane in three-space, given … three non-collinear points on the plane, two non-parallel lines lying in the plane, [or] two parallel lines lying in the plane."
