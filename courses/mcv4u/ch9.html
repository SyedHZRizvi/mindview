📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Geometric intersection problems become algebraic systems once the objects are expressed in vector or scalar form. To intersect a line with a plane, substitute the parametric line equations into the scalar plane equation and solve for the parameter \(t\). One solution gives a single intersection point; no solution means the line is parallel to (and outside) the plane; infinitely many means the line lies in the plane.

The three outcomes are diagnosed by the relationship between the line's direction \(\vec{d}\) and the plane's normal \(\vec{n}\). If \(\vec{d}\cdot\vec{n} \ne 0\) the line crosses the plane at a unique point. If \(\vec{d}\cdot\vec{n} = 0\) the line is parallel to the plane and either lies in it or misses it entirely — distinguish these by testing whether a single line point satisfies the plane equation.

  • Substitution method — plug parametric \(x(t), y(t), z(t)\) into the scalar plane equation.
  • One solution — single intersection point at the value of \(t\) solving the equation.
  • No solution — line parallel to plane and not in it.
  • Infinitely many solutions — line lies in the plane.
  • Diagnostic via dot product — \(\vec{d}\cdot\vec{n} = 0\) signals parallelism.

Worked Example: Intersection of a line and a plane

Line: \(\vec{r} = (1, 0, 2) + t(2, -1, 3)\). Plane: \(x + 2y - z + 4 = 0\). Find the point of intersection.

Parametric line: \(x = 1+2t\), \(y = -t\), \(z = 2 + 3t\).

Substitute into the plane: \((1+2t) + 2(-t) - (2+3t) + 4 = 0\).

Simplify: \(1 + 2t - 2t - 2 - 3t + 4 = 0 \implies 3 - 3t = 0 \implies t = 1\).

Intersection point: \((x,y,z) = (1+2, -1, 2+3) = (3, -1, 5)\). Verify in the plane: \(3 + 2(-1) - 5 + 4 = 0\) ✓.

  • Claiming "no intersection" when the system actually has infinitely many solutions (the line lies in the plane).
  • Forgetting to verify the intersection point satisfies the plane equation.
  • Mixing up "parallel to plane" (\(\vec{d}\cdot\vec{n}=0\)) with "perpendicular to plane" (\(\vec{d}\) parallel to \(\vec{n}\)).

📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Two lines in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) can be coincident (same line), parallel and distinct (no intersection, same direction up to scalar), intersecting (one common point), or skew (no intersection, non-parallel directions). The skew case occurs only in three dimensions — in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) two non-parallel lines must cross.

To test for intersection, write both lines parametrically using different parameters (say \(s\) for the first, \(t\) for the second). Setting the position vectors equal gives a system of three equations (one per coordinate) in two unknowns. If the system is consistent, the lines intersect at the resulting \((s, t)\); if inconsistent, the lines are skew or parallel — distinguish by checking whether the direction vectors are scalar multiples.

  • Coincident lines — same line; infinitely many points in common.
  • Parallel and distinct — same direction up to scalar; no common point.
  • Intersecting — one common point; non-parallel directions.
  • Skew lines — non-parallel and non-intersecting; impossible in \(\mathbb{R}^2\).
  • Parameter-distinct trick — use \(s\) and \(t\), not both \(t\).

Worked Example: Test for intersection

Line 1: \(\vec{r}_1 = (1,2,0) + s(2,1,-1)\). Line 2: \(\vec{r}_2 = (4,0,1) + t(1,-1,2)\).

Equate: \(1 + 2s = 4 + t\), \(2 + s = -t\), \(-s = 1 + 2t\).

From the second: \(t = -2 - s\). Sub into the first: \(1 + 2s = 4 + (-2 - s) \implies 3s = 1 \implies s = \tfrac{1}{3}\), so \(t = -\tfrac{7}{3}\).

Check the third: \(-\tfrac{1}{3} \stackrel{?}{=} 1 + 2(-\tfrac{7}{3}) = 1 - \tfrac{14}{3} = -\tfrac{11}{3}\). Not equal — the lines are skew.

  • Using the same parameter \(t\) for two different lines when checking intersection — use \(s\) for one and \(t\) for the other.
  • Stopping after solving two of the three equations without checking the third — an inconsistent third equation reveals skew lines.
  • Treating "no intersection" as automatically meaning parallel; could be skew.

📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

A linear system in \(n\) unknowns is most efficiently solved by writing it as an augmented matrix — coefficients on the left, constants on the right — and applying elementary row operations: swap two rows, multiply a row by a non-zero scalar, or add a multiple of one row to another. Each operation preserves the solution set. The goal is to reach row-echelon form, a staircase pattern of leading entries from which we can read solutions by back-substitution.

A system is consistent if it has at least one solution, inconsistent if no solution. A consistent system has a unique solution when the number of leading entries equals the number of unknowns; otherwise it has a one- or two-parameter family of solutions. Row-reduction is the universal procedure behind every intersection problem in this unit.

  • Augmented matrix — matrix representation of a linear system; coefficients left, constants right.
  • Elementary row operations — row swap, row scaling by non-zero scalar, adding a multiple of one row to another.
  • Row-echelon form — staircase pattern; allows back-substitution.
  • Consistent system — at least one solution.
  • Inconsistent system — no solution; signalled by a row \([0\;0\;0\;|\;c]\) with \(c\ne 0\).

Worked Example: Row-reducing a \(2\times 2\) system

Solve \(2x + 3y = 7\), \(x - y = 1\).

Augmented matrix: \(\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3 & 7 \\ 1 & -1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\). Swap to put 1 in position (1,1): \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 & 1 \\ 2 & 3 & 7 \end{bmatrix}\).

\(R_2 \to R_2 - 2R_1\): \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 & 1 \\ 0 & 5 & 5 \end{bmatrix}\). Back-substitute: \(5y = 5 \implies y = 1\); \(x - 1 = 1 \implies x = 2\).

  • Confusing "no solution" (parallel/inconsistent) with "infinitely many solutions" (dependent/coincident) when interpreting row-reduced systems.
  • Forgetting that an operation like multiplying a row by zero is not elementary — it would destroy information.
  • Mixing row and column operations; only row operations are allowed in this method.

📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Two planes in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) can be coincident (same plane), parallel and distinct (no intersection), or intersecting in a line (the generic case). Planes can never be skew because they are infinite in two directions. The intersection of two non-parallel planes is always a line, parameterized by one free variable.

To find this line, treat the two scalar equations as a system of two equations in three unknowns and row-reduce. One variable will be free; setting it equal to a parameter \(t\) yields parametric equations for the intersection line. Geometrically, the line of intersection is perpendicular to both normal vectors, so its direction is \(\vec{n}_1 \times \vec{n}_2\) — a convenient shortcut when only the direction (not a specific point) is needed.

  • Two-plane intersection — line in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) (generic case).
  • Coincident planes — same equation up to a non-zero scalar multiple.
  • Parallel and distinct — normals proportional but constants don't match.
  • Direction shortcut — \(\vec{d} = \vec{n}_1 \times \vec{n}_2\) for the intersection line.
  • Free parameter — one variable remains free after row reduction.

Worked Example: Line of intersection of two planes

Planes: \(\Pi_1: x + y - z = 2\) and \(\Pi_2: 2x - y + z = 1\).

Add the equations: \(3x = 3 \implies x = 1\). From \(\Pi_1\): \(1 + y - z = 2 \implies y = 1 + z\). Let \(z = t\): \(y = 1 + t\).

Parametric form: \(x = 1\), \(y = 1 + t\), \(z = t\). Direction: \((0, 1, 1)\); a point on the line: \((1, 1, 0)\).

  • Claiming "no intersection" when two planes are actually coincident — infinitely many points lie on both.
  • Forgetting to introduce a free parameter when reporting the line of intersection.
  • Confusing the normal to a plane with the direction of the intersection line; they are perpendicular.

Curriculum expectation: Strand C, Expectation C4.3 — "recognize that the solution points \((x, y, z)\) in three-space of a single linear equation in three variables form a plane and that the solution points \((x, y, z)\) of a system of two linear equations in three variables form the line of intersection of two planes, if the planes are not parallel."

📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Three planes in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) can intersect in a single point, a common line, a common plane (all three coincident), or not at all. Row-reduction of the \(3\times 4\) augmented matrix distinguishes the cases automatically: three leading entries with consistent rows give a unique point; two leading entries plus a consistent free-variable row give a line; and an inconsistent row \([0\;0\;0\;|\;c]\) with \(c\ne 0\) signals no intersection (a "prism" of three parallel-edge planes).

Geometric configurations to recognize: three coincident planes (one solution surface, all equations multiples), three planes meeting in a line (two equations independent, third redundant), three planes meeting at a point (all three normals linearly independent), and three planes with no common point (parallel pairs, prism shapes, or a triangle of pairwise intersection lines).

  • Unique point — three independent equations; the \(3\times 3\) coefficient matrix is invertible.
  • Common line — one equation is redundant; one free parameter remains.
  • Common plane — two equations are redundant; two free parameters remain.
  • No intersection — row reduction yields a contradiction.
  • Prism configuration — pairwise intersections are three distinct parallel lines.

Worked Example: Three-plane system via row reduction

Solve \(\;\;\Pi_1\!:\, x + y + z = 6,\;\; \Pi_2\!:\, 2x - y + z = 3,\;\; \Pi_3\!:\, x + 2y - z = 2\).

Augmented matrix: \[\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 & 6 \\ 2 & -1 & 1 & 3 \\ 1 & 2 & -1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}.\]

\(R_2 \to R_2 - 2R_1\) and \(R_3 \to R_3 - R_1\): \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 & 6 \\ 0 & -3 & -1 & -9 \\ 0 & 1 & -2 & -4 \end{bmatrix}\).

\(R_3 \to 3R_3 + R_2\): \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 & 6 \\ 0 & -3 & -1 & -9 \\ 0 & 0 & -7 & -21 \end{bmatrix}\). Back-substitute: \(-7z = -21 \implies z = 3\); \(-3y - 3 = -9 \implies y = 2\); \(x + 2 + 3 = 6 \implies x = 1\).

Unique solution \((1, 2, 3)\) — the three planes meet at exactly one point.

  • Substituting numerical values for parameters before solving the system, then losing the parameter family description.
  • Calling the system "inconsistent" when a row of zeros appears; \([0\;0\;0\;|\;0]\) means dependent, not inconsistent.
  • Forgetting to interpret the geometry once the algebra is done — a unique solution corresponds to a single intersection point.

The distance from a point \(Q\) to a line with direction \(\vec{d}\) and known point \(P_0\) is the length of the perpendicular dropped from \(Q\) onto the line. Compactly, in \(\mathbb{R}^3\): \[d = \frac{|\vec{P_0Q}\times\vec{d}|}{|\vec{d}|}.\] The cross product produces a vector whose magnitude is \(|\vec{P_0Q}||\vec{d}|\sin\theta\); dividing by \(|\vec{d}|\) leaves \(|\vec{P_0Q}|\sin\theta\), which is exactly the perpendicular distance.

In \(\mathbb{R}^2\), the same logic applies, but the cross product is replaced by the determinant-like scalar \(|d_1 \cdot (Q_y - P_{0y}) - d_2 \cdot (Q_x - P_{0x})|\) (the "2D cross"). Alternatively, in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) the line has a Cartesian form \(ax + by + c = 0\) and the distance becomes \(\frac{|ax_1 + by_1 + c|}{\sqrt{a^2 + b^2}}\), mirroring the point-to-plane formula.

  • Distance point-to-line (\(\mathbb{R}^3\)) — \(\displaystyle d = \frac{|\vec{P_0Q}\times\vec{d}|}{|\vec{d}|}\).
  • Distance point-to-line (\(\mathbb{R}^2\), Cartesian form) — \(\displaystyle d = \frac{|ax_1 + by_1 + c|}{\sqrt{a^2 + b^2}}\).
  • Perpendicular foot — the closest point on the line to \(Q\), obtained by orthogonal projection.
  • Magnitude of cross — \(|\vec{P_0Q}\times\vec{d}| = |\vec{P_0Q}||\vec{d}|\sin\theta\); the geometric source of the formula.
  • Sign of numerator — irrelevant: absolute value ensures non-negative distance.

Worked Example: Distance from a point to a line in \(\mathbb{R}^3\)

Find the distance from \(Q(2, 1, 4)\) to the line \(\vec{r} = (1, 0, 1) + t(1, 2, 2)\).

\(\vec{P_0Q} = (2-1,\,1-0,\,4-1) = (1, 1, 3)\). \(\vec{d} = (1, 2, 2)\).

Cross product: \(\vec{P_0Q}\times\vec{d} = (1\cdot 2 - 3\cdot 2,\;3\cdot 1 - 1\cdot 2,\;1\cdot 2 - 1\cdot 1) = (-4, 1, 1)\).

Magnitudes: \(|\vec{P_0Q}\times\vec{d}| = \sqrt{16+1+1} = \sqrt{18}\); \(|\vec{d}| = \sqrt{1+4+4} = 3\).

Distance: \(\frac{\sqrt{18}}{3} = \sqrt{2} \approx 1.41\).

  • Forgetting absolute value in the formula — distance is non-negative.
  • Confusing the position vector with the displacement \(\vec{P_0Q}\); the formula needs the displacement.
  • Using just \(|\vec{P_0Q}|\) instead of dividing by \(|\vec{d}|\) after the cross product.

📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The distance from a point \(Q(x_1, y_1, z_1)\) to the plane \(ax + by + cz + d = 0\) is \[d = \frac{|ax_1 + by_1 + cz_1 + d|}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2+c^2}}.\] The numerator is the value of the plane expression at \(Q\); the denominator \(|\vec{n}| = \sqrt{a^2+b^2+c^2}\) normalizes by the magnitude of the normal vector. The absolute value ensures the result is non-negative regardless of which side of the plane \(Q\) lies on.

This formula generalizes the familiar point-to-line distance in \(\mathbb{R}^2\), \(\frac{|ax_1+by_1+c|}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2}}\). Both come from projecting the displacement from a known point on the plane (or line) onto the normal direction. Many distance problems — including distance between parallel planes, or between a line and a parallel plane — reduce to a point-to-plane calculation by picking any convenient point.

  • Distance point-to-plane — \(\displaystyle d = \frac{|ax_1 + by_1 + cz_1 + d|}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2+c^2}}\).
  • Numerator — value of the plane expression at the point; can be positive or negative before the absolute value.
  • Denominator — magnitude of the normal vector.
  • Parallel-plane distance — pick any point on one plane, then apply the point-to-plane formula with the other.
  • Sign of \((ax_1 + by_1 + cz_1 + d)\) — indicates which side of the plane \(Q\) is on.

Worked Example: Distance from a point to a plane

Find the distance from \(Q(2, -1, 3)\) to the plane \(2x - y + 2z - 5 = 0\).

Apply the formula with \((a,b,c,d) = (2,-1,2,-5)\) and \((x_1, y_1, z_1) = (2,-1,3)\):

Numerator: \(|2(2) + (-1)(-1) + 2(3) - 5| = |4 + 1 + 6 - 5| = 6\).

Denominator: \(\sqrt{2^2 + (-1)^2 + 2^2} = \sqrt{9} = 3\).

Distance: \(\frac{6}{3} = 2\) units.

  • Forgetting absolute value in the point-to-plane distance formula — distance is non-negative.
  • Using \(|\vec{n}|^2\) (instead of \(|\vec{n}|\)) in the denominator.
  • Plugging in the point but forgetting the constant \(d\) in the numerator.
