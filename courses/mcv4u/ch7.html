Unit 7: Vector Applications

Forces, dot product, cross product, projections

📚 Strand C: Vectors 📹 8 lessons
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

This unit equips vectors with two multiplication operations that have no analogue in elementary algebra: the dot product and the cross product. The dot product takes two vectors and returns a scalar; the cross product takes two vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) and returns a vector perpendicular to both. Each carries deep geometric meaning, and together they let calculus describe rigid-body physics, illumination, computer graphics, and 3D geometry rigorously.

The dot product is defined either algebraically as \(\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v} = u_1v_1 + u_2v_2 + u_3v_3\) or geometrically as \(\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v} = |\vec{u}||\vec{v}|\cos\theta\). Equating these gives the cosine angle formula \(\cos\theta = \frac{\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v}}{|\vec{u}||\vec{v}|}\). Two non-zero vectors are perpendicular precisely when their dot product is zero. The dot product also computes work: if a constant force \(\vec{F}\) moves an object through displacement \(\vec{d}\), the work is \(W = \vec{F}\cdot\vec{d}\).

The cross product \(\vec{u}\times\vec{v}\) returns a vector perpendicular to both \(\vec{u}\) and \(\vec{v}\), with magnitude \(|\vec{u}\times\vec{v}| = |\vec{u}||\vec{v}|\sin\theta\) — which equals the area of the parallelogram they span. The direction is given by the right-hand rule. Importantly, \(\vec{u}\times\vec{v} = -\vec{v}\times\vec{u}\): the cross product is anti-commutative. Projections decompose one vector along the direction of another: the scalar projection of \(\vec{u}\) on \(\vec{v}\) is \(\frac{\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v}}{|\vec{v}|}\), and the vector projection is that scalar times the unit vector \(\hat{v}\).

  • Dot product (scalar product) — \(\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v} = |\vec{u}||\vec{v}|\cos\theta = u_1v_1 + u_2v_2 + u_3v_3\).
  • Cross product (vector product) — \(\vec{u}\times\vec{v}\) is the vector perpendicular to both \(\vec{u}\) and \(\vec{v}\) with magnitude \(|\vec{u}||\vec{v}|\sin\theta\) and direction by the right-hand rule.
  • Orthogonal vectors — \(\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v} = 0\) for non-zero \(\vec{u}, \vec{v}\).
  • Scalar projection of \(\vec{u}\) onto \(\vec{v}\) — \(\operatorname{proj}_{\vec{v}}\vec{u}\) magnitude is \(\frac{\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v}}{|\vec{v}|}\).
  • Vector projection of \(\vec{u}\) onto \(\vec{v}\) — \(\left(\frac{\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v}}{|\vec{v}|^2}\right)\vec{v}\).
  • Work — \(W = \vec{F}\cdot\vec{d}\), measured in joules when \(\vec{F}\) is in newtons and \(\vec{d}\) is in metres.
  • Torque — \(\vec{\tau} = \vec{r}\times\vec{F}\); a vector perpendicular to both the lever arm \(\vec{r}\) and the applied force \(\vec{F}\).
  • Triple scalar product — \(\vec{u}\cdot(\vec{v}\times\vec{w})\); its absolute value is the volume of the parallelepiped spanned by the three vectors.

Worked Example: Angle between vectors

Find the angle between \(\vec{u} = (2, 1, -2)\) and \(\vec{v} = (1, 3, 1)\).

Dot product: \(\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v} = (2)(1) + (1)(3) + (-2)(1) = 2 + 3 - 2 = 3\).

Magnitudes: \(|\vec{u}| = \sqrt{4+1+4} = 3\); \(|\vec{v}| = \sqrt{1+9+1} = \sqrt{11}\).

Cosine: \(\cos\theta = \frac{3}{3\sqrt{11}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{11}}\). So \(\theta = \cos^{-1}\!\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{11}}\right) \approx 72.5^\circ\).

Worked Example: Cross product and area

For \(\vec{a} = (1, 2, 3)\) and \(\vec{b} = (4, 5, 6)\), compute \(\vec{a}\times\vec{b}\) and the area of the parallelogram they span.

Use the determinant formula: \[\vec{a}\times\vec{b} = \begin{vmatrix} \hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k} \\ 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \end{vmatrix}.\]

Expand: \(\hat{i}\bigl((2)(6)-(3)(5)\bigr) - \hat{j}\bigl((1)(6)-(3)(4)\bigr) + \hat{k}\bigl((1)(5)-(2)(4)\bigr)\) \(= \hat{i}(12-15) - \hat{j}(6-12) + \hat{k}(5-8) = -3\hat{i} + 6\hat{j} - 3\hat{k} = (-3, 6, -3)\).

Area: \(|\vec{a}\times\vec{b}| = \sqrt{9+36+9} = \sqrt{54} = 3\sqrt{6} \approx 7.35\) square units.

Verify perpendicularity: \(\vec{a}\cdot(-3,6,-3) = -3+12-9 = 0\) ✓; \(\vec{b}\cdot(-3,6,-3) = -12+30-18 = 0\) ✓.

  • Confusing dot product (returns a scalar) with cross product (returns a vector).
  • Forgetting that the cross product is only defined in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) — there is no cross product of two vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^2\).
  • Mis-signing the middle term in the determinant expansion: the \(\hat{j}\) component carries a minus sign.
  • Dropping the \(|\vec{v}|^2\) (not \(|\vec{v}|\)) in the denominator of the vector projection formula.
  • Treating \(\vec{u}\times\vec{v} = \vec{v}\times\vec{u}\) — the cross product is anti-commutative; sign matters.
  • Using the cross product to find the angle — use the dot product instead, since \(\sin\theta\) has two solutions in \([0,180^\circ]\) while \(\cos\theta\) is unique.

Curriculum expectation: Strand C, Expectation C2.3 — "determine, through investigation with and without technology, some properties (e.g., commutative, associative, and distributive properties) of the operations of addition, subtraction, and scalar multiplication of vectors." Also C3.2 — "perform the operation of dot product on two vectors represented as directed line segments … and in Cartesian form in two-space and three-space, and describe applications of the dot product (e.g., determining the angle between two vectors; determining the projection of one vector onto another)." And C3.3 — "determine, through investigation, properties of the dot product (e.g., investigate whether it is commutative, distributive, or associative; investigate the dot product of a vector with itself and the dot product of orthogonal vectors)." And C3.5 — "perform the operation of cross product on two vectors represented in Cartesian form in three-space, determine the magnitude of the cross product … and describe applications of the cross product (e.g., determining a vector orthogonal to two given vectors; determining the turning effect [or torque] when a force is applied to a wrench at different angles)."
