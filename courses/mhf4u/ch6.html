6.1 — Inverse of Exponential Functions
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

An exponential function \(f(x)=b^{x}\) with base \(b>0,\,b\ne 1\) takes a real input \(x\) and produces a positive output \(b^{x}\). The function is one-to-one (each input gives a distinct output), so it has an inverse. The inverse takes a positive number and returns the exponent needed to produce it — that inverse function is the logarithm: \(\log_{b}y=x\) means exactly \(b^{x}=y\).

Graphically, the inverse of \(y=b^{x}\) is its reflection across the line \(y=x\). Every key feature swaps: the exponential's \(y\)-intercept \((0,1)\) becomes the logarithm's \(x\)-intercept \((1,0)\); the horizontal asymptote \(y=0\) becomes a vertical asymptote \(x=0\); the domain of one becomes the range of the other. So \(y=b^{x}\) has domain \(\mathbb{R}\) and range \((0,\infty)\); \(y=\log_{b}x\) has domain \((0,\infty)\) and range \(\mathbb{R}\).

The point of this section is to anchor logarithms as inverses, not abstract symbols. Whenever you see \(\log_{b}y=x\), translate it to "the exponent that takes base \(b\) to \(y\)". That habit makes every later log law feel inevitable.

  • Exponential function — \(f(x)=b^{x}\), \(b>0,\,b\ne 1\); domain \(\mathbb{R}\), range \((0,\infty)\).
  • Logarithm — the inverse of the exponential: \(\log_{b}y=x\iff b^{x}=y\), defined for \(y>0\).
  • Inverse relationship — graphs of \(y=b^{x}\) and \(y=\log_{b}x\) are reflections in \(y=x\).

Worked Example — Find the inverse algebraically

Find the inverse of \(f(x)=3^{x}-2\).

Step 1. Write \(y=3^{x}-2\) and swap \(x\) and \(y\): \(x=3^{y}-2\).

Step 2. Solve for \(y\): \(3^{y}=x+2\), so \(y=\log_{3}(x+2)\).

Step 3. \(f^{-1}(x)=\log_{3}(x+2)\). Domain of inverse: \(x+2>0\Rightarrow x>-2\).

Worked Example — Key features from inverse

State the domain, range, intercepts, and asymptote of \(g(x)=\log_{2}x\).

Step 1. Domain: \((0,\infty)\). Range: \(\mathbb{R}\).

Step 2. \(x\)-intercept where \(\log_{2}x=0\): \(x=2^{0}=1\). Intercept \((1,0)\).

Step 3. No \(y\)-intercept (zero is not in the domain).

Step 4. Vertical asymptote at \(x=0\) (the boundary of the domain).

  • Logarithms only accept positive inputs — the argument of every log must be positive.
  • The base of a logarithm must be positive and not equal to 1; otherwise the inverse fails.
  • Reflecting across \(y=x\) means swapping every \((a,b)\) on the original to \((b,a)\) — useful for sketching by hand.

Curriculum expectation: Strand A, Expectation A1.1 — "recognize the logarithm of a number to a given base as the exponent to which the base must be raised to get the number… and evaluate simple logarithmic expressions."

6.2 — Introduction to Logarithms
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The defining relationship of logarithms is the exponential–logarithmic equivalence: \(\log_{b}x=y\) is the same statement as \(b^{y}=x\). Reading equations both ways — exponential to logarithmic, logarithmic to exponential — is the most fundamental skill in this chapter. It is the move you make every time you evaluate a log, solve a log equation, or apply a log law.

Two special logarithms have shortened names. The common logarithm has base 10 and is written \(\log x\) (no base shown). The natural logarithm has base \(e\approx 2.71828\) and is written \(\ln x\). Both appear constantly in science: \(\log\) shows up in pH, dB, magnitude scales; \(\ln\) shows up in growth, decay, and calculus.

To evaluate a logarithm exactly, ask: "to what power must I raise \(b\) to get \(x\)?" For example, \(\log_{2}8=3\) because \(2^{3}=8\); \(\log_{5}\tfrac{1}{25}=-2\) because \(5^{-2}=\tfrac{1}{25}\); \(\log_{4}2=\tfrac{1}{2}\) because \(4^{1/2}=2\). Recognising these patterns quickly is the goal.

  • Exponential–log equivalence — \(\log_{b}x=y\iff b^{y}=x\).
  • Common logarithm — \(\log x=\log_{10}x\).
  • Natural logarithm — \(\ln x=\log_{e}x\).
  • Key identities — \(\log_{b}1=0\), \(\log_{b}b=1\), \(\log_{b}b^{n}=n\), and \(b^{\log_{b}x}=x\).

Worked Example — Evaluate exact logs

Evaluate (a) \(\log_{3}81\); (b) \(\log_{8}2\); (c) \(\log_{1/2}16\).

(a) \(81=3^{4}\), so \(\log_{3}81=4\).

(b) \(2=8^{1/3}\) since \(8^{1/3}=\sqrt[3]{8}=2\). So \(\log_{8}2=\tfrac{1}{3}\).

(c) Need \(\bigl(\tfrac{1}{2}\bigr)^{x}=16=2^{4}\). Rewrite: \(2^{-x}=2^{4}\Rightarrow x=-4\). So \(\log_{1/2}16=-4\).

Worked Example — Convert between forms

Rewrite \(2^{5}=32\) in logarithmic form, and \(\log_{7}49=2\) in exponential form.

Step 1. \(2^{5}=32\Leftrightarrow\log_{2}32=5\).

Step 2. \(\log_{7}49=2\Leftrightarrow 7^{2}=49\).

  • Negative arguments inside a log are undefined — always check the input is positive.
  • Do not confuse \(\log_{b}(x\cdot y)\) with \(\log_{b}x\cdot\log_{b}y\) — the second is meaningless.
  • \(\log 100=2\), not \(100\) — students sometimes mix up the input and output of a log.

Curriculum expectation: Strand A, Expectation A1.2 — "determine, with technology, the approximate logarithm of a number to any base, including base 10."

6.3 — Transformations of Log Functions
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The general transformed logarithm is \(g(x)=a\log_{b}\!\bigl(k(x-d)\bigr)+c\). Each parameter does what it always did: \(a\) controls vertical stretch and reflection across the \(x\)-axis; \(k\) controls horizontal stretch and reflection across the \(y\)-axis; \(d\) translates horizontally; \(c\) translates vertically. The parent curve \(y=\log_{b}x\) has its vertical asymptote at \(x=0\) and its \(x\)-intercept at \((1,0)\); these key features move with the transformation.

The most important consequence is the location of the vertical asymptote: it moves with the horizontal shift \(d\). Setting the argument of the log equal to zero, \(k(x-d)=0\), gives the asymptote \(x=d\). The domain restriction also shifts: instead of \(x>0\), you need \(k(x-d)>0\).

When sketching by hand, take two parent points like \((1,0)\) and \((b,1)\), apply the mapping \((x,y)\to\bigl(\tfrac{x}{k}+d,\;ay+c\bigr)\), and connect them following the asymptote at \(x=d\).

  • General form — \(g(x)=a\log_{b}\!\bigl(k(x-d)\bigr)+c\); four parameters, same roles as for other transformations.
  • Vertical asymptote — at \(x=d\) (when \(k>0\)); shifts with the horizontal translation.
  • Domain — solve \(k(x-d)>0\); not always \(x>d\) if \(k\) is negative.
  • Mapping rule — parent point \((x,y)\to\bigl(\tfrac{x}{k}+d,\;ay+c\bigr)\).

Worked Example — Sketch by transformation

Sketch \(g(x)=2\log_{3}(x-1)+4\) from the parent \(y=\log_{3}x\).

Step 1. Parameters: \(a=2\), \(k=1\), \(d=1\), \(c=4\).

Step 2. Vertical asymptote moves from \(x=0\) to \(x=1\).

Step 3. Map parent point \((1,0)\): \(\bigl(\tfrac{1}{1}+1,\,2(0)+4\bigr)=(2,4)\). Map \((3,1)\): \(\bigl(3+1,\,2(1)+4\bigr)=(4,6)\).

Step 4. Sketch: vertical asymptote \(x=1\); curve passes through \((2,4)\) and \((4,6)\), increasing.

Worked Example — Domain with reflection

Find the domain of \(h(x)=\log(3-x)\).

Step 1. Argument must be positive: \(3-x>0\Rightarrow x<3\).

Step 2. Domain: \((-\infty,3)\). Vertical asymptote at \(x=3\); curve approaches from the left.

  • Domain depends on the sign of the argument, not just the value of \(d\). Always solve the inequality.
  • The vertical asymptote remains a critical feature — never draw the curve crossing it.
  • Reflecting horizontally (\(k<0\)) flips the asymptote-to-curve orientation; check by plotting one point.

Curriculum expectation: Strand A, Expectation A2.1 — "determine, through investigation… key features of the graphs of logarithmic functions… and connect these features to those of the corresponding exponential function."

6.4 — Power Law of Logarithms
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The Power Law of Logarithms says \(\log_{b}(x^{n})=n\log_{b}x\). An exponent inside the logarithm comes out front as a coefficient. The reason is simple if you trace it through the exponential equivalence: \(\log_{b}(x^{n})\) asks "to what power do I raise \(b\) to get \(x^{n}\)?" — and if \(b^{p}=x\), then \(b^{np}=x^{n}\), giving the exponent \(np=n\log_{b}x\).

The power law is the single most useful log identity for solving exponential equations. When you take the log of both sides of \(5^{x}=20\), the \(x\) drops out of the exponent to become a coefficient — converting an exponential equation into a linear one in \(x\). This is the standard move for any equation where the variable lives in an exponent.

The law works in both directions: you can pull exponents out front, but you can also push coefficients up into exponents. For example, \(3\log_{2}x=\log_{2}x^{3}\). Be alert for chances to compress (or expand) expressions to a useful form.

  • Power law — \(\log_{b}(x^{n})=n\log_{b}x\), valid for \(x>0\).
  • Reverse application — \(c\log_{b}x=\log_{b}(x^{c})\); useful for combining log expressions.
  • Change-of-base formula — \(\log_{b}x=\dfrac{\log x}{\log b}=\dfrac{\ln x}{\ln b}\); lets you evaluate any log on a standard calculator.

Worked Example — Simplify using the power law

Simplify \(\log_{2}(32^{3})\) to a single number.

Step 1. Power law: \(\log_{2}(32^{3})=3\log_{2}32\).

Step 2. \(32=2^{5}\), so \(\log_{2}32=5\).

Step 3. Result: \(3\cdot 5=15\).

Worked Example — Solve an exponential equation

Solve \(7^{x}=50\) for \(x\) to four decimal places.

Step 1. Take a common log of both sides: \(\log(7^{x})=\log 50\).

Step 2. Power law: \(x\log 7=\log 50\).

Step 3. Solve: \(x=\dfrac{\log 50}{\log 7}\approx\dfrac{1.6990}{0.8451}\approx 2.0103\).

  • The power law requires the argument to be positive — be careful when \(n\) is even and \(x\) is negative.
  • \(\log_{b}(x+y)\) is not \(\log_{b}x+\log_{b}y\) — that is the next section's law and applies only to multiplication.
  • The change-of-base formula is the bridge to using a calculator for any logarithm.

Curriculum expectation: Strand A, Expectation A2.4 — "recognize the laws of logarithms… and use these laws to simplify and evaluate numerical expressions."

6.5 — Product and Quotient Laws
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The Product Law states \(\log_{b}(xy)=\log_{b}x+\log_{b}y\); the Quotient Law states \(\log_{b}\!\bigl(\tfrac{x}{y}\bigr)=\log_{b}x-\log_{b}y\). Both follow from the fact that logarithms convert multiplication of arguments into addition of exponents — which is the entire reason logarithms were invented in the seventeenth century (to turn multiplication of large numbers into addition, before electronic calculators existed).

Combined with the Power Law from section 6.4, these three laws form the algebraic toolkit for all log manipulation. You will use them in two directions: expand a single complicated log into a sum of simpler logs (useful in calculus), and condense a sum or difference of logs into a single log (useful for solving log equations).

Always state restrictions when you apply log laws. Each input to a log must be positive, so combining \(\log x+\log(x-2)\) into \(\log\!\bigl(x(x-2)\bigr)\) is correct only when both \(x>0\) and \(x-2>0\), i.e., \(x>2\). Forgetting the restriction is a routine cause of extraneous solutions.

  • Product law — \(\log_{b}(xy)=\log_{b}x+\log_{b}y\).
  • Quotient law — \(\log_{b}\!\bigl(\tfrac{x}{y}\bigr)=\log_{b}x-\log_{b}y\).
  • Restrictions — every individual log argument must be positive.

Worked Example — Expand

Expand \(\log_{2}\!\left(\dfrac{8x^{3}}{y}\right)\) into a sum/difference of simpler logs.

Step 1. Quotient law: \(\log_{2}(8x^{3})-\log_{2}y\).

Step 2. Product law: \(\log_{2}8+\log_{2}(x^{3})-\log_{2}y\).

Step 3. Power law: \(\log_{2}8+3\log_{2}x-\log_{2}y\).

Step 4. Evaluate \(\log_{2}8=3\): final expression \(3+3\log_{2}x-\log_{2}y\).

Worked Example — Condense

Condense \(2\log x+\log(x-3)-\log(x+1)\) into a single logarithm.

Step 1. Power law on the first term: \(\log x^{2}+\log(x-3)-\log(x+1)\).

Step 2. Product law: \(\log\!\bigl(x^{2}(x-3)\bigr)-\log(x+1)\).

Step 3. Quotient law: \(\log\!\left(\dfrac{x^{2}(x-3)}{x+1}\right)\). Restrictions: \(x>3\) (the most restrictive of \(x>0,\,x>3,\,x>-1\)).

  • The product law is \(\log(xy)\), not \(\log(x+y)\) — confusion between these is the most common log error.
  • State the combined restriction (the intersection of individual restrictions) after condensing.
  • Coefficients become exponents before products combine — apply the Power Law first when condensing.

Curriculum expectation: Strand A, Expectation A2.4 — "recognize the laws of logarithms (i.e., the product law, \(\log_{b}(mn)=\log_{b}m+\log_{b}n\); the quotient law, \(\log_{b}\frac{m}{n}=\log_{b}m-\log_{b}n\); and the power law, \(\log_{b}m^{n}=n\log_{b}m\))."

6.6 — Logarithmic Scales
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Many physical quantities span enormous ranges — earthquake energies vary by factors of millions, sound intensities cover \(10^{12}\) orders of magnitude, hydrogen-ion concentrations in water range over ten powers of ten. Logarithmic scales compress these vast ranges into manageable numbers by reporting the exponent rather than the raw value.

The three scales you must know in MHF4U: pH defined as \(\text{pH}=-\log[H^{+}]\), where \([H^{+}]\) is the hydrogen-ion concentration in mol/L. Richter magnitude: \(M=\log\!\bigl(\tfrac{I}{I_{0}}\bigr)\), where \(I\) is amplitude and \(I_{0}\) is a reference. Sound intensity in decibels: \(\beta=10\log\!\bigl(\tfrac{I}{I_{0}}\bigr)\), where \(I_{0}=10^{-12}\) W/m\(^{2}\) is the threshold of hearing.

Because the scales are logarithmic, each unit increase represents a constant multiplicative factor: pH down by 1 means concentration up by 10×; Richter magnitude up by 1 means amplitude up by 10× (and energy roughly 32×); dB up by 10 means intensity up by 10×. Interpreting these ratios is the heart of the application questions.

  • pH scale — \(\text{pH}=-\log[H^{+}]\); each pH unit corresponds to a 10× change in concentration.
  • Richter scale — \(M=\log\!\bigl(\tfrac{I}{I_{0}}\bigr)\); each magnitude unit corresponds to a 10× change in amplitude.
  • Decibel scale — \(\beta=10\log\!\bigl(\tfrac{I}{I_{0}}\bigr)\); the factor of 10 means each 10 dB corresponds to 10× intensity.

Worked Example — Earthquake magnitude ratio

An earthquake of magnitude 6.3 is how many times more intense (in amplitude) than one of magnitude 4.8?

Step 1. Difference in magnitude: \(6.3-4.8=1.5\).

Step 2. Each magnitude unit is a factor of 10 in amplitude, so ratio \(=10^{1.5}\).

Step 3. \(10^{1.5}=10\cdot 10^{0.5}\approx 31.6\). The first earthquake's amplitude is about 32 times the second's.

Worked Example — pH to concentration

A solution has \(\text{pH}=3.4\). Find the hydrogen-ion concentration.

Step 1. \(3.4=-\log[H^{+}]\Rightarrow\log[H^{+}]=-3.4\).

Step 2. Convert to exponential: \([H^{+}]=10^{-3.4}\).

Step 3. \(10^{-3.4}\approx 3.98\times 10^{-4}\) mol/L.

  • The pH formula has a leading minus sign — easy to drop, and it changes everything.
  • "How many times louder" in dB needs careful interpretation: a 10 dB increase is 10× intensity, not 10 dB more loudness perceived.
  • Always state units (mol/L for concentration, W/m\(^{2}\) for intensity).

Curriculum expectation: Strand A, Expectation A3.4 — "solve problems involving exponential and logarithmic equations algebraically, including problems arising from real-world applications."
