7.1 — Solving Exponential Equations
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

An exponential equation has the variable in an exponent. Two situations arise. If you can rewrite both sides as powers of the same base, equate the exponents and solve algebraically. For instance, \(2^{x+1}=8\) becomes \(2^{x+1}=2^{3}\), giving \(x+1=3\). This works whenever the constants are obvious powers of a common base.

When a common base is not available, take the logarithm of both sides — using any base, though \(\log\) (base 10) or \(\ln\) (base \(e\)) is most convenient on a calculator — and apply the Power Law to bring the exponent down. The equation \(5^{x}=12\) becomes \(x\log 5=\log 12\), so \(x=\dfrac{\log 12}{\log 5}\).

More complex equations may need substitution. The equation \(4^{x}-6\cdot 2^{x}+8=0\) becomes the quadratic \(u^{2}-6u+8=0\) under the substitution \(u=2^{x}\). Recognising this pattern — exponential-quadratic — is a recurring exam skill.

  • Common base method — rewrite both sides as \(b^{\text{something}}\), then equate exponents.
  • Take a log method — apply \(\log\) or \(\ln\) to both sides, then Power Law to free the exponent.
  • Substitution method — let \(u=b^{x}\) when the equation is quadratic in the exponential.

Worked Example — Common base

Solve \(3^{2x-1}=27^{x+2}\).

Step 1. Rewrite 27 in base 3: \(27=3^{3}\), so \(27^{x+2}=3^{3(x+2)}=3^{3x+6}\).

Step 2. Equate exponents: \(2x-1=3x+6\).

Step 3. Solve: \(-7=x\), so \(x=-7\).

Worked Example — Take a log

Solve \(2^{x+1}=15\) to two decimal places.

Step 1. \(\log\bigl(2^{x+1}\bigr)=\log 15\). Power Law: \((x+1)\log 2=\log 15\).

Step 2. \(x+1=\dfrac{\log 15}{\log 2}\approx\dfrac{1.1761}{0.3010}\approx 3.9069\).

Step 3. \(x\approx 2.91\).

Worked Example — Quadratic substitution

Solve \(9^{x}-4\cdot 3^{x}+3=0\).

Step 1. Note \(9^{x}=(3^{x})^{2}\). Let \(u=3^{x}\): \(u^{2}-4u+3=0\).

Step 2. Factor: \((u-1)(u-3)=0\), so \(u=1\) or \(u=3\).

Step 3. Back-substitute: \(3^{x}=1\Rightarrow x=0\); \(3^{x}=3\Rightarrow x=1\).

  • Exponential expressions are always positive — reject any substitution solution \(u\le 0\).
  • When taking logs, you can use any base; the calculator's \(\log\) (base 10) or \(\ln\) (base \(e\)) is fine.
  • Always verify the final answer by substituting back into the original equation.

Curriculum expectation: Strand A, Expectation A3.1 — "recognize equivalent algebraic expressions involving logarithms and exponents, and simplify expressions of these types"; A3.3 — "solve exponential equations in one variable… by determining a common base, and by using logarithms."

7.2 — Solving Logarithmic Equations
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

A logarithmic equation has the variable inside a logarithm. The strategy is to convert the equation into a form you already know how to solve, using two main moves. If the equation looks like \(\log_{b}(\text{expression})=c\), convert it to exponential form: \(\text{expression}=b^{c}\). Then solve the resulting polynomial or rational equation. If the equation contains multiple logs on different sides, use the laws of logarithms to condense each side into a single log; then the one-to-one property says \(\log_{b}A=\log_{b}B\Rightarrow A=B\) (assuming \(A,B>0\)).

You must always state restrictions before solving and check every candidate against them at the end. Logarithms accept only positive arguments, so the algebra may produce a "valid" candidate that violates a restriction — that candidate is extraneous and must be rejected. Forgetting this check is the single most common cause of lost marks in Chapter 7.

For equations with logs of different bases, use the change-of-base formula to convert everything to a single base first.

  • Exponential conversion — \(\log_{b}A=c\Leftrightarrow A=b^{c}\); the basic move for log equations.
  • One-to-one property — \(\log_{b}A=\log_{b}B\) implies \(A=B\) (with both positive).
  • Extraneous solutions — candidates that satisfy the converted equation but violate the original log's domain.

Worked Example — Convert to exponential

Solve \(\log_{2}(x-3)=4\).

Step 1. Restriction: \(x-3>0\Rightarrow x>3\).

Step 2. Convert: \(x-3=2^{4}=16\).

Step 3. Solve: \(x=19\). Check restriction: \(19>3\). ✓

Worked Example — Condense then one-to-one

Solve \(\log(x+2)+\log(x-1)=\log 4\).

Step 1. Restrictions: \(x+2>0\) and \(x-1>0\), giving \(x>1\).

Step 2. Product Law: \(\log\!\bigl((x+2)(x-1)\bigr)=\log 4\).

Step 3. One-to-one: \((x+2)(x-1)=4\), so \(x^{2}+x-2=4\), giving \(x^{2}+x-6=0\).

Step 4. Factor: \((x+3)(x-2)=0\). Candidates \(x=-3,\,2\).

Step 5. Check restriction \(x>1\): reject \(-3\); accept \(2\). Solution: \(x=2\).

  • Always write restrictions before solving; checking them is non-negotiable.
  • The one-to-one property requires both sides to be single logarithms — condense first.
  • When converting \(\log_{b}A=c\) to exponential form, the result is \(A=b^{c}\), not \(b\cdot c\).

Curriculum expectation: Strand A, Expectation A3.2 — "solve simple logarithmic equations in one variable algebraically (e.g., \(\log_{3}(5x+6)=2\), \(\log_{6}(x+4)=2-\log_{6}(x-2)\))."

7.3 — Chapters 6-7 Summary & Review
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Chapters 6 and 7 together cover the complete story of exponential and logarithmic functions: their inverse relationship, the laws of logarithms, transformations, equation solving in both directions, and applications via logarithmic scales. The unifying idea is that exponentials and logarithms are inverse operations — every move in one direction has a precise counterpart in the other.

For review, organise by skill: (1) translate between exponential and logarithmic forms instantly; (2) know all three laws (product, quotient, power) and apply them in both directions (expand / condense); (3) sketch transformed log graphs from parent \(y=\log_{b}x\); (4) solve exponential equations by common base or by taking logs; (5) solve logarithmic equations by exponential conversion or condensing-then-one-to-one, always with restrictions; (6) interpret pH, Richter, and decibel scales.

Application-style word problems often blend exponential growth/decay with logarithm-based solving. For example, a doubling-time question \(P(t)=P_{0}\cdot 2^{t/k}\) requires log manipulation. Practice these in both decimal-precision and exact-form contexts.

  • Conversion fluency — \(\log_{b}A=c\Leftrightarrow A=b^{c}\) in both directions.
  • Three laws — product, quotient, power; restrictions on each argument.
  • Equation toolkit — common base, take a log, exponential conversion, one-to-one.
  • Real-world scales — pH, Richter, decibels; interpret each unit change as a multiplicative factor.

Worked Example — Doubling-time application

A bacteria population doubles every 5 hours. If the initial population is 200, how long until it reaches 1500?

Step 1. Model: \(P(t)=200\cdot 2^{t/5}\). Set \(P(t)=1500\): \(200\cdot 2^{t/5}=1500\).

Step 2. Isolate: \(2^{t/5}=\dfrac{1500}{200}=7.5\).

Step 3. Take a log: \(\dfrac{t}{5}\log 2=\log 7.5\), so \(\dfrac{t}{5}=\dfrac{\log 7.5}{\log 2}\approx\dfrac{0.8751}{0.3010}\approx 2.907\).

Step 4. \(t\approx 14.5\) hours.

  • Watch the difference between \(\log\) and \(\ln\) when setting up; either works but be consistent.
  • Word problems often have a "round to a sensible value" instruction — note units (hours vs days) at the end.
  • Always check restrictions on log equations, even when the algebra looks clean.

Curriculum expectation: Strand A, Overall Expectation A3 — "solve exponential and simple logarithmic equations in one variable algebraically, including those in problems arising from real-world applications."
