Unit 1: Introduction to Calculus

Limits, slopes of tangents, rates of change, continuity

📚 Strand A: Rate of Change 📹 6 lessons
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Calculus begins with a deceptively simple question: how do we measure the slope of a curve at a single point, when "slope" was originally defined only between two distinct points? The historical breakthrough — credited to Newton and Leibniz in the late 17th century — was to study what happens to the slope of a secant line \(\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\) as the second point slides toward the first, that is, as \(h \to 0\). The numerator and denominator both approach zero, so naïve substitution gives the indeterminate form \(\tfrac{0}{0}\). The limit is the rigorous tool that lets us extract a meaningful number from this expression.

This unit develops three intertwined ideas: the slope of a tangent line at a point, the instantaneous rate of change of a function, and the formal limit. The "why" matters more than the "what" — every later topic (derivatives, optimization, related rates) is built on this foundation. A function is continuous at \(x=a\) precisely when its limit exists, its value exists, and the two agree; this is why we factor, rationalize, or use conjugates to convert apparent \(\tfrac{0}{0}\) forms into algebraic expressions that survive the limiting process.

Average rate of change is geometric (a secant slope between two points); instantaneous rate of change is the limit of those secant slopes as the interval shrinks to zero. Understanding this distinction is the conceptual jump from Grade 11 functions to Grade 12 calculus.

  • Limit — the value \(L\) that \(f(x)\) approaches as \(x\) approaches \(a\); written \(\lim_{x\to a} f(x) = L\).
  • Secant line — a straight line passing through two points on a curve; its slope is \(\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}\).
  • Tangent line — the limiting position of secant lines as the two points coalesce; its slope is the instantaneous rate of change.
  • Average rate of change — \(\frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}\) over a finite interval.
  • Instantaneous rate of change — \(\lim_{h\to 0} \frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}\).
  • Continuity at \(x=a\) — \(f(a)\) exists, \(\lim_{x\to a} f(x)\) exists, and the two are equal.
  • Indeterminate form — an expression such as \(\tfrac{0}{0}\) whose value is not determined by the form alone and requires algebraic manipulation.

Worked Example: Slope of the tangent to \(f(x) = x^2\) at \(x = 3\)

Set up the difference quotient: \[m_{\text{tan}} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(3+h)-f(3)}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{(3+h)^2 - 9}{h}.\]

Expand the numerator: \((3+h)^2 - 9 = 9 + 6h + h^2 - 9 = 6h + h^2\).

Factor and cancel: \[\lim_{h\to 0} \frac{6h + h^2}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{h(6+h)}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0} (6 + h) = 6.\]

The tangent slope at \(x=3\) is \(m=6\). The cancellation step is legitimate because the limit only cares about values where \(h \ne 0\).

Worked Example: Limit requiring a conjugate

Evaluate \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 4} \frac{\sqrt{x}-2}{x-4}\). Direct substitution yields \(\tfrac{0}{0}\).

Multiply by the conjugate \(\frac{\sqrt{x}+2}{\sqrt{x}+2}\): \[\frac{\sqrt{x}-2}{x-4}\cdot\frac{\sqrt{x}+2}{\sqrt{x}+2} = \frac{x-4}{(x-4)(\sqrt{x}+2)} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{x}+2}.\]

Now substitute: \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 4} \frac{1}{\sqrt{x}+2} = \frac{1}{2+2} = \frac{1}{4}.\)

  • Substituting \(h=0\) before simplifying — the whole point of factoring/rationalizing is to remove the \(\tfrac{0}{0}\) before substitution.
  • Forgetting that \(\lim_{x\to a} f(x)\) does not require \(f(a)\) to exist or equal the limit.
  • Confusing average rate of change (a secant slope, needs two points) with instantaneous rate of change (a limit, at one point).
  • Writing \(\frac{0}{0} = 0\) or \(\frac{0}{0} = 1\) — both wrong; the form is indeterminate.
  • Cancelling factors carelessly when the limit point is exactly the root of the cancelled factor — be sure the resulting function is defined at the limit point.

Before we tackle limits and tangent slopes, we need an algebraic toolkit that lets us rewrite expressions whose direct substitution gives an indeterminate form. The key technique introduced in 1.1 is rationalizing — multiplying numerator and denominator by a carefully chosen conjugate so that the troublesome radical disappears from the denominator (or, more usefully later, from the numerator). The conjugate of \(\sqrt{a}-\sqrt{b}\) is \(\sqrt{a}+\sqrt{b}\), and their product \((\sqrt{a}-\sqrt{b})(\sqrt{a}+\sqrt{b}) = a-b\) is rational.

This matters in calculus because difference quotients of the form \(\frac{\sqrt{x+h}-\sqrt{x}}{h}\) cannot be evaluated at \(h=0\) until the \(h\) is removed from the denominator. Rationalizing the numerator by multiplying by \(\frac{\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x}}{\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x}}\) converts the numerator to \((x+h)-x = h\), which then cancels with the \(h\) in the denominator. The resulting expression has a well-defined limit as \(h \to 0\).

You will also encounter expressions with sums of three radicals or with cube roots, where the conjugate is built from the difference-of-cubes identity \(a^3-b^3 = (a-b)(a^2+ab+b^2)\) instead. The strategy generalizes: identify the algebraic identity that converts an irrational factor to a rational one, then multiply by its other factor over itself.

  • Radical expression — an algebraic expression containing a root, e.g., \(\sqrt{x}, \sqrt[3]{x+1}\).
  • Conjugate — for \(\sqrt{a}-\sqrt{b}\), the conjugate is \(\sqrt{a}+\sqrt{b}\) (same terms, opposite middle sign).
  • Rationalizing — multiplying numerator and denominator by a conjugate (or other factor) to eliminate radicals from the desired location.
  • Difference of squares — \((a-b)(a+b) = a^2-b^2\); the engine behind rationalizing square-root conjugates.
  • Indeterminate form — a substitution that yields \(\tfrac{0}{0}\), requiring algebraic manipulation before the limit is evaluable.

Worked Example: Rationalize the numerator of \(\dfrac{\sqrt{x+9}-3}{x}\) and evaluate the limit as \(x\to 0\).

Multiply top and bottom by the conjugate of the numerator: \[\frac{\sqrt{x+9}-3}{x} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{x+9}+3}{\sqrt{x+9}+3} = \frac{(x+9)-9}{x(\sqrt{x+9}+3)} = \frac{x}{x(\sqrt{x+9}+3)}.\]

Cancel the common \(x\): \(\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{x+9}+3}\). Now substitute \(x=0\): \(\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{9}+3} = \dfrac{1}{6}\).

  • Multiplying only the numerator (or only the denominator) by the conjugate — you must multiply BOTH or you change the value of the expression.
  • Forgetting to use the correct sign change: the conjugate of \(\sqrt{a}+\sqrt{b}\) is \(\sqrt{a}-\sqrt{b}\), not \(-\sqrt{a}-\sqrt{b}\).
  • Squaring instead of using the conjugate: \((\sqrt{a}-\sqrt{b})^2 \ne a-b\); it equals \(a - 2\sqrt{ab} + b\), which is worse.
  • Cancelling factors that aren't common — only cancel \(x\) after rationalizing has produced a matching factor on top.

Curriculum expectation: Strand A, Expectation A1.1 — "describe examples of real-world applications of rates of change, represented in a variety of ways" and the prerequisite algebra needed to "simplify radical expressions [with] rationalizing denominators" so that limits of the form \(\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{\sqrt{a+h}-\sqrt{a}}{h}\) become evaluable.

The slope of a tangent line is the central geometric idea that motivates the derivative. A secant line through two points \((a, f(a))\) and \((a+h, f(a+h))\) on the graph of \(f\) has slope \(\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}\) — the average rate of change. As the second point slides toward the first (i.e., \(h\to 0\)), the secant rotates and approaches the tangent line at \((a, f(a))\). Its slope, \(\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}\), is what we mean by the instantaneous slope of the curve at that point.

This limiting process is the defining moment of differential calculus: every later formula — the power rule, product rule, chain rule — is a shortcut for this limit. Geometrically, the tangent at \((a, f(a))\) is the unique non-vertical line that "best fits" the curve locally; algebraically, it has equation \(y - f(a) = m(x - a)\) where \(m\) is the limit above.

Not every curve has a tangent at every point. If the difference quotient does not approach a single finite value, the tangent fails to exist — at sharp corners, cusps, or vertical-tangent points. This is the first hint that differentiability is a stronger condition than continuity.

  • Secant line — a line through two distinct points of a curve; its slope is the average rate of change.
  • Tangent line — the limiting position of secant lines as the two intersection points merge.
  • Slope of tangent at \(x=a\) — \(m_{tan}=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}\), provided the limit exists.
  • Difference quotient — \(\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}\); the discrete version that becomes the derivative in the limit.
  • Tangent equation — \(y - f(a) = m_{tan}(x - a)\), point-slope form using the computed slope.

Worked Example: Find the equation of the tangent to \(f(x) = x^2 + 1\) at \(x = 2\).

Set up the difference quotient: \[m_{tan} = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(2+h)-f(2)}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{(2+h)^2+1 - (4+1)}{h}.\]

Expand: \((2+h)^2+1 = 4+4h+h^2+1 = 5+4h+h^2\). The numerator becomes \(5+4h+h^2 - 5 = 4h+h^2 = h(4+h)\).

Cancel \(h\): \(\lim_{h\to 0}(4+h) = 4\). So the slope is \(4\) and the tangent equation through \((2,5)\) is \(y - 5 = 4(x-2)\), i.e., \(y = 4x - 3\).

  • Confusing average rate of change (secant slope, two points) with instantaneous rate of change (tangent slope, one point obtained by a limit).
  • Substituting \(h=0\) directly into the difference quotient before simplifying — this gives \(\tfrac{0}{0}\), not the slope.
  • Forgetting to cancel the \(h\) before taking the limit, then concluding the limit doesn't exist.
  • Using \(f(a)\) in the tangent equation as a slope instead of the computed \(m_{tan}\).

Curriculum expectation: Strand A, Expectation A2.2 — "determine, through investigation, the equation of the line tangent to the graph of a polynomial function at a given point, by determining the slope of the line by using a limit, in the form \(\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}\)."

Topic 1.3 promotes the slope idea from pure geometry into the language of rates. The average rate of change of a quantity \(y = f(x)\) on \([a, a+h]\) is \(\frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}\) — exactly the secant slope from 1.2, but interpreted in problem units (m/s, dollars per item, °C per minute, etc.). The instantaneous rate of change at \(x=a\) is the limit \(\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}\), which is the tangent slope reinterpreted as a rate.

This dual identity — slope and rate — is what makes calculus so universally applicable. A position-time graph \(s(t)\) has secant slopes equal to average velocity and tangent slopes equal to instantaneous velocity. A cost-quantity graph has secants equal to average cost per unit and tangents equal to marginal cost. Even when no graph is drawn, the same algebraic limit governs the underlying rate.

A useful approximation technique: when the limit is hard to evaluate algebraically, use a table of difference quotients with progressively smaller \(h\) (e.g., \(0.1, 0.01, 0.001\)) and look for the value they approach. This numerical estimate of the instantaneous rate is often enough to motivate a conjecture about the exact value.

  • Average rate of change on \([a,b]\) — \(\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}\); the slope of the secant.
  • Instantaneous rate of change at \(x=a\) — \(\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}\); the slope of the tangent.
  • Average velocity — \(\frac{\Delta s}{\Delta t}\); change in displacement over change in time.
  • Instantaneous velocity — the limit of average velocities as \(\Delta t \to 0\); equals \(s'(t)\).
  • Units — always (output units)/(input units); a rate has dimensions, so keep them attached.

Worked Example: A ball's height in metres is \(h(t) = -5t^2 + 20t\). Find the average velocity on \([1,3]\) and the instantaneous velocity at \(t=1\).

Average velocity: \(\frac{h(3)-h(1)}{3-1} = \frac{(-45+60)-(-5+20)}{2} = \frac{15-15}{2} = 0\) m/s. (The ball is at the same height at \(t=1\) and \(t=3\).)

Instantaneous velocity at \(t=1\): \[\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{-5(1+h)^2+20(1+h) - 15}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{-5(1+2h+h^2)+20+20h - 15}{h}.\]

Simplify the numerator: \(-5 -10h -5h^2 + 20+20h -15 = 10h - 5h^2 = 5h(2-h)\). Divide by \(h\): \(5(2-h)\). The limit as \(h\to 0\) is \(10\) m/s.

  • Reporting an average rate without units, or attaching the wrong units (e.g., m instead of m/s).
  • Confusing \(h(3) - h(1)\) (a difference of heights) with the average rate (which requires dividing by \(3-1\)).
  • Plugging \(h=0\) into \(\frac{h(t+h)-h(t)}{h}\) directly — get \(\tfrac{0}{0}\), an indeterminate form requiring simplification.
  • Sign errors when the displacement is decreasing — instantaneous velocity can be negative, and that information matters physically.

Curriculum expectation: Strand A, Expectation A1.2 — "describe connections between the average rate of change of a function that is smooth (i.e., continuous with no corners) over an interval and the slope of the corresponding secant, and between the instantaneous rate of change of a smooth function at a point and the slope of the tangent at that point."

Topic 1.4 introduces the formal idea of a limit. We write \(\lim_{x\to a} f(x) = L\) to mean: as \(x\) approaches \(a\) (from either side, but not equal to \(a\)), \(f(x)\) approaches the single value \(L\). The crucial subtlety is that the limit cares about the behaviour of \(f\) near \(a\), not the value of \(f\) at \(a\) — those can disagree, or \(f(a)\) may even be undefined while the limit exists.

One-sided limits sharpen the picture: \(\lim_{x\to a^-} f(x)\) considers only \(x < a\), and \(\lim_{x\to a^+} f(x)\) only \(x > a\). The two-sided limit \(\lim_{x\to a} f(x)\) exists if and only if both one-sided limits exist and agree. When they disagree — typical at jump discontinuities — the two-sided limit does not exist.

Numerical, graphical, and algebraic approaches all play a role: build a table of \(f(x)\) values for \(x\) very close to \(a\); read the limit off a graph by tracing toward \(a\) from each side; or manipulate the algebra (factor, rationalize, common denominator) until direct substitution becomes valid.

  • Limit — \(\lim_{x\to a} f(x) = L\) if \(f(x)\) can be made arbitrarily close to \(L\) by choosing \(x\) sufficiently close to (but not equal to) \(a\).
  • Left-hand limit — \(\lim_{x\to a^-} f(x)\); approach from values less than \(a\).
  • Right-hand limit — \(\lim_{x\to a^+} f(x)\); approach from values greater than \(a\).
  • Two-sided limit existence — requires \(\lim_{x\to a^-}f(x) = \lim_{x\to a^+}f(x)\).
  • Hole/removable discontinuity — limit exists but \(f(a)\) is undefined or different.

Worked Example: Evaluate \(\lim_{x\to 2} \frac{x^2-4}{x-2}\).

Direct substitution gives \(\tfrac{0}{0}\), an indeterminate form. Factor the numerator: \(x^2-4 = (x-2)(x+2)\).

Cancel the common factor (valid because \(x \ne 2\) in the limit): \(\frac{(x-2)(x+2)}{x-2} = x+2\).

Now substitute: \(\lim_{x\to 2}(x+2) = 4\). The original function has a hole at \(x=2\), but the limit is still well-defined.

Worked Example: Decide whether \(\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{|x|}{x}\) exists.

For \(x>0\), \(|x| = x\) so \(\frac{|x|}{x} = 1\); thus \(\lim_{x\to 0^+}\frac{|x|}{x} = 1\). For \(x<0\), \(|x| = -x\) so \(\frac{|x|}{x} = -1\); thus \(\lim_{x\to 0^-}\frac{|x|}{x} = -1\).

The one-sided limits disagree (\(1 \ne -1\)), so the two-sided limit does not exist.

  • Concluding the limit equals \(f(a)\) without checking that \(f\) is continuous at \(a\) — for limits of indeterminate forms this is wrong.
  • Stopping when you see \(\tfrac{0}{0}\) and declaring "does not exist" — \(\tfrac{0}{0}\) means "try harder algebraically", not "no limit".
  • Forgetting to check both one-sided limits when the function is defined piecewise around \(a\).
  • Cancelling factors without noting the implicit \(x \ne a\) — the cancellation is legitimate inside a limit because the limit excludes the point itself.

Topic 1.5 develops the algebraic limit laws that let you compute limits without resorting to tables every time. If \(\lim_{x\to a} f(x)=L\) and \(\lim_{x\to a} g(x)=M\), then the limit of a sum is the sum of limits, product is product of limits, quotient is the quotient (provided \(M\ne 0\)), and constant multiple is the constant times the limit. Polynomial functions, in particular, are continuous everywhere, so for any polynomial \(p\), \(\lim_{x\to a} p(x) = p(a)\).

The harder cases produce the indeterminate form \(\tfrac{0}{0}\), where direct substitution fails. The standard repair toolkit: factor the numerator and denominator and cancel a common factor; rationalize when there are radicals; combine fractions over a common denominator; or apply a change of variable (\(u\)-substitution) so a hidden structure becomes visible.

For example, in \(\lim_{x\to 1}\frac{\sqrt{x}-1}{x-1}\), let \(u=\sqrt{x}\), so \(x=u^2\) and \(x-1 = u^2-1 = (u-1)(u+1)\). The limit becomes \(\lim_{u\to 1}\frac{u-1}{(u-1)(u+1)} = \lim_{u\to 1}\frac{1}{u+1} = \tfrac{1}{2}\). The substitution converted an obscure problem into a routine cancellation.

  • Limit laws — sum, difference, product, quotient (\(M\ne 0\)), constant multiple, power, and root.
  • Direct substitution property — if \(f\) is continuous at \(a\), then \(\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=f(a)\).
  • Indeterminate form — \(\tfrac{0}{0}\), \(\tfrac{\infty}{\infty}\), \(\infty-\infty\), \(0\cdot\infty\): direct substitution is uninformative.
  • Factoring — common-factor cancellation; the cornerstone repair for \(\tfrac{0}{0}\).
  • u-substitution — let \(u\) represent a sub-expression to convert the limit into a simpler one in \(u\).

Worked Example: Evaluate \(\lim_{x\to 3}\frac{x^2-9}{x^2-x-6}\).

Substitution gives \(\tfrac{0}{0}\). Factor both: \(x^2-9 = (x-3)(x+3)\); \(x^2-x-6 = (x-3)(x+2)\).

Cancel \((x-3)\): \(\frac{x+3}{x+2}\). Substitute \(x=3\): \(\frac{6}{5}\).

Worked Example: Evaluate \(\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{(x+1)^3 - 1}{x}\).

Expand the numerator: \((x+1)^3 = x^3 + 3x^2 + 3x + 1\), so the numerator is \(x^3 + 3x^2 + 3x\).

Factor out \(x\): \(\frac{x(x^2+3x+3)}{x} = x^2+3x+3\). Substitute \(x=0\): \(3\).

  • Applying the quotient law when the denominator's limit is \(0\) — the law requires \(M\ne 0\) and a separate analysis is needed when it fails.
  • Forgetting to factor completely; partially factored expressions hide the common factor that would cancel.
  • Cancelling \(x\) from \(\tfrac{\sin x}{x}\) — these are not algebraic factors; the famous limit equals \(1\), not the result of an illegitimate cancel.
  • After u-substitution, forgetting to translate the limit endpoint to the new variable (e.g., if \(u=\sqrt{x}\) and \(x\to 4\), then \(u\to 2\)).

A function \(f\) is continuous at \(x=a\) when three conditions all hold: (1) \(f(a)\) is defined, (2) \(\lim_{x\to a} f(x)\) exists, and (3) \(\lim_{x\to a} f(x) = f(a)\). Informally, you can draw the graph through \(x=a\) without lifting your pencil. Any continuous function on its domain is determined by its values at every nearby point.

Three classic ways continuity can fail: a removable discontinuity (a "hole" — the limit exists but doesn't match \(f(a)\), or \(f(a)\) is undefined); a jump discontinuity (the left- and right-hand limits exist but disagree); and an infinite discontinuity (the function blows up to \(\pm\infty\) at \(a\)). Each appears in graphs of rational and piecewise functions, and each requires a different diagnostic.

Continuity matters because powerful theorems (Intermediate Value Theorem, Extreme Value Theorem, the existence of definite integrals later in calculus) all assume continuity on a closed interval. It also matters for differentiation: differentiability implies continuity, so if you can show \(f\) is discontinuous at \(a\), you immediately know \(f'(a)\) doesn't exist.

  • Continuous at \(a\) — \(\lim_{x\to a} f(x) = f(a)\), with both sides defined.
  • Continuous on an interval — continuous at every point of the interval.
  • Removable discontinuity — \(\lim_{x\to a}f(x)\) exists but \(f(a)\) is missing or different.
  • Jump discontinuity — \(\lim_{x\to a^-}f(x) \ne \lim_{x\to a^+}f(x)\); both finite.
  • Infinite discontinuity — vertical asymptote at \(a\); one or both one-sided limits are \(\pm\infty\).

Worked Example: Is \(f(x)=\begin{cases}x^2 & x<1\\ 3-x & x\ge 1\end{cases}\) continuous at \(x=1\)?

Check \(f(1)\): the second branch applies, so \(f(1) = 3-1 = 2\). Defined.

Compute one-sided limits. From the left: \(\lim_{x\to 1^-} x^2 = 1\). From the right: \(\lim_{x\to 1^+}(3-x) = 2\).

The one-sided limits disagree (\(1 \ne 2\)), so \(\lim_{x\to 1}f(x)\) does not exist. Therefore \(f\) is not continuous at \(x=1\); it has a jump discontinuity.

Worked Example: For what value of \(k\) is \(f(x)=\begin{cases}\frac{x^2-4}{x-2} & x\ne 2\\ k & x=2\end{cases}\) continuous at \(x=2\)?

Compute the limit: \(\lim_{x\to 2}\frac{x^2-4}{x-2} = \lim_{x\to 2}(x+2) = 4\). For continuity, we need \(f(2)=k=4\). So \(k=4\) removes the discontinuity.

  • Forgetting to check all three conditions — a function might satisfy two of them and still be discontinuous.
  • Assuming a rational function is continuous everywhere — it has discontinuities wherever the denominator vanishes.
  • Confusing "limit exists" with "continuous": holes give limits but break continuity.
  • Believing every continuous function is differentiable — \(f(x)=|x|\) is continuous at \(0\) but not differentiable there.

Curriculum expectation: Strand A, Expectation A1.6 — "compare, through investigation, the calculation of instantaneous rates of change at a point \((a, f(a))\) for polynomial functions \(\big(\)e.g., \(f(x)=x^2, f(x)=x^3\big)\), with and without simplifying the expression \(\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}\) before substituting values of \(h\) that approach zero," with the prerequisite that \(f\) be continuous at \(a\).

Unit 2: Derivatives

Derivative rules: power, product, quotient, chain rule

📚 Strand A: Rate of Change 📹 6 lessons
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The derivative \(f'(x) = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\) is the engine of differential calculus. Computing it from first principles every time would be tedious, so this unit establishes a toolkit of rules that let us differentiate by inspection. The power rule, product rule, quotient rule and chain rule are not arbitrary recipes — each is provable from the limit definition, and each addresses a specific way functions combine.

Why so many rules? Because functions combine in distinct ways: addition (handled by the linearity of limits), multiplication (product rule, because \((fg)' \ne f'g'\)), division (quotient rule, derived from the product rule), and composition (chain rule, the most consequential of all). The chain rule expresses how rates of change cascade through nested processes: if \(y\) depends on \(u\) and \(u\) depends on \(x\), then \(\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du}\cdot\frac{du}{dx}\). This is precisely how related rates and implicit differentiation become tractable.

A subtle point: \(\frac{d}{dx}[f(x)g(x)] = f'(x)g(x) + f(x)g'(x)\) — both terms are required, because at any instant both factors are changing. Treating it like \(\frac{d}{dx}(c\cdot f) = c\cdot f'\) (which is correct only when one factor is a constant) is the single most common error in this unit.

  • Derivative — \(f'(x) = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\); also written \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) or \(Df(x)\).
  • Power rule — for any real \(n\), \(\frac{d}{dx}[x^n] = n x^{n-1}\).
  • Product rule — \((fg)' = f'g + fg'\).
  • Quotient rule — \(\left(\frac{f}{g}\right)' = \frac{f'g - fg'}{g^2}\), valid where \(g \ne 0\).
  • Chain rule — \(\frac{d}{dx}f(g(x)) = f'(g(x))\cdot g'(x)\).
  • Differentiability — \(f\) is differentiable at \(x=a\) if \(f'(a)\) exists; differentiability implies continuity, but not vice versa.
  • Implicit differentiation — applying \(\frac{d}{dx}\) to both sides of an equation in \(x\) and \(y\), treating \(y\) as an implicit function of \(x\) and applying the chain rule whenever \(y\) is differentiated.

Worked Example: Differentiate \(y = (3x^2+1)(x^3-4x)\)

Apply the product rule with \(f = 3x^2+1\) and \(g = x^3-4x\): \[y' = f'g + fg' = (6x)(x^3-4x) + (3x^2+1)(3x^2-4).\]

Expand each piece: \((6x)(x^3-4x) = 6x^4 - 24x^2\); \((3x^2+1)(3x^2-4) = 9x^4 - 12x^2 + 3x^2 - 4 = 9x^4 - 9x^2 - 4\).

Combine: \(y' = 6x^4 - 24x^2 + 9x^4 - 9x^2 - 4 = 15x^4 - 33x^2 - 4\).

Worked Example: Chain rule on \(y = (2x^3 - 5)^4\)

Identify outer function \(f(u) = u^4\) and inner function \(u = 2x^3 - 5\). Then \(\frac{dy}{du} = 4u^3\) and \(\frac{du}{dx} = 6x^2\).

Apply the chain rule: \[\frac{dy}{dx} = 4u^3 \cdot 6x^2 = 4(2x^3-5)^3 \cdot 6x^2 = 24x^2(2x^3-5)^3.\]

Notice the inner derivative \(6x^2\) does not get absorbed into the exponent — it multiplies the whole expression.

  • Writing \((fg)' = f'g'\) — this is wrong; the product rule requires both cross terms.
  • Forgetting the inner derivative in the chain rule — \(\frac{d}{dx}(\sin(3x))=3\cos(3x)\), not \(\cos(3x)\).
  • Mis-signing the quotient rule numerator: it is \(f'g - fg'\), not \(fg' - f'g\).
  • Applying the power rule to non-power expressions (e.g., \(\frac{d}{dx}[2^x] \ne x\cdot 2^{x-1}\); that requires the exponential rule in Unit 5).
  • Forgetting the \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) factor when implicitly differentiating a \(y\)-term.

Sketching the derivative function \(f'\) from a graph of \(f\) — without computing any algebra — is a powerful diagnostic of conceptual understanding. The graph of \(f'\) records the slope of \(f\) at each \(x\)-value as a height. Where \(f\) is rising, \(f'\) is positive (above the \(x\)-axis); where \(f\) is falling, \(f'\) is negative; where \(f\) has a horizontal tangent (local max, local min, or horizontal inflection), \(f'\) crosses the \(x\)-axis.

Steepness translates to magnitude: a steep portion of \(f\) corresponds to a \(y\)-value of \(f'\) that is far from zero, while a nearly-flat portion corresponds to \(f'\) close to zero. A linear segment of \(f\) gives a horizontal segment in \(f'\) (constant slope). A cusp or corner in \(f\) — where the slope jumps or becomes undefined — produces a discontinuity in \(f'\).

Reverse engineering is just as useful: given \(f'\), what does \(f\) look like? Wherever \(f' > 0\), \(f\) ascends; wherever \(f' < 0\), \(f\) descends; the zeros of \(f'\) are the critical points of \(f\). The vertical position of \(f\) is one degree of freedom we cannot recover (an arbitrary constant), but the shape is determined.

  • Slope-to-height correspondence — slope of \(f\) at \(x=a\) equals height of \(f'\) at \(x=a\).
  • Critical \(x\)-value — where \(f'(x)=0\) or is undefined; appears as an \(x\)-intercept or hole in the graph of \(f'\).
  • Sign chart — divide the \(x\)-axis at critical points; on each region record the sign of \(f'\); use to predict where \(f\) rises or falls.
  • Cusp/corner — sharp change of direction in \(f\); the derivative has a jump discontinuity or one-sided limits that disagree.
  • Antidifference — informally, "reading backward" from \(f'\) to \(f\) is the reverse of differentiation.

Worked Example: \(f\) is a parabola opening up with vertex at \((2,-3)\). Sketch \(f'\).

At \(x=2\), \(f\) has a horizontal tangent, so \(f'(2)=0\); the graph of \(f'\) crosses the \(x\)-axis at \(x=2\).

For \(x<2\), \(f\) is decreasing — \(f'\) is negative there. For \(x>2\), \(f\) is increasing — \(f'\) is positive.

The further from \(x=2\) we go, the steeper \(f\) becomes (in either direction), so \(|f'|\) grows linearly. The result: \(f'\) is a straight line through \((2,0)\) with positive slope — consistent with the fact that \(f(x) = a(x-2)^2 - 3\) gives \(f'(x) = 2a(x-2)\), a linear function.

  • Plotting \(f'\) as a shifted copy of \(f\) — the relationship is between slope and height, not between heights.
  • Forgetting that \(f'=0\) appears at MAX, MIN, AND horizontal inflection points; you can't tell which from \(f'\) alone without checking sign changes.
  • Drawing a continuous \(f'\) through a corner of \(f\) — corners make \(f'\) discontinuous.
  • Reading "above the axis" as "function is positive" — the graph being plotted is \(f'\), so above the axis means \(f' > 0\), i.e., \(f\) is increasing.

The derivative function \(f'\) is built point-by-point from the limit definition: \(f'(x) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\). Rather than computing a slope at a specific number \(x=a\), we treat \(x\) as a variable and produce a new function whose value at any \(x\) is the slope of \(f\) at that location. This is the function version of the tangent-slope idea.

Computing derivatives from first principles means executing this limit explicitly, without invoking the power rule or other shortcuts. The procedure is mechanical but fragile: (1) write the difference quotient \(\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\); (2) expand and simplify the numerator until \(h\) factors out; (3) cancel the \(h\); (4) take the limit by substituting \(h=0\). Algebra errors at step (2) are the usual failure mode.

Why bother when the power rule gives the answer in one line? Because first principles is the only proof that the power rule works, and because some functions (with absolute values, piecewise definitions, or non-standard bases) yield to first principles before any shortcut applies. The technique is also the bridge to the formal definition of the derivative used in university analysis.

  • Derivative function — \(f'(x) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\); domain consists of \(x\) where this limit exists.
  • First principles — computing \(f'\) directly from the limit, not via shortcut rules.
  • Difference quotient — \(\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\); the algebraic object on which the limit is taken.
  • Differentiable at \(a\) — \(f'(a)\) exists as a finite real number.
  • Leibniz notation — \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) as an alternative to \(f'(x)\), emphasizing the variable of differentiation.

Worked Example: Compute \(f'(x)\) from first principles for \(f(x) = 3x^2 - 2x\).

Difference quotient: \(\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h} = \frac{3(x+h)^2 - 2(x+h) - (3x^2 - 2x)}{h}\).

Expand: \(3(x+h)^2 = 3x^2 + 6xh + 3h^2\), so the numerator is \(3x^2 + 6xh + 3h^2 - 2x - 2h - 3x^2 + 2x = 6xh + 3h^2 - 2h\).

Factor \(h\): \(\frac{h(6x + 3h - 2)}{h} = 6x + 3h - 2\).

Take the limit as \(h\to 0\): \(f'(x) = 6x - 2\). (Confirm with the power rule: \(f'(x) = 6x - 2\). Agreement.)

Worked Example: Compute \(f'(x)\) from first principles for \(f(x) = \sqrt{x}\).

Difference quotient: \(\frac{\sqrt{x+h} - \sqrt{x}}{h}\). Multiply top and bottom by the conjugate \(\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x}\): \[\frac{(x+h)-x}{h(\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x})} = \frac{h}{h(\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x})} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x}}.\]

Limit as \(h\to 0\): \(f'(x) = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}\) — confirming the power rule for \(n=\tfrac{1}{2}\).

  • Forgetting to include the \(-f(x)\) (with both terms!) in the numerator; many students lose a minus sign here.
  • Treating \(h\) as a constant after substitution: it isn't — it is the variable of the limit.
  • Cancelling \(h\) before factoring it out — the cancellation requires it to be a common factor.
  • Forgetting that the result is a function of \(x\), not a number — students sometimes substitute the original \(x\) value before taking the limit, defeating the purpose.

Topic 2.2 promotes the power rule from a single-term shortcut to a tool for differentiating any polynomial. Three building blocks combine: the power rule \(\frac{d}{dx}[x^n] = n x^{n-1}\) for any real \(n\); the constant multiple rule \(\frac{d}{dx}[c\cdot f(x)] = c\cdot f'(x)\); and the sum/difference rule \(\frac{d}{dx}[f(x)\pm g(x)] = f'(x)\pm g'(x)\). Together they allow term-by-term differentiation: differentiate each monomial separately, attach its coefficient, then sum.

The derivative of a constant is zero — \(\frac{d}{dx}[c] = 0\) — because the graph of a constant is a horizontal line with slope zero. Equivalently, the power rule applied to \(c = c\cdot x^0\) gives \(0\cdot c\cdot x^{-1} = 0\). Knowing this saves time: any constant term in a polynomial vanishes under differentiation.

The power rule also handles fractional and negative exponents, which is essential once we rewrite radicals (\(\sqrt{x} = x^{1/2}\)) and reciprocals (\(\tfrac{1}{x^2} = x^{-2}\)) in exponent form. So before differentiating, simplify each term to a single power of \(x\) — this prevents the temptation to apply the quotient rule when it isn't needed.

  • Power rule — \(\frac{d}{dx}[x^n] = n x^{n-1}\), valid for any real \(n\).
  • Constant multiple rule — \(\frac{d}{dx}[c f(x)] = c f'(x)\).
  • Sum/difference rule — derivative of a sum is the sum of derivatives.
  • Derivative of a constant — \(\frac{d}{dx}[c] = 0\).
  • Term-by-term differentiation — combine the three rules to differentiate any polynomial.

Worked Example: Differentiate \(p(x) = 4x^5 - 7x^3 + \tfrac{2}{x} - 6\sqrt{x} + 11\).

Rewrite \(\tfrac{2}{x} = 2x^{-1}\) and \(6\sqrt{x} = 6 x^{1/2}\). The polynomial becomes \(4x^5 - 7x^3 + 2x^{-1} - 6x^{1/2} + 11\).

Apply the power rule term-by-term: \[p'(x) = 20x^4 - 21x^2 - 2x^{-2} - 3x^{-1/2} + 0.\]

Rewrite in original form if desired: \(p'(x) = 20x^4 - 21x^2 - \tfrac{2}{x^2} - \tfrac{3}{\sqrt{x}}\).

Worked Example: Find the slope of \(y = x^4 - 3x^2 + 1\) at \(x=-1\).

Differentiate: \(y' = 4x^3 - 6x\). Substitute \(x=-1\): \(y'(-1) = 4(-1)^3 - 6(-1) = -4 + 6 = 2\). The tangent slope at \(x=-1\) is \(2\).

  • Forgetting to reduce the exponent by one: \(\frac{d}{dx}[x^5] = 5x^4\), not \(5x^5\).
  • Mishandling \(\tfrac{1}{x}\): it is \(x^{-1}\), so its derivative is \(-x^{-2} = -\tfrac{1}{x^2}\), not \(\ln x\) or anything else.
  • Forgetting that the derivative of a constant is zero — students sometimes carry the constant unchanged.
  • Multiplying instead of subtracting the exponent's coefficient: from \(7x^3\) the derivative is \(21x^2\), not \(7\cdot 3 \cdot x^3\).

When a function is a product \(y = f(x)g(x)\), the temptation is to differentiate each factor and multiply, but this is incorrect. The correct formula, the product rule, is \[(fg)' = f'g + fg'.\] Both factors are changing simultaneously, and the total instantaneous change picks up contributions from each — \(f'g\) (the rate at which \(f\) changes weighted by current \(g\)) plus \(fg'\) (rate of \(g\) weighted by current \(f\)). The derivation from first principles \(\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x+h)g(x+h) - f(x)g(x)}{h}\) requires a clever "add-and-subtract" trick \(f(x+h)g(x+h) - f(x+h)g(x) + f(x+h)g(x) - f(x)g(x)\), which factors into the two product-rule terms.

The product rule applies whenever you can identify two non-trivially-variable factors. If one factor is a constant, the constant-multiple rule already handles it. If a factor is itself a composition, you'll need to layer the chain rule on top.

A common strategic question: should you expand first, then differentiate, or apply the product rule directly? For small polynomial products either approach works; expanding is sometimes faster. For factors that don't expand cleanly (radicals, transcendentals later in Unit 5, or compositions), the product rule is essential.

  • Product rule — \((fg)' = f'g + fg'\); symmetric in \(f\) and \(g\).
  • Identify factors — choose \(f\) and \(g\) such that each is easy to differentiate.
  • Both terms required — \((fg)' \ne f' g'\); leaving out the second cross term is the canonical error.
  • Generalizes to three factors — \((fgh)' = f'gh + fg'h + fgh'\); each factor takes its turn being differentiated.
  • Strategic expansion — sometimes faster to multiply out, then use the power rule.

Worked Example: Differentiate \(y = (x^2 + 3)(2x - 5)\) using the product rule.

Let \(f = x^2 + 3\), \(g = 2x - 5\). Then \(f' = 2x\) and \(g' = 2\).

Apply the rule: \(y' = f'g + fg' = (2x)(2x-5) + (x^2+3)(2)\).

Expand: \((2x)(2x-5) = 4x^2 - 10x\); \((x^2+3)(2) = 2x^2 + 6\). Sum: \(6x^2 - 10x + 6\).

Cross-check by expanding first: \(y = (x^2+3)(2x-5) = 2x^3 - 5x^2 + 6x - 15\). Differentiate: \(y' = 6x^2 - 10x + 6\). Agreement.

Worked Example: Differentiate \(y = x^2\sqrt{x+1}\).

Let \(f = x^2\), \(g = (x+1)^{1/2}\). Then \(f' = 2x\), and (using chain rule on \(g\)) \(g' = \tfrac{1}{2}(x+1)^{-1/2} \cdot 1 = \tfrac{1}{2\sqrt{x+1}}\).

Product rule: \(y' = 2x\sqrt{x+1} + x^2 \cdot \tfrac{1}{2\sqrt{x+1}} = \tfrac{4x(x+1) + x^2}{2\sqrt{x+1}} = \tfrac{5x^2 + 4x}{2\sqrt{x+1}}\).

  • Writing \((fg)' = f'g'\) — this is the most common error in this unit.
  • Forgetting one of the two terms; the rule is symmetric and you need both.
  • Computing \(f'\) and then carrying \(g\) untouched into the second term too — re-read the formula.
  • Forgetting the chain rule on factors that are compositions (e.g., \((x+1)^{1/2}\)).

The quotient rule handles derivatives of fractions: \[\left(\frac{f}{g}\right)' = \frac{f'g - fg'}{g^2}, \qquad g(x) \ne 0.\] The sign and order matter: it is \(f' g\) minus \(f g'\), not the other way around. A standard mnemonic — "low d-high minus high d-low, over the square of what's below" — encodes the formula in a memorable rhythm.

The quotient rule is a consequence of the product rule plus the chain rule. Writing \(\tfrac{f}{g} = f \cdot g^{-1}\) and applying the product rule gives \(f' g^{-1} + f \cdot (-g^{-2}) g'\), which simplifies to \(\tfrac{f' g - f g'}{g^2}\). Knowing this derivation explains why the \(g^2\) appears in the denominator and locks in the sign convention.

When should you use it? Only when the denominator genuinely involves \(x\). If the denominator is a constant, factor it out and use the constant-multiple rule. If the numerator is a constant, rewrite as \(c\cdot g(x)^{-1}\) and use the chain rule. The quotient rule is reserved for honest \(x\)-in-both-places fractions.

  • Quotient rule — \(\left(\tfrac{f}{g}\right)' = \tfrac{f'g - fg'}{g^2}\), \(g\ne 0\).
  • Sign convention — \(f'g\) is positive, \(fg'\) is subtracted; the order matters and reversing it is a common mistake.
  • Square the denominator — the \(g^2\) is required; forgetting it produces the wrong rate.
  • Equivalent product form — \(\tfrac{f}{g} = f g^{-1}\); useful when chain rule is more familiar.
  • Domain restriction — \(g(x)=0\) is excluded; the rule fails (and so does the function) there.

Worked Example: Differentiate \(y = \dfrac{2x+1}{x^2-3}\).

Let \(f = 2x+1\), \(g = x^2-3\). Then \(f' = 2\), \(g' = 2x\).

Apply the rule: \(y' = \dfrac{f'g - fg'}{g^2} = \dfrac{2(x^2-3) - (2x+1)(2x)}{(x^2-3)^2}\).

Expand the numerator: \(2x^2 - 6 - (4x^2 + 2x) = 2x^2 - 6 - 4x^2 - 2x = -2x^2 - 2x - 6\).

So \(y' = \dfrac{-2x^2 - 2x - 6}{(x^2-3)^2} = \dfrac{-2(x^2 + x + 3)}{(x^2-3)^2}\).

Worked Example: Find the slope of \(y = \dfrac{x^2}{x+1}\) at \(x=2\).

Let \(f = x^2, g = x+1\). Then \(f' = 2x, g' = 1\). The derivative is \(y' = \dfrac{2x(x+1) - x^2 \cdot 1}{(x+1)^2} = \dfrac{2x^2 + 2x - x^2}{(x+1)^2} = \dfrac{x^2 + 2x}{(x+1)^2}\).

At \(x=2\): \(y'(2) = \dfrac{4 + 4}{9} = \dfrac{8}{9}\).

  • Reversing the numerator sign: \(\tfrac{fg' - f'g}{g^2}\) gives the negative of the correct derivative.
  • Forgetting to square the denominator — writing \(g\) instead of \(g^2\) at the bottom.
  • Applying the rule when the denominator is a constant (e.g., \(\tfrac{x^2}{5}\)) — use the constant-multiple rule instead.
  • Skipping simplification of the numerator, then misreading the final expression as zero when it isn't.

Topic 2.5 introduces the chain rule, the most important differentiation rule in calculus. If \(y\) is a function of \(u\) and \(u\) is a function of \(x\), then \[\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du}\cdot\frac{du}{dx}.\] Equivalently, for the composition \(h(x) = f(g(x))\), \(h'(x) = f'(g(x))\cdot g'(x)\) — differentiate the outer function (leaving the inner alone), then multiply by the derivative of the inner.

The intuition is that rates compose: if \(y\) changes 3 units for every unit of \(u\), and \(u\) changes 5 units for every unit of \(x\), then \(y\) changes \(3\cdot 5 = 15\) units per unit of \(x\). The chain rule formalizes this cascade. Almost every function in the wild is a composition once you strip away simple polynomials, so practising chain rule patterns is essential for the rest of the course.

Layer the chain rule with itself for deeper nesting: \(\frac{d}{dx}f(g(h(x))) = f'(g(h(x)))\cdot g'(h(x))\cdot h'(x)\). The pattern is "differentiate from outside in, multiplying by each inner derivative as you peel off a layer."

  • Composition — \(h(x) = f(g(x))\) means apply \(g\) first, then \(f\); \(g\) is "inner", \(f\) is "outer".
  • Chain rule — \(\frac{d}{dx}f(g(x)) = f'(g(x))\cdot g'(x)\).
  • Leibniz form — \(\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du}\frac{du}{dx}\); helpful when the layers have natural names.
  • Inner derivative — never forget the \(g'(x)\) factor; missing it is the single biggest chain-rule error.
  • General power rule — \(\frac{d}{dx}[g(x)]^n = n[g(x)]^{n-1}\cdot g'(x)\); the most-used special case of the chain rule.

Worked Example: Differentiate \(y = (3x^2 - 7)^5\).

Outer: \(u^5\), so \(\frac{dy}{du} = 5u^4\). Inner: \(u = 3x^2 - 7\), so \(\frac{du}{dx} = 6x\).

Chain rule: \(\frac{dy}{dx} = 5u^4 \cdot 6x = 30x(3x^2 - 7)^4\).

Worked Example: Differentiate \(y = \sqrt{1 + x^2}\) (a triple-layer composition once we write \(\sqrt{u} = u^{1/2}\)).

Rewrite: \(y = (1+x^2)^{1/2}\). Apply the general power rule: \(y' = \tfrac{1}{2}(1+x^2)^{-1/2}\cdot 2x = \dfrac{x}{\sqrt{1+x^2}}\).

  • Forgetting the inner derivative — writing \((3x^2-7)^5\)' as \(5(3x^2-7)^4\) without the \(6x\) factor.
  • Differentiating both the inner and the outer at the same time (e.g., trying to "expand" \(u^5\) into \(x\) immediately).
  • Mis-identifying outer and inner for square roots: \(\sqrt{f(x)}\) has outer \(\sqrt{u}\) and inner \(f(x)\); the derivative is \(\tfrac{f'(x)}{2\sqrt{f(x)}}\).
  • Stopping after one layer for a triple composition — keep multiplying derivatives as you unwrap.

Implicit differentiation is the technique for finding \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) when \(y\) is defined implicitly by an equation in \(x\) and \(y\) — for example \(x^2 + y^2 = 25\) or \(x^3 + y^3 = 6xy\) — rather than as an explicit function \(y = f(x)\). The procedure: differentiate both sides of the equation with respect to \(x\), treating \(y\) as a differentiable function of \(x\) and applying the chain rule every time \(y\) is differentiated; then solve algebraically for \(\frac{dy}{dx}\).

The key mechanical move is that \(\frac{d}{dx}[y^n] = n y^{n-1} \cdot \frac{dy}{dx}\), not just \(n y^{n-1}\). The extra \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) factor is the chain rule contribution: \(y\) is itself changing as \(x\) changes, and that change rate must be carried along. The same applies to \(\frac{d}{dx}[\sin y] = \cos y \cdot \frac{dy}{dx}\), \(\frac{d}{dx}[xy] = y + x\frac{dy}{dx}\) (product rule!), and so on.

Why bother? Some curves — circles, ellipses, the folium of Descartes \(x^3 + y^3 = 6xy\) — cannot be expressed as a single function \(y = f(x)\), yet they have well-defined tangent slopes at most points. Implicit differentiation gives those slopes without solving for \(y\). It also dramatically simplifies derivatives that, when written explicitly, would be lengthy.

  • Implicit equation — an equation relating \(x\) and \(y\) that may define multiple branches of \(y\).
  • Implicit differentiation — apply \(\frac{d}{dx}\) to both sides; treat \(y\) as \(y(x)\); attach \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) whenever \(y\) is differentiated.
  • Chain rule on \(y\)-terms — \(\frac{d}{dx}[y^n] = n y^{n-1} y'\); the most common application.
  • Product rule on \(xy\)-terms — \(\frac{d}{dx}[xy] = y + x y'\), not just \(y'\).
  • Solve for \(y'\) — collect all \(y'\) terms on one side; factor; divide.

Worked Example: Find \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) for \(x^2 + y^2 = 25\), then the slope at \((3,4)\).

Differentiate both sides: \(2x + 2y\frac{dy}{dx} = 0\).

Solve for \(\frac{dy}{dx}\): \(2y\frac{dy}{dx} = -2x \Rightarrow \frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{x}{y}\).

At \((3,4)\): \(\frac{dy}{dx} = -\tfrac{3}{4}\). The tangent to the circle at \((3,4)\) has slope \(-\tfrac{3}{4}\), as expected from elementary geometry (perpendicular to the radius).

Worked Example: Find \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) for \(x^2 y + xy^2 = 6\).

Differentiate term by term (each is a product, so use product rule and chain rule): \(\frac{d}{dx}[x^2 y] = 2xy + x^2 y'\); \(\frac{d}{dx}[xy^2] = y^2 + x\cdot 2y y' = y^2 + 2xy y'\).

So \(2xy + x^2 y' + y^2 + 2xy y' = 0\). Group \(y'\) terms: \((x^2 + 2xy)y' = -(2xy + y^2)\).

Solve: \(y' = -\frac{2xy + y^2}{x^2 + 2xy} = -\frac{y(2x + y)}{x(x + 2y)}\).

  • Forgetting the \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) factor when differentiating a \(y\)-term — by far the most common mistake.
  • Treating \(xy\) as a single variable rather than a product; \(\frac{d}{dx}[xy] = y + xy'\).
  • Failing to solve algebraically for \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) at the end — the answer should be in terms of both \(x\) and \(y\).
  • Plugging a point in too early; differentiate first, then substitute.

Higher-order derivatives are derivatives of derivatives. If \(f'(x)\) is the first derivative, then \(f''(x)\) (read "f double-prime") is the derivative of \(f'(x)\) — the second derivative. Continuing, \(f'''(x), f^{(4)}(x), \ldots, f^{(n)}(x)\) record successive instantaneous rates of change. Each takes the previous derivative as input and differentiates again with the usual rules.

Physical meaning: if \(s(t)\) is position, then \(s'(t) = v(t)\) is velocity, \(s''(t) = a(t)\) is acceleration, and \(s'''(t)\) is "jerk" (the rate of change of acceleration). Knowing one of these tells you about the others — for example, an object's velocity is constant iff its acceleration is zero, and its acceleration is positive iff its velocity is increasing.

In curve sketching (Unit 4), \(f''(x)\) determines concavity: where \(f''>0\), the graph of \(f\) is concave up (smiling); where \(f''<0\), concave down (frowning); where \(f''\) changes sign, the graph has an inflection point. So higher-order derivatives are not just abstract: they encode geometric and physical information the first derivative alone cannot.

  • Second derivative — \(f''(x) = \frac{d}{dx}[f'(x)]\); also \(\frac{d^2 y}{dx^2}\).
  • Higher-order derivative — \(f^{(n)}(x)\), iterated differentiation \(n\) times.
  • Position/velocity/acceleration — \(s(t), s'(t)=v(t), s''(t)=a(t)\).
  • Concavity sign — \(f''>0\) means concave up; \(f''<0\) means concave down.
  • Inflection point — where \(f''\) changes sign; concavity reverses.

Worked Example: For \(f(x) = x^4 - 6x^2 + 5\), find \(f'(x), f''(x), f'''(x)\).

\(f'(x) = 4x^3 - 12x\).

\(f''(x) = 12x^2 - 12\).

\(f'''(x) = 24x\). (And \(f^{(4)}(x) = 24\), \(f^{(5)}(x) = 0\) — eventually a polynomial's derivatives become zero.)

Worked Example: A particle has position \(s(t) = t^3 - 6t^2 + 9t\) (in metres, \(t\) in seconds). Find velocity and acceleration at \(t=2\).

Velocity: \(v(t) = s'(t) = 3t^2 - 12t + 9\). At \(t=2\): \(v(2) = 12 - 24 + 9 = -3\) m/s (moving backward).

Acceleration: \(a(t) = s''(t) = 6t - 12\). At \(t=2\): \(a(2) = 0\) m/s². (Velocity is at its minimum and about to start increasing.)

  • Confusing \(f''(x)\) with \([f'(x)]^2\) — they are different; \(f''\) is a second derivative, \([f']^2\) is a square.
  • Forgetting to differentiate each time before substituting numerical values.
  • Stopping after one differentiation when asked for the second derivative.
  • Mistakenly thinking acceleration is the second derivative of velocity — it is the first derivative of velocity, equivalently the second derivative of position.

This unit turns the derivative from an abstract limit into a problem-solving instrument. If \(s(t)\) is position, then \(v(t) = s'(t)\) is velocity and \(a(t) = v'(t) = s''(t)\) is acceleration. The sign of \(v(t)\) tells us direction of motion; the sign of \(a(t)\) tells us whether the object is speeding up or slowing down (when \(v\) and \(a\) have the same sign, speed increases). These second-derivative ideas reappear in curve sketching as concavity.

Optimization is the most economically important application of calculus. Whenever you want to minimize cost, maximize area, or find the most efficient size or shape, you translate the situation into a function of one variable, differentiate, set the derivative to zero, and check that you have a maximum rather than a minimum (or vice versa). The Extreme Value Theorem guarantees that a continuous function on a closed interval attains both an absolute maximum and an absolute minimum — these occur either at critical points (where \(f'=0\) or is undefined) or at the endpoints.

Related rates problems use the chain rule to connect rates that change together: if a balloon's radius and volume both depend on time, then \(\frac{dV}{dt} = \frac{dV}{dr}\cdot \frac{dr}{dt}\). The key is to differentiate an equation in \(r\) and \(V\) implicitly with respect to \(t\), substituting numerical values only after differentiation.

  • Velocity — \(v(t) = s'(t)\); a signed quantity indicating speed and direction.
  • Acceleration — \(a(t) = v'(t) = s''(t)\).
  • Critical number — value of \(x\) in the domain of \(f\) where \(f'(x) = 0\) or \(f'(x)\) is undefined.
  • Absolute (global) maximum/minimum — largest/smallest value of \(f\) on its domain.
  • Local (relative) maximum/minimum — largest/smallest value of \(f\) on some open interval around the point.
  • Extreme Value Theorem — a continuous function on a closed interval \([a,b]\) attains both an absolute maximum and an absolute minimum on that interval.
  • Related rates — problems in which several quantities related by an equation vary with time; the chain rule links their rates.

Worked Example: Closed-interval optimization

Find the absolute maximum and minimum of \(f(x) = x^3 - 3x^2 + 2\) on \([-1, 3]\).

Differentiate: \(f'(x) = 3x^2 - 6x = 3x(x-2)\). Set \(f'(x)=0\): \(x=0\) or \(x=2\). Both lie in \([-1,3]\).

Evaluate at critical points and endpoints: \(f(-1) = -1 - 3 + 2 = -2\); \(f(0) = 2\); \(f(2) = 8 - 12 + 2 = -2\); \(f(3) = 27 - 27 + 2 = 2\).

Absolute maximum is \(2\) (attained at \(x=0\) and \(x=3\)); absolute minimum is \(-2\) (attained at \(x=-1\) and \(x=2\)).

Worked Example: Box optimization

An open-top box is built from a 12 cm by 18 cm sheet by cutting squares of side \(x\) from each corner and folding up the sides. Maximize the volume.

Volume: \(V(x) = x(12-2x)(18-2x) = x(216 - 60x + 4x^2) = 4x^3 - 60x^2 + 216x\), with \(0 < x < 6\).

Differentiate: \(V'(x) = 12x^2 - 120x + 216 = 12(x^2 - 10x + 18)\). Solve \(x^2 - 10x + 18 = 0\) by the quadratic formula: \(x = \frac{10 \pm \sqrt{100-72}}{2} = \frac{10 \pm \sqrt{28}}{2} = 5 \pm \sqrt{7}\).

Only \(x = 5 - \sqrt{7} \approx 2.354\) lies in \((0,6)\). Check it is a maximum: \(V''(x) = 24x - 120\); \(V''(5-\sqrt{7}) = 24(5-\sqrt{7}) - 120 = -24\sqrt{7} < 0\), so the function is concave down, confirming a maximum.

Maximum volume: \(V(5-\sqrt{7}) \approx 228.2\) cm\(^3\).

  • Forgetting to check endpoints on a closed-interval optimization.
  • Solving \(f'(x)=0\) and reporting that value as the maximum without verifying it actually is a maximum (second-derivative test or sign chart needed).
  • Substituting numerical values for variables before differentiating in a related rates problem — the variables must remain symbolic through the differentiation step.
  • Confusing velocity (signed) with speed \(=|v(t)|\) (unsigned).
  • Reporting a maximum value that lies outside the domain dictated by the physical constraints of the problem.

Topic 3.1 applies derivatives to rectilinear motion — motion along a straight line. If \(s(t)\) gives an object's position (signed displacement from a fixed origin) at time \(t\), then \(v(t) = s'(t)\) is the instantaneous velocity and \(a(t) = v'(t) = s''(t)\) is the instantaneous acceleration. The signs of these functions carry physical meaning: \(v > 0\) means motion in the positive direction, \(v < 0\) means motion in the negative direction, and \(v = 0\) marks an instant when the object is momentarily at rest (possibly reversing direction).

Speed and velocity are not the same in calculus: speed is \(|v(t)|\), always non-negative, while velocity is the signed quantity \(v(t)\). An object's speed is increasing when \(v\) and \(a\) have the same sign — the engine pushes it in the direction it's already moving. Its speed is decreasing when they have opposite signs — the brake works against motion.

Free-fall and projectile problems give clean examples. For a ball thrown upward, the position function is typically \(s(t) = -\tfrac{1}{2}gt^2 + v_0 t + s_0\). Differentiation gives \(v(t) = -gt + v_0\), and the ball reaches maximum height when \(v(t)=0\), i.e., \(t = v_0/g\). The acceleration is constant: \(a(t) = -g\).

  • Position function — \(s(t)\); signed displacement from a reference origin.
  • Velocity — \(v(t) = s'(t)\); rate of change of position; can be positive or negative.
  • Acceleration — \(a(t) = v'(t) = s''(t)\); rate of change of velocity.
  • Speed — \(|v(t)|\); always non-negative; magnitude of velocity.
  • At rest — \(v(t) = 0\); the object is momentarily stationary, often a turning point.

Worked Example: A particle moves along a line with \(s(t) = t^3 - 9t^2 + 15t + 5\) (metres, seconds). When is the particle at rest? What is its acceleration then?

Velocity: \(v(t) = 3t^2 - 18t + 15 = 3(t^2 - 6t + 5) = 3(t-1)(t-5)\). At rest when \(v(t)=0\): \(t=1\) or \(t=5\).

Acceleration: \(a(t) = 6t - 18\). At \(t=1\): \(a=-12\) m/s² (deceleration; direction change ahead). At \(t=5\): \(a=12\) m/s² (turning back into positive motion).

Worked Example: A ball is thrown upward with initial velocity 20 m/s from a height of 5 m. Using \(g=10\) m/s², find the maximum height.

Position: \(s(t) = -5t^2 + 20t + 5\). Velocity: \(v(t) = -10t + 20\). Max height when \(v(t)=0\): \(t = 2\) s.

\(s(2) = -20 + 40 + 5 = 25\) m. The ball reaches a maximum height of 25 m.

  • Confusing speed with velocity — a problem asking for "when speed is decreasing" requires comparing signs of \(v\) and \(a\), not just looking at \(v\).
  • Assuming the object stops permanently at \(v=0\) — usually it reverses direction.
  • Forgetting to differentiate twice when asked for acceleration; \(a(t) = s''(t)\), not \(s'(t)\).
  • Ignoring direction sign — \(v(t)=-5\) m/s means moving backward at 5 m/s, not "negative speed".

The Extreme Value Theorem guarantees that any function continuous on a closed interval \([a,b]\) attains an absolute maximum and an absolute minimum somewhere on that interval. The strategy to find them is the closed-interval method: (1) compute \(f'(x)\); (2) find critical numbers in \((a,b)\) — values where \(f'(x)=0\) or \(f'(x)\) is undefined; (3) evaluate \(f\) at every critical number and at both endpoints; (4) the largest value is the absolute max, the smallest is the absolute min.

The reason this finite-list method works: an absolute extremum can occur only at a point where the tangent is horizontal (\(f'=0\)), where the derivative fails to exist (corners, cusps), or at the boundary of the interval. The critical numbers cover the first two cases; the endpoints cover the last. Test all candidates and compare.

This differs from local extrema (covered in Unit 4), which require sign-change tests. For absolute extrema on a closed interval, simply comparing function values at the finite list is enough. If the interval is open or unbounded, the EVT doesn't apply and you need additional arguments (asymptotics, monotonicity) to guarantee existence.

  • Absolute (global) maximum on \([a,b]\) — the largest \(f(x)\) value over all \(x\) in \([a,b]\).
  • Absolute (global) minimum on \([a,b]\) — the smallest \(f(x)\) value over all \(x\) in \([a,b]\).
  • Critical number — \(x = c\) in \((a,b)\) where \(f'(c) = 0\) or \(f'(c)\) is undefined.
  • Extreme Value Theorem — continuous \(f\) on closed \([a,b]\) attains absolute max and min.
  • Closed-interval method — compare \(f\) values at critical numbers and at endpoints.

Worked Example: Find the absolute extrema of \(f(x) = x^3 - 3x + 1\) on \([-2, 3]\).

Derivative: \(f'(x) = 3x^2 - 3 = 3(x-1)(x+1)\). Critical numbers: \(x=1\) and \(x=-1\), both in the interval.

Evaluate: \(f(-2) = -8 + 6 + 1 = -1\); \(f(-1) = -1 + 3 + 1 = 3\); \(f(1) = 1 - 3 + 1 = -1\); \(f(3) = 27 - 9 + 1 = 19\).

Absolute max: \(19\) at \(x=3\). Absolute min: \(-1\) at \(x=-2\) and at \(x=1\) (tied).

Worked Example: Find the absolute extrema of \(f(x) = x + \tfrac{4}{x}\) on \([1, 4]\).

Derivative: \(f'(x) = 1 - \tfrac{4}{x^2}\). Set equal to zero: \(x^2 = 4\), so \(x = 2\) (positive value in the interval).

Evaluate: \(f(1) = 1 + 4 = 5\); \(f(2) = 2 + 2 = 4\); \(f(4) = 4 + 1 = 5\).

Absolute max: \(5\) at both endpoints. Absolute min: \(4\) at \(x=2\).

  • Forgetting to check the endpoints — they are critical candidates even when \(f'\ne 0\) there.
  • Including critical numbers outside the interval — only \(c \in (a,b)\) count.
  • Reporting only the \(x\)-value; the absolute max is the \(y\)-value (a function value), and the \(x\) is "where" it occurs.
  • Forgetting to check points where \(f'\) is undefined (cusps, vertical tangents within the domain).

Optimization problems take a real-world scenario (maximize area, minimize cost, fastest route) and convert it into a single-variable extremum problem. The general procedure: (1) read the problem carefully and identify what to maximize or minimize; (2) draw a diagram and label variables; (3) write the objective function in terms of two or more variables; (4) use any constraint equation to eliminate variables until the objective depends on just one; (5) determine its domain; (6) find critical numbers and apply the closed-interval or first-derivative test; (7) interpret the answer in original problem units.

Step 4 is usually where calculus students stumble: you must reduce the objective to one variable before differentiating. If you have area \(A = xy\) plus a perimeter constraint \(2x + 2y = 40\), solve for \(y = 20 - x\) and substitute to get \(A(x) = x(20-x) = 20x - x^2\). Now \(A\) depends only on \(x\), and \(A'(x) = 20 - 2x = 0\) gives \(x = 10\).

Step 7 is where rigor is rewarded: justify that the critical number you found really is the optimum (not a minimum disguised as a maximum, or a boundary value). Use the second-derivative test or check the sign of \(f'\) around the critical number; or note that \(f\to 0\) at the domain endpoints in fencing problems, so an interior critical max must be the absolute max.

  • Objective function — the quantity to be maximized or minimized.
  • Constraint — an equation relating the variables; used to reduce the objective to one variable.
  • Domain of optimization — the set of physically/realistically valid inputs (e.g., positive lengths).
  • Justification — second-derivative test, first-derivative sign chart, or boundary comparison.
  • Interpretation — state the answer with units and identify which variable achieves the optimum.

Worked Example: A rectangular pen with one side along a river (so no fencing needed there) is to be built with 60 m of fencing. What dimensions maximize the area?

Let the side perpendicular to the river be \(x\) (two such sides), and the side parallel to the river be \(y\) (one such side). Constraint: \(2x + y = 60\), so \(y = 60 - 2x\).

Area: \(A(x) = xy = x(60 - 2x) = 60x - 2x^2\). Domain: \(0 < x < 30\).

\(A'(x) = 60 - 4x = 0 \Rightarrow x = 15\). \(A''(x) = -4 < 0\), so this is a maximum.

Then \(y = 60 - 30 = 30\). Maximum area = \(15 \cdot 30 = 450\) m². Pen dimensions: 15 m by 30 m.

Worked Example: An open-top box is made from a 12 cm × 12 cm sheet by cutting equal squares of side \(x\) from each corner and folding. Find the value of \(x\) that maximizes volume.

Volume: \(V(x) = x(12 - 2x)^2\). Domain: \(0 < x < 6\).

\(V'(x) = (12-2x)^2 + x\cdot 2(12-2x)(-2) = (12-2x)[(12-2x) - 4x] = (12-2x)(12-6x)\).

Critical numbers: \(x=6\) (boundary) and \(x=2\). Test \(x=2\): \(V''<0\) (or sign chart confirms max). So \(x=2\), \(V = 2 \cdot 8^2 = 128\) cm³.

  • Trying to optimize a two-variable expression directly without using the constraint to eliminate a variable.
  • Forgetting to specify the domain; some critical numbers fall outside realistic ranges.
  • Skipping the second-derivative or sign test; assuming the critical number is automatically the answer.
  • Stopping at \(x = \) some value without reporting the actual maximum/minimum value and units.

Topic 3.4 applies the optimization machinery to economics and science contexts. In economics, the central new vocabulary is the marginal family of functions: marginal cost \(MC(x) = C'(x)\) is the instantaneous rate at which total cost \(C(x)\) changes per additional unit produced; marginal revenue \(MR(x) = R'(x)\); and marginal profit \(MP(x) = P'(x)\) where \(P(x) = R(x) - C(x)\). Maximum profit occurs at a critical number of \(P(x)\), i.e., where \(MR(x) = MC(x)\).

In economics problems the constraints often come implicitly through a demand equation: if selling at price \(p\) yields demand \(x = x(p)\) units, then revenue is \(R = p\cdot x(p)\) — a product that requires the product rule when you differentiate. Average cost \(\bar{C}(x) = C(x)/x\) is minimized when \(\bar{C}'(x) = 0\), which simplifies to \(\bar{C}(x) = C'(x)\) — the famous result that minimum average cost occurs where marginal cost equals average cost.

In science contexts the same machinery applies to chemical reaction rates, population growth, drug concentration in the bloodstream, and so on. The objective function might be a rational or exponential expression, and the constraint domain may be open or unbounded — additional care is needed to confirm the critical number is a true extremum.

  • Cost function \(C(x)\), revenue function \(R(x)\), profit function \(P(x) = R(x) - C(x)\).
  • Marginal cost \(C'(x)\), marginal revenue \(R'(x)\), marginal profit \(P'(x)\).
  • Average cost \(\bar{C}(x) = C(x)/x\); minimized where \(\bar{C}'(x) = 0\), equivalently where \(C'(x) = \bar{C}(x)\).
  • Demand equation — relates price \(p\) and quantity demanded \(x\); used to write revenue in one variable.
  • Equilibrium principle — maximum profit at \(MR = MC\), provided this is a true maximum.

Worked Example: A company's cost (in dollars) is \(C(x) = 0.01x^2 + 5x + 1000\) and revenue is \(R(x) = 25x - 0.02x^2\). Find the production level that maximizes profit.

Profit: \(P(x) = R(x) - C(x) = (25x - 0.02x^2) - (0.01x^2 + 5x + 1000) = -0.03x^2 + 20x - 1000\).

\(P'(x) = -0.06x + 20 = 0 \Rightarrow x = \tfrac{20}{0.06} \approx 333.3\).

\(P''(x) = -0.06 < 0\), so this is a maximum. Producing about 333 units maximizes profit. \(P(333) \approx -0.03(110889) + 20(333) - 1000 \approx 2333\) dollars.

Worked Example: A drug's concentration in the bloodstream is modeled by \(C(t) = \frac{5t}{t^2 + 4}\) (mg/L, \(t\) hours after dose). When is concentration maximal?

By the quotient rule: \(C'(t) = \frac{5(t^2+4) - 5t(2t)}{(t^2+4)^2} = \frac{5(4 - t^2)}{(t^2+4)^2}\).

Critical numbers: \(t^2 = 4\), so \(t = 2\) (positive). \(C(2) = \tfrac{10}{8} = 1.25\) mg/L. Maximum concentration: 1.25 mg/L at \(t=2\) h.

  • Forgetting that maximum revenue is not the same as maximum profit — they differ by the cost function.
  • Treating "marginal cost" as the total cost — it's the derivative, with units of dollars per unit.
  • Ignoring units (units sold, dollars per item, mg per litre); the answer needs context to be meaningful.
  • Optimizing average cost by setting \(C(x)/x = 0\) — that doesn't optimize; differentiating \(\bar{C}\) does.

Related rates problems ask: given that two quantities are connected by an equation, and one is changing at a known rate, how fast is the other changing? The technique relies on implicit differentiation with respect to time. Each variable in the relating equation is viewed as a function of \(t\); differentiating both sides yields an equation in derivatives like \(\frac{dx}{dt}, \frac{dy}{dt}\) — the rates — and you solve for the unknown one.

The standard recipe: (1) draw a labelled diagram; (2) write an equation that relates the variables (Pythagoras, area formula, similar triangles, volume formula, etc.); (3) differentiate both sides with respect to \(t\); (4) substitute the instantaneous numerical values and the known rate; (5) solve for the unknown rate and report with units. The crucial discipline: do not substitute values until after differentiating — otherwise the relationships between variables vanish.

Common geometric setups: a sliding ladder (Pythagorean relation), a filling cone (volume formula, often with similar triangles to express one variable in terms of another), a moving shadow (similar triangles), or expanding circles/spheres (\(A = \pi r^2\) or \(V = \tfrac{4}{3}\pi r^3\)).

  • Related rates — multiple time-dependent quantities tied by an equation; differentiating gives a relation among their rates.
  • Implicit time differentiation — \(\frac{d}{dt}\) applied to each side; chain rule attaches \(\frac{dx}{dt}\), \(\frac{dy}{dt}\), etc.
  • Constraint equation — the geometric or physical relation (Pythagoras, similar triangles, volume formula).
  • Substitute last — plug in numerical values only after differentiation; otherwise the relationship is destroyed.
  • Sign convention — negative rates indicate decreasing quantities (water draining, ladder slipping).

Worked Example: A 5 m ladder leans against a wall. The bottom slides outward at 0.3 m/s. How fast is the top sliding down when the bottom is 3 m from the wall?

Let \(x\) = horizontal distance from wall, \(y\) = height on wall. Pythagoras: \(x^2 + y^2 = 25\).

Differentiate with respect to \(t\): \(2x\frac{dx}{dt} + 2y\frac{dy}{dt} = 0 \Rightarrow \frac{dy}{dt} = -\frac{x}{y}\cdot\frac{dx}{dt}\).

When \(x=3\), \(y = \sqrt{25-9} = 4\). With \(\frac{dx}{dt} = 0.3\): \(\frac{dy}{dt} = -\tfrac{3}{4}(0.3) = -0.225\) m/s. The top slides DOWN at 0.225 m/s.

Worked Example: Water pours into a conical tank (point down) at 2 m³/min. The tank has height 10 m and top radius 5 m. How fast is the water level rising when the water is 6 m deep?

By similar triangles, \(\frac{r}{h} = \frac{5}{10} = \tfrac{1}{2}\), so \(r = h/2\). Volume: \(V = \tfrac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h = \tfrac{1}{3}\pi (h/2)^2 h = \tfrac{\pi h^3}{12}\).

Differentiate: \(\frac{dV}{dt} = \tfrac{\pi h^2}{4}\frac{dh}{dt}\). Solve: \(\frac{dh}{dt} = \frac{4}{\pi h^2}\frac{dV}{dt}\).

At \(h=6\): \(\frac{dh}{dt} = \frac{4 \cdot 2}{\pi \cdot 36} = \frac{2}{9\pi} \approx 0.0707\) m/min.

  • Substituting numbers before differentiating, eliminating the variables that the chain rule needs.
  • Forgetting the sign convention — the "ladder slipping down" rate is negative.
  • Not using similar triangles to express two related geometric quantities in one variable when the constraint demands it.
  • Reporting the rate without units (m/min, m/s, etc.); the units encode the physical meaning.

Unit 4: Curve Sketching

Increasing/decreasing, critical points, concavity, asymptotes

📚 Strand B: Derivatives & Applications 📹 10 lessons
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The first derivative tells us about the direction of a curve: \(f'(x) > 0\) means \(f\) is increasing, \(f'(x) < 0\) means decreasing. Where \(f'\) changes sign — at a critical number — the function may have a local maximum or minimum. The first derivative test diagnoses these: at a critical number \(c\), if \(f'\) changes from positive to negative, \(c\) is a local max; if negative to positive, a local min; if no sign change, neither.

The second derivative reveals curvature. When \(f''(x) > 0\), the curve is concave up (cup-shaped, holding water); when \(f''(x) < 0\), it is concave down. A point where concavity changes is a point of inflection. The second derivative test at a critical number \(c\) with \(f'(c)=0\): if \(f''(c) > 0\), local min; if \(f''(c) < 0\), local max; if \(f''(c)=0\), the test is inconclusive (fall back to the first derivative test).

Asymptotes describe end behaviour. Vertical asymptotes appear where a rational function's denominator is zero but the numerator is not. Horizontal asymptotes emerge from \(\lim_{x\to\pm\infty} f(x)\); for rational functions, compare degrees of numerator and denominator. Oblique (slant) asymptotes occur when the numerator degree exceeds the denominator's by exactly one — long division reveals the linear asymptote. A complete sketch synthesizes domain, intercepts, asymptotes, intervals of increase/decrease, local extrema, concavity, and inflection points.

  • Increasing — \(f(x_1) < f(x_2)\) whenever \(x_1 < x_2\); equivalent to \(f'(x) \ge 0\) on the interval (positive everywhere except possibly isolated points).
  • Critical number — \(x = c\) where \(f'(c) = 0\) or \(f'(c)\) does not exist (and \(c\) is in the domain of \(f\)).
  • Concave up / down — concave up when \(f''(x) > 0\); concave down when \(f''(x) < 0\).
  • Point of inflection — a point on the graph where concavity changes and the tangent line exists.
  • Vertical asymptote — line \(x=a\) such that \(\lim_{x\to a^\pm} f(x) = \pm\infty\).
  • Horizontal asymptote — line \(y=L\) such that \(\lim_{x\to\pm\infty} f(x) = L\).
  • Oblique asymptote — non-horizontal line \(y = mx + b\) that the curve approaches as \(x \to \pm\infty\).

Worked Example: Full analysis of \(f(x) = x^3 - 6x^2 + 9x\)

Domain: all real \(x\). Intercepts: \(f(0)=0\); \(f(x) = x(x-3)^2 = 0\) at \(x=0\) and \(x=3\) (double root).

First derivative: \(f'(x) = 3x^2 - 12x + 9 = 3(x-1)(x-3)\). Critical numbers: \(x=1\), \(x=3\).

Sign of \(f'\): positive on \((-\infty,1)\), negative on \((1,3)\), positive on \((3,\infty)\). So \(f\) increases on \((-\infty,1)\), decreases on \((1,3)\), increases on \((3,\infty)\). Local max at \(x=1\): \(f(1) = 1-6+9 = 4\). Local min at \(x=3\): \(f(3) = 27 - 54 + 27 = 0\).

Second derivative: \(f''(x) = 6x - 12 = 6(x-2)\). Inflection at \(x=2\); \(f(2) = 8-24+18 = 2\). Concave down on \((-\infty,2)\), concave up on \((2,\infty)\).

Verify max/min with second derivative test: \(f''(1) = -6 < 0\) confirms local max; \(f''(3) = 6 > 0\) confirms local min.

Worked Example: Asymptotes of \(\displaystyle f(x) = \frac{2x^2 + 3x - 1}{x - 2}\)

Vertical: denominator zero at \(x=2\); numerator at \(x=2\) is \(8 + 6 - 1 = 13 \ne 0\), so \(x=2\) is a vertical asymptote.

Degree of numerator (2) exceeds denominator (1) by one, so there is an oblique asymptote (and no horizontal asymptote). Polynomial long division: \[\frac{2x^2 + 3x - 1}{x-2} = 2x + 7 + \frac{13}{x-2}.\]

As \(x\to\pm\infty\), the remainder \(\frac{13}{x-2} \to 0\), so the oblique asymptote is \(y = 2x + 7\).

  • Claiming an inflection point wherever \(f''=0\), without verifying that the sign of \(f''\) actually changes.
  • Treating every point where the denominator is zero as a vertical asymptote — if the numerator also vanishes there and the factor cancels, you have a hole, not an asymptote.
  • Forgetting that \(x=c\) is only a critical number if \(c\) is in the domain of \(f\).
  • Using the second derivative test when \(f''(c) = 0\) — the test gives no information; revert to the first derivative test.
  • Mis-reading a sign chart by testing values outside the interval being studied.

Topic 4.1 formalizes how the first derivative tells us where a function is rising or falling. A function \(f\) is increasing on an interval \(I\) if \(x_1 < x_2 \Rightarrow f(x_1) < f(x_2)\) for all \(x_1, x_2 \in I\); it is decreasing if \(x_1 < x_2 \Rightarrow f(x_1) > f(x_2)\). The first-derivative connection: if \(f'(x) > 0\) throughout \(I\), then \(f\) is increasing on \(I\); if \(f'(x) < 0\), then decreasing. This is a consequence of the Mean Value Theorem.

The practical procedure is a sign chart for \(f'\): (1) compute \(f'(x)\); (2) find critical numbers, where \(f'\) is zero or undefined; (3) determine the sign of \(f'\) on each interval between critical numbers and at the domain boundary, by testing a representative \(x\)-value or factoring; (4) state the intervals of increase (\(f'>0\)) and decrease (\(f'<0\)).

Domain matters: include all numbers where \(f'\) is undefined but \(f\) is defined (cusps, vertical tangents), and exclude any number not in the domain of \(f\) (like the asymptote of a rational function). Critical numbers where \(f\) is undefined are NOT part of the function's intervals; they break the number line into separate analysis regions.

  • Increasing on \(I\) — \(f'(x) > 0\) for all \(x \in I\) (strict version).
  • Decreasing on \(I\) — \(f'(x) < 0\) for all \(x \in I\).
  • Critical number — value where \(f'(x) = 0\) or \(f'(x)\) is undefined (but \(f(x)\) is defined).
  • Sign chart — a number-line diagram showing signs of \(f'\) on each subinterval.
  • Mean Value Theorem — between any two values of \(x\) where \(f'>0\) throughout, \(f\) increases (the underlying theorem).

Worked Example: Find the intervals on which \(f(x) = x^3 - 6x^2 + 9x + 2\) is increasing and decreasing.

\(f'(x) = 3x^2 - 12x + 9 = 3(x^2 - 4x + 3) = 3(x-1)(x-3)\). Critical numbers: \(x = 1, 3\).

Sign chart: for \(x<1\), \(f'>0\) (test \(x=0\): \(3(-1)(-3) = 9 > 0\)). For \(1 < x < 3\), \(f'<0\) (test \(x=2\): \(3(1)(-1) = -3 < 0\)). For \(x>3\), \(f'>0\) (test \(x=4\): \(3(3)(1) = 9 > 0\)).

Increasing on \((-\infty, 1) \cup (3, \infty)\); decreasing on \((1, 3)\).

Worked Example: For \(g(x) = \frac{x}{x^2 + 1}\), find the intervals of increase/decrease.

\(g'(x) = \frac{(x^2+1) - x(2x)}{(x^2+1)^2} = \frac{1 - x^2}{(x^2+1)^2}\). Denominator always positive; sign of \(g'\) matches \(1 - x^2 = (1-x)(1+x)\).

Critical numbers: \(x = \pm 1\). Sign: \(g'<0\) for \(x<-1\); \(g'>0\) for \(-11\).

Decreasing on \((-\infty, -1) \cup (1, \infty)\); increasing on \((-1, 1)\).

  • Reporting "increasing where \(f > 0\)" — that's about the function's sign, not its slope; you want \(f' > 0\).
  • Forgetting to factor \(f'\) before sign-charting; partially factored expressions hide critical numbers.
  • Including critical numbers in BOTH the increasing and decreasing intervals — convention is to use open intervals at critical numbers.
  • Forgetting to remove points outside the domain (e.g., vertical asymptotes of a rational function).

Topic 4.2 classifies critical points as local maxima, local minima, or neither using the first derivative test. At a critical number \(x=c\) (where \(f'(c)=0\) or \(f'\) is undefined), examine the sign of \(f'\) immediately to the left and right: if \(f'\) goes from \(+\) to \(-\), then \(c\) is a local max; from \(-\) to \(+\), local min; same sign on both sides (e.g., \(+/+\) or \(-/-\)), neither — typically a horizontal point of inflection.

Local extrema are a local property: \(f(c)\) is larger (or smaller) than nearby \(f\) values, but not necessarily than all values on the function's domain. This contrasts with the absolute extrema on a closed interval (Topic 3.2). A function on an unbounded or open domain may have local extrema without absolute ones, or vice versa.

The first-derivative test is a sign-change test, which makes it robust: it works even when \(f''\) is hard to compute, when \(f''(c) = 0\) (the second-derivative test becomes inconclusive), and when \(f'\) is undefined at \(c\) (e.g., a cusp like in \(f(x) = x^{2/3}\), where \(c=0\) is a local minimum despite \(f'(0)\) being undefined).

  • Local maximum at \(c\) — \(f(c) \ge f(x)\) for all \(x\) near \(c\).
  • Local minimum at \(c\) — \(f(c) \le f(x)\) for all \(x\) near \(c\).
  • Critical point — the point \((c, f(c))\) where \(c\) is a critical number.
  • First derivative test — sign change of \(f'\) at \(c\): \(+/-\) → max; \(-/+\) → min; same sign → neither.
  • Local vs. absolute — local extrema concern small neighbourhoods; absolute extrema concern the whole domain or interval.

Worked Example: Locate and classify the local extrema of \(f(x) = 2x^3 - 9x^2 + 12x - 1\).

\(f'(x) = 6x^2 - 18x + 12 = 6(x^2 - 3x + 2) = 6(x-1)(x-2)\). Critical numbers: \(x=1, x=2\).

Sign of \(f'\): at \(x=0\), \(f'(0) = 12 > 0\); at \(x = 1.5\), \(f'(1.5) = 6(0.5)(-0.5) = -1.5 < 0\); at \(x=3\), \(f'(3) = 6(2)(1) = 12 > 0\).

So \(f'\) goes \(+,-,+\). At \(x=1\) (\(+\to -\)): local maximum, \(f(1) = 2 - 9 + 12 - 1 = 4\). At \(x=2\) (\(-\to +\)): local minimum, \(f(2) = 16 - 36 + 24 - 1 = 3\).

Worked Example: Classify the critical point at \(x=0\) for \(f(x) = x^3\).

\(f'(x) = 3x^2\). \(f'(0) = 0\), so \(x=0\) is a critical number.

Sign of \(f'\): for \(x<0\), \(3x^2 > 0\); for \(x>0\), \(3x^2 > 0\). No sign change.

Therefore \(x=0\) is neither a local max nor a local min; it's a horizontal point of inflection.

  • Concluding \(x=0\) is a local extremum for \(f(x)=x^3\) just because \(f'(0)=0\) — sign of \(f'\) doesn't change, so it isn't an extremum.
  • Reporting only \(x\)-values without the corresponding \(f(c)\) value; the "extremum" is the function value.
  • Forgetting critical numbers where \(f'\) is undefined (cusps).
  • Confusing local max (\(+\to -\)) with local min (\(-\to +\)) — the direction matters.

Asymptotes describe how a curve behaves at infinity or near a vertical line of indefinition. A vertical asymptote at \(x=a\) occurs when \(\lim_{x\to a^\pm}f(x) = \pm\infty\). For a rational function \(\frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}\) in lowest terms, this happens precisely when \(Q(a)=0\) and \(P(a)\ne 0\) — the denominator vanishes faster than the numerator. If both vanish, factor and cancel: the cancellation produces a removable discontinuity (a hole) rather than a vertical asymptote.

A horizontal asymptote is a horizontal line \(y=L\) such that \(\lim_{x\to\pm\infty} f(x) = L\). For rationals, compare the degrees of numerator (\(n\)) and denominator (\(d\)): if \(nd\), there is no horizontal asymptote — but an oblique (slant) asymptote exists when \(n = d+1\), obtained by polynomial long division.

The slant asymptote is the polynomial quotient. For \(\frac{x^2+1}{x-1}\), long division gives \(x + 1 + \frac{2}{x-1}\); as \(x\to\pm\infty\), the remainder term \(\frac{2}{x-1}\to 0\), so \(y = x+1\) is the slant asymptote. The curve approaches this line as \(x\) grows large in either direction.

  • Vertical asymptote at \(x=a\) — \(\lim_{x\to a^\pm}f(x)=\pm\infty\); for rationals in lowest terms, \(Q(a)=0, P(a)\ne 0\).
  • Horizontal asymptote — \(\lim_{x\to\pm\infty}f(x)=L\); compare numerator and denominator degrees.
  • Oblique (slant) asymptote — exists for rationals when \(\deg P = \deg Q + 1\); obtained by polynomial division.
  • Removable discontinuity — common factor of \(P\) and \(Q\); a hole, not an asymptote.
  • Behaviour near asymptote — examine one-sided limits to determine \(+\infty\) vs. \(-\infty\).

Worked Example: Find all asymptotes of \(f(x) = \dfrac{2x^2 - 3}{x^2 - 4}\).

Denominator \(x^2 - 4 = (x-2)(x+2) = 0\) at \(x=\pm 2\); numerator at those points: \(2(4)-3 = 5\) and \(2(4)-3=5\), both nonzero. Vertical asymptotes at \(x=-2\) and \(x=2\).

Degrees: numerator 2, denominator 2; horizontal asymptote at \(y = \tfrac{2}{1} = 2\).

Worked Example: Find the oblique asymptote of \(g(x) = \dfrac{x^2 + 2x + 5}{x + 1}\).

Polynomial long division: \(x^2 + 2x + 5 \div (x+1)\). Divide \(x^2 \div x = x\); multiply back: \(x(x+1) = x^2 + x\); subtract: \(x + 5\). Then \(x \div x = 1\); multiply: \(x+1\); subtract: \(4\).

So \(g(x) = x + 1 + \frac{4}{x+1}\). As \(x \to \pm\infty\), \(\frac{4}{x+1} \to 0\); the slant asymptote is \(y = x + 1\).

  • Mistaking a hole for a vertical asymptote — always check whether the numerator also vanishes at the suspect point.
  • Reporting a horizontal asymptote when degrees are unequal in the wrong direction (\(n>d\)): there isn't one.
  • Forgetting that horizontal and oblique asymptotes are mutually exclusive for rationals.
  • Setting up the limit \(\lim_{x\to a}f(x)\) without distinguishing left- and right-hand approaches at a vertical asymptote.

Topic 4.4 uses the second derivative to analyze a curve's concavity — the way it bends. A graph is concave up on an interval if the curve lies above its tangent lines (smiling, holds water); it is concave down if the curve lies below its tangent lines (frowning, sheds water). Algebraically, concavity is governed by the sign of \(f''\): \(f''(x) > 0\) means concave up, \(f''(x) < 0\) means concave down.

A point of inflection is a point on the graph where concavity changes — from up to down or vice versa. To find them: (1) compute \(f''(x)\); (2) find where \(f''=0\) or \(f''\) is undefined; (3) verify that \(f''\) actually changes sign at each candidate (a zero of \(f''\) without sign change is not an inflection point — like \(x=0\) for \(f(x) = x^4\)).

The second derivative test classifies critical points: at a critical number \(c\) with \(f'(c) = 0\), if \(f''(c) > 0\) the graph is concave up at \(c\) so \(c\) is a local min; if \(f''(c) < 0\), local max; if \(f''(c) = 0\), inconclusive — fall back to the first derivative test. The test is fast when \(f''\) is easy to compute, but it can fail at degenerate critical points.

  • Concave up — \(f''(x) > 0\); curve bends upward; tangent lies below the curve.
  • Concave down — \(f''(x) < 0\); curve bends downward; tangent lies above the curve.
  • Point of inflection — a point where concavity changes sign; \(f''\) must change sign there.
  • Second derivative test — at \(f'(c)=0\): \(f''(c)>0\) → local min; \(<0\) → local max; \(=0\) → inconclusive.
  • Sign chart for \(f''\) — analogous to the \(f'\) chart; intervals separated by zeros and discontinuities of \(f''\).

Worked Example: Find the inflection points and intervals of concavity for \(f(x) = x^3 - 6x^2 + 5\).

\(f'(x) = 3x^2 - 12x\); \(f''(x) = 6x - 12\). Set \(f''=0\): \(x=2\).

Sign of \(f''\): for \(x<2\), \(f''<0\) (concave down); for \(x>2\), \(f''>0\) (concave up). Sign changes at \(x=2\), so \((2, f(2)) = (2, -11)\) is an inflection point.

Worked Example: Use the second derivative test on \(f(x) = x^4 - 4x^3\).

\(f'(x) = 4x^3 - 12x^2 = 4x^2(x-3)\). Critical numbers: \(x=0\) and \(x=3\).

\(f''(x) = 12x^2 - 24x\). \(f''(3) = 108 - 72 = 36 > 0\) → \(x=3\) is local min. \(f''(0) = 0\) → inconclusive.

At \(x=0\), fall back to the first derivative test: \(f'(x) = 4x^2(x-3)\), and \(4x^2 \ge 0\) always. So \(f'\) has the same sign as \(x-3\); for \(x\) near 0 (and \(<3\)), \(f' < 0\) on both sides. No sign change — \(x=0\) is neither (a horizontal point of inflection at \((0,0)\)).

  • Concluding that \(f''(c)=0\) automatically means an inflection point — a sign change is required.
  • Forgetting that the second derivative test is inconclusive when \(f''(c) = 0\).
  • Confusing concave up with increasing — they describe different things (bending vs. rising).
  • Reporting only the \(x\)-value of an inflection point; it's a point, so include \(f(x)\).

Topic 4.5 integrates everything from Topics 4.1-4.4 into a single procedure: produce an accurate sketch of \(y = f(x)\) using calculus. The standard curve-sketching algorithm follows seven steps, each contributing a feature to the graph: (1) Domain — exclude points where \(f\) is undefined; (2) Intercepts — set \(y=0\) for \(x\)-intercepts (often hard, sometimes skipped) and evaluate \(f(0)\) for \(y\)-intercept; (3) Symmetry — check \(f(-x)\) against \(f(x)\) for even/odd parity; (4) Asymptotes — vertical (where denominator vanishes), horizontal (limits at \(\pm\infty\)), and oblique (polynomial long division if applicable).

(5) First derivative analysis — compute \(f'(x)\), find critical numbers, build a sign chart, and identify intervals of increase/decrease plus local extrema; (6) Second derivative analysis — compute \(f''(x)\), find candidate inflection points, build a concavity chart; (7) Sketch — plot intercepts, asymptotes, extrema, and inflection points, then draw a smooth curve consistent with the sign charts. Label everything.

The algorithm is rote, but the skill is in interpreting the algebra. A negative second derivative at a critical number tells you both "concave down here" AND "local maximum". A vertical asymptote near a critical number determines whether the curve runs to \(+\infty\) or \(-\infty\). Reading all signals together gives the global shape without plotting a hundred points.

  • Curve-sketching algorithm — domain, intercepts, symmetry, asymptotes, first-derivative analysis, second-derivative analysis, sketch.
  • Even function — \(f(-x) = f(x)\); symmetric about the \(y\)-axis.
  • Odd function — \(f(-x) = -f(x)\); symmetric about the origin.
  • Behaviour near asymptotes — examine one-sided limits to determine \(\pm\infty\).
  • Synthesis — first- and second-derivative charts together encode shape; intercepts and asymptotes pin down position.

Worked Example: Sketch \(f(x) = \dfrac{x^2}{x^2 - 4}\).

Domain: \(x \ne \pm 2\). Y-intercept: \(f(0) = 0\). X-intercept: numerator = 0 at \(x=0\). Symmetry: \(f(-x) = f(x)\) — even.

Vertical asymptotes at \(x = \pm 2\). Horizontal: \(\lim_{x\to\pm\infty}\frac{x^2}{x^2-4} = 1\), so \(y=1\).

\(f'(x) = \frac{2x(x^2-4) - x^2(2x)}{(x^2-4)^2} = \frac{-8x}{(x^2-4)^2}\). Critical numbers: \(x=0\) (and \(\pm 2\) are not critical, they're excluded). \(f' > 0\) for \(x<0\) (except \(x=-2\)); \(f' < 0\) for \(x>0\) (except \(x=2\)). So \(x=0\) is a local max with \(f(0)=0\).

The curve passes through \((0,0)\) as a local max, dives down to \(-\infty\) at the asymptotes, then climbs to \(+\infty\) on the outside of the asymptotes, leveling toward \(y=1\) at the far ends.

  • Skipping the domain step and ending up with extraneous features near excluded \(x\)-values.
  • Forgetting to check symmetry — it cuts work in half for even or odd functions.
  • Mixing up vertical vs. horizontal asymptote conditions; they require different limit setups.
  • Drawing a sketch that contradicts the sign charts (e.g., a "concave up" piece where \(f''<0\)).

Derivatives of exponential, sine, cosine, tangent functions

The exponential function \(y = e^x\) is the unique function (up to scaling) that is its own derivative: \(\frac{d}{dx}[e^x] = e^x\). The number \(e \approx 2.71828\) is precisely the base for which this happens. For a general base, \(\frac{d}{dx}[b^x] = b^x \ln b\), because \(b^x = e^{x\ln b}\) by the change-of-base identity, and the chain rule then provides the extra \(\ln b\) factor. This is the calculus reason \(e\) is "natural" — it eliminates a multiplicative constant that would otherwise appear forever.

Trigonometric derivatives all flow from two foundational limits: \(\lim_{x\to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} = 1\) and \(\lim_{x\to 0} \frac{\cos x - 1}{x} = 0\). Using these and the angle-addition formulas, you can derive \(\frac{d}{dx}[\sin x] = \cos x\) from first principles. From this, the quotient rule gives \(\frac{d}{dx}[\tan x] = \sec^2 x\), and the chain rule gives \(\frac{d}{dx}[\sin(g(x))] = \cos(g(x))\cdot g'(x)\). All angles must be in radians for these formulas to hold — using degrees introduces a factor of \(\frac{\pi}{180}\) that breaks the limits.

Combining these new derivatives with the rules from Unit 2 unlocks modelling of natural phenomena: radioactive decay (\(N(t) = N_0 e^{-kt}\)), population growth, alternating current, sound waves, daylight hours, and tides. Optimization with exponential and trigonometric functions extends Unit 3's toolkit to a much wider class of problems.

  • Euler's number \(e\) — \(e = \lim_{n\to\infty}(1+\tfrac{1}{n})^n \approx 2.71828\); the unique base for which \((b^x)' = b^x\).
  • \((e^x)' = e^x\) — the natural exponential is its own derivative.
  • \((b^x)' = b^x \ln b\) — derivative of a general exponential.
  • \((\sin x)' = \cos x\) and \((\cos x)' = -\sin x\) — fundamental trigonometric derivatives (radians required).
  • \((\tan x)' = \sec^2 x\) — from quotient rule on \(\tan x = \sin x / \cos x\).
  • Special limit — \(\lim_{x\to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} = 1\); the foundation of all trig derivatives.
  • Radian — angle measure such that arc length on a unit circle equals the angle; required for calculus formulas to work cleanly.

Worked Example: Differentiate \(y = x^2 e^{3x}\)

Product rule with \(f = x^2\), \(g = e^{3x}\): \(f' = 2x\) and (chain rule on \(g\)) \(g' = e^{3x}\cdot 3 = 3 e^{3x}\).

\[y' = f'g + fg' = 2x\cdot e^{3x} + x^2 \cdot 3 e^{3x} = e^{3x}(2x + 3x^2) = x e^{3x}(2 + 3x).\]

Factoring out the common \(e^{3x}\) at the end is good practice — it simplifies later analysis (e.g., finding critical numbers).

Worked Example: Differentiate \(y = \sin(\cos(2x))\)

Triple application of the chain rule. Identify layers: outermost \(\sin(\cdot)\); middle \(\cos(\cdot)\); inner \(2x\).

\[\frac{dy}{dx} = \cos(\cos(2x)) \cdot \frac{d}{dx}[\cos(2x)] = \cos(\cos(2x)) \cdot \bigl(-\sin(2x)\bigr) \cdot 2.\]

Final form: \(y' = -2\sin(2x)\cos(\cos(2x))\).

  • Writing \(\frac{d}{dx}[e^x] = x e^{x-1}\) — confusing the exponential rule with the power rule. The power rule applies when the variable is in the base, not the exponent.
  • Forgetting the \(\ln b\) factor in \(\frac{d}{dx}[b^x] = b^x \ln b\).
  • Doing trig calculus in degrees — formulas like \((\sin x)' = \cos x\) require radians.
  • Dropping the inner derivative when differentiating composites like \(\sin(3x^2)\); the answer is \(6x \cos(3x^2)\), not \(\cos(3x^2)\).
  • Mis-signing \((\cos x)' = -\sin x\) — the negative sign is easy to overlook.

Topic 5.1 introduces the most important non-polynomial derivative: \[\frac{d}{dx}[e^x] = e^x.\] The natural exponential function is its own derivative — a property that distinguishes it among all functions and makes it the fundamental "rate-equals-amount" model. The number \(e \approx 2.71828\) is chosen precisely so that the slope of \(y=b^x\) at \(x=0\) is exactly 1; this calibration produces \(\frac{d}{dx}[e^x]=e^x\) at every point.

Combined with the chain rule, this generalizes: \(\frac{d}{dx}[e^{g(x)}] = e^{g(x)}\cdot g'(x)\). So \(\frac{d}{dx}[e^{3x}] = 3e^{3x}\) and \(\frac{d}{dx}[e^{x^2}] = 2x e^{x^2}\). Exponentials are stable under differentiation: derivatives of exponentials are still exponentials, multiplied by the rate at which the exponent is changing.

This rule is the basis of all exponential growth and decay models. If a population satisfies \(P(t) = P_0 e^{kt}\), then \(P'(t) = kP_0 e^{kt} = kP(t)\) — the instantaneous rate of growth is proportional to the current population, with proportionality constant \(k\). The same model fits radioactive decay (\(k<0\)), continuously compounded interest, and Newton's law of cooling.

  • Natural exponential function — \(y = e^x\); the unique exponential whose slope at \(x=0\) is 1.
  • Self-derivative property — \(\frac{d}{dx}[e^x] = e^x\); every higher derivative is also \(e^x\).
  • Chain rule extension — \(\frac{d}{dx}[e^{g(x)}] = e^{g(x)}\cdot g'(x)\).
  • Euler's number — \(e = \lim_{n\to\infty}(1+\tfrac{1}{n})^n \approx 2.71828\).
  • Exponential model — \(y = A e^{kt}\); \(k>0\) growth, \(k<0\) decay.

Worked Example: Differentiate \(y = x^2 e^{3x}\).

Product rule with \(f = x^2\) and \(g = e^{3x}\). Then \(f' = 2x\) and (chain rule) \(g' = 3e^{3x}\).

\(y' = f'g + fg' = 2x e^{3x} + x^2 \cdot 3e^{3x} = e^{3x}(2x + 3x^2) = x e^{3x}(2 + 3x)\).

Worked Example: A bacterial colony has population \(P(t) = 500 e^{0.4t}\) (\(t\) in hours). When is the population growing at 1000 bacteria/hour?

\(P'(t) = 500 \cdot 0.4 \cdot e^{0.4t} = 200 e^{0.4t}\). Set \(P'(t) = 1000\): \(200 e^{0.4t} = 1000 \Rightarrow e^{0.4t} = 5 \Rightarrow 0.4t = \ln 5 \Rightarrow t = \tfrac{\ln 5}{0.4} \approx 4.02\) hours.

  • Treating \(e^x\) like a power function: \(\frac{d}{dx}[e^x] \ne x e^{x-1}\) — that's the power rule for \(x^n\), not for \(b^x\).
  • Forgetting the inner derivative on \(e^{g(x)}\): \(\frac{d}{dx}[e^{3x}] = 3e^{3x}\), not \(e^{3x}\).
  • Confusing \(e\) (constant) with \(e^x\) (function): \(\frac{d}{dx}[e] = 0\) but \(\frac{d}{dx}[e^x] = e^x\).
  • Writing the answer with \(\ln\) of \(e\) — \(\ln e = 1\), so simplify.

Topic 5.2 generalizes the derivative of \(e^x\) to arbitrary positive base \(b\). The result is \[\frac{d}{dx}[b^x] = b^x \ln b,\] which reduces to \(\frac{d}{dx}[e^x] = e^x\) when \(b = e\) (since \(\ln e = 1\)). The factor \(\ln b\) arises naturally: rewriting \(b^x = e^{x\ln b}\) and applying the chain rule gives \(e^{x\ln b}\cdot \ln b = b^x \ln b\).

This formula highlights why \(e\) is the privileged base: it eliminates the awkward \(\ln b\) factor. For computational ease we sometimes convert \(2^x\) or \(10^x\) into \(e^{x\ln 2}\) or \(e^{x\ln 10}\) before differentiating, but the direct rule is just as quick. This rule is important in modeling because real-world quantities are often given in base-10 or base-2 form: doubling times of populations, half-lives of radioactive isotopes, and decibel scales. Knowing how to differentiate \(b^x\) directly avoids unnecessary base conversions and keeps the model in its native units.

Worked Example: Differentiate \(y = 5^x + x^5\).

Two different rules apply: \(\frac{d}{dx}[5^x] = 5^x \ln 5\) (exponential), and \(\frac{d}{dx}[x^5] = 5x^4\) (power).

The derivative of the natural logarithm is one of the cleanest rules in calculus: \[\frac{d}{dx}[\ln x] = \frac{1}{x}, \qquad x > 0.\] This is derived by implicit differentiation: if \(y = \ln x\), then \(e^y = x\); differentiating both sides yields \(e^y\frac{dy}{dx} = 1\), and substituting \(e^y = x\) gives \(\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{x}\). The exponential and logarithmic derivatives are mirror images of each other (inverse functions).

For a general base, the change-of-base identity \(\log_b x = \frac{\ln x}{\ln b}\) leads to \[\frac{d}{dx}[\log_b x] = \frac{1}{x \ln b}, \qquad x > 0, b > 0, b\ne 1.\] When \(b = e\), \(\ln b = 1\) and we recover \(\frac{1}{x}\). The chain-rule extensions are essential: \(\frac{d}{dx}[\ln g(x)] = \frac{g'(x)}{g(x)}\), and \(\frac{d}{dx}[\log_b g(x)] = \frac{g'(x)}{g(x)\ln b}\).

The fact that \(\frac{d}{dx}[\ln x] = \frac{1}{x}\) means \(\ln\) "fills in" the missing antiderivative of \(\frac{1}{x}\) — the one case the power rule cannot handle (\(\int x^{-1}\,dx \ne \frac{x^0}{0}\)). It also explains why logarithms grow so slowly: their derivative drops to zero as \(x\to\infty\).

  • Natural log derivative — \(\frac{d}{dx}[\ln x] = \frac{1}{x}\), \(x > 0\).
  • General log derivative — \(\frac{d}{dx}[\log_b x] = \frac{1}{x\ln b}\).
  • Chain rule on logs — \(\frac{d}{dx}[\ln g(x)] = \frac{g'(x)}{g(x)}\); a quotient, not a product.
  • Domain restriction — \(\ln x\) is defined only for \(x>0\); the formula does not extend to negative \(x\) without an absolute value.
  • Logarithm of absolute value — \(\frac{d}{dx}[\ln|x|] = \frac{1}{x}\) for \(x\ne 0\) — extends the rule to all nonzero \(x\).

Worked Example: Differentiate \(y = \ln(x^2 + 1)\).

Apply chain rule with \(g(x) = x^2 + 1\): \(g'(x) = 2x\). So \(y' = \frac{g'(x)}{g(x)} = \frac{2x}{x^2 + 1}\).

Worked Example: Differentiate \(y = x\ln x - x\).

Product rule on \(x\ln x\): \((1)(\ln x) + (x)(\tfrac{1}{x}) = \ln x + 1\). Derivative of \(-x\) is \(-1\).

\(y' = \ln x + 1 - 1 = \ln x\). (Side fact: this shows \(x\ln x - x\) is an antiderivative of \(\ln x\) — useful in later integral calculus.)

  • Writing \(\frac{d}{dx}[\ln(g(x))] = \ln(g'(x))\) — completely wrong; the derivative is \(\frac{g'(x)}{g(x)}\).
  • Forgetting the \(\ln b\) factor for non-natural logs: \(\frac{d}{dx}[\log_{10} x] = \frac{1}{x \ln 10}\), not \(\frac{1}{x}\).
  • Differentiating \(\ln x\) outside its domain (\(x \le 0\)) without using \(\ln|x|\).
  • Confusing \(\ln(x^2)\) with \((\ln x)^2\) — they are different functions with different derivatives.

Topic 5.3 combines exponential calculus with optimization. Exponential models arise naturally in biology (population growth, drug elimination), finance (compound interest, present value), engineering (cooling, RC circuits), and economics. The optimization procedure is unchanged from Unit 3: identify the objective, eliminate variables via constraints, set the derivative to zero, and justify the extremum. The new wrinkle is that derivatives of exponentials and combinations like \(x e^{-x}\) or \(\frac{\ln x}{x}\) require the product, quotient, and chain rules from Unit 2 layered with the exponential and logarithmic rules from 5.1-5.2.

Critical points of products like \(f(x) = x e^{-x}\) frequently arise. Differentiating: \(f'(x) = e^{-x} + x(-e^{-x}) = e^{-x}(1 - x)\). Since \(e^{-x}>0\) always, the sign of \(f'\) matches \(1-x\): positive for \(x<1\), negative for \(x>1\). So the function has a single critical maximum at \(x=1\), \(f(1) = e^{-1}\). This pattern — exponential decay times a polynomial — appears in countless models.

Maximum profit from a continuous-decay product, minimum drug concentration after dosing, optimal harvest timing for a growing population — all fit this template. The exponential's monotone nature means there's typically a unique interior optimum, which simplifies justification: a single sign change in \(f'\) gives the absolute extremum on the relevant domain.

  • Exponential growth model — \(P(t) = P_0 e^{kt}\); \(P'(t) = kP(t)\); never has a critical point on its own.
  • Product with exponential — \(f(x) = g(x) e^{ax}\); apply the product rule to find critical numbers.
  • Logistic-style optima — many real models combine growth and resource limits, yielding a single interior optimum.
  • Sign of \(e^{ax}\) — always positive, so sign of \(f'\) is governed by the polynomial factor.
  • Justification — \(f''\) of products with exponentials, or first-derivative sign change.

Worked Example: Find the absolute maximum of \(f(x) = x e^{-x^2/2}\) for \(x \ge 0\).

Product rule: \(f'(x) = e^{-x^2/2} + x \cdot e^{-x^2/2} \cdot (-x) = e^{-x^2/2}(1 - x^2)\).

Critical numbers: \(1 - x^2 = 0\), so \(x = 1\) (only positive value). \(f''\) sign or quick check: \(f'\) goes from positive (when \(x<1\)) to negative (when \(x>1\)), so \(x=1\) is a maximum.

\(f(1) = e^{-1/2} = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{e}} \approx 0.607\). Boundary check: \(f(0) = 0\) and \(\lim_{x\to\infty}f(x)=0\); so \(x=1\) gives the absolute max.

Worked Example: A drug's blood concentration is \(C(t) = 5te^{-0.5t}\) (mg/L, \(t\) in hours). When does peak concentration occur, and what is it?

\(C'(t) = 5e^{-0.5t} + 5t(-0.5)e^{-0.5t} = 5e^{-0.5t}(1 - 0.5t)\).

\(C'(t) = 0\) when \(1 - 0.5t = 0\), i.e., \(t = 2\) hours. \(C(2) = 5(2)e^{-1} = \tfrac{10}{e} \approx 3.68\) mg/L. Peak concentration is about 3.68 mg/L at \(t=2\) h.

  • Setting \(e^{ax} = 0\) — exponentials are never zero; they cannot contribute to critical numbers.
  • Forgetting the product rule when differentiating \(x e^{ax}\); both factors depend on \(x\).
  • Reporting a critical \(x\)-value without the corresponding function value — for context the value is more informative.
  • Ignoring the domain restriction (\(t \ge 0\), \(x \in [0, T]\)); some critical numbers may fall outside.

Topic 5.4 introduces the derivatives of the two foundational trigonometric functions: \[\frac{d}{dx}[\sin x] = \cos x, \qquad \frac{d}{dx}[\cos x] = -\sin x.\] These are proven from first principles using the limit identities \(\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{\sin h}{h} = 1\) and \(\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{\cos h - 1}{h} = 0\), combined with the angle-addition formulas for \(\sin(x+h)\) and \(\cos(x+h)\). Both proofs require \(x\) and \(h\) to be in radians; the formulas fail if \(x\) is in degrees (you'd need an extra \(\frac{\pi}{180}\) factor).

With these two rules and the chain rule, you can differentiate composite trig expressions: \(\frac{d}{dx}[\sin(g(x))] = \cos(g(x))\cdot g'(x)\), and similarly for cosine. A common pattern is \(\sin(\omega x)\), whose derivative is \(\omega \cos(\omega x)\) — the frequency parameter \(\omega\) gets multiplied out by the chain rule.

The sign asymmetry is striking: \(\sin\) differentiates to \(\cos\) (no sign change), but \(\cos\) differentiates to \(-\sin\). Iterating, the derivative pattern of \(\sin x\) cycles \(\sin x \to \cos x \to -\sin x \to -\cos x \to \sin x\), returning every four derivatives. This pattern is the algebraic shadow of the geometric fact that sine and cosine are circular functions: differentiation rotates by a quarter turn.

  • Sine derivative — \(\frac{d}{dx}[\sin x] = \cos x\) (radians).
  • Cosine derivative — \(\frac{d}{dx}[\cos x] = -\sin x\); note the minus sign.
  • Radians required — formulas fail in degree mode; convert if necessary.
  • Chain rule — \(\frac{d}{dx}[\sin(g(x))] = \cos(g(x))\cdot g'(x)\).
  • Higher derivatives cycle — \(\sin^{(n)}x\) follows a period-4 pattern.

Worked Example: Differentiate \(y = \sin(3x^2)\).

Chain rule with \(g(x) = 3x^2\): \(g'(x) = 6x\). Then \(y' = \cos(3x^2)\cdot 6x = 6x\cos(3x^2)\).

Worked Example: Differentiate \(y = x^2 \cos x\) and find \(y'\) at \(x = \tfrac{\pi}{2}\).

Product rule: \(y' = 2x\cos x + x^2(-\sin x) = 2x\cos x - x^2 \sin x\).

At \(x = \pi/2\): \(\cos(\pi/2) = 0\), \(\sin(\pi/2) = 1\). So \(y'(\pi/2) = 2(\pi/2)(0) - (\pi/2)^2(1) = -\pi^2/4\).

  • Forgetting the minus sign in \(\frac{d}{dx}[\cos x] = -\sin x\) — this is the single most common error.
  • Working in degrees instead of radians; the formulas hold only in radian mode.
  • Missing the inner derivative in chain rule: \(\frac{d}{dx}[\sin(3x)] = 3\cos(3x)\), not \(\cos(3x)\).
  • Confusing \(\sin^2 x = (\sin x)^2\) with \(\sin(x^2)\); their derivatives are completely different.

Topic 5.5 derives the remaining basic trig derivatives by rewriting them in terms of \(\sin\) and \(\cos\) and applying the quotient rule. Starting with \(\tan x = \frac{\sin x}{\cos x}\): \[\frac{d}{dx}[\tan x] = \frac{\cos x \cdot \cos x - \sin x \cdot (-\sin x)}{\cos^2 x} = \frac{\cos^2 x + \sin^2 x}{\cos^2 x} = \frac{1}{\cos^2 x} = \sec^2 x.\] So \(\frac{d}{dx}[\tan x] = \sec^2 x\). Similarly, \(\cot x = \frac{\cos x}{\sin x}\) yields \(\frac{d}{dx}[\cot x] = -\csc^2 x\).

By the same method, the secant and cosecant derivatives are \(\frac{d}{dx}[\sec x] = \sec x \tan x\) and \(\frac{d}{dx}[\csc x] = -\csc x \cot x\). The pattern is: co-functions (cosine, cotangent, cosecant) pick up a minus sign when differentiated. With the chain rule, all extend: \(\frac{d}{dx}[\tan(g(x))] = \sec^2(g(x))\cdot g'(x)\), and so on.

Trigonometric derivatives behave well except at the points where the original function blows up (where \(\cos x = 0\) for tan/sec, or \(\sin x = 0\) for cot/csc). At those points the function and its derivative are both undefined. Knowing the domain restrictions is essential when finding critical numbers or testing for extrema in trig contexts.

  • Tangent derivative — \(\frac{d}{dx}[\tan x] = \sec^2 x\); always non-negative where defined.
  • Cotangent derivative — \(\frac{d}{dx}[\cot x] = -\csc^2 x\); always non-positive where defined.
  • Secant derivative — \(\frac{d}{dx}[\sec x] = \sec x \tan x\).
  • Cosecant derivative — \(\frac{d}{dx}[\csc x] = -\csc x \cot x\).
  • Domain caveats — \(\tan, \sec\) undefined at odd multiples of \(\pi/2\); \(\cot, \csc\) undefined at integer multiples of \(\pi\).

Worked Example: Differentiate \(y = \tan(x^3 + 2)\).

Chain rule with \(g(x) = x^3 + 2\): \(g'(x) = 3x^2\). So \(y' = \sec^2(x^3+2)\cdot 3x^2 = 3x^2 \sec^2(x^3 + 2)\).

Worked Example: Find the slope of \(y = \sec x\) at \(x = \tfrac{\pi}{3}\).

\(y' = \sec x \tan x\). At \(x = \pi/3\): \(\sec(\pi/3) = 2\) and \(\tan(\pi/3) = \sqrt{3}\). Slope = \(2\sqrt{3}\).

  • Forgetting the minus sign on co-functions: \(\frac{d}{dx}[\cot x] = -\csc^2 x\), not \(\csc^2 x\).
  • Writing \(\frac{d}{dx}[\tan^2 x] = \sec^2 x\) — this confuses \(\tan^2 x = (\tan x)^2\) with \(\tan x\). The correct derivative uses the chain rule: \(2\tan x \sec^2 x\).
  • Trying to evaluate \(\sec x\) or \(\tan x\) at \(x = \pi/2\) — both are undefined there.
  • Confusing \(\sec x\) (\(1/\cos x\)) with \(\arccos x\) or \(\cos^{-1} x\) (inverse cosine); they are different functions.

Unit 6: Introduction to Vectors

Vector representation, operations, R2 and R3, spanning sets

📚 Strand C: Vectors 📹 9 lessons
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

A scalar is a quantity that has only magnitude (temperature, mass, time). A vector has both magnitude and direction (displacement, velocity, force). Vectors are represented geometrically as directed arrows whose length encodes magnitude and whose orientation encodes direction. Algebraically, a vector in two dimensions can be written in component form \(\vec{u} = (u_1, u_2)\) and in three dimensions as \(\vec{v} = (v_1, v_2, v_3)\). Two vectors are equal if and only if they have the same magnitude and direction — position in space is irrelevant.

Vectors are added using the triangle (or parallelogram) rule: place the tail of \(\vec{v}\) at the head of \(\vec{u}\); the resultant \(\vec{u}+\vec{v}\) runs from the tail of \(\vec{u}\) to the head of \(\vec{v}\). In component form, addition is componentwise. Subtraction reverses one vector: \(\vec{u} - \vec{v} = \vec{u} + (-\vec{v})\). Scalar multiplication by \(k\) scales magnitude by \(|k|\) and reverses direction if \(k<0\); collinear vectors are exactly those that are scalar multiples of each other.

A linear combination of vectors \(\vec{v}_1, \dots, \vec{v}_n\) is any expression \(c_1\vec{v}_1 + \dots + c_n\vec{v}_n\). The set of all such combinations is the spanning set. In \(\mathbb{R}^2\), any two non-collinear vectors span the whole plane; in \(\mathbb{R}^3\), any three non-coplanar vectors span the whole space. The Cartesian unit vectors \(\hat{i} = (1,0,0)\), \(\hat{j} = (0,1,0)\), \(\hat{k} = (0,0,1)\) are the standard basis: every vector in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) has a unique expression as \(a\hat{i} + b\hat{j} + c\hat{k}\).

  • Scalar — a real number; magnitude only.
  • Vector — a directed quantity with magnitude and direction; written \(\vec{u}\) or in bold.
  • Magnitude — \(|\vec{u}| = \sqrt{u_1^2 + u_2^2 + u_3^2}\) in \(\mathbb{R}^3\); the length of the arrow.
  • Zero vector — \(\vec{0}\); has magnitude 0 and undefined direction; identity for addition.
  • Unit vector — vector with magnitude 1; \(\hat{u} = \frac{\vec{u}}{|\vec{u}|}\).
  • Collinear vectors — vectors parallel to the same line; one is a scalar multiple of the other.
  • Linear combination — \(c_1\vec{v}_1 + c_2\vec{v}_2 + \dots + c_n\vec{v}_n\) for scalars \(c_i\).
  • Spanning set — a set of vectors whose linear combinations produce every vector in the given space.
  • Cartesian (standard) basis — \(\hat{i}, \hat{j}, \hat{k}\) in \(\mathbb{R}^3\); every vector decomposes uniquely.

Worked Example: Vector arithmetic in \(\mathbb{R}^3\)

Given \(\vec{u} = (2,-1,3)\) and \(\vec{v} = (1,4,-2)\), compute \(3\vec{u} - 2\vec{v}\) and its magnitude.

Scalar multiples: \(3\vec{u} = (6,-3,9)\); \(2\vec{v} = (2,8,-4)\).

Subtract componentwise: \(3\vec{u} - 2\vec{v} = (6-2, -3-8, 9-(-4)) = (4, -11, 13)\).

Magnitude: \(|3\vec{u} - 2\vec{v}| = \sqrt{4^2 + (-11)^2 + 13^2} = \sqrt{16 + 121 + 169} = \sqrt{306} \approx 17.49\).

Worked Example: Expressing a vector as a linear combination

Write \(\vec{w} = (7,4)\) as a linear combination of \(\vec{a} = (1,2)\) and \(\vec{b} = (3,-1)\).

Set \(\vec{w} = s\vec{a} + t\vec{b}\): \((7,4) = (s + 3t,\; 2s - t)\). System: \(s + 3t = 7\) and \(2s - t = 4\).

From the second equation \(t = 2s - 4\). Substitute: \(s + 3(2s-4) = 7 \implies 7s - 12 = 7 \implies s = \frac{19}{7}\). Then \(t = 2(\tfrac{19}{7}) - 4 = \tfrac{38}{7} - \tfrac{28}{7} = \tfrac{10}{7}\).

So \(\vec{w} = \tfrac{19}{7}\vec{a} + \tfrac{10}{7}\vec{b}\). Verify: \(\tfrac{19}{7}(1,2) + \tfrac{10}{7}(3,-1) = (\tfrac{19+30}{7}, \tfrac{38-10}{7}) = (7, 4)\). ✓

  • Treating a vector as a point — vectors do not have a fixed location; only magnitude and direction matter.
  • Adding magnitudes of vectors instead of components: \(|\vec{u}+\vec{v}| \ne |\vec{u}| + |\vec{v}|\) in general.
  • Forgetting that the zero vector is parallel to every vector by convention (some texts disagree, but in Ontario MCV4U \(\vec{0}\) is taken to be parallel to all).
  • Confusing notation: \((2,-1,3)\) and \(2\hat{i} - \hat{j} + 3\hat{k}\) are the same vector written two ways.
  • Saying two vectors with the same magnitude are equal — direction must match too.

Also C1.3 — "represent a vector in two-space geometrically as a directed line segment, with directions expressed in different ways … and algebraically (e.g., using Cartesian coordinates)." And C2.2 — "perform the operations of addition, subtraction, and scalar multiplication on vectors represented as directed line segments in two-space, and on vectors represented in Cartesian form in two-space."

Unit 7: Vector Applications

Forces, dot product, cross product, projections

📚 Strand C: Vectors 📹 8 lessons
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

This unit equips vectors with two multiplication operations that have no analogue in elementary algebra: the dot product and the cross product. The dot product takes two vectors and returns a scalar; the cross product takes two vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) and returns a vector perpendicular to both. Each carries deep geometric meaning, and together they let calculus describe rigid-body physics, illumination, computer graphics, and 3D geometry rigorously.

The dot product is defined either algebraically as \(\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v} = u_1v_1 + u_2v_2 + u_3v_3\) or geometrically as \(\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v} = |\vec{u}||\vec{v}|\cos\theta\). Equating these gives the cosine angle formula \(\cos\theta = \frac{\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v}}{|\vec{u}||\vec{v}|}\). Two non-zero vectors are perpendicular precisely when their dot product is zero. The dot product also computes work: if a constant force \(\vec{F}\) moves an object through displacement \(\vec{d}\), the work is \(W = \vec{F}\cdot\vec{d}\).

The cross product \(\vec{u}\times\vec{v}\) returns a vector perpendicular to both \(\vec{u}\) and \(\vec{v}\), with magnitude \(|\vec{u}\times\vec{v}| = |\vec{u}||\vec{v}|\sin\theta\) — which equals the area of the parallelogram they span. The direction is given by the right-hand rule. Importantly, \(\vec{u}\times\vec{v} = -\vec{v}\times\vec{u}\): the cross product is anti-commutative. Projections decompose one vector along the direction of another: the scalar projection of \(\vec{u}\) on \(\vec{v}\) is \(\frac{\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v}}{|\vec{v}|}\), and the vector projection is that scalar times the unit vector \(\hat{v}\).

  • Dot product (scalar product) — \(\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v} = |\vec{u}||\vec{v}|\cos\theta = u_1v_1 + u_2v_2 + u_3v_3\).
  • Cross product (vector product) — \(\vec{u}\times\vec{v}\) is the vector perpendicular to both \(\vec{u}\) and \(\vec{v}\) with magnitude \(|\vec{u}||\vec{v}|\sin\theta\) and direction by the right-hand rule.
  • Orthogonal vectors — \(\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v} = 0\) for non-zero \(\vec{u}, \vec{v}\).
  • Scalar projection of \(\vec{u}\) onto \(\vec{v}\) — \(\operatorname{proj}_{\vec{v}}\vec{u}\) magnitude is \(\frac{\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v}}{|\vec{v}|}\).
  • Vector projection of \(\vec{u}\) onto \(\vec{v}\) — \(\left(\frac{\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v}}{|\vec{v}|^2}\right)\vec{v}\).
  • Work — \(W = \vec{F}\cdot\vec{d}\), measured in joules when \(\vec{F}\) is in newtons and \(\vec{d}\) is in metres.
  • Torque — \(\vec{\tau} = \vec{r}\times\vec{F}\); a vector perpendicular to both the lever arm \(\vec{r}\) and the applied force \(\vec{F}\).
  • Triple scalar product — \(\vec{u}\cdot(\vec{v}\times\vec{w})\); its absolute value is the volume of the parallelepiped spanned by the three vectors.

Worked Example: Angle between vectors

Find the angle between \(\vec{u} = (2, 1, -2)\) and \(\vec{v} = (1, 3, 1)\).

Dot product: \(\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v} = (2)(1) + (1)(3) + (-2)(1) = 2 + 3 - 2 = 3\).

Magnitudes: \(|\vec{u}| = \sqrt{4+1+4} = 3\); \(|\vec{v}| = \sqrt{1+9+1} = \sqrt{11}\).

Cosine: \(\cos\theta = \frac{3}{3\sqrt{11}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{11}}\). So \(\theta = \cos^{-1}\!\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{11}}\right) \approx 72.5^\circ\).

Worked Example: Cross product and area

For \(\vec{a} = (1, 2, 3)\) and \(\vec{b} = (4, 5, 6)\), compute \(\vec{a}\times\vec{b}\) and the area of the parallelogram they span.

Use the determinant formula: \[\vec{a}\times\vec{b} = \begin{vmatrix} \hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k} \\ 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \end{vmatrix}.\]

Expand: \(\hat{i}\bigl((2)(6)-(3)(5)\bigr) - \hat{j}\bigl((1)(6)-(3)(4)\bigr) + \hat{k}\bigl((1)(5)-(2)(4)\bigr)\) \(= \hat{i}(12-15) - \hat{j}(6-12) + \hat{k}(5-8) = -3\hat{i} + 6\hat{j} - 3\hat{k} = (-3, 6, -3)\).

Area: \(|\vec{a}\times\vec{b}| = \sqrt{9+36+9} = \sqrt{54} = 3\sqrt{6} \approx 7.35\) square units.

Verify perpendicularity: \(\vec{a}\cdot(-3,6,-3) = -3+12-9 = 0\) ✓; \(\vec{b}\cdot(-3,6,-3) = -12+30-18 = 0\) ✓.

  • Confusing dot product (returns a scalar) with cross product (returns a vector).
  • Forgetting that the cross product is only defined in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) — there is no cross product of two vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^2\).
  • Mis-signing the middle term in the determinant expansion: the \(\hat{j}\) component carries a minus sign.
  • Dropping the \(|\vec{v}|^2\) (not \(|\vec{v}|\)) in the denominator of the vector projection formula.
  • Treating \(\vec{u}\times\vec{v} = \vec{v}\times\vec{u}\) — the cross product is anti-commutative; sign matters.
  • Using the cross product to find the angle — use the dot product instead, since \(\sin\theta\) has two solutions in \([0,180^\circ]\) while \(\cos\theta\) is unique.

Unit 8: Lines and Planes

Equations of lines in 2D/3D, equations of planes

📚 Strand C: Vectors 📹 7 lessons
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

In Grade 10 we wrote lines as \(y = mx + b\). That representation works in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) but breaks down in \(\mathbb{R}^3\), where a single linear equation in \(x, y, z\) describes a plane, not a line. Vector geometry solves this. A line is uniquely determined by a point \(P_0\) on it and a direction vector \(\vec{d}\); its vector equation is \(\vec{r} = \vec{r}_0 + t\vec{d}\), where \(t\) is a real parameter. Splitting into components gives the parametric equations, and (in \(\mathbb{R}^3\)) eliminating \(t\) gives the symmetric equations \(\frac{x-x_0}{d_1} = \frac{y-y_0}{d_2} = \frac{z-z_0}{d_3}\), valid only where the direction components are non-zero.

A plane in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) is uniquely determined by a point and a normal vector \(\vec{n}\) (a vector perpendicular to the plane). The scalar (Cartesian) equation of the plane is \(ax + by + cz + d = 0\), where \((a,b,c)\) are the components of \(\vec{n}\) and \(d = -\vec{n}\cdot\vec{r}_0\). Alternatively, a plane can be specified by two direction vectors lying within it, giving the vector equation \(\vec{r} = \vec{r}_0 + s\vec{a} + t\vec{b}\). To convert between the two forms, the normal is \(\vec{n} = \vec{a}\times\vec{b}\).

The power of these representations is that they handle parallelism, perpendicularity, and intersections uniformly. Two lines are parallel iff their direction vectors are scalar multiples; two planes are parallel iff their normals are; a line is parallel to a plane iff its direction is perpendicular to the plane's normal (\(\vec{d}\cdot\vec{n} = 0\)).

  • Direction vector — a non-zero vector \(\vec{d}\) parallel to a line; any non-zero scalar multiple is also a direction vector.
  • Vector equation of a line — \(\vec{r} = \vec{r}_0 + t\vec{d}\), \(t \in \mathbb{R}\).
  • Parametric equations — \(x = x_0 + t d_1\), \(y = y_0 + t d_2\), \(z = z_0 + t d_3\).
  • Symmetric equations (\(\mathbb{R}^3\), nonzero direction components) — \(\frac{x-x_0}{d_1} = \frac{y-y_0}{d_2} = \frac{z-z_0}{d_3}\).
  • Normal vector to a plane — vector perpendicular to every direction in the plane.
  • Scalar equation of a plane — \(ax + by + cz + d = 0\), where \(\vec{n} = (a,b,c)\) is normal.
  • Vector equation of a plane — \(\vec{r} = \vec{r}_0 + s\vec{a} + t\vec{b}\) for non-collinear \(\vec{a}, \vec{b}\) in the plane.
  • Skew lines — lines in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) that are neither parallel nor intersecting.

Worked Example: All three forms of a line in \(\mathbb{R}^3\)

Find vector, parametric, and symmetric equations for the line through \(P(2, -1, 4)\) and \(Q(5, 3, -2)\).

Direction vector: \(\vec{d} = \vec{PQ} = (5-2,\, 3-(-1),\, -2-4) = (3, 4, -6)\).

Vector form: \(\vec{r} = (2,-1,4) + t(3,4,-6)\).

Parametric form: \(x = 2 + 3t\), \(y = -1 + 4t\), \(z = 4 - 6t\).

Symmetric form: \(\frac{x-2}{3} = \frac{y+1}{4} = \frac{z-4}{-6}\).

Check that \(Q\) lies on the line: \(t = 1\) gives \((2+3, -1+4, 4-6) = (5, 3, -2)\) ✓.

Worked Example: Scalar equation of a plane from three points

Find the scalar equation of the plane through \(A(1,2,3)\), \(B(2,0,1)\), \(C(0,1,4)\).

Two direction vectors in the plane: \(\vec{AB} = (1,-2,-2)\) and \(\vec{AC} = (-1,-1,1)\).

Normal vector: \(\vec{n} = \vec{AB}\times\vec{AC}\). Compute the determinant: \[\vec{n} = \begin{vmatrix} \hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k} \\ 1 & -2 & -2 \\ -1 & -1 & 1 \end{vmatrix} = \hat{i}((-2)(1)-(-2)(-1)) - \hat{j}((1)(1)-(-2)(-1)) + \hat{k}((1)(-1)-(-2)(-1)).\]

Evaluate: \(\hat{i}(-2-2) - \hat{j}(1-2) + \hat{k}(-1-2) = -4\hat{i} + \hat{j} - 3\hat{k} = (-4, 1, -3)\).

Use point \(A\): \(-4(x-1) + 1(y-2) - 3(z-3) = 0 \implies -4x + 4 + y - 2 - 3z + 9 = 0 \implies -4x + y - 3z + 11 = 0\).

Verify with \(B\): \(-4(2) + 0 - 3(1) + 11 = -8 + 0 - 3 + 11 = 0\) ✓.

  • Writing a single linear equation in \(x, y, z\) and calling it a line — in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) that is a plane.
  • Forgetting that direction vectors and normal vectors are not unique — any non-zero scalar multiple works.
  • Using \(y = mx + b\) form in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) — slope-intercept does not generalize.
  • Writing symmetric equations when a direction component is zero — the corresponding fraction is undefined and that component must be treated separately.
  • Mixing up "parallel to the plane" (\(\vec{d}\cdot\vec{n} = 0\)) with "perpendicular to the plane" (\(\vec{d}\) parallel to \(\vec{n}\)).

Also C4.2 — "determine, through investigation with technology … and without technology, the equation of a line in two-space, given the slope and a point on the line, two points on the line, the equation of a line parallel to the desired line and a point on the desired line, or the equation of a line perpendicular to the desired line and a point on the desired line." And C4.3 — "recognize that the solution points \((x, y, z)\) in three-space of a single linear equation in three variables form a plane and that the solution points \((x, y, z)\) of a system of two linear equations in three variables form the line of intersection of two planes, if the planes are not parallel."

Unit 9: Intersections

Intersection of lines, planes, distances, matrices

📚 Strand C: Vectors 📹 9 lessons
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Geometric intersection problems become algebraic systems once the objects are expressed in vector or scalar form. Two lines in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) can be coincident (same line), parallel and distinct (no intersection, same direction up to scalar), intersecting (one common point), or skew (no intersection, non-parallel directions). The first three cases also occur for two planes — but planes can never be skew. Three planes give richer possibilities: one common point, a common line, no intersection, or coincidence.

To intersect a line and a plane, substitute the parametric line equations into the scalar plane equation and solve for \(t\). One solution gives a single intersection point; no solution means the line is parallel to (and outside) the plane; infinitely many means the line lies in the plane. To intersect two planes, treat the system as two equations in three unknowns; the solution is one parameter family — a line. To intersect three planes, solve the \(3\times 3\) system, often using row reduction on an augmented matrix.

Distances are computed using projections. The distance from a point \(Q\) to a line with direction \(\vec{d}\) and point \(P_0\) is the magnitude of the component of \(\vec{P_0Q}\) perpendicular to \(\vec{d}\); compactly, \(\frac{|\vec{P_0Q}\times\vec{d}|}{|\vec{d}|}\). The distance from a point \(Q(x_1, y_1, z_1)\) to a plane \(ax + by + cz + d = 0\) is \[\frac{|ax_1 + by_1 + cz_1 + d|}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2+c^2}}.\]These formulas pack the entire chapter's geometry into clean one-line expressions.

  • Coincident lines/planes — same object, infinitely many points in common.
  • Skew lines — lines in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) that are non-parallel and non-intersecting.
  • Consistent system — system with at least one solution.
  • Inconsistent system — system with no solution (contradictory equations).
  • Augmented matrix — matrix representation of a linear system; coefficients on the left, constants on the right.
  • Elementary row operations — swap two rows, multiply a row by a non-zero scalar, add a multiple of one row to another. They preserve the solution set.
  • Row-echelon form — staircase pattern of leading entries; allows solving by back-substitution.
  • Distance point-to-plane — \(\displaystyle d = \frac{|ax_1 + by_1 + cz_1 + d|}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2+c^2}}\).
  • Distance point-to-line (\(\mathbb{R}^3\)) — \(\displaystyle d = \frac{|\vec{P_0Q}\times\vec{d}|}{|\vec{d}|}\).

Worked Example: Intersection of a line and a plane

Line: \(\vec{r} = (1, 0, 2) + t(2, -1, 3)\). Plane: \(x + 2y - z + 4 = 0\). Find the point of intersection.

Parametric line: \(x = 1+2t\), \(y = -t\), \(z = 2 + 3t\).

Substitute into the plane: \((1+2t) + 2(-t) - (2+3t) + 4 = 0\).

Simplify: \(1 + 2t - 2t - 2 - 3t + 4 = 0 \implies 3 - 3t = 0 \implies t = 1\).

Intersection point: \((x,y,z) = (1+2, -1, 2+3) = (3, -1, 5)\). Verify in the plane: \(3 + 2(-1) - 5 + 4 = 0\) ✓.

Worked Example: Distance from a point to a plane

Find the distance from \(Q(2, -1, 3)\) to the plane \(2x - y + 2z - 5 = 0\).

Apply the formula with \((a,b,c,d) = (2,-1,2,-5)\) and \((x_1, y_1, z_1) = (2,-1,3)\):

Numerator: \(|2(2) + (-1)(-1) + 2(3) - 5| = |4 + 1 + 6 - 5| = 6\).

Denominator: \(\sqrt{2^2 + (-1)^2 + 2^2} = \sqrt{9} = 3\).

Distance: \(\frac{6}{3} = 2\) units.

Worked Example: Three-plane system via row reduction

Solve \(\;\;\Pi_1\!:\, x + y + z = 6,\;\; \Pi_2\!:\, 2x - y + z = 3,\;\; \Pi_3\!:\, x + 2y - z = 2\).

Augmented matrix: \[\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 & 6 \\ 2 & -1 & 1 & 3 \\ 1 & 2 & -1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}.\]

\(R_2 \to R_2 - 2R_1\) and \(R_3 \to R_3 - R_1\): \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 & 6 \\ 0 & -3 & -1 & -9 \\ 0 & 1 & -2 & -4 \end{bmatrix}\).

\(R_3 \to 3R_3 + R_2\): \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 & 6 \\ 0 & -3 & -1 & -9 \\ 0 & 0 & -7 & -21 \end{bmatrix}\). Back-substitute: \(-7z = -21 \implies z = 3\); \(-3y - 3 = -9 \implies y = 2\); \(x + 2 + 3 = 6 \implies x = 1\).

Unique solution \((1, 2, 3)\) — the three planes meet at exactly one point.

  • Claiming "no intersection" when the system actually has infinitely many solutions (the line lies in the plane or two planes are coincident).
  • Forgetting absolute value in the point-to-plane distance formula — distance is non-negative.
  • Substituting numerical values for parameters before solving the system, then losing the parameter family description.
  • Using the same parameter \(t\) for two different lines when checking intersection — use \(s\) for one and \(t\) for the other.
  • Confusing "no solution" (parallel/inconsistent) with "infinitely many solutions" (dependent/coincident) when interpreting row-reduced systems.
