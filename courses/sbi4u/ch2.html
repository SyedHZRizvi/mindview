🌿 Photosynthesis
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Photosynthesis is the process by which autotrophs convert light energy into chemical energy stored in glucose. The overall equation \(6CO_2 + 6H_2O + \text{light} \rightarrow C_6H_{12}O_6 + 6O_2\) hides considerable complexity. In plants, photosynthesis takes place in the chloroplast, an organelle with a double membrane and an internal system of thylakoid sacs stacked into grana. The light-dependent reactions happen on the thylakoid membrane; the Calvin cycle (light-independent) happens in the surrounding fluid called the stroma. This compartmentalisation is essential — it lets the cell maintain a proton gradient across the thylakoid membrane that drives ATP synthesis.

Light-dependent reactions begin when chlorophyll a in photosystem II (PSII, P680) absorbs a photon and is oxidised. Water is split by the oxygen-evolving complex (\(2H_2O \rightarrow 4H^+ + 4e^- + O_2\)) — the source of all atmospheric oxygen. Excited electrons travel through plastoquinone, the cytochrome b₆f complex, plastocyanin, then to photosystem I (PSI, P700), which re-energises them. The final acceptor is NADP⁺, reduced to NADPH by ferredoxin and NADP⁺ reductase. Along the way, protons are pumped into the thylakoid lumen, building the gradient that ATP synthase uses to make ATP from ADP + Pᵢ (chemiosmosis). The net products are ATP, NADPH, and \(O_2\) — the first two power the Calvin cycle, the third is released as a "waste" product.

Wavelength matters: chlorophyll a absorbs maximally in the blue (~430 nm) and red (~660 nm) regions and reflects green, which is why leaves appear green. Accessory pigments (chlorophyll b, carotenoids, xanthophylls) broaden the absorption spectrum and protect chlorophyll from oxidative damage. An action spectrum overlaid on an absorption spectrum shows that photosynthetic rate closely tracks pigment absorption — strong historical evidence (Engelmann, 1882) that pigments drive photosynthesis.

  • Thylakoid / granum / stroma — flattened sac / stack of thylakoids / fluid matrix of the chloroplast.
  • Photosystem II (P680) / Photosystem I (P700) — light-harvesting complexes.
  • Electron transport chain (ETC) — series of carriers (plastoquinone, cyt b₆f, plastocyanin, ferredoxin) that move electrons and pump \(H^+\).
  • Chemiosmosis — ATP production driven by \(H^+\) flowing through ATP synthase down its gradient.
  • NADPH — electron carrier supplying reducing power to the Calvin cycle.
  • Absorption vs action spectrum — wavelengths absorbed vs wavelengths driving photosynthesis.

Worked example — Tracing oxygen isotopes

An experiment uses \(H_2{}^{18}O\) and \(C{}^{16}O_2\) to feed a plant. Predict whether the released \(O_2\) carries the \({}^{18}O\) or the \({}^{16}O\) label, and explain.

The oxygen released comes from the splitting of water at PSII, not from \(CO_2\). Therefore the released \(O_2\) is labelled \({}^{18}O_2\). This Ruben and Kamen experiment (1941) established that water is the source of photosynthetic oxygen.

  • Saying "\(O_2\) comes from \(CO_2\)" — \(O_2\) comes from the splitting of \(H_2O\) at PSII.
  • Confusing the order: PSII fires first (despite the number), then PSI.
  • Locating the Calvin cycle in the thylakoid membrane — it occurs in the stroma.
  • Forgetting that ATP synthase is powered by the \(H^+\) gradient (chemiosmosis), not directly by electrons.

Curriculum expectation: Strand C, Expectation C3.2 — "explain the chemical changes and energy conversions that occur in metabolic processes related to photosynthesis (e.g., the light reactions and Calvin cycle)."

🌱 Calvin Cycle (Light-Independent Reactions)
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The Calvin cycle (also called the C₃ cycle or carbon fixation pathway) takes place in the stroma of the chloroplast and converts the ATP and NADPH generated by the light reactions into a stable carbohydrate. It is called "light-independent" because it does not directly require photons, but it is wholly dependent on the products of the light reactions and effectively stops in the dark. The cycle has three phases — carbon fixation, reduction, and regeneration of RuBP — and turns three times to produce one molecule of glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate (G3P), the immediate sugar product. Six turns are required to make a hexose like glucose.

In the carbon fixation phase, the enzyme RuBisCO (ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate carboxylase/oxygenase) attaches \(CO_2\) to the 5-carbon sugar RuBP. The unstable 6-carbon intermediate splits immediately into two molecules of 3-phosphoglycerate (3-PGA). RuBisCO is the most abundant protein on Earth, but it is also slow (3 reactions per second) and prone to grab \(O_2\) instead of \(CO_2\) — a wasteful side reaction called photorespiration. In the reduction phase, each 3-PGA is phosphorylated by ATP (giving 1,3-BPG) and reduced by NADPH to G3P. Some G3P leaves the cycle to make glucose; most re-enters the cycle. In the regeneration phase, ATP-driven reshuffling rebuilds RuBP from five G3P molecules so the cycle can continue.

The energy bookkeeping for one G3P is precise: 3 \(CO_2\) in, 9 ATP and 6 NADPH consumed, 1 G3P out (plus regenerated RuBP). For one glucose (2 G3P), that is 18 ATP and 12 NADPH. \(C_4\) plants (corn, sugarcane) and CAM plants (cacti, pineapples) have evolved mechanisms to concentrate \(CO_2\) around RuBisCO and suppress photorespiration in hot, dry climates.

  • RuBP (ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate) — 5-carbon \(CO_2\) acceptor.
  • RuBisCO — enzyme that fixes \(CO_2\) to RuBP.
  • 3-PGA / G3P — 3-carbon intermediates of the cycle; G3P is the net product.
  • Photorespiration — wasteful oxygenation by RuBisCO when \(O_2\) outcompetes \(CO_2\).
  • C₄ pathway / CAM — adaptations that concentrate \(CO_2\) before delivering it to the Calvin cycle.

Worked example — ATP and NADPH accounting

How many ATP and NADPH molecules are required to synthesise one molecule of glucose (\(C_6H_{12}O_6\)) via the Calvin cycle?

Glucose has 6 carbons, so 6 turns of the cycle (one \(CO_2\) fixed per turn) are needed, yielding 2 G3P. Each turn uses 3 ATP (2 in reduction, 1 in regeneration) and 2 NADPH (in reduction). Total: \(6 \times 3 = 18\) ATP and \(6 \times 2 = 12\) NADPH. The cycle also regenerates 6 RuBP, so RuBP is catalytic, not net consumed.

  • Calling the Calvin cycle the "dark reaction" — it can occur in light; it simply does not require photons directly.
  • Saying RuBisCO fixes \(O_2\) only — it fixes \(CO_2\) preferentially, but oxygenation occurs and is wasteful (photorespiration).
  • Reporting glucose as the direct product — G3P is the immediate product; glucose is built from two G3P downstream.
  • Forgetting that ATP is used in both reduction and regeneration phases.

Curriculum expectation: Strand C, Expectation C3.2 — "explain the chemical changes and energy conversions that occur in metabolic processes related to photosynthesis (e.g., the light reactions and Calvin cycle)."

🔋 Cellular Respiration
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Cellular respiration is the catabolic process that extracts chemical energy from glucose and stores it in ATP. The overall equation \(C_6H_{12}O_6 + 6O_2 \rightarrow 6CO_2 + 6H_2O + \text{ATP} + \text{heat}\) sums four stages: glycolysis, pyruvate oxidation, the Krebs cycle, and oxidative phosphorylation. Glycolysis is the only stage that occurs in the cytoplasm — it is universal across life and evolutionarily ancient, predating oxygenic conditions on Earth. The remaining three stages take place in the mitochondrion: pyruvate oxidation and the Krebs cycle in the mitochondrial matrix; the electron transport chain and ATP synthase on the inner mitochondrial membrane (folded into cristae to maximise surface area).

In glycolysis, one 6-carbon glucose is split into two 3-carbon pyruvates, generating 2 ATP net (4 made, 2 invested) and 2 NADH. Pyruvate oxidation (in the matrix) converts each pyruvate to acetyl-CoA, releasing \(CO_2\) and producing NADH. The two-carbon acetyl group then enters the Krebs cycle, which spins twice per glucose and yields, per turn, 3 NADH, 1 FADH₂, 1 ATP (or GTP), and 2 \(CO_2\). The real energy harvest happens in oxidative phosphorylation: NADH and FADH₂ deliver electrons to Complex I and Complex II of the inner-membrane electron transport chain. As electrons fall energetically through Complexes I → III → IV (via mobile carriers ubiquinone and cytochrome c), \(H^+\) ions are pumped from matrix to intermembrane space. The proton gradient drives ATP synthase to produce roughly 26 ATP. Oxygen is the terminal electron acceptor, combining with \(H^+\) to form water; without it, the chain backs up and the cycle stops.

If oxygen is absent, cells run fermentation to regenerate NAD⁺ so glycolysis can continue. Lactate fermentation (muscle, lactobacillus) reduces pyruvate to lactate; alcohol fermentation (yeast) releases \(CO_2\) and produces ethanol. Fermentation makes no additional ATP beyond glycolysis's 2 — a steep penalty for anaerobic life.

  • Substrate-level phosphorylation — direct ATP synthesis by transfer of a phosphate from a substrate.
  • Oxidative phosphorylation — ATP synthesis driven by the proton gradient established by the ETC.
  • NADH / FADH₂ — reduced electron carriers feeding the ETC.
  • Cristae / matrix / intermembrane space — mitochondrial compartments separated by inner and outer membranes.
  • Chemiosmosis — coupling of \(H^+\) flow through ATP synthase to ATP synthesis.
  • Fermentation — anaerobic regeneration of NAD⁺ from NADH.

Worked example — ATP yield per glucose

Using the conventional yields (2.5 ATP per NADH, 1.5 ATP per FADH₂), calculate the total ATP per glucose under aerobic conditions.

Glycolysis: 2 ATP + 2 NADH (≈ 5 ATP) = 7 ATP. Pyruvate oxidation: 2 NADH (≈ 5 ATP). Krebs cycle: 2 ATP + 6 NADH (≈ 15 ATP) + 2 FADH₂ (≈ 3 ATP) = 20 ATP. Grand total ≈ 7 + 5 + 20 = 32 ATP per glucose. (Older texts quote 36–38 if they assume 3 ATP/NADH and 2 ATP/FADH₂.)

  • Locating glycolysis in the mitochondrion — it occurs in the cytoplasm.
  • Forgetting that the Krebs cycle turns twice per glucose (one turn per pyruvate-derived acetyl-CoA).
  • Confusing oxidative phosphorylation (uses ETC and \(H^+\) gradient) with substrate-level phosphorylation (direct transfer).
  • Saying \(O_2\) is "used in the Krebs cycle" — \(O_2\) is consumed only at Complex IV as the final electron acceptor.

Curriculum expectation: Strand C, Expectation C3.1 — "explain the chemical changes and energy conversions that occur in metabolic processes related to cellular respiration (e.g., glycolysis, the Krebs cycle, electron transport, oxidative phosphorylation)."
