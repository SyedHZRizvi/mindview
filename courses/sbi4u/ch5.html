📈 Exponential & Logistic Population Growth
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

A population is a group of organisms of the same species living in a defined area and capable of interbreeding. Population ecology asks how and why populations change over time. Four processes drive change: births (natality, B), deaths (mortality, D), immigration (I), and emigration (E). The change in population size is \(\Delta N = (B + I) - (D + E)\). When resources are unlimited, populations grow exponentially: \(\frac{dN}{dt} = rN\), where \(r\) is the per-capita rate of increase. Integrated, this gives \(N_t = N_0 e^{rt}\) — a J-shaped curve that looks straight on a semi-log plot. Bacteria splitting every 20 minutes in a fresh nutrient broth, the early-stage colonisation of cleared land, and the first few decades of an invasive-species outbreak all approximate exponential growth.

Real environments have limits. Food, water, nesting sites, sunlight, predators, and disease impose a carrying capacity \(K\) — the maximum population the environment can sustain indefinitely. The logistic growth model adds this constraint: \(\frac{dN}{dt} = rN\left(\frac{K - N}{K}\right)\). When \(N\) is much smaller than \(K\), the term in brackets is near 1 and growth is nearly exponential. As \(N\) approaches \(K\), the brackets shrink toward zero and growth slows. If \(N\) exceeds \(K\), the term goes negative and the population declines. The resulting curve is S-shaped (sigmoidal).

Limiting factors are density-dependent (intensify as the population grows — competition for food, predation, infectious disease) or density-independent (act regardless of size — droughts, fires, hurricanes, cold snaps). Species also fall along an r-selected / K-selected continuum: r-strategists (mosquitoes, dandelions) produce many small offspring with little parental care and thrive in disturbed habitats; K-strategists (elephants, oak trees, humans) produce few large offspring with heavy investment and dominate stable habitats near \(K\). Population survivorship curves capture mortality patterns: Type I (low juvenile mortality, late-life decline, e.g., humans), Type II (constant mortality, e.g., many birds), Type III (high juvenile mortality, e.g., oysters, frogs).

  • Carrying capacity (\(K\)) — maximum sustainable population size.
  • Intrinsic rate of increase (\(r\)) — per-capita growth under ideal conditions.
  • Exponential vs logistic growth — unlimited (J-curve) vs limited (S-curve).
  • Density-dependent vs independent — strength scales with population size or not.
  • r-strategist / K-strategist — many cheap offspring vs few expensive offspring.
  • Survivorship curve — graphical depiction of cohort survival over age.

Worked example — Calculating exponential and logistic growth rates

A population of 1 000 deer has an intrinsic rate of increase \(r = 0.15\) yr⁻¹ and a carrying capacity \(K = 5\,000\). Compute (a) the instantaneous growth rate under exponential conditions and (b) under logistic conditions at the current size.

(a) \(\frac{dN}{dt} = rN = 0.15 \times 1000 = 150\) deer/yr. (b) \(\frac{dN}{dt} = rN\,\frac{K - N}{K} = 0.15 \times 1000 \times \frac{5000 - 1000}{5000} = 150 \times 0.8 = 120\) deer/yr. As \(N\) approaches \(K\), the logistic rate slows toward zero; at \(N = K\) the population is stable.

  • Reading a J-curve on a semi-log plot as linear growth — exponential growth is linear ONLY after a log transformation.
  • Confusing carrying capacity with the maximum ever observed value — \(K\) is the sustainable level.
  • Treating "r-selected" as inferior — both strategies are evolutionarily successful in their respective environments.
  • Forgetting that \(\frac{dN}{dt}\) can be negative if \(N > K\).

Curriculum expectation: Strand F, Expectation F3.1 — "describe the characteristics of populations and the factors that affect population growth (e.g., birth rate, death rate, immigration, emigration, carrying capacity, limiting factors)."

🦁 Community Ecology & Predator-Prey
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

A community is the assemblage of all populations that interact in a given area. Community ecology asks how these populations are connected and how their interactions shape diversity, distribution, and stability. The major interactions are competition (both species are harmed when they share a limiting resource), predation (one benefits, one is killed), herbivory (a special case of predation on plants), parasitism (one benefits, the host is harmed but usually not killed), mutualism (both benefit, e.g., bees and flowers, mycorrhizae and tree roots), and commensalism (one benefits, the other is unaffected).

Predator-prey dynamics often produce coupled oscillations. As prey numbers rise, predators have abundant food and reproduce; growing predator numbers depress prey; reduced prey leads to predator decline; predator decline allows prey to recover — and the cycle repeats. The Lotka–Volterra equations \(\frac{dN_{\text{prey}}}{dt} = rN_{\text{prey}} - aN_{\text{prey}}N_{\text{pred}}\) and \(\frac{dN_{\text{pred}}}{dt} = baN_{\text{prey}}N_{\text{pred}} - mN_{\text{pred}}\) model this with characteristic out-of-phase cycles. The classic Hudson's Bay Company fur-record data for snowshoe hare and Canada lynx, spanning over a century, shows ~10-year oscillations where lynx peaks lag hare peaks by roughly 1–2 years.

Competition has two flavours: interspecific (between species) and intraspecific (within a species, usually more intense because needs are identical). The competitive exclusion principle (Gause) states that two species cannot coexist indefinitely if they occupy identical ecological niches; one will out-compete the other. Coexistence is possible through resource partitioning, in which species subdivide the niche (different feeding times, depths, or prey sizes). MacArthur's classic study of five warbler species in spruce trees showed each species feeding in a different vertical zone, an example of partitioning. Keystone species exert disproportionately large effects on community structure relative to their abundance (sea otters maintain kelp forests by suppressing urchins); invasive species can collapse community structure by lacking natural enemies in their new range.

  • Ecological niche — total set of biotic and abiotic conditions a species uses.
  • Competitive exclusion — two species with identical niches cannot stably coexist.
  • Resource partitioning — niche subdivision allowing coexistence.
  • Predator-prey cycle — coupled oscillations with predator peaks lagging prey peaks.
  • Symbiosis types — mutualism (+/+), commensalism (+/0), parasitism (+/−).
  • Keystone species — species whose impact greatly exceeds its abundance.

Worked example — Interpreting a predator-prey graph

A graph shows snowshoe hare populations peaking in 1865, 1875, 1885, and 1895. Lynx populations peak in 1866, 1876, 1886, and 1896. Explain the phase relationship and predict what happens when the hare population crashes.

Lynx peaks lag hare peaks by ~1 year — classic predator-prey coupling. The lag arises because predator reproduction depends on abundant prey, and predator decline follows prey decline. When hares crash, lynx food supply collapses; some lynx starve, others fail to reproduce, and lynx numbers fall sharply within 1–2 years. Reduced predation then lets hares recover, restarting the cycle.

  • Saying predator-prey graphs are "in phase" — predator peaks lag prey peaks.
  • Confusing commensalism (+/0) with mutualism (+/+).
  • Forgetting that intraspecific competition is usually stronger than interspecific.
  • Treating every conspicuous predator as a keystone species — keystone status depends on disproportionate impact.

Curriculum expectation: Strand F, Expectation F3.2 — "describe the interactions among populations within a community (e.g., predator-prey relationships, competition, symbiosis) and explain their effect on population dynamics."

🌳 Ecological Succession
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Ecological succession is the directional, often predictable, change in community composition over time following disturbance or colonisation of new ground. Two main types are recognised. Primary succession begins on bare substrate where no soil exists — newly cooled lava flows, glacial moraines exposed by retreating ice, or bare rock revealed by landslides. The first colonisers, called pioneer species, are usually lichens and cyanobacteria that break down rock and add organic matter; mosses and small herbs follow as a thin soil forms; grasses and shrubs come next; finally, trees of increasing size establish until a mature climax community is reached. The timescale is centuries to millennia. Secondary succession begins on disturbed ground where soil already exists — after fire, logging, abandoned farmland, or flooding. Because soil and seed banks are present, recovery is much faster (decades to a few centuries) and starts with grasses and herbs rather than lichens.

Two contrasting views explain succession's directionality. The classic Clementsian view treats the community as a "superorganism" with a predictable, deterministic climax. The modern Gleasonian view emphasises individual species' responses to local conditions and stochastic processes, so multiple climax outcomes are possible. Mechanisms of replacement include facilitation (early species modify the environment in ways that favour later species — nitrogen-fixing alder enriches soil for spruce), tolerance (later species can establish under early ones without help or harm), and inhibition (early species block later ones until disturbance reopens the system).

Disturbance is not always negative; intermediate disturbance hypothesis (Connell) predicts maximum diversity at moderate disturbance frequencies — frequent enough to prevent competitive exclusion by dominant species, rare enough to allow late-successional species to mature. Fire-adapted ecosystems (jack pine forests, prairies) depend on periodic burns for regeneration; suppressing fire entirely allows fuel to build up and eventually produces catastrophic crown fires.

  • Primary succession — starts on bare rock or new substrate without soil.
  • Secondary succession — starts on disturbed ground where soil persists.
  • Pioneer species — first colonisers (lichens, mosses, hardy weeds).
  • Climax community — relatively stable end-stage assemblage.
  • Facilitation / tolerance / inhibition — mechanisms of species replacement.
  • Intermediate disturbance hypothesis — diversity peaks at moderate disturbance.

Worked example — Classifying a succession

A forest in Algonquin Park burns in a lightning fire. Over the next 80 years, fireweed and grasses dominate, then aspen and birch, then white pine and maple. Identify the succession type and the mechanism driving the transition from fireweed to trees.

Soil and root systems survived the fire, so this is secondary succession. Fireweed and grasses are pioneer-like species that stabilise soil and contribute organic matter — they facilitate aspen and birch by modifying nutrient and light conditions. As aspen/birch grow they shade the herbaceous layer, suppressing fireweed (an example of inhibition mixed with tolerance), and create conditions in which shade-tolerant pine and maple can establish. The white pine–maple stage approximates a climax community for the boreal-mixed-wood region.

  • Confusing primary with secondary succession — the key difference is the presence of soil.
  • Treating the climax community as eternal — disturbance resets succession repeatedly.
  • Forgetting that pioneer species are usually r-strategists and climax species are usually K-strategists.
  • Assuming succession always increases diversity linearly — diversity often peaks mid-succession.

Curriculum expectation: Strand F, Expectation F3.3 — "describe the process of ecological succession (e.g., primary and secondary succession, pioneer species, climax community)."

🌍 Human Population Growth & Environmental Impact
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Human population has grown from roughly 1 billion in 1800 to over 8 billion in 2023 — a 700% increase in roughly two centuries. Three factors drove this expansion: the agricultural Green Revolution (synthetic fertilisers, irrigation, high-yield crops, mechanisation) boosted food supply; advances in public health (sanitation, vaccination, antibiotics) cut mortality, especially child mortality; and the industrial revolution provided energy and economic capacity to support larger populations. Growth has not been spatially or temporally even. Most developed countries have completed the demographic transition, in which high birth and death rates (Stage 1) drop sequentially through declining death rates (Stage 2, rapid growth), declining birth rates (Stage 3, slowing growth), and finally low birth and death rates (Stage 4, near-stable population). Many lower-income countries are in Stage 2 or 3, contributing most of current global growth.

Two measures matter for environmental impact: total population and per-capita resource use. The ecological footprint estimates the productive land and water area required to supply an individual or population's consumption and absorb its waste. Canadian per-capita ecological footprints (around 8 global hectares) are roughly 4× the global average and 30× a typical low-income-country footprint. The IPAT identity \(I = P \times A \times T\) (Impact = Population × Affluence × Technology) emphasises that environmental impact depends on consumption and technology, not population alone. A small wealthy population can have a larger total footprint than a large poor one.

Human activity is now a dominant geological force — the proposed Anthropocene. Land conversion for agriculture and cities has reduced wild habitat; CO₂ emissions from fossil fuels are driving climate change; nitrogen and phosphorus overload from fertiliser runoff produces eutrophic dead zones; plastic pollution permeates oceans; the current extinction rate runs 100–1000× background. Sustainability requires reducing per-capita impact (efficiency, renewable energy, dietary shifts) alongside policies that voluntarily slow population growth through education and reproductive health access.

  • Demographic transition — four-stage shift from high birth/death to low birth/death rates.
  • Age structure pyramid — graphical age-and-sex distribution predicting growth or decline.
  • Doubling time — \(t_d \approx \frac{70}{r\,(\\%)}\) years for an exponentially growing population.
  • Ecological footprint — productive area required to sustain consumption levels.
  • IPAT — Impact = Population × Affluence × Technology framework.
  • Carrying capacity for humans — contested estimate (8–16 billion) depending on diet, technology, and equity.

Worked example — Doubling time calculation

A country has an annual population growth rate of 2.0%. Estimate how long it would take the population to double, and compare with a country growing at 0.5%/yr.

The rule of 70 (or "rule of 72") gives \(t_d \approx 70 / r\,(\\%)\). At 2.0% per year: \(t_d \approx 70 / 2.0 = 35\) years. At 0.5% per year: \(t_d \approx 70 / 0.5 = 140\) years. A four-fold difference in growth rate produces a four-fold difference in doubling time, illustrating how small changes in \(r\) compound enormously over decades.

  • Reading log-scale population graphs as linear — exponential curves appear linear ONLY after log transformation.
  • Equating population growth with environmental impact — affluence and technology are part of the IPAT equation.
  • Confusing the demographic transition's stages — Stage 2 is rapid growth from falling death rates; Stage 3 is slowing growth from falling birth rates.
  • Assuming Earth has a single, fixed human carrying capacity — \(K\) depends on diet, technology, equity, and what quality of life is accepted.

Curriculum expectation: Strand F, Expectation F2.4 — "analyse, on the basis of research, the effects of human activities on the population dynamics of a species or community (e.g., habitat destruction, climate change, introduction of invasive species), and propose possible solutions."
