🍁 4.1 What is "Canadian Literature"?
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

"Canadian literature" is a contested category. Margaret Atwood's 1972 study Survival argued that Canadian writing returns obsessively to the experience of victimhood and the struggle with a hostile landscape; Northrop Frye described the Canadian imagination as a "garrison mentality," small civilised settlements surrounded by an indifferent wilderness. Both descriptions are partial, both have been challenged, and both still circulate. ENG4U does not ask you to choose the correct definition; it asks you to read Canadian texts with awareness of the conversation about what "Canadian" means.

Three pressures complicate the category. Settler-colonial history means that "Canadian" literature begins inside a politics it cannot escape: whose land, whose language, whose stories count? Bilingualism means a Canadian literary canon written in English coexists with — and frequently ignores — a Canadian canon written in French. Indigenous literatures predate Canada itself and resist being absorbed into the settler category as if they were a regional flavour of it. Reading Canadian literature carefully means holding these tensions visible.

For ENG4U, the workable approach is thematic and formal. Themes recur: place and identity; the immigrant or settler arrival narrative; the body as site of political pressure (Atwood); the small-town and rural psyche (Munro); orality and trickster narrative (King); the failures of multicultural mythology. Forms repeat too: the short story has been disproportionately significant in CanLit, perhaps because of geography and economics that long made novels precarious.

Reading lists for the unit should hold breadth without pretending to representativeness. A defensible Grade 12 CanLit short-list might include Munro's "Boys and Girls" or "The Found Boat," Atwood's "The City Planners" and "Spelling," Thomas King's "Borders" and a passage from The Truth About Stories, Joseph Boyden or Eden Robinson for novel-length fiction, Dionne Brand or M. NourbeSe Philip for poetry, and Lawrence Hill or Esi Edugyan for the diasporic Black-Canadian voice. The list both inherits the canon and edits it — and the editing is itself a critical claim about what "Canadian literature" should mean now.

  • CanLit — informal shorthand for Canadian literature.
  • Garrison mentality — Northrop Frye's term for a settler culture circled against the wilderness.
  • Survival theme — Atwood's argument for a recurring Canadian preoccupation with endurance.
  • Settler-colonial — describing societies founded by colonisation that displaced Indigenous peoples.
  • Indigenous literatures — literatures of First Nations, Métis, and Inuit peoples; not a subset of CanLit.
  • Regionalism — writing rooted in a specific place (Maritimes, Prairies, North).
  • Multiculturalism — official Canadian policy (1971) and recurring literary subject.

Worked Example — Reading Place in a Canadian Opening

Compare two famous opening sentences of Canadian fiction:

Margaret Laurence, The Stone Angel (1964): "Above the town, on the hill brow, the stone angel used to stand."
Alice Munro, "Walker Brothers Cowboy" (1968): "After supper my father says, 'Want to go down and see if the Lake's still there?'"

Both anchor identity in place — Laurence's stone angel marks a settler town's effort to monumentalise itself against the prairie; Munro's matter-of-fact question (is the lake still there?) registers a child's sense that the world might be unstable enough to vanish. Both sentences also frame the protagonist as small relative to landscape: the angel "above" the town, the lake larger than memory. A Level 4 paragraph would argue that the recurring CanLit move of placing the human inside a landscape that exceeds them is not nature-writing nostalgia but a strategy for thinking about how settler identity is, even now, provisional.

  • Treating "Canadian" as a self-evident, uncontested category.
  • Folding Indigenous literatures into CanLit without acknowledging the political move.
  • Forgetting Québécois literature exists when discussing the national canon.
  • Reducing CanLit to landscape clichés (snow, wilderness, mounties).
  • Reading Atwood's "survival" thesis as descriptive truth instead of an argument.

Curriculum expectation: Strand B, Expectation A5 — "extend understanding of texts… by making connections between the ideas in them and personal knowledge, experience, and insights; other texts; and the world around them."

📖 4.2 Alice Munro & the Canadian Short Story
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Alice Munro (1931–2024), Nobel laureate in Literature (2013), is the central figure in the modern Canadian short story. Her stories — set most often in small-town southwestern Ontario, narrated in voices that are precise, unhurried, and quietly devastating — redefined what the form could do. She compresses what most novelists need three hundred pages to achieve into thirty: the slow accretion of an ordinary life, the betrayals it survives, the recognitions it almost survives.

Munro's technical signature is the time-shift. A typical Munro story will open in one moment, jump forward thirty years in a single paragraph, dwell in that future briefly, and then snap back to the original moment with a meaning the reader could not have constructed without the detour. The form enacts memory: in Munro, the present is what the past has done to it, and you do not understand what an event meant until you see what it became.

"Boys and Girls" (1968) is the canonical Munro story for ENG4U because of how openly it works on gender. The unnamed narrator, a farm girl, watches the slow narrowing of permissible identity: she begins as a useful child indistinguishable from a son, becomes "only a girl" by the final scene, and accepts the renaming with a strange relief that is more damning than protest would be. The story is not a lesson; it is a record.

Munro is also a writer of place. Her stories return to the small towns and farms of Huron County, Ontario, with such specificity that the region has become a literary territory of its own — sometimes called "Alice Munro country." The places are never backdrop. They impose schedules, weather, social codes, and gendered labour that shape what her characters can imagine for themselves. A close reader of Munro learns to treat the landscape and the architecture (kitchens, barns, second floors, motel rooms) as functioning characters that constrain the human ones. This regionalism is not provincialism: by writing one small place with the precision of a microscope, Munro produced an oeuvre that the Nobel committee read as universal.

  • Short story — short prose fiction with unified effect.
  • First-person retrospective narrator — an older self looking back on a younger self.
  • Free indirect style — narration that borrows the character's diction without quoting it.
  • Epiphany — a moment of sudden understanding, often muted in Munro.
  • Time-shift / analepsis / prolepsis — backwards (flashback) or forwards (flashforward) movement.
  • Realism — fidelity to the textures of ordinary life.
  • Gender as performance — the idea that gender is enacted under social pressure, not innate.

Worked Example — The Final Sentence of "Boys and Girls"

The story ends with the father's casual verdict on the narrator:

"'Never mind,' my father said, with humour. 'She's only a girl.' / I didn't protest that, even in my heart. Maybe it was true."

The devastation is in the last sentence. Notice what Munro does not do: she does not have the narrator weep, rebel, or write the manifesto. The narrator simply concedes, and the concession is what the story has been building toward. "I didn't protest that, even in my heart" measures the depth of the surrender — not even silent resistance survives. "Maybe it was true" is the chilling final move: the narrator has accepted not just the label but its possible truth. Munro lets the social verdict colonise interiority. A Level 4 essay would notice that the entire story has been narrated in a voice that already knows this ending — the retrospective adult is the one who can name what the child could not — and that the calm tone of the narration is itself part of the indictment.

  • Reading Munro as a regional realist who happens to write about women — gender is structural, not topical.
  • Missing the time-shifts because the prose is so calm.
  • Treating Munro's quietness as absence of judgment; the judgment is in the selection.
  • Identifying the narrator with the author (Munro grew up in rural Ontario, but the stories are fiction).
  • Calling Munro's stories "depressing" instead of analysing what produces the affect.

Curriculum expectation: Strand B, Expectation A4 — "make and explain inferences about both simple and complex texts, supporting their explanations with stated and implied ideas from the texts."

🪶 4.3 Indigenous Voices & Thomas King
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Indigenous literatures of Turtle Island predate Canada and are not a subset of "Canadian literature" — they are literatures that have been forced into conversation with Canada by colonisation. Reading them well requires three commitments: respect for orality (much Indigenous literature lives first as told story), attention to specific nations and traditions (Cree, Anishinaabe, Haudenosaunee, Mi'kmaq, Cherokee — each different), and refusal to read Indigenous writers as ethnographic informants rather than artists.

Thomas King (b. 1943), Cherokee and Greek heritage, is among the most widely taught Indigenous writers in Ontario classrooms because his work explicitly theorises storytelling. His 2003 CBC Massey Lectures, The Truth About Stories, opens with a sentence now famous in Canadian classrooms:

"The truth about stories is that that's all we are." For King, narrative is not a vehicle for truth; narrative is what humans are made of. His repeated opening — "There is a story I know. It's about the earth and how it floats in space on the back of a turtle" — is doing two jobs: telling a Haudenosaunee creation story, and modelling that the story-form itself, including its repetitions and its address to an audience, is part of what it means.

King's fiction (Green Grass, Running Water; A Short History of Indians in Canada; The Inconvenient Indian) layers traditional story, trickster figures, and biting comedy over critique of settler institutions. Students should read his comedy as serious analysis: humour is one of the few critical tools that can hold a reader's attention long enough to deliver a difficult truth.

The unit should also acknowledge the wider field. Tomson Highway's plays (The Rez Sisters, Dry Lips Oughta Move to Kapuskasing) bring Cree cosmology onto the Canadian stage; Eden Robinson's novels (Monkey Beach, Son of a Trickster) braid Haisla and Heiltsuk storytelling with contemporary realism; Lee Maracle and Maria Campbell's memoirs reframe autobiography as testimony; Joshua Whitehead's Jonny Appleseed writes Two-Spirit Indigiqueer experience into the canon. ENG4U does not require every student to read every text, but it does require students to leave the unit knowing that Indigenous literatures are plural, contemporary, and aesthetically demanding — and that "diversity" framing, which treats them as one category among many, is itself a settler convenience.

  • Oral tradition — narrative passed and performed in speech.
  • Trickster — recurring figure (Coyote, Nanabush, Raven) who breaks rules to reveal truth.
  • Creation story — foundational narrative of origins.
  • Two-eyed seeing — Etuaptmumk; Mi'kmaw concept of Indigenous and Western knowledge held together.
  • Decolonisation — undoing colonial assumptions in institutions and texts.
  • Land acknowledgement — formal recognition of Indigenous territory.
  • Sovereignty — Indigenous nations' political and cultural self-determination.
  • Code-switching — moving between linguistic or cultural registers.

Worked Example — Reading the Opening of The Truth About Stories

King begins each chapter with the same sentence:

"There is a story I know. It's about the earth and how it floats in space on the back of a turtle."

The repetition is not laziness. It enacts the oral convention that a story is renewed each time it is told; the formula signals that the listener is being invited into a transmitted tradition. The sentence is also doing analytical work — the earth "floats in space on the back of a turtle" is a Haudenosaunee cosmology placed beside (without subordination to) the Western cosmology of "earth orbits sun." King is not arguing that the turtle story is symbolic of the scientific one; he is arguing that both are stories, and that the Western refusal to recognise this is itself a colonial habit. A Level 4 paragraph would resist the urge to "translate" the turtle into a metaphor, since translation is precisely the move King is critiquing.

  • Treating Indigenous literature as folklore or anthropology rather than literary art.
  • Reading the trickster as a simple "fool" character — tricksters expose social truth.
  • Collapsing distinct nations and traditions into a generic "Indigenous" category.
  • Quoting King's irony as if it were sincerity (or vice versa).
  • Writing about Indigenous texts without including any Indigenous critical voices in your bibliography.

Curriculum expectation: Strand B, Expectation A7 — "evaluate the effectiveness of both simple and complex texts based on evidence, examples from the texts, and the student's own experience."

🌲 4.4 Atwood and Speculative Canada
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Margaret Atwood (b. 1939) is the Canadian writer who has done the most to demonstrate that "Canadian literature" can be politically global. Her novels — The Edible Woman, Surfacing, The Handmaid's Tale, the MaddAddam trilogy, The Testaments — and her poetry have insisted on Canadian voice while addressing women's bodies, environmental collapse, theocracy, and the precariousness of liberal democracy. Atwood prefers the term speculative fiction to science fiction: she writes only about technologies and political arrangements that already exist somewhere on earth, and the speculative move is to ask what happens if those existing pressures intensify.

The Handmaid's Tale (1985) imagines the United States after a theocratic coup, fertile women conscripted as state-owned breeding bodies, language constricted to the religiously sanctioned ("Blessed be the fruit"; "Under his eye"). Offred, the narrator, tells the story in present-tense fragments that the reader later learns were recorded on cassette tapes after escape. The frame — academic historians in 2195 discussing Offred's tapes — makes the novel itself an artifact, asking who interprets women's stories and on whose terms.

Atwood's poetry is equally important for ENG4U. "The City Planners" examines suburban order as a form of violence; "Spelling" links the literal teaching of letters to the political teaching of voice. Atwood's signature is irony delivered in flat, precise diction — never sentimental, never reassuring, almost always pulling the rug out at the last line.

The Canadian context inside Atwood's work is more present than the international reception sometimes notices. Surfacing (1972) is set in Quebec wilderness and reads American tourism as a slow encroachment; the MaddAddam trilogy maps near-future ecological collapse onto recognisably North American suburban geography; the poetry of The Journals of Susanna Moodie rewrites a foundational Canadian settler text from inside the unsettled mind of the settler herself. Reading Atwood as both a Canadian writer and a writer of global dystopia is not a contradiction; the smallness and politeness of her Canadian register is part of what makes the violence of her speculative worlds land so hard. Students who notice the gap between the surface calm and the underlying dread have understood her tonal signature.

  • Speculative fiction — Atwood's preferred term; fiction that extrapolates from present realities.
  • Dystopia — imagined society organised around suffering or oppression.
  • Frame narrative — a story enclosed within an outer story (the Historical Notes in Handmaid's).
  • Unreliable narrator — a narrator whose account cannot be fully trusted.
  • Theocracy — government by religious authority.
  • Patriarchy — social system organised around male authority.
  • Defamiliarisation — making the ordinary look strange so it can be re-examined.
  • Tone of flat irony — Atwood's signature; deadpan delivery of devastating content.

Worked Example — Atwood's "The City Planners"

Atwood opens by describing what at first looks like an entirely tame suburb:

"Cruising these residential Sunday / streets in dry August sunlight: / what offends us is / the sanities…"

The provocation lands on the noun "sanities." Suburban order — the clipped lawns, the matched houses — is not described as boring or stifling. It is described as offensive, and what offends is precisely its sanity, its excess of order. Atwood then catalogues small disturbances ("the rational whine of a power mower / cutting a straight swath in the discouraged grass") in which the diction relentlessly anthropomorphises the suburb's tools and pathologises its calm: a "rational whine" is the sound of order producing the irrational, and grass is "discouraged," not merely cut. The poem's final image of city planners "sketching transitory lines rigid as wooden borders / on a wall in the white vanishing air" reveals planning itself as denial of impermanence. A strong analytical paragraph would argue that for Atwood the speculative-Canadian move is to expose what looks like ordinary domestic peace as a hard-won, brittle, and politically charged achievement.

  • Calling The Handmaid's Tale "science fiction" without engaging Atwood's own preferred label.
  • Reading Offred's narration as transparent reportage rather than reconstructed memory.
  • Ignoring the Historical Notes — they are part of the novel.
  • Mistaking Atwood's flat tone for emotional absence; the flatness is the affect.
  • Reducing her work to feminist commentary — Atwood's range includes environment, politics, religion, language.

Curriculum expectation: Strand D, Expectation A2 — "interpret media texts, identifying and explaining the overt and implied messages they convey."
