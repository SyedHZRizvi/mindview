🎬 6.1 Media Forms & Conventions
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Media studies treats film, television, advertising, news, social-media platforms, music video, podcasts, video games, and memes as texts — that is, structured artifacts that produce meaning through conventions and codes. The skills used to analyse Hamlet transfer cleanly: identify the form, name its conventions, attend to who made it and for whom, ask what assumptions it asks the audience to share, and account for the techniques by which it produces its effect. The text just looks different.

Marshall McLuhan, the Canadian theorist whose work founded modern media studies, argued that "the medium is the message" — that the form a message takes shapes what it can say at least as much as its content does. A newsstory rendered as a TikTok and as a 4,000-word feature article are not the same story in different containers; they are different stories. Strong Grade 12 media analysis takes the form itself as content.

Each medium has conventions: in film, mise-en-scène, cinematography, editing, sound design; in news, the inverted-pyramid structure and headline-deck-lede architecture; in advertising, the visual hierarchy, brand colour palette, calls to action; in social media, the affordances of the platform itself (character limits, algorithmic visibility, replies). A media analyst learns to name conventions and to argue what they make possible and impossible.

An additional Canadian framing matters here. Canadian media exist within a distinctive regulatory environment — the CRTC, the Broadcasting Act, the Canadian content rules — and within a public-service tradition (CBC/Radio-Canada, NFB) that produces media texts on assumptions different from purely commercial systems. ENG4U media analysis should be able to name this context: that the audience for a CBC news story is conceived partly as a public to be served, while the audience for a Buzzfeed video is conceived primarily as an engagement metric. Neither framing is intrinsically nobler, but pretending the difference does not exist is itself a media-illiterate move.

  • Medium — the carrier of the message (print, screen, audio, network).
  • Convention — established expectation of a form (a news lede, a film cut).
  • Code — a system of signs (visual, verbal, technical) that carries meaning.
  • Mise-en-scène — everything arranged within a single shot.
  • Editing / montage — the arrangement of shots in sequence.
  • Sound design — diegetic vs. non-diegetic sound; music as argument.
  • Affordance — what a medium makes easy or hard to do.
  • Semiotics — the study of signs and how they mean.

Worked Example — Reading a Single Shot

Consider a typical opening shot of a CBC evening newscast: an anchor centred and shoulders-up, soft three-point lighting, neutral grey background, full Canadian-news lower-third graphic, and the camera at eye level. None of this is accidental.

The conventions code authority. Centre framing puts the anchor in the dominant position; eye-level camera makes the viewer the anchor's equal (compare with low-angle, which renders the subject powerful, or high-angle, which renders them small). Soft three-point lighting flattens skin tone toward neutrality and removes shadow, suggesting transparency. The grey background is professional but plain enough to read as "studio" without competing for attention. The lower-third graphic borrows the network's brand reliability. Every one of these choices is shared with virtually every other broadcast newsroom in the world. The convention is the credibility appeal. A media essay would argue that "objectivity" in television news is, technically speaking, a particular set of visual codes — and that becoming aware of those codes is the first step toward asking what they hide.

  • Treating a medium as a transparent window onto content — every medium shapes content.
  • Citing McLuhan's slogan without engaging the argument behind it.
  • Analysing media texts as if they had no producers, owners, or audiences.
  • Calling something "biased" without naming the convention that produced the bias.
  • Mistaking aesthetic style ("looks professional") for analytic substance.

Curriculum expectation: Strand D, Expectation D1.1 — "explain how various media texts address their intended audience and create a specific impression of their subject."

📰 6.2 News, Bias, and Source Evaluation
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

"Bias" is not a synonym for "wrong" or "evil." All news is selective — there is no neutral choice about which stories to cover, which sources to call, which adjective to use, which photograph to put above the fold. The Grade 12 media-literate reader does not look for sources without bias; she looks for sources whose biases she can identify, whose factual claims she can verify, and whose method she can hold to a standard.

The most useful distinction is among news (fact-led reporting with verified sources), analysis (interpretation by a reporter or expert), opinion / editorial (argued position from a writer or board), and sponsored / native content (paid material designed to resemble news). Reputable outlets label these clearly; less reputable ones blur them. The first move on any digital story is to find the label.

Source evaluation lives in five questions. Who wrote this and what is their record? Where is it published, and what are the publication's standards? When — is this current, or has it been recirculated out of context? What evidence is cited, and can it be traced? Why was this story selected for me — what algorithm or human editor decided this should appear here? The five questions are not a checklist; they are the habit of mind that produces critical media literacy.

Lateral reading, popularised by the Stanford History Education Group, is the single most useful evaluation technique now available to students. Instead of staying on a source and judging it by its design or self-presentation, open three new tabs and search what other reputable outlets say about the source itself. Lateral reading shifts the question from "does this site look trustworthy?" — a question of design — to "what do independent sources say about this site?" — a question of evidence. The cost is thirty seconds; the upgrade in judgment is substantial. The Grade 12 student who lateral-reads automatically becomes harder to deceive in a way that no amount of cynicism alone produces.

  • News vs. opinion vs. sponsored — categories an outlet should distinguish.
  • Primary source — original document or first-hand account.
  • Lateral reading — checking the source by leaving the source and searching elsewhere.
  • Triangulation — confirming a fact across three independent sources.
  • Cherry-picking — selecting only evidence that supports a desired conclusion.
  • Framing — the choice of context, headline, and lede that shapes how facts read.
  • Misinformation / disinformation — false content; deliberately false content.
  • Filter bubble / echo chamber — algorithmically narrowed exposure to like-minded views.

Worked Example — Same Story, Two Headlines

Consider how the same parliamentary vote on a transit-funding bill could be headlined two ways:

(A) "Federal government commits $4 billion to transit; opposition warns of debt impact."
(B) "Liberals push through $4 billion transit spending despite Conservative debt warnings."

The facts are identical. The framing is not. Headline A leads with the action ("commits") and treats opposition as a contingent voice ("warns"); the verb is neutral. Headline B leads with the actor's party label ("Liberals push through"), uses an aggressive verb ("push through"), and elevates the opposition warning to a near-equal noun ("Conservative debt warnings"). A media-literate Grade 12 reader does not conclude that one headline is true and the other false; she notices that the framing has done political work before any reader has begun the article. Lateral reading — searching the same story on Reuters, CBC, The Globe and Mail, and the Toronto Star — exposes which framings are local to which outlets and lets the reader triangulate.

  • Treating "balanced" coverage as automatically true — false balance gives equal weight to unequal claims.
  • Conflating opinion columns with news reporting.
  • Sharing a story without reading past the headline.
  • Mistaking the absence of obvious bias for the absence of bias.
  • Trusting "looks professional" as a credibility cue — design is cheap.

Curriculum expectation: Strand D, Expectation D1.4 — "explain how a variety of production, marketing, and distribution factors influence the media industry and influence the content of media texts."

📱 6.3 Advertising & Social Media Rhetoric
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Advertising is the most refined rhetoric humans have ever made. A thirty-second commercial does, in compressed time, what Cicero's speeches did across hours: identifies an audience, raises a perceived lack, offers an apparent solution, and binds the solution to an identity worth wanting. Studying advertising is therefore a fast way to study rhetoric — the techniques the Greek and Roman orators named are still in the toolkit, refined for a smaller screen.

The classical appeals (ethos, pathos, logos) all appear in ads, but advertising's distinctive technique is identification. Kenneth Burke argued that persuasion happens not when an audience accepts a logical claim but when they identify with the speaker. An ad rarely says "buy this"; it stages a scene the viewer wants to be inside, and offers the product as the entry ticket. The work the ad does is to make you want to be the person who already owns this product.

Social-media rhetoric extends the toolbox. The platform itself is part of the message: an Instagram post composes meaning through filtered image, caption, hashtag, and audience response (likes, shares, comments) in ways that a print magazine ad cannot. Influencer marketing weaponises parasocial relationships — the viewer feels they know the speaker, which compounds ethos. Algorithmic visibility means content has to be optimised not only for human attention but for ranking systems that reward engagement (often the engagement of outrage).

  • Ethos / pathos / logos — the classical appeals, alive in advertising.
  • Identification (Burke) — persuasion via shared identity, not logical argument.
  • Branding — the long-term identity of a product across campaigns.
  • USP (unique selling proposition) — the one claim a campaign anchors to.
  • Targeted advertising — ads selected for an individual based on data.
  • Influencer / parasocial relationship — perceived intimacy between viewer and online personality.
  • Algorithmic visibility — content ranking determined by engagement metrics.
  • Greenwashing / pinkwashing — appropriating environmental or social-justice imagery for sales.
  • Disclosure (#ad) — required notice that a post is sponsored.

Worked Example — Reading a Luxury-Watch Ad

A typical full-page magazine ad for a Swiss luxury watch shows a black-and-white photograph of an athlete at the apex of difficulty — climbing, sailing, conducting — with the watch on the wrist and a single line of copy: "The pursuit of excellence. Since 1875."

Identification is doing the work. The viewer is not invited to buy a timepiece; she is invited to recognise herself as the kind of person whose pursuits warrant a 150-year-old instrument. Logos is minimal (no specs about movement or water resistance); ethos is overwhelming (the date "1875" alone carries a century and a half of credibility); pathos is dignified rather than sentimental (the photograph stages an emotion the viewer is permitted to share by purchase). The black-and-white treatment removes a contemporary marker — the ad floats outside time, which lets the brand claim timelessness. Crucially, the watch is small in the frame: the brand is confident enough to make the product almost incidental, because the real product is the identity it confers. A Level 4 essay would notice that the campaign is selling not a watch but membership.

  • Calling all advertising "manipulative" without naming the technique.
  • Ignoring the platform: an Instagram ad and a magazine ad work differently.
  • Treating influencer endorsements as personal opinion rather than commercial speech.
  • Missing the role of algorithmic visibility in what reaches you.
  • Forgetting that the absence of overt product information is itself a rhetorical choice.

Curriculum expectation: Strand D, Expectation D2.1 — "identify a variety of forms of media texts and explain how the conventions and techniques associated with them are used to create meaning."

🎨 6.4 Creating an Original Media Text
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The Grade 12 culminating media task asks you to make a media text — a short film, a podcast episode, an advertising campaign, a social-media awareness series, a print zine, an interactive piece — and to defend the choices you made. Production is where analytical knowledge becomes practical knowledge: you discover that "good editing" is invisible only when it has been done well, that lighting takes longer than dialogue, and that a thirty-second video is a draft, a rough cut, a fine cut, a colour pass, and a sound mix before anyone sees it.

Strong media production begins with a written brief, not a camera. The brief names the purpose, audience, platform, and intended effect. "Convince students at our school to vote in the upcoming student-council election, distributed via Instagram and TikTok, intended to feel energetic rather than scolding" is a workable brief. "Make a video about voting" is not. Every later production choice — music, framing, length, captions — should be answerable back to the brief.

The accompanying reflection or production journal is part of the assessment. You explain what you intended, what conventions you used, what trade-offs you accepted, and what you would change next time. The reflection is where the analytical strand and the production strand meet: a strong reflection demonstrates that the maker has been a media studies critic of their own work.

  • Creative brief — written statement of purpose, audience, platform, effect.
  • Pre-production — script, storyboard, shot list, location scouting.
  • Production — capture stage (filming, recording, photographing).
  • Post-production — editing, colour, sound mix, captions, distribution.
  • Storyboard — sequence of sketches showing shots in order.
  • Rough cut / fine cut — sequential editing stages.
  • Accessibility — captions, alt text, contrast; ethical and legal necessity.
  • Copyright / fair dealing — Canadian framework for educational use.
  • Reflection / production journal — self-critique against the brief.

Worked Example — A Production Plan and Reflection

Brief: A 60-second PSA encouraging Grade 11–12 students to register for the federal voter Future Voter list, delivered as a vertical TikTok with captions.

Production plan: Open with rapid jump-cuts of student interview replies ("I'm too young," "It won't count," "I don't even know what they vote on") to acknowledge the audience's hesitation; pivot at 0:25 with a clean cut to silence and a single statistic on screen; close with a five-second action card (URL + QR code) and a calm voiceover. Music low under interview, drops out at the stat, returns under the action card. Captions in high-contrast white with a black stroke for accessibility on muted feeds.

Reflection (after cut): "I followed the brief on length and platform, but the rough cut was 75 seconds and I had to lose the strongest interview line ('voting is the only piece of homework adults grade you on') because the rhythm needed the silence at 0:25. I chose to keep the silence over the line, because the silence works as both a pause and a rhetorical caesura. If I were to do this again I would film B-roll on a second day; my first edit was constrained by only having interview footage, which is why the cuts feel front-loaded. The captions caught a transcription error I would not have noticed without them — captioning is not just accessibility, it is editorial proofreading by other means."

The reflection is doing the analytical work the curriculum asks for: it names a trade-off, defends it with reference to effect, identifies a production limit, and articulates what was learned for next time.

  • Starting with the camera before the brief.
  • Treating the storyboard as optional — it saves more time than it costs.
  • Reflections that describe what you did rather than evaluate why.
  • Skipping captions and alt text; the project does not meet the brief if it is not accessible.
  • Using copyrighted music without licence or fair-dealing argument — find royalty-free or seek permission.

Curriculum expectation: Strand D, Expectation D3.1 — "produce media texts for several different purposes and audiences, using appropriate forms, conventions, and techniques."
