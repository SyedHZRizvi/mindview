8.1 — Scatter Plots & Correlation
Constructing scatter plots; describing the form, direction, and strength of a relationship; explanatory vs response variable.
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

One-variable statistics summarise a single quantity. Two-variable (bivariate) analysis asks whether two quantities tend to vary together. Each observation contributes an ordered pair \((x, y)\); plotting these pairs gives a scatter plot, the foundational picture for studying relationships. By convention, the explanatory (independent) variable \(x\) goes on the horizontal axis and the response (dependent) variable \(y\) goes on the vertical axis. The choice of which is which depends on context: when modelling crop yield from rainfall, rainfall is the explanatory variable.

A good description of a scatter plot mentions four features: form (linear, curved, no apparent pattern), direction (positive, negative, none), strength (how tightly the points cluster around a curve), and unusual features (outliers, clusters, separate groups). These qualitative descriptions complement the quantitative measure (Pearson's \(r\), Topic 8.2). A weak linear pattern with a strong curved pattern is common — and warns you that a straight-line fit will mislead.

  • Bivariate data: measurements of two variables on the same individuals.
  • Explanatory variable \(x\): the predictor; horizontal axis.
  • Response variable \(y\): the outcome; vertical axis.
  • Form: linear, quadratic, exponential, none.
  • Direction: positive (both increase together) or negative (one up, one down).
  • Strength: how closely the points cluster around the underlying pattern.
  • Outlier: a point far from the overall pattern.

Example: Twelve students plot study hours per week against final exam scores. The scatter plot shows points clustering along a rising straight line from \((2, 55)\) to \((15, 92)\), with one outlier at \((10, 50)\).

Describing the relationship: form linear; direction positive (more study, higher score); strength moderately strong (points cluster tightly but the outlier weakens the fit); unusual feature one outlier — possibly a student who studied with poor strategy or had a difficult exam. The explanatory variable is study hours, the response variable is exam score; reversing them would change the regression line and prediction.

  • Switching axes: putting \(y\) on the \(x\)-axis changes the regression line and the meaning of slope.
  • Treating a strong curved pattern as if it had no relationship just because the linear correlation is weak.
  • Ignoring outliers in the description — they often carry the most important information.

Curriculum expectation: Strand C, Expectation C3.1 — "create a graphical summary of two-variable data using a scatter plot (e.g., by identifying and justifying the dependent and independent variables; by drawing the line of best fit, when appropriate)."

8.2 — The Pearson Correlation Coefficient \( r \)
\( r = \dfrac{1}{n-1}\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n} \dfrac{x_i-\bar{x}}{s_x}\cdot\dfrac{y_i-\bar{y}}{s_y} \); interpreting \( r \in [-1, 1] \); correlation \(\ne\) causation.
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Pearson's correlation coefficient \(r\) is a single number that summarises the strength and direction of the linear relationship between two quantitative variables. Defined as \[ r = \frac{1}{n-1}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(\frac{x_i - \bar{x}}{s_x}\right)\left(\frac{y_i - \bar{y}}{s_y}\right), \] \(r\) is the average product of standardised \(x\) and \(y\) deviations. Standardising removes units, so \(r\) is dimensionless and lies in \([-1, 1]\) regardless of the original variables.

Interpretation: \(r = +1\) means the points lie exactly on a line of positive slope; \(r = -1\) on a line of negative slope; \(r = 0\) means no linear association (although a strong non-linear pattern may still exist). Common qualitative bands: \(|r| < 0.3\) weak, \(0.3 \le |r| < 0.7\) moderate, \(|r| \ge 0.7\) strong. Crucially, correlation does not imply causation: a strong \(r\) between ice-cream sales and drownings is created by the lurking variable "summer". Both variables must be quantitative and the relationship roughly linear for \(r\) to mean what it suggests; \(r\) is also sensitive to outliers, which can artificially inflate or shrink the value.

  • Pearson \(r\): measures linear association; \(r \in [-1, 1]\).
  • Sign: positive = both increase together; negative = one up, other down.
  • Magnitude: closer to \(\pm 1\) = stronger linear pattern.
  • \(r^2\) (coefficient of determination): proportion of variance in \(y\) explained by a linear model on \(x\).
  • Correlation \(\ne\) causation: a strong \(r\) cannot prove cause-and-effect.

Example: For 5 students, hours of sleep (\(x\)) and quiz scores (\(y\)) are recorded:

(5, 60), (6, 65), (7, 78), (8, 82), (9, 90). Then \(\bar{x} = 7\), \(\bar{y} = 75\).

Deviations: \(x_i - \bar{x}\): \(-2, -1, 0, 1, 2\); \(y_i - \bar{y}\): \(-15, -10, 3, 7, 15\). Products: \(30, 10, 0, 7, 30\); sum = 77.

\(s_x = \sqrt{\tfrac{10}{4}} \approx 1.581\); \(s_y = \sqrt{\tfrac{608}{4}} \approx 12.328\).

\[ r = \frac{77}{(n-1)\,s_x\,s_y} = \frac{77}{4 \cdot 1.581 \cdot 12.328} \approx \frac{77}{77.96} \approx 0.988. \]

This is a very strong positive linear relationship. So \(r^2 \approx 0.976\): about 97.6% of the variation in quiz scores is explained by hours of sleep — in this small sample.

  • Concluding causation from correlation, especially in observational (non-experimental) data.
  • Reporting \(r\) for clearly non-linear data — \(r\) only captures the linear part.
  • Letting a single outlier dominate \(r\); always look at the scatter plot before trusting the number.

Curriculum expectation: Strand C, Expectation C3.2 — "recognize and distinguish different types of relationships between two variables that have a mathematical correlation (e.g., the cause-and-effect relationship between the age of a tree and its diameter; the common-cause relationship between ice-cream sales and forest fires over the course of a year)."

8.3 — Linear Regression & Line of Best Fit
Least-squares line \( \hat{y} = b_0 + b_1 x \) where \( b_1 = r\dfrac{s_y}{s_x} \); interpreting slope and intercept; using \( \hat{y} \) for prediction.
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

When a scatter plot shows an approximately linear pattern, we can summarise the trend by a single straight line — the line of best fit. The standard mathematical choice is the least-squares regression line \(\hat{y} = b_0 + b_1 x\), where \(\hat{y}\) is the predicted response for a given \(x\). "Least squares" refers to choosing \(b_0\) and \(b_1\) so that the sum of squared vertical distances from the points to the line, \(\sum (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2\), is as small as possible.

The formulas link to material we already have. The slope is \( b_1 = r\,\dfrac{s_y}{s_x} \) and the intercept is \(b_0 = \bar{y} - b_1 \bar{x}\). The slope tells you the predicted change in \(y\) for each one-unit increase in \(x\); the intercept is the predicted \(y\) when \(x = 0\) (often a meaningless extrapolation if \(x = 0\) is outside the data range). Using \(\hat{y}\) to predict \(y\) inside the range of observed \(x\) values is called interpolation; outside that range it is extrapolation, which is dangerous because the linear pattern may not continue. Most importantly, the regression line is asymmetric: regressing \(y\) on \(x\) gives a different line than regressing \(x\) on \(y\) — choose your explanatory and response variables based on context.

  • Least-squares line: \(\hat{y} = b_0 + b_1 x\) minimising \(\sum (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2\).
  • Slope: \(b_1 = r\,\dfrac{s_y}{s_x}\).
  • Intercept: \(b_0 = \bar{y} - b_1\bar{x}\).
  • Interpolation vs extrapolation: predicting inside (safe) vs outside (risky) the data range.
  • Asymmetry: regressing \(y\) on \(x\) is not the same as regressing \(x\) on \(y\).

Example: Using the sleep / quiz data from Topic 8.2 (\(\bar{x} = 7, \bar{y} = 75, r = 0.988, s_x = 1.581, s_y = 12.328\)), find the line of best fit and predict the score for 6.5 hours of sleep.

Slope: \(b_1 = 0.988 \cdot \dfrac{12.328}{1.581} \approx 0.988 \cdot 7.797 \approx 7.704\). Intercept: \(b_0 = 75 - 7.704 \cdot 7 = 75 - 53.93 = 21.07\).

\[ \hat{y} = 21.07 + 7.704\, x. \]

Prediction for \(x = 6.5\): \(\hat{y} = 21.07 + 7.704(6.5) \approx 21.07 + 50.08 = 71.15\). The model predicts a quiz score of about 71.

  • Treating the intercept as meaningful when \(x = 0\) is outside the data range.
  • Extrapolating far beyond the observed \(x\) values — slope drift, curvature, or saturation can invalidate predictions.
  • Forgetting that the regression line passes through \((\bar{x}, \bar{y})\); use this as a sanity check.

Curriculum expectation: Strand C, Expectation C3.3 — "generate, using available technology, the line of best fit (e.g., the least-squares line) for a scatter plot, and recognize this line as the line that minimizes the sum of the squares of the deviations from the line to the points in the scatter plot."

8.4 — Residuals & Goodness of Fit
Residual \( e_i = y_i - \hat{y}_i \); residual plots; identifying when a non-linear model fits better.
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

A regression line is only as useful as its fit, and the fit is judged by residuals. For each observation, the residual is \(e_i = y_i - \hat{y}_i\) — the vertical distance from the actual point to the line. Residuals can be positive (point above the line) or negative (below). The least-squares method ensures that \(\sum e_i = 0\) (residuals balance) and that \(\sum e_i^2\) is minimised; it does not guarantee a good fit, only the best linear fit.

The crucial diagnostic is the residual plot: residuals on the vertical axis against \(x\) (or against \(\hat{y}\)) on the horizontal axis. A "good" residual plot shows a random, structureless scatter band around zero. Patterns in the residual plot reveal model inadequacy: a curve indicates a non-linear true relationship (try quadratic, exponential, or power models); a fan shape (increasing spread) indicates heteroscedasticity (changing variance); a single isolated point indicates an outlier. The coefficient of determination \(r^2\) reports the fraction of the variance in \(y\) explained by the regression on \(x\); higher \(r^2\) means smaller residuals. Always look at both \(r^2\) and the residual plot before trusting a model.

  • Residual: \(e_i = y_i - \hat{y}_i\) — vertical distance from point to line.
  • Properties: \(\sum e_i = 0\) and \(\sum e_i^2\) is minimised by least squares.
  • Residual plot: residuals vs \(x\); should look structureless if the linear model is appropriate.
  • Patterns to watch: curvature → non-linear model needed; fanning → unequal variance; outlier → isolated point far from band.
  • Coefficient of determination: \(r^2\) (square of Pearson \(r\)) = proportion of variance explained.

Example: Using \(\hat{y} = 21.07 + 7.704\,x\) from Topic 8.3, compute residuals for the observed pairs (5, 60) and (8, 82).

For \(x = 5\): \(\hat{y} = 21.07 + 7.704(5) = 21.07 + 38.52 = 59.59\). Residual: \(60 - 59.59 = +0.41\) (above the line by 0.41).

For \(x = 8\): \(\hat{y} = 21.07 + 7.704(8) = 21.07 + 61.63 = 82.70\). Residual: \(82 - 82.70 = -0.70\) (below the line by 0.70).

Both residuals are small relative to the typical \(y\)-spread of 12.3, consistent with the high \(r^2 \approx 0.976\). A residual plot should show no obvious pattern.

  • Reporting only \(r^2\) and skipping the residual plot — a misleadingly high \(r^2\) can hide curvature.
  • Confusing residuals with errors — residuals are observed, errors are unobservable model deviations.
  • Forgetting that the sign of the residual matters: above vs below the line communicates real information.

Curriculum expectation: Strand C, Expectation C3.3 — "generate, using available technology, the line of best fit (e.g., the least-squares line) for a scatter plot, and recognize this line as the line that minimizes the sum of the squares of the deviations from the line to the points in the scatter plot."

8.5 — Two-Variable Analysis & Causation
Cause-and-effect, common cause, reverse cause-and-effect, accidental and presumed relationships; lurking and confounding variables.
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Even when a strong statistical correlation exists between two variables, the relationship between them can come in several flavours. Ontario MDM4U asks students to classify and critique causal claims — a vital skill in a world saturated with statistical headlines. The five categories typically discussed are: cause-and-effect (changes in \(x\) directly produce changes in \(y\)); common-cause (a third variable \(z\) causes both \(x\) and \(y\)); reverse cause-and-effect (in fact \(y\) causes \(x\)); accidental (coincidence with no underlying mechanism); and presumed (cause-and-effect is plausible but not proven).

A lurking variable is one that is not measured but influences both \(x\) and \(y\); a confounding variable is one whose effect on \(y\) cannot be separated from \(x\)'s effect. The single best protection against these is the randomised controlled experiment: by randomly assigning subjects to treatment and control groups, the experimenter ensures that other variables (measured or not) are balanced across groups, allowing a genuine causal inference. Observational data — surveys, historical records — can only suggest associations; causation requires either a randomised experiment or a careful argument (mechanism, dose-response, temporal precedence, etc., as in the Bradford-Hill criteria used by epidemiology).

  • Cause-and-effect: \(x\) directly produces changes in \(y\).
  • Common-cause: hidden third variable causes both.
  • Reverse cause-and-effect: \(y\) actually causes \(x\).
  • Accidental: coincidence with no plausible mechanism.
  • Presumed: causal direction plausible but unproven.
  • Lurking / confounding variable: unmeasured (or inseparable) third factor.
  • Randomised experiment: the gold standard for establishing causation.

Example: Classify each correlation.

(a) Children's shoe size and reading ability are positively correlated. → Common-cause: age causes both shoe size and reading ability.

(b) Smoking and incidence of lung cancer are strongly positively correlated. → Cause-and-effect, supported by extensive randomised animal experiments, mechanistic studies, and dose-response data.

(c) Annual cheese consumption and number of people who drown by falling into a pool show a 95% correlation across 10 years. → Accidental: no plausible mechanism — a classic "spurious correlation".

(d) Hours spent on social media and self-reported anxiety. → Presumed (or reverse): plausible that social media causes anxiety, but anxious people might also use social media more.

  • Asserting causation from a correlation, especially in observational data without controls.
  • Ignoring lurking variables — the most famous traps in statistical reasoning.
  • Forgetting that correlation can be misleading when one variable is a known time-trend (cheese and drownings both rise with population).

Curriculum expectation: Strand C, Expectation C3.4 — "describe possible misuses of regression (e.g., the danger of using the regression line of best fit to make a prediction for a value of the independent variable that is not in the domain of the data being considered) and how they could affect the interpretation of the data."

8.6 — Culminating Data-Management Investigation
Independent inquiry: develop a research question, collect/find primary or secondary data, apply two-variable analysis, present a written and oral report.
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The Culminating Data-Management Investigation is the synthesising experience of MDM4U — a full Strand E independent inquiry that draws on every other strand. Students pose a meaningful research question, collect or gather data, analyse the data using the tools developed in Strands A through D, and communicate the findings in both written and oral form. The project counts toward the 30% Final Evaluation along with the final exam, so it is the single most important deliverable of the course.

An effective investigation has a clear, focused research question that is genuinely answerable with statistical methods. Data may be primary (collected by the student via survey or experiment) or secondary (from Statistics Canada, OECD, Gapminder, or other credible sources). The analysis typically combines one-variable summaries (means, medians, standard deviation, histograms) with two-variable methods (scatter plots, regression, correlation). The written report should follow a recognisable structure: introduction and research question, methodology and data source, analysis with appropriate graphs and statistics, discussion of bias and limitations, and conclusion. The oral presentation must explain — not just display — the findings, anticipating questions about sampling method, statistical inference, and alternative interpretations. Ontario's Growing Success policy requires assessment across all four achievement categories: Knowledge & Understanding, Thinking, Communication, and Application.

  • Research question: focused, statistical, answerable with available data.
  • Primary data: collected by the student (survey, experiment, simulation).
  • Secondary data: obtained from credible existing sources.
  • Analysis tools: descriptive statistics, scatter plot, regression, correlation, probability distributions where applicable.
  • Report structure: introduction, methodology, analysis, discussion, conclusion, references.
  • Achievement chart: Knowledge, Thinking, Communication, Application — each /25.

Example: A sample research question — "Is there a relationship between average annual screen time and self-reported sleep quality among Grade 12 students at our school?"

Variables: explanatory \(x\) = weekly screen time (hours); response \(y\) = sleep-quality score (1–10). Methodology: anonymous online survey of ~80 Grade 12 students, with stratified random sampling across the four homeroom sections (5.4). Analysis steps:

(1) Compute \(\bar{x}, \bar{y}, s_x, s_y\). (2) Build a scatter plot and describe form, direction, strength. (3) Compute Pearson \(r\) and the regression line \(\hat{y} = b_0 + b_1 x\) using Excel's SLOPE, INTERCEPT, and CORREL functions. (4) Examine the residual plot. (5) Discuss sources of bias (self-report, voluntary response within the sample, lurking variables like part-time work). (6) Conclude with statistical and contextual interpretation; avoid causal language unless an experiment was performed.

  • Choosing a vague or unanswerable research question — sharpen it before collecting data.
  • Asserting causation from observational data — limit conclusions to "associated with" rather than "causes".
  • Skipping the discussion of bias and limitations — Ontario rubrics weight this heavily under Thinking and Communication.
  • Ignoring the four achievement categories — strong analysis alone will not earn full marks if Communication is weak.

Curriculum expectation: Strand E (Culminating Data Management Investigation), Expectation E1.1 — "design a culminating investigation that requires the integration and application of the knowledge and skills related to the expectations of this course."
