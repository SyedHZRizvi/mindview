7.1 — Population, Sample & Sampling Methods
Census vs sample; simple random, stratified, systematic, cluster, voluntary-response, and convenience samples.
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

A population is the entire set of individuals about which a statistical question is being asked; a sample is a subset of the population from which data are actually collected. A census measures every member of the population, which is rarely feasible (cost, time, destructive testing). Instead, statisticians select a sample and use it to make inferences. The quality of those inferences depends almost entirely on how the sample was selected.

The "gold standard" is the simple random sample (SRS), in which every individual has an equal chance of selection and every possible sample of the chosen size is equally likely. Variations refine the SRS for practical reasons: stratified sampling divides the population into homogeneous strata (e.g. by grade level) and samples randomly within each stratum, improving precision when strata differ; systematic sampling selects every \(k\)-th individual from a list; cluster sampling randomly selects intact groups (clusters) and includes everyone in the chosen clusters; multi-stage sampling combines these techniques. Voluntary-response and convenience samples are non-random and almost always biased — they should be flagged whenever encountered.

  • Population vs sample: whole group vs measured subset.
  • SRS: every individual equally likely; every sample equally likely.
  • Stratified: SRS within homogeneous subgroups.
  • Systematic: every \(k\)-th item after a random start.
  • Cluster: randomly chosen intact groups, all members included.
  • Convenience / voluntary-response: non-random and biased; valid samples must be random.

Example: A school of 800 students contains 250 in Grade 9, 220 in Grade 10, 180 in Grade 11, and 150 in Grade 12. To survey 80 students using a stratified random sample, how many should be drawn from each grade?

The sampling fraction is \(\tfrac{80}{800} = \tfrac{1}{10}\). Apply it to each stratum:

\[ \text{Gr 9}: 250 \cdot \tfrac{1}{10} = 25, \quad \text{Gr 10}: 22, \quad \text{Gr 11}: 18, \quad \text{Gr 12}: 15. \]

Total: \(25 + 22 + 18 + 15 = 80\;\checkmark\). Within each grade, students are selected by SRS.

  • Calling a non-random sample a "random" sample. "Random" has a precise technical meaning.
  • Confusing stratified (random within groups) with cluster (whole groups chosen randomly).
  • Generalising from a convenience sample to the population — almost always invalid.

Curriculum expectation: Strand C, Expectation C1.1 — "recognize and describe the role of data in statistical studies (e.g., the use of statistical techniques to extract or mine knowledge of relationships from data), describe examples of applications of statistical studies (e.g., in medical research, political decision making, market research), and recognize that conclusions drawn from statistical studies of the same relationship may differ (e.g., conclusions about the effect of increasing jail sentences on crime rates)."

7.2 — Sources of Bias in Surveys
Sampling bias, non-response bias, response bias, measurement bias, household bias; identifying bias in real surveys.
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Bias is a systematic error that causes a statistic to differ from the population parameter in a particular direction. Unlike random variation, bias does not shrink as the sample size grows — a famous example is the 1936 Literary Digest poll, which surveyed 2.4 million people but predicted Alf Landon would defeat Franklin D. Roosevelt because the sample was drawn from telephone directories and car-registration lists (skewed toward wealthier voters). Recognising and minimising bias is a core skill of statistical literacy.

Bias comes in several distinct flavours. Sampling (selection) bias arises when the sampling method systematically excludes part of the population. Non-response bias occurs when people who refuse or fail to respond differ from those who do. Response bias includes question-wording effects, leading questions, social-desirability effects, and interviewer effects. Measurement bias is caused by flawed instruments or definitions. Household bias arises when households of varying sizes are equally weighted, over-representing individuals in small households. Always evaluate a real-world poll by asking: who was sampled, who responded, how the questions were worded, and what was measured.

  • Sampling bias: the sampling frame excludes part of the population.
  • Non-response bias: respondents differ from non-respondents.
  • Response bias: wording, ordering, or social pressure distorts answers.
  • Measurement bias: instruments or definitions skew the data.
  • Household bias: people in larger households are under-represented per-person.
  • Voluntary-response bias: only strongly motivated respondents reply.

Example: Identify the type(s) of bias in each scenario.

(a) A magazine asks readers to mail in their opinions on a new policy. → Voluntary-response bias: only readers with strong feelings (often negative) bother to reply.

(b) A survey on cell-phone use is conducted by calling landlines during the day. → Sampling bias (excludes mobile-only households) and non-response bias (people at work do not answer).

(c) "Don't you agree the school's dress code is too strict?" → Response bias from a leading question.

(d) Body fat is measured using only BMI, which doesn't account for muscle mass. → Measurement bias.

  • Equating "large sample" with "unbiased sample" — a huge biased sample is still biased.
  • Confusing bias (systematic) with sampling variability (random).
  • Ignoring response bias when designing questionnaires — wording matters more than people realise.

Curriculum expectation: Strand C, Expectation C1.2 — "describe characteristics of an effective statistical study, and explain why studies can be biased (e.g., by the wording of the questions in a survey, by the type of sampling used, by the assumptions made), and provide examples of how surveys, questionnaires, or experiments could be designed to avoid bias."

7.3 — Measures of Central Tendency
Mean \( \bar{x} = \tfrac{\sum x_i}{n} \), median, mode; weighted mean; effect of outliers; trimmed mean.
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

A measure of central tendency is a single number that summarises where the bulk of a data set lies. The three classical measures are the mean \(\bar{x} = \tfrac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i\), the median (the middle value once data are ordered), and the mode (the most frequent value). Each captures "centre" differently. The mean is the balance point of the distribution; the median is the 50th percentile; the mode is the peak of the histogram.

The choice between them depends on the shape of the distribution and the presence of outliers. The mean is sensitive to extreme values — a single billionaire raises the mean income of a town dramatically — while the median is resistant: it changes little. For skewed distributions (income, house prices, response times) the median is usually a better summary; for symmetric distributions the mean and median coincide. The weighted mean \(\bar{x} = \tfrac{\sum w_i x_i}{\sum w_i}\) allows some observations to count more than others (e.g. averaging course grades with different credit weights). A trimmed mean discards a fixed percentage of extreme values before averaging, providing a compromise between mean and median.

  • Mean: \(\bar{x} = \dfrac{1}{n}\sum x_i\) — balance point; sensitive to outliers.
  • Median: middle value of sorted data; resistant to outliers.
  • Mode: most frequent value (can be undefined, unique, or multiple).
  • Weighted mean: \(\dfrac{\sum w_i x_i}{\sum w_i}\) — used when observations have different importance.
  • Trimmed mean: discards a fixed % of top and bottom values, then averages.

Example: A student earns 78%, 82%, 90%, and 65% on four assignments. The exam (counted twice as heavily) is 88%. Find the weighted mean.

Weights: each assignment = 1, exam = 2. Total weight = \(1 + 1 + 1 + 1 + 2 = 6\).

\[ \bar{x} = \frac{1(78) + 1(82) + 1(90) + 1(65) + 2(88)}{6} = \frac{78 + 82 + 90 + 65 + 176}{6} = \frac{491}{6} \approx 81.83\%. \]

If we had used the simple mean of all five scores (treating the exam as a single observation), we would have obtained \(\tfrac{78+82+90+65+88}{5} = 80.6\%\) — a different answer.

  • Reporting the mean of skewed data without mentioning the median (which is often more representative).
  • Forgetting to divide by the total weight in a weighted mean — many students divide by the number of items instead.
  • Confusing "mode" with "most representative". For continuous data, every value may appear once.

Curriculum expectation: Strand C, Expectation C2.1 — "recognize that the analysis of one-variable data involves the frequency distribution of values of a variable, and demonstrate an understanding of the appropriate use of different types of representations (e.g., histograms, frequency tables, frequency polygons, cumulative frequency diagrams, and box-and-whisker plots) for one-variable data."

7.4 — Measures of Spread
Range, IQR, variance \( s^2 = \dfrac{\sum(x_i-\bar{x})^2}{n-1} \), standard deviation \( s \); five-number summary, box-and-whisker plots.
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Two data sets with the same mean can look completely different. Measures of spread describe how tightly or loosely values cluster around the centre. The simplest is the range, max − min, but the range depends on only two values and is highly sensitive to outliers. A more informative measure is the interquartile range (IQR) \(Q_3 - Q_1\), the spread of the middle 50% of the data. The IQR is resistant to outliers and pairs naturally with the median for skewed data. The five-number summary (min, \(Q_1\), median, \(Q_3\), max) is the data ingredient for a box-and-whisker plot, an excellent visual.

For approximately symmetric data the standard summary is the sample standard deviation \(s = \sqrt{\dfrac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2}{n - 1}}\). The denominator \(n - 1\) (rather than \(n\)) is called the "degrees of freedom" correction; it makes \(s^2\) an unbiased estimator of the population variance \(\sigma^2\). Standard deviation measures the typical deviation from the mean and shares units with the original data. Outliers strongly affect \(s\). A useful rule of thumb is the IQR-based outlier test: a value is an outlier if it falls below \(Q_1 - 1.5(\text{IQR})\) or above \(Q_3 + 1.5(\text{IQR})\).

  • Range: max − min — fast but outlier-sensitive.
  • IQR: \(Q_3 - Q_1\) — resistant to outliers; pairs with the median.
  • Sample variance: \(s^2 = \dfrac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2}{n - 1}\).
  • Sample standard deviation: \(s = \sqrt{s^2}\) — in original units.
  • Five-number summary: min, \(Q_1\), median, \(Q_3\), max.
  • Outlier rule: outside \([Q_1 - 1.5\,\text{IQR},\;Q_3 + 1.5\,\text{IQR}]\).

Example: Find the sample standard deviation of the data set 4, 8, 6, 5, 3.

Mean: \(\bar{x} = \tfrac{4+8+6+5+3}{5} = \tfrac{26}{5} = 5.2\).

Squared deviations: \((4-5.2)^2 = 1.44\), \((8-5.2)^2 = 7.84\), \((6-5.2)^2 = 0.64\), \((5-5.2)^2 = 0.04\), \((3-5.2)^2 = 4.84\). Sum = \(14.80\).

\[ s^2 = \frac{14.80}{n - 1} = \frac{14.80}{4} = 3.70, \quad s = \sqrt{3.70} \approx 1.924. \]

The data typically deviate about 1.92 units from the mean of 5.2.

  • Dividing by \(n\) instead of \(n - 1\) — common when calculators or formulas labeled "population" are used by mistake.
  • Forgetting to take the square root after computing the variance.
  • Reporting standard deviation for highly skewed data without also reporting the IQR.

Curriculum expectation: Strand C, Expectation C2.2 — "recognize and describe the role of mean, median, mode, and the standard deviation in summarizing data, and select an appropriate measure of central tendency to describe a one-variable data set."

7.5 — Frequency Distributions & Histograms
Class intervals, frequency, relative-frequency, cumulative-frequency tables; histograms and ogives; shape (symmetric, skewed).
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

When a data set has many distinct values, summarising it as a list is useless. A frequency distribution groups data into class intervals (or bins) of equal width and reports how many observations fall in each. Adding a column of relative frequencies (frequencies divided by \(n\)) converts the table into proportions, and a running total of these proportions gives the cumulative frequency — extremely useful for percentile questions.

The graphical companion is the histogram: bars whose widths span each class interval and whose heights equal the frequency or relative frequency. Unlike bar charts (used for categorical data), histogram bars touch because the underlying variable is continuous. The graph of cumulative frequencies versus the upper bound of each class is an ogive; the median, quartiles, and percentiles can be read off an ogive by drawing horizontal reference lines. The shape of a histogram — symmetric, right-skewed (long upper tail), left-skewed (long lower tail), bimodal — tells you whether the mean and median will agree, whether transformations are appropriate, and whether the normal model is plausible.

  • Class interval (bin): a sub-range; should have equal widths.
  • Frequency: count of observations in each bin.
  • Relative frequency: frequency divided by \(n\); proportion.
  • Cumulative frequency: running total of frequencies (or relative frequencies).
  • Histogram: connected bars whose heights are frequencies.
  • Ogive: line plot of cumulative frequency vs upper class boundary.
  • Shape vocabulary: symmetric, skewed left/right, unimodal, bimodal, uniform.

Example: Exam scores (out of 100) for 30 students are summarised as:

40–49: 2; 50–59: 5; 60–69: 8; 70–79: 9; 80–89: 4; 90–99: 2.

Relative frequencies: \(\tfrac{2}{30} \approx 0.067, \tfrac{5}{30} \approx 0.167, \tfrac{8}{30} \approx 0.267, \tfrac{9}{30} = 0.300, \tfrac{4}{30} \approx 0.133, \tfrac{2}{30} \approx 0.067\). Their sum is 1.00.\;\checkmark\)

Cumulative frequencies: 2, 7, 15, 24, 28, 30. The 15th and 16th students fall in the 60–69 and 70–79 bins respectively, so the median is approximately 70. The histogram shows a roughly symmetric distribution centred near the high 60s, with no strong skew.

  • Using unequal class widths without adjusting bar heights to "frequency density". Otherwise, the visual is misleading.
  • Confusing bar charts (categorical, gaps between bars) with histograms (continuous, no gaps).
  • Reading cumulative frequencies from a histogram instead of an ogive.

Curriculum expectation: Strand C, Expectation C2.1 — "recognize that the analysis of one-variable data involves the frequency distribution of values of a variable, and demonstrate an understanding of the appropriate use of different types of representations (e.g., histograms, frequency tables, frequency polygons, cumulative frequency diagrams, and box-and-whisker plots) for one-variable data."
