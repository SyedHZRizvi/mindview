5.1 — The Binomial Distribution
Fixed number of independent Bernoulli trials: \( P(X=k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k} \). Mean \( np \), variance \( np(1-p) \).
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The binomial distribution is the natural model when an experiment consists of a fixed number of independent, identical trials, each producing one of two outcomes (often called "success" and "failure"). Such individual trials are called Bernoulli trials. If we perform \(n\) trials with constant success probability \(p\), the random variable \(X\) = "number of successes" is binomial, denoted \(X \sim B(n, p)\). The probability of exactly \(k\) successes is \( P(X = k) = \binom{n}{k} p^{k}(1-p)^{n-k}\). The factor \(\binom{n}{k}\) counts the orderings of \(k\) successes among \(n\) positions, and \(p^{k}(1-p)^{n-k}\) is the probability of any one such ordering.

Four conditions (BINS) must hold: Binary outcomes, Independent trials, fixed Number of trials, identical Success probability. When these hold, the distribution has the very clean mean and variance \(E(X) = np\) and \(\mathrm{Var}(X) = np(1-p)\); the standard deviation is \(\sigma = \sqrt{np(1-p)}\). The shape of the distribution is symmetric when \(p = 0.5\), right-skewed when \(p < 0.5\), and left-skewed when \(p > 0.5\). For large \(n\), the binomial becomes approximately normal (Topic 6.4).

  • Binomial random variable: \(X \sim B(n, p)\) — count of successes in \(n\) Bernoulli trials.
  • Pmf: \(P(X = k) = \binom{n}{k} p^{k}(1-p)^{n-k}\), \(k = 0, 1, \ldots, n\).
  • Mean and variance: \(\mu = np\), \(\sigma^2 = np(1-p)\).
  • BINS conditions: Binary, Independent, Number fixed, Success probability constant.

Example: A multiple-choice quiz has 8 questions, each with 4 options. A student guesses randomly. Let \(X\) = number of correct answers.

The BINS conditions are satisfied: each question is a Bernoulli trial with \(p = 0.25\), trials are independent, \(n = 8\) is fixed. So \(X \sim B(8, 0.25)\).

\[ P(X = 3) = \binom{8}{3}(0.25)^{3}(0.75)^{5} = 56 \cdot 0.015625 \cdot 0.2373 \approx 0.2076. \]

Mean and standard deviation: \(\mu = 8(0.25) = 2\); \(\sigma = \sqrt{8(0.25)(0.75)} = \sqrt{1.5} \approx 1.225\).

  • Using the binomial model when sampling without replacement — the probability of success then changes between trials, breaking the constant-\(p\) condition (use hypergeometric instead).
  • Forgetting the combinatorial coefficient \(\binom{n}{k}\) and computing only \(p^k(1-p)^{n-k}\), which gives the probability of a specific ordering, not of \(k\) successes.
  • Treating \(p\) as the probability of failure when the problem actually defines it as success.

Curriculum expectation: Strand B, Expectation B2.1 — "recognize conditions (e.g., independent trials) that give rise to a random variable that follows a binomial probability distribution, calculate the probability associated with each value of the random variable, represent the distribution numerically using a table and graphically using a probability histogram, and make connections to the algebraic representation \(P(X = x) = \binom{n}{x} p^x (1-p)^{n-x}\)."

5.2 — The Geometric Distribution
Number of independent Bernoulli trials until the first success: \( P(X = k) = (1-p)^{k-1} p \). Mean \( \tfrac{1}{p} \).
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The geometric distribution arises when we keep repeating a Bernoulli trial until the first success occurs. The random variable \(X\) = "trial number on which the first success appears" can take values 1, 2, 3, …, with no upper bound. The probability \(P(X = k)\) requires \(k - 1\) failures followed by 1 success: \( P(X = k) = (1-p)^{k-1}\,p\) for \(k = 1, 2, 3, \ldots\). Like the binomial, the geometric assumes independent trials with constant success probability \(p\).

Geometric reasoning answers "how long must I wait?" questions: how many phone calls until you get someone home; how many free-throw attempts until your first miss; how many widgets you must inspect before encountering a defect. The mean of a geometric random variable is \(\mu = \tfrac{1}{p}\) — a tidy formula whose intuition is "if you succeed with probability 0.20, expect to need 5 trials on average". The variance is \(\sigma^2 = \tfrac{1-p}{p^2}\). The distribution is always right-skewed: most successes come early, but a long tail of late successes is possible. The geometric has a unique memoryless property: \(P(X > m + n \mid X > m) = P(X > n)\) — past failures provide no information about how much longer you must wait.

  • Geometric random variable: trial of first success, \(P(X = k) = (1-p)^{k-1} p\).
  • Mean: \(\mu = \tfrac{1}{p}\).
  • Variance: \(\sigma^2 = \tfrac{1-p}{p^2}\).
  • Memoryless property: future waiting time is independent of past failures.
  • Cumulative: \(P(X \le k) = 1 - (1-p)^{k}\).

Example: A basketball player makes a free throw with probability \(p = 0.7\). Let \(X\) = number of attempts until her first miss. Find \(P(X = 4)\) and \(E(X)\).

A "miss" is now the "success" event of interest, so the probability of a miss is \(0.3\). For the first miss on attempt 4, we need 3 makes followed by a miss:

\[ P(X = 4) = (0.7)^{3}(0.3) = 0.343 \cdot 0.3 = 0.1029. \]

Expected number of attempts until first miss: \(E(X) = \tfrac{1}{0.3} \approx 3.33\).

  • Mixing up the role of \(p\) — make sure \(p\) is the probability of the event you are waiting for.
  • Treating the geometric distribution as if it had a fixed number of trials (that would be binomial).
  • Confusing the two conventions for "\(X\)": some texts count trials including the success (giving \(\mu = 1/p\)); others count only failures before success (giving \(\mu = (1-p)/p\)). Always read the definition.

Curriculum expectation: Strand B, Expectation B2.2 — "recognize conditions that give rise to discrete random variables that follow uniform, hypergeometric, and geometric probability distributions, and determine probabilities associated with values of these random variables."

5.3 — The Hypergeometric Distribution
Sampling without replacement from a finite population: \( P(X=k) = \dfrac{\binom{r}{k}\binom{N-r}{n-k}}{\binom{N}{n}} \).
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The hypergeometric distribution arises when we sample without replacement from a finite population that has been pre-partitioned into "successes" and "failures". Suppose a population of size \(N\) contains \(r\) successes and \(N - r\) failures, and we randomly select \(n\) items. Let \(X\) = the number of successes in our sample. Then \( P(X = k) = \dfrac{\binom{r}{k}\binom{N - r}{n - k}}{\binom{N}{n}} \) for \(k = \max(0, n - (N-r)), \ldots, \min(n, r)\).

The structure is "(ways to choose \(k\) successes) × (ways to choose \(n - k\) failures) ÷ (ways to choose any \(n\) items)". Unlike the binomial, the trials here are not independent: drawing a success reduces the proportion of successes available on the next draw. As a result, the hypergeometric has the same mean as the binomial, \(E(X) = n \cdot \tfrac{r}{N}\), but a slightly smaller variance, \(\mathrm{Var}(X) = n \cdot \tfrac{r}{N} \cdot \tfrac{N-r}{N} \cdot \tfrac{N-n}{N-1}\). The extra factor \(\tfrac{N-n}{N-1}\) is the finite-population correction. When \(n\) is small compared to \(N\) (a rule of thumb is \(n \le 0.05 N\)), the hypergeometric is well-approximated by the binomial with \(p = r/N\).

  • Hypergeometric variable: successes drawn in a sample of size \(n\) taken without replacement from population \(N\) containing \(r\) successes.
  • Pmf: \(P(X = k) = \dfrac{\binom{r}{k}\binom{N-r}{n-k}}{\binom{N}{n}}\).
  • Mean: \(E(X) = n \cdot \tfrac{r}{N}\).
  • Variance: \(\mathrm{Var}(X) = n \cdot \tfrac{r}{N} \cdot \tfrac{N-r}{N} \cdot \tfrac{N-n}{N-1}\).
  • Without replacement: trials are dependent; the binomial does not apply exactly.

Example: A box contains 15 light bulbs, of which 4 are defective. An inspector randomly selects 5 bulbs without replacement. Find \(P(\text{exactly 2 defective})\).

Here \(N = 15\), \(r = 4\), \(n = 5\), \(k = 2\). Apply the hypergeometric formula:

\[ P(X = 2) = \frac{\binom{4}{2}\binom{11}{3}}{\binom{15}{5}} = \frac{6 \cdot 165}{3\,003} = \frac{990}{3\,003} \approx 0.3297. \]

Mean number of defectives in the sample: \(E(X) = 5 \cdot \tfrac{4}{15} = \tfrac{4}{3} \approx 1.33\).

  • Applying the binomial formula when the sample is large relative to the population — the constant-\(p\) assumption fails.
  • Confusing the values of \(N, r, n, k\). Use a diagram: total / successes-in-population / sample / successes-in-sample.
  • Forgetting that \(k\) cannot exceed \(\min(n, r)\) or fall below \(\max(0, n - (N - r))\) — these are the valid \(k\) values for a non-zero probability.

Curriculum expectation: Strand B, Expectation B2.2 — "recognize conditions that give rise to discrete random variables that follow uniform, hypergeometric, and geometric probability distributions, and determine probabilities associated with values of these random variables."

5.4 — Choosing the Right Distribution
Decision tree: replacement? fixed trials? counting trials or successes? — selecting binomial vs. geometric vs. hypergeometric.
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Once three discrete distributions are in your toolkit, the strategic question is "which one applies?" The answer rests on three diagnostic questions about the experiment: (i) Is sampling done with or without replacement? (ii) Is the number of trials fixed in advance, or are we waiting for the first success? (iii) Are we counting successes in a fixed sample, or counting trials until something happens?

A short decision tree summarises the choices. If the trial count is fixed AND each trial has the same success probability (typically because we sample with replacement or from a very large population) — use the binomial. If the trial count is fixed AND we sample without replacement from a finite population with known sub-population sizes — use the hypergeometric. If we are waiting for the first success and trials are independent with constant \(p\) — use the geometric. A uniform discrete distribution (each value equally likely, like a fair die) applies when no waiting or sampling is involved. Identifying the right model is half the battle; once the model is named, formulas and parameters follow.

  • Uniform (discrete): finite list of equally likely outcomes.
  • Binomial \(B(n,p)\): fixed \(n\), independent trials, constant \(p\).
  • Hypergeometric \(H(N, r, n)\): fixed sample of size \(n\) drawn without replacement from population of \(N\) with \(r\) successes.
  • Geometric: waiting time to first success; independent trials, constant \(p\), no fixed \(n\).
  • Rule of thumb: hypergeometric ≈ binomial when \(n \le 0.05N\).

Example: Classify each scenario.

(a) "A bag has 6 red and 4 blue chips. Five chips are drawn without replacement; \(X\) = number of red chips drawn." Sample without replacement from a finite population → hypergeometric, \(N = 10, r = 6, n = 5\).

(b) "20 free throws are attempted with \(p = 0.8\); \(X\) = number made." Fixed \(n\), independent, constant \(p\) → binomial, \(B(20, 0.8)\).

(c) "A telemarketer calls until someone answers (\(p = 0.10\) per call); \(X\) = number of calls until first answer." Waiting time to first success → geometric, \(p = 0.10\), so \(P(X=k) = (0.9)^{k-1}(0.1)\).

  • Using the binomial when sampling without replacement from a small population.
  • Using the hypergeometric when sampling with replacement — independence holds, so binomial is correct.
  • Forgetting that the geometric has no fixed upper bound on \(X\) — sums over \(k = 1, 2, 3, \ldots\) (a geometric series).

Curriculum expectation: Strand B, Expectation B2.3 — "compare, with technology and using numeric and graphical representations, the probability distributions of discrete random variables (e.g., compare binomial distributions with the same probability of success for increasing numbers of trials; compare the shapes of a hypergeometric distribution and a binomial distribution)."
