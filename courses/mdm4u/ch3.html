3.1 — Combinations: Counting Without Order
Number of subsets of size \( r \) from \( n \): \( C(n,r) = \binom{n}{r} = \dfrac{n!}{r!(n-r)!} \).
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

A combination is a selection of objects where order does not matter. To count the number of ways to choose an unordered subset of size \(r\) from \(n\) distinct objects, start with the permutation count \(P(n,r) = \tfrac{n!}{(n-r)!}\) and then divide by \(r!\) to remove the over-counting created by the \(r!\) orderings of any chosen subset. This produces the celebrated combination formula \(C(n,r) = \binom{n}{r} = \dfrac{n!}{r!\,(n-r)!}\).

The defining contrast with permutations: if the question concerns ordered outcomes (race finishes, locker codes, three different officer titles) use \(P(n,r)\); if it concerns an unordered group (a committee of three, a poker hand, a lottery ticket) use \(C(n,r)\). The notation \(\binom{n}{r}\), read "\(n\) choose \(r\)", emphasises the choice idea. Note that \(\binom{n}{0} = 1\) (the empty subset is the only way to choose nothing) and \(\binom{n}{n} = 1\) (only one way to choose everything).

  • Combination \(C(n,r)\), \(_nC_r\), \(\binom{n}{r}\): unordered selection.
  • Formula: \(\binom{n}{r} = \dfrac{n!}{r!\,(n-r)!}\).
  • Order does not matter: ABC and CBA are the same combination.
  • Relation to permutations: \(P(n,r) = r! \cdot C(n,r)\).

Example: How many five-card poker hands can be dealt from a 52-card deck?

Cards are dealt without regard to order in which they reach your hand, so we use \(C(52, 5)\):

\[ \binom{52}{5} = \frac{52!}{5!\,47!} = \frac{52 \cdot 51 \cdot 50 \cdot 49 \cdot 48}{5!} = \frac{311\,875\,200}{120} = 2\,598\,960. \]

To find the probability of being dealt a flush in spades: \(\binom{13}{5} = 1\,287\). So \(P(\text{spade flush}) = \tfrac{1\,287}{2\,598\,960} \approx 0.000495\).

  • Using \(P(n,r)\) when order does not matter (over-counting by a factor of \(r!\)).
  • Confusing "5 cards" (combination) with "5 letters in a row" (permutation). Read the verb: choose, select, form a committee → combination; arrange, line up, rank, assign roles → permutation.
  • Forgetting that the formula assumes \(n\) distinct objects — repeats require a separate technique (stars-and-bars).

Curriculum expectation: Strand A, Expectation A2.3 — "solve problems, using a variety of strategies, that involve the calculation of the number of combinations of n distinct items taken r at a time."

3.2 — Combination Identities
\( \binom{n}{r} = \binom{n}{n-r} \) and \( \binom{n}{r} + \binom{n}{r+1} = \binom{n+1}{r+1} \); the symmetry property.
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Two identities power most combinatorial manipulations. The first is the symmetry identity \(\binom{n}{r} = \binom{n}{n-r}\). The algebraic proof is one line: \(\tfrac{n!}{r!(n-r)!} = \tfrac{n!}{(n-r)!\,r!}\). The combinatorial proof is more illuminating — choosing which \(r\) objects go into a subset is the same as choosing which \(n - r\) objects go out. This is why \(\binom{10}{7} = \binom{10}{3} = 120\), which is much faster to compute than expanding \(\tfrac{10!}{7!\,3!}\) by brute force.

The second is Pascal's identity: \(\binom{n}{r} + \binom{n}{r+1} = \binom{n+1}{r+1}\). Combinatorially, to form a subset of size \(r+1\) from \(n+1\) people, pick a focal person Alice: either she is in (then choose \(r\) more from the remaining \(n\), giving \(\binom{n}{r}\)) or she is out (choose all \(r+1\) from the other \(n\), giving \(\binom{n}{r+1}\)). The sum is exactly \(\binom{n+1}{r+1}\). This identity is the recursion underlying Pascal's triangle (Topic 3.3): every entry equals the sum of the two entries diagonally above.

  • Symmetry: \(\binom{n}{r} = \binom{n}{n-r}\).
  • Pascal's identity: \(\binom{n}{r} + \binom{n}{r+1} = \binom{n+1}{r+1}\).
  • Boundary values: \(\binom{n}{0} = \binom{n}{n} = 1\).
  • Row-sum: \(\sum_{r=0}^{n} \binom{n}{r} = 2^n\) — the number of subsets of an \(n\)-element set.

Example: Verify Pascal's identity for \(n = 7\), \(r = 3\).

Compute each side:

\[ \binom{7}{3} + \binom{7}{4} = 35 + 35 = 70, \qquad \binom{8}{4} = \frac{8!}{4!\,4!} = 70. \;\checkmark \]

The combinatorial reading: choosing a 4-person team from 8 is either choosing a focal player (then 3 of the remaining 7) or not (then 4 of the remaining 7).

  • Mis-stating Pascal's identity as \(\binom{n}{r} + \binom{n+1}{r} = \binom{n+1}{r+1}\). The second index also increases on the right.
  • Computing \(\binom{20}{18}\) the long way — use symmetry: \(\binom{20}{18} = \binom{20}{2} = 190\).
  • Forgetting that the identities require non-negative integer indices with \(r \le n\).

Curriculum expectation: Strand A, Expectation A2.4 — "make connections, through investigation, between combinations (i.e., n choose r) and Pascal's triangle [e.g., between \(\binom{n}{r}\) and entries in Pascal's triangle]."

3.3 — Pascal's Triangle & Patterns
Constructing Pascal's triangle; recursion, row sums \( 2^n \), hockey-stick identity, Fibonacci numbers in diagonals.
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Pascal's triangle is constructed by placing 1s along both edges and filling each interior entry with the sum of the two entries diagonally above it. Rows are numbered starting from \(n = 0\), and within each row the positions are numbered \(r = 0, 1, \ldots, n\). The entry in row \(n\), position \(r\) is exactly \(\binom{n}{r}\). The construction rule is a direct visualisation of Pascal's identity (Topic 3.2).

The triangle is a hive of numerical patterns. Row sums double: \(\sum_{r=0}^n \binom{n}{r} = 2^n\) (every element of an \(n\)-set is either in or out of a subset). The diagonals deliver familiar sequences: the second diagonal is the natural numbers, the third diagonal is the triangular numbers \(\tfrac{n(n+1)}{2}\). Shallow diagonals — summing entries diagonally up-and-right — give the Fibonacci numbers. The hockey-stick identity \(\sum_{k=r}^{n} \binom{k}{r} = \binom{n+1}{r+1}\) bundles a diagonal run into a single combination. These connections make Pascal's triangle a wonderful playground for "explore-then-explain" investigations required by Ontario's mathematical processes.

  • Construction rule: each entry = sum of the two entries diagonally above.
  • Row sums: row \(n\) sums to \(2^n\); alternating sums give 0 for \(n \ge 1\).
  • Hockey-stick identity: \(\sum_{k=r}^{n}\binom{k}{r} = \binom{n+1}{r+1}\).
  • Fibonacci diagonals: shallow sums up-and-right yield 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, …
  • Symmetry: rows read the same left-to-right and right-to-left.

Example: Use Pascal's triangle to find the number of lattice paths from \((0,0)\) to \((4,3)\) moving only right (R) or up (U).

Each path is a sequence of 7 moves with exactly 4 R's and 3 U's, so the count is \(\binom{7}{3}\). Reading row 7 of Pascal's triangle: 1, 7, 21, 35, 35, 21, 7, 1. The entry at \(r = 3\) is \(35\). So there are 35 distinct shortest paths.

Cross-check: \(\binom{7}{3} = \dfrac{7!}{3!\,4!} = \dfrac{5\,040}{6 \cdot 24} = 35. \;\checkmark\)

  • Indexing rows from 1 instead of 0 — Pascal's triangle starts at row 0 (the single 1).
  • Confusing the row number with the row's largest entry; row \(n\) has \(n+1\) entries.
  • Adding the wrong direction's neighbours when filling the triangle; always two entries diagonally above.

Curriculum expectation: Strand A, Expectation A2.4 — "make connections, through investigation, between combinations (i.e., n choose r) and Pascal's triangle."

3.4 — The Binomial Theorem
Expansion \( (a+b)^n = \displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} a^{n-k} b^k \); finding specific terms.
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The binomial theorem expands \((a+b)^n\) as a finite sum of \(n+1\) terms whose coefficients are the entries of row \(n\) of Pascal's triangle: \[ (a+b)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} a^{n-k} b^k. \] The combinatorial proof is illuminating: when we expand the product \((a+b)(a+b)\cdots(a+b)\) (n factors) using the distributive law, every term of the expansion arises by choosing either \(a\) or \(b\) from each of the \(n\) factors. The term \(a^{n-k}b^k\) appears exactly as many times as we can choose which \(k\) of the \(n\) factors contribute their \(b\); that count is \(\binom{n}{k}\).

Two practical skills follow. First, the general term formula \(t_{k+1} = \binom{n}{k} a^{n-k} b^k\) lets you write down any specific term without expanding the whole expression — invaluable for "find the term containing \(x^7\)" problems. Second, evaluating both sides at \(a = b = 1\) reproduces the row-sum identity \(2^n = \sum \binom{n}{k}\), and \(a = 1, b = -1\) gives the alternating-sum identity. These give algebraic proofs of combinatorial facts.

  • Binomial theorem: \((a+b)^n = \displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{n}\binom{n}{k} a^{n-k} b^k\).
  • General term: \(t_{k+1} = \binom{n}{k}\,a^{n-k}\,b^k\); terms are numbered from \(k = 0\).
  • Binomial coefficient: \(\binom{n}{k}\) — same numbers as row \(n\) of Pascal's triangle.
  • Sign-tracking: when expanding \((a-b)^n\), the sign of \(t_{k+1}\) is \((-1)^k\).

Example: Find the term containing \(x^7\) in the expansion of \((2x - 3)^{10}\).

Use the general-term formula with \(a = 2x\), \(b = -3\), \(n = 10\). We need the power of \(x\) to equal 7, so \(10 - k = 7\), giving \(k = 3\):

\[ t_{4} = \binom{10}{3}(2x)^{7}(-3)^{3} = 120 \cdot 128 x^{7} \cdot (-27) = 120 \cdot 128 \cdot (-27)\, x^{7}. \]

Multiplying: \(120 \cdot 128 = 15\,360\); \(15\,360 \cdot 27 = 414\,720\), and the negative sign gives \(t_{4} = -414\,720\, x^{7}\).

  • Off-by-one in term numbering — \(t_1\) corresponds to \(k = 0\), not \(k = 1\).
  • Forgetting the sign when one of the binomial terms is negative; \(b^k\) carries the sign.
  • Not raising the entire \(a\) (or \(b\)) to its power — for \((2x)^7\), it is \(2^7 \cdot x^7 = 128 x^7\), not \(2 x^7\).

3.5 — Combinations & Probability
Computing probabilities using combinations: \( P = \dfrac{\binom{\text{favourable}}{r}}{\binom{n}{r}} \).
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

When a probability experiment involves selecting an unordered subset (cards from a deck, balls from an urn, applicants for a committee), the classical probability formula \(P(A) = \tfrac{n(A)}{n(S)}\) combines beautifully with combinations. The denominator counts every possible selection — usually a single combination \(\binom{n}{r}\). The numerator counts favourable selections by partitioning the population into "successes" and "failures" and using the multiplicative principle on a product of combinations: \(\binom{\text{successes available}}{\text{successes wanted}} \cdot \binom{\text{failures available}}{\text{failures wanted}}\).

This pattern — a ratio of products of combinations — is precisely the hypergeometric probability formula introduced formally in Chapter 5, but it is worth practising early because it appears in poker hands, raffles, lottery analyses, quality control sampling, and many lottery-style word problems. The conceptual habit is: identify the sub-populations first, then count favourable outcomes as a product, then divide by the total number of equally-likely subsets.

  • Equally likely subsets: assumed when "randomly selected" without replacement.
  • Successes / failures: partition the population so that the event of interest can be expressed by how many of each are chosen.
  • Product of combinations: count favourable outcomes as \(\binom{S}{s}\binom{F}{f}\).
  • Total selections: \(\binom{S+F}{s+f} = \binom{n}{r}\).

Example: A box contains 8 red and 5 blue balls. Four balls are drawn at random without replacement. Find \(P(\text{exactly 2 red})\).

Total ways to draw 4 balls: \(\binom{13}{4} = 715\). Favourable: choose 2 of the 8 red and 2 of the 5 blue:

\[ \binom{8}{2}\binom{5}{2} = 28 \cdot 10 = 280. \]

So \(P(\text{exactly 2 red}) = \dfrac{280}{715} = \dfrac{56}{143} \approx 0.3916.\)

  • Using \(P(n,r)\) instead of \(C(n,r)\) — once we are computing probabilities of unordered selections, order has been removed from both numerator and denominator.
  • Adding instead of multiplying combinations: \(\binom{8}{2} + \binom{5}{2}\) is meaningless here; the events "choose reds" and "choose blues" both happen, so multiply.
  • Forgetting cases when the event allows multiple breakdowns (e.g. "at least 2 red" requires summing \(P(2)+P(3)+P(4)\)).

Curriculum expectation: Strand A, Expectation A2.6 — "solve probability problems involving combinations of simple events, using counting techniques."
