4.1 — Theoretical & Empirical Probability
Theoretical \( P(A) = \dfrac{n(A)}{n(S)} \) for equally likely outcomes; empirical (relative-frequency) probability from data; subjective probability.
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Probability comes in three flavours, each suited to different problem situations. Theoretical (classical) probability applies when a sample space has equally likely outcomes; we simply count favourable outcomes and divide by total outcomes: \(P(A) = \tfrac{n(A)}{n(S)}\). This is the model behind fair dice, fair coins, and random card draws. Empirical (experimental, relative-frequency) probability applies when outcomes are not obviously equally likely; we estimate \(P(A)\) by the relative frequency \(\tfrac{\text{# of times } A \text{ occurred}}{\text{# of trials}}\) and rely on the law of large numbers to ensure that this estimate converges to the true probability as the sample size grows.

Subjective probability is a numerical expression of personal belief — used when an experiment cannot be repeated and theory does not apply ("the probability that Canada wins the next World Cup is 4%"). All three are valid in different contexts, and a competent data manager chooses the right one. Real research projects often combine them: theory predicts a probability; an empirical study tests the prediction; subjective belief frames the question.

  • Theoretical probability: \(P(A) = \dfrac{n(A)}{n(S)}\) when outcomes are equally likely.
  • Empirical probability: \(P(A) \approx \dfrac{f}{n}\) where \(f\) is the observed frequency in \(n\) trials.
  • Subjective probability: a quantified belief, often used for one-time events.
  • Law of large numbers: as \(n \to \infty\), the empirical relative frequency converges to the theoretical probability.

Example: A factory inspects 500 light bulbs and finds 12 defective.

Empirical probability of a defective bulb:

\[ P(\text{defective}) \approx \frac{12}{500} = 0.024 = 2.4\%. \]

Theoretical reasoning is unavailable because the underlying defect rate is unknown. If the company later improves the line and tests 2 000 more bulbs with 30 defects, the updated estimate is \(\tfrac{30}{2000} = 0.015 = 1.5\%\) — a more precise empirical probability because the sample is larger.

  • Applying classical probability to outcomes that are not equally likely (e.g. assuming "winning the lottery" and "losing the lottery" each have probability \(\tfrac{1}{2}\)).
  • Drawing strong conclusions from very small empirical samples — the relative frequency is unreliable until \(n\) is large.
  • Treating subjective probabilities as objective facts.

Curriculum expectation: Strand A, Expectation A1.1 — "recognize the use of probabilities and odds in contexts, and recognize and describe how probabilities and odds are determined using empirical or theoretical methods."

4.2 — Conditional Probability
\( P(B \mid A) = \dfrac{P(A \cap B)}{P(A)} \); restricting the sample space; Bayes-style reasoning.
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Conditional probability asks: given that we already know event \(A\) has happened, what is the chance that event \(B\) also happens? Formally, \(P(B \mid A) = \dfrac{P(A \cap B)}{P(A)}\), provided \(P(A) > 0\). The intuition is to restrict the sample space: instead of considering all of \(S\), we now restrict attention to the slice in which \(A\) occurred, and within that slice we measure the proportion that also satisfies \(B\). Two-way tables make this restriction visible: just zoom in on the row (or column) where the condition holds.

Rearranging the formula gives the general multiplication rule \(P(A \cap B) = P(A)\,P(B \mid A)\). When \(P(B \mid A) = P(B)\), the events are independent and the rule collapses to the simple product. The conditional formula also underlies Bayes-style reasoning: given the conditional probability in one direction, one can sometimes flip it via \(P(A \mid B) = \dfrac{P(A)\,P(B \mid A)}{P(B)}\). This shows up in medical-test interpretation (the chance you actually have a disease given a positive test) and is famously counter-intuitive because most students confuse \(P(B \mid A)\) with \(P(A \mid B)\).

  • Conditional probability: \(P(B \mid A) = \dfrac{P(A \cap B)}{P(A)}\), read "the probability of \(B\) given \(A\)".
  • Multiplication rule: \(P(A \cap B) = P(A)\,P(B \mid A) = P(B)\,P(A \mid B)\).
  • Bayes flip: \(P(A \mid B) = \dfrac{P(A)\,P(B \mid A)}{P(B)}\).
  • Independence test: \(A\) and \(B\) are independent iff \(P(B \mid A) = P(B)\) (equivalently \(P(A \cap B) = P(A)P(B)\)).

Example: At a high school, 60% of students take French and 25% take both French and Spanish. What is the probability that a student takes Spanish given that she takes French?

Let \(F\) = "takes French" and \(S\) = "takes Spanish". We are told \(P(F) = 0.60\) and \(P(F \cap S) = 0.25\). Apply the conditional formula:

\[ P(S \mid F) = \frac{P(F \cap S)}{P(F)} = \frac{0.25}{0.60} \approx 0.417 = 41.7\%. \]

Among the 60% of students who take French, about 42% also take Spanish.

  • Confusing \(P(A \cap B)\) with \(P(A \mid B)\) — the intersection is unconditional; the conditional divides by \(P(B)\).
  • Flipping the condition: \(P(A \mid B) \ne P(B \mid A)\) in general. This is the heart of Bayes' theorem.
  • Using the conditional formula when the condition has probability zero — \(P(B \mid A)\) is undefined if \(P(A) = 0\).

Curriculum expectation: Strand A, Expectation A1.5 — "determine the probability of an event involving combinations of up to 3 simple events, using counting techniques."

4.3 — Discrete Random Variables & Probability Distributions
Definition of discrete random variable \( X \); probability distribution table; conditions \( 0 \le P(X=x) \le 1 \) and \( \sum P(X=x) = 1 \).
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

A random variable is a numerical summary of the outcome of a chance experiment: a function that assigns a real number to each outcome in the sample space. When the random variable can take only countably many values — typically integers — we call it discrete. Common examples include the number of heads in 10 coin tosses, the number of cars passing a sensor in one hour, or the score on a single die.

A probability distribution of a discrete random variable \(X\) lists each value \(x\) the variable can take alongside its probability \(P(X = x)\). Two axioms govern such tables: every probability is in \([0, 1]\) and the probabilities sum to 1. The first ensures probabilities are physically meaningful; the second enforces that something must happen. Together they make the distribution a complete description of all probabilistic statements about \(X\) — for instance \(P(X \ge 3) = \sum_{x \ge 3} P(X = x)\) and \(P(2 \le X \le 4) = P(X = 2) + P(X = 3) + P(X = 4)\).

  • Random variable \(X\): a numerical function on a sample space.
  • Discrete: takes countably many values, often integers.
  • Probability distribution / pmf: a table or formula listing \(P(X = x)\) for every possible \(x\).
  • Conditions: \(0 \le P(X = x) \le 1\) and \(\sum_x P(X = x) = 1\).
  • Cumulative distribution: \(F(x) = P(X \le x)\) — useful for "at most" probabilities.

Example: Let \(X\) = "the sum of two fair six-sided dice". Build the probability distribution.

The 36 ordered outcomes are equally likely. Counting the outcomes that give each sum:

\[ P(X=2)=\tfrac{1}{36},\,P(3)=\tfrac{2}{36},\,P(4)=\tfrac{3}{36},\,P(5)=\tfrac{4}{36},\,P(6)=\tfrac{5}{36},\,P(7)=\tfrac{6}{36}, \]\[ P(8)=\tfrac{5}{36},\,P(9)=\tfrac{4}{36},\,P(10)=\tfrac{3}{36},\,P(11)=\tfrac{2}{36},\,P(12)=\tfrac{1}{36}. \]

Check: the eleven probabilities sum to \(\tfrac{36}{36} = 1.\;\checkmark\) Compute \(P(X \ge 10) = \tfrac{3+2+1}{36} = \tfrac{6}{36} = \tfrac{1}{6}\).

  • Forgetting an outcome (sum probabilities must equal 1; if your table does not, you have miscounted).
  • Confusing the values \(x\) with the probabilities \(P(X = x)\); only the probabilities sum to 1, the values do not.
  • Listing only some values of \(X\) — the distribution must cover every possible value.

Curriculum expectation: Strand B, Expectation B1.1 — "recognize and identify a discrete random variable X (i.e., a variable that takes on a finite or countable number of values), and generate a probability distribution by calculating the probabilities associated with all values of a random variable."

4.4 — Expected Value
\( E(X) = \displaystyle\sum_{i} x_i \cdot P(X = x_i) \); fair games; insurance pricing; long-run averages.
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The expected value \(E(X)\) of a discrete random variable is the long-run average value we would observe if we repeated the experiment many times. It is computed as the probability-weighted sum of the values: \(E(X) = \mu_X = \sum_{i} x_i \cdot P(X = x_i)\). The term "expected" can mislead — \(E(X)\) need not be a value that \(X\) ever takes (the expected number of heads in 1 toss of a fair coin is 0.5). What it does describe is the centre of gravity of the distribution: place a unit mass at each \(x_i\) of size \(P(X = x_i)\), and \(E(X)\) is the balance point.

Expected value is the standard tool for evaluating fair games, insurance pricing, lottery analysis, and decision-making under uncertainty. A game is "fair" when \(E(\text{net winnings}) = 0\); favourable when \(E > 0\); unfavourable when \(E < 0\). Insurance companies set premiums so that, accounting for administrative costs, their expected payout per customer is less than the premium charged. The same logic applies to investment decisions: even if a payoff is huge, a tiny probability can make the expected return negative.

  • Expected value: \(E(X) = \mu_X = \sum_i x_i\,P(X = x_i)\).
  • Fair game: \(E(\text{net winnings}) = 0\).
  • Linearity: \(E(aX + b) = a\,E(X) + b\) and \(E(X + Y) = E(X) + E(Y)\).
  • Long-run interpretation: the sample mean of many trials converges to \(E(X)\) by the law of large numbers.

Example: A carnival game costs $2 to play. You win $10 with probability 0.10, $1 with probability 0.30, and nothing otherwise. Is this game fair?

Let \(X\) = net winnings (after paying the $2 fee). Then \(X = 8\) with probability 0.10, \(X = -1\) with probability 0.30, and \(X = -2\) with probability 0.60.

\[ E(X) = 8(0.10) + (-1)(0.30) + (-2)(0.60) = 0.80 - 0.30 - 1.20 = -0.70. \]

The expected loss is $0.70 per play. Over the long run, the player loses 70 cents per game — the game is unfair (favours the house).

  • Forgetting to subtract the cost of playing when computing expected net winnings.
  • Adding probabilities (instead of multiplying \(x_i \cdot P(X = x_i)\)).
  • Interpreting "expected" as "most likely". The expected value is the mean, not the mode.

Curriculum expectation: Strand B, Expectation B1.2 — "calculate the expected value for a given probability distribution (i.e., using \(E(X) = \sum x P(X = x)\)), interpret the expected value in applications, and make connections between the expected value and the weighted mean of the values of the discrete random variable."

4.5 — Variance & Standard Deviation of Discrete Random Variables
\( \mathrm{Var}(X) = E(X^2) - [E(X)]^2 \); \( \sigma_X = \sqrt{\mathrm{Var}(X)} \).
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The expected value tells us where the centre of the distribution lies, but two distributions can share a mean and yet behave very differently. Variance measures how spread out the values of \(X\) are around \(\mu_X\): \( \mathrm{Var}(X) = \sigma_X^2 = E\bigl[(X - \mu_X)^2\bigr] = \sum_i (x_i - \mu_X)^2\,P(X = x_i)\). Squaring the deviations makes positive and negative deviations contribute equally and emphasises large gaps. Since the units of \(\sigma_X^2\) are the units of \(X\) squared, we typically report the standard deviation \(\sigma_X = \sqrt{\mathrm{Var}(X)}\), which is back in the same units as \(X\).

The computational shortcut \(\mathrm{Var}(X) = E(X^2) - \bigl[E(X)\bigr]^2\) is algebraically equivalent and almost always easier in practice: compute \(E(X)\), compute \(E(X^2) = \sum x_i^2 P(X = x_i)\), and subtract. The standard deviation has a probabilistic interpretation through Chebyshev's inequality and (for nice distributions) the empirical rule: most values lie within 2 or 3 standard deviations of the mean. Two distributions with the same mean and standard deviation are essentially "as risky" in financial terms; standard deviation is therefore the canonical measure of investment risk.

  • Variance: \(\sigma_X^2 = \sum_i (x_i - \mu_X)^2 P(X = x_i) = E(X^2) - [E(X)]^2\).
  • Standard deviation: \(\sigma_X = \sqrt{\mathrm{Var}(X)}\) — in the same units as \(X\).
  • Scaling: \(\mathrm{Var}(aX + b) = a^2\,\mathrm{Var}(X)\); shifts do not affect spread.
  • Independence: \(\mathrm{Var}(X + Y) = \mathrm{Var}(X) + \mathrm{Var}(Y)\) only when \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent.

Example: \(X\) takes values 1, 2, 3 with probabilities 0.5, 0.3, 0.2 respectively. Find \(\mathrm{Var}(X)\) and \(\sigma_X\).

First, \(E(X) = 1(0.5) + 2(0.3) + 3(0.2) = 0.5 + 0.6 + 0.6 = 1.7\).

Next, \(E(X^2) = 1^2(0.5) + 2^2(0.3) + 3^2(0.2) = 0.5 + 1.2 + 1.8 = 3.5\).

\[ \mathrm{Var}(X) = E(X^2) - [E(X)]^2 = 3.5 - (1.7)^2 = 3.5 - 2.89 = 0.61. \]

\(\sigma_X = \sqrt{0.61} \approx 0.781.\) The values of \(X\) typically deviate about 0.78 from the mean of 1.70.

  • Computing \([E(X)]^2\) as \(E(X^2)\) — these are different. \(E(X^2) \ge [E(X)]^2\) always.
  • Forgetting to square the deviations (giving a sum that always equals 0).
  • Using \(\mathrm{Var}(X + Y) = \mathrm{Var}(X) + \mathrm{Var}(Y)\) when \(X\) and \(Y\) are dependent.

Curriculum expectation: Strand B, Expectation B1.2 — "calculate the expected value for a given probability distribution, interpret the expected value in applications, and make connections between the expected value and the weighted mean of the values of the discrete random variable."
