1.1 — Power Functions
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

A power function has the form \(f(x)=ax^{n}\), where \(a\in\mathbb{R}\) and \(n\) is a whole number. Power functions are the building blocks of every polynomial you will study this year — so understanding how the leading term \(ax^{n}\) controls a graph's overall shape is the foundation everything else rests on. The key intuition is that for large \(|x|\), the term with the highest power dominates: it dictates the function's end behaviour, while smaller-degree terms only fine-tune the middle of the graph.

Two characteristics determine the end behaviour of \(y=ax^{n}\): the parity of \(n\) (whether \(n\) is even or odd) and the sign of \(a\). Even-degree power functions are symmetric about the \(y\)-axis; their two ends point the same direction. Odd-degree power functions are symmetric about the origin; their two ends point in opposite directions. Knowing this lets you predict the rough silhouette of a polynomial before ever plotting a point.

You will describe end behaviour using arrow notation such as "as \(x\to\infty,\;f(x)\to-\infty\)". This vocabulary is identical to what you will see in calculus, so building fluency now pays dividends in MCV4U.

  • Power function — a function of the form \(f(x)=ax^{n}\) with whole-number exponent.
  • Degree — the value of the exponent \(n\); it controls the maximum number of turning points and \(x\)-intercepts.
  • Leading coefficient — the constant \(a\); a positive \(a\) keeps the right end pointing up, a negative \(a\) reflects the graph vertically.
  • End behaviour — the direction of \(f(x)\) as \(x\to\pm\infty\), determined entirely by the leading term in a polynomial.

Worked Example — Classifying end behaviour

Describe the end behaviour of \(f(x)=-3x^{5}\).

Step 1. Identify degree and leading coefficient: \(n=5\) (odd), \(a=-3\) (negative).

Step 2. Odd degree means the two ends behave oppositely. Negative leading coefficient flips the parent \(y=x^{5}\) vertically.

Step 3. The parent \(y=x^{5}\) goes from quadrant III to quadrant I. A vertical reflection sends it from quadrant II to quadrant IV.

Conclusion: as \(x\to-\infty,\;f(x)\to\infty\); as \(x\to\infty,\;f(x)\to-\infty\). The graph starts in quadrant II and ends in quadrant IV.

Worked Example — Domain, range, symmetry

State the domain, range, and symmetry of \(g(x)=2x^{4}\).

Step 1. Any whole-number power is defined for every real input, so \(\text{Domain}=\{x\in\mathbb{R}\}\).

Step 2. With even degree and \(a>0\), the graph opens upward with minimum value \(g(0)=0\). Range \(=\{y\in\mathbb{R}\mid y\ge 0\}\).

Step 3. Check symmetry: \(g(-x)=2(-x)^{4}=2x^{4}=g(x)\), so \(g\) is even and symmetric about the \(y\)-axis.

  • Do not confuse "degree" with "leading coefficient" — they control different features.
  • A negative \(a\) flips end behaviour vertically; it does not change whether the function is even or odd in parity.
  • End behaviour is determined by the highest-degree term only; lower-degree terms are irrelevant as \(|x|\to\infty\).

1.2 — Stating Intervals
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

When you describe a function's behaviour — where it is increasing, where it is positive, where it is undefined — you need a precise way to talk about regions of the real line. Mathematicians use three equivalent notations: interval notation, set-builder notation, and inequalities. Each has a place. Interval notation is compact and standard in calculus textbooks; set-builder is precise and forces you to name the variable; inequalities feel intuitive for hand-drawn number lines.

The single most important distinction is whether an endpoint is included or excluded. Square brackets [ ] include the endpoint, round brackets ( ) exclude it. The symbols \(\infty\) and \(-\infty\) are not numbers — they describe unbounded behaviour — so they always get round brackets. Getting this notation right matters because in calculus, missing a single endpoint can change whether a maximum exists or whether a function is continuous.

You will use intervals to state domains, ranges, increasing/decreasing intervals, and intervals of positive/negative values for every function this year. Master the notation now and the rest of the course goes much more smoothly.

  • Interval notation — \([a,b]\), \((a,b)\), \([a,b)\), \((a,b]\), \((-\infty,b)\), etc., with square brackets for inclusion and round brackets for exclusion.
  • Set-builder notation — \(\{x\in\mathbb{R}\mid a\le x<b\}\), read "the set of real \(x\) such that…".
  • Union — the symbol \(\cup\) combines disjoint intervals into a single set, e.g., \((-\infty,-2)\cup(3,\infty)\).
  • Open / closed interval — an interval is open when both endpoints are excluded, closed when both are included.

Worked Example — Translating between notations

Express "\(x\) is greater than \(-4\) and at most \(7\)" in interval and set-builder notation.

Step 1. "Greater than \(-4\)" excludes \(-4\); "at most \(7\)" includes \(7\).

Step 2. Inequality: \(-4<x\le 7\).

Step 3. Interval: \((-4,\,7]\). Set-builder: \(\{x\in\mathbb{R}\mid -4<x\le 7\}\).

Worked Example — Domain of a graph with two pieces

A function is graphed for all \(x\) less than \(-1\) and again for all \(x\) at least \(2\). State the domain.

Step 1. Left piece: \(x<-1\) gives \((-\infty,-1)\).

Step 2. Right piece: \(x\ge 2\) gives \([2,\infty)\).

Step 3. Combine with a union: \(\text{Domain}=(-\infty,-1)\cup[2,\infty)\).

  • Always put the smaller value first: \([3,7)\) is correct, \([7,3)\) is meaningless.
  • Use \(\cup\) (union), not a comma, to glue disjoint intervals together.
  • Infinity is never closed: write \((-\infty,5]\), never \([-\infty,5]\).

Curriculum expectation: Strand D, Expectation D1.1 — "recognize and describe key features of the graphs of functions… including domain and range, intervals of increase and decrease, and zeros."

1.3 — Characteristics of Polynomial Functions
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

A polynomial function of degree \(n\) has the standard form \(f(x)=a_{n}x^{n}+a_{n-1}x^{n-1}+\dots+a_{1}x+a_{0}\), where \(a_{n}\ne 0\) and every exponent is a non-negative integer. From this single expression you can read off five characteristics that completely sketch the graph's silhouette: degree, leading coefficient, end behaviour, number of \(x\)-intercepts, and number of turning points. Investing time here means that later — when you graph a quintic or analyze a rational function — you can describe its shape in seconds.

The relationship to remember is this: a polynomial of degree \(n\) has at most \(n\) real zeros and at most \(n-1\) turning points. The phrase "at most" matters: a cubic could have one real zero or three, and either zero or two turning points. End behaviour, however, is fully determined by the leading term, so it is always predictable.

You should also be comfortable identifying when an expression is not a polynomial. Negative exponents, fractional exponents, radicals over the variable, and variables in denominators all disqualify an expression. Spotting these quickly will save errors later.

  • Polynomial function — a sum of terms \(a_{k}x^{k}\) with non-negative integer exponents and real coefficients.
  • Turning point — a point where the function changes from increasing to decreasing or vice versa; at most \(n-1\) of them for degree \(n\).
  • Zero (root, \(x\)-intercept) — a value of \(x\) where \(f(x)=0\); at most \(n\) for degree \(n\).
  • Constant term — the term \(a_{0}\), equal to \(f(0)\); the \(y\)-intercept.

Worked Example — Full characteristics from an equation

For \(f(x)=-2x^{4}+5x^{2}-x+3\), state the degree, leading coefficient, end behaviour, maximum number of zeros, maximum number of turning points, and \(y\)-intercept.

Step 1. Degree \(=4\) (highest exponent); leading coefficient \(=-2\).

Step 2. Even degree with negative leading coefficient: both ends point down. As \(x\to\pm\infty,\;f(x)\to-\infty\).

Step 3. Maximum zeros: \(4\). Maximum turning points: \(4-1=3\).

Step 4. \(y\)-intercept: \(f(0)=3\), so \((0,3)\).

Worked Example — Is it a polynomial?

Classify each: (a) \(p(x)=4x^{3}-\tfrac{1}{2}x+7\); (b) \(q(x)=x^{2}+\sqrt{x}-1\); (c) \(r(x)=\dfrac{3}{x}+2x\).

(a) All exponents are non-negative integers, so \(p\) is a cubic polynomial.

(b) \(\sqrt{x}=x^{1/2}\) is a fractional exponent — not a polynomial.

(c) \(\tfrac{3}{x}=3x^{-1}\) has a negative exponent — not a polynomial.

  • Maximum turning points is \(n-1\), not \(n\) — a frequent off-by-one mistake.
  • A polynomial can have fewer real zeros than its degree if some zeros are complex.
  • The constant term is the \(y\)-intercept; do not confuse it with the leading coefficient.

1.4 — Finite Differences
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Given a table of values from a polynomial sampled at equally spaced \(x\)-values, the method of finite differences lets you recover the polynomial's degree — and even its leading coefficient — without graphing or factoring. The idea is simple: subtract consecutive \(y\)-values to get first differences; subtract consecutive first differences to get second differences; and keep going until the differences become constant. The "level" at which they go constant equals the degree of the polynomial.

This works because differentiation lowers the degree of a polynomial by one, and finite differences are the discrete cousin of differentiation. So a cubic flattens out to a constant on the third difference, a quartic on the fourth, and so on. The leading coefficient is then recovered from the formula \(a_{n}=\dfrac{\Delta_{n}}{n!\,h^{n}}\), where \(\Delta_{n}\) is the constant \(n\)-th difference and \(h\) is the common spacing between \(x\)-values.

The method is also a powerful diagnostic: if no level of differences ever becomes constant, the data is not polynomial — it might be exponential, trigonometric, or rational. Finite differences therefore give you a fast way to test what kind of function you are looking at.

  • First differences — \(\Delta y_{k}=y_{k+1}-y_{k}\); constant first differences indicate a linear function.
  • \(n\)-th differences — the differences of the \((n-1)\)-th differences; constant at level \(n\) indicates degree \(n\).
  • Common spacing \(h\) — the constant gap between consecutive \(x\)-values; finite differences require this.
  • Leading coefficient formula — \(a_{n}=\dfrac{\Delta_{n}}{n!\,h^{n}}\) recovers the highest-power coefficient.

Worked Example — Degree from a table

Find the degree and leading coefficient of the polynomial whose values at \(x=0,1,2,3,4\) are \(1,2,11,34,77\).

Step 1. First differences: \(2-1,11-2,34-11,77-34=1,9,23,43\).

Step 2. Second differences: \(9-1,23-9,43-23=8,14,20\).

Step 3. Third differences: \(14-8,20-14=6,6\). Constant at level 3, so degree \(=3\).

Step 4. Leading coefficient: \(a_{3}=\dfrac{6}{3!\cdot 1^{3}}=\dfrac{6}{6}=1\).

Worked Example — Identifying non-polynomial data

For \(y=2,4,8,16,32\) at \(x=0,1,2,3,4\), test whether the data is polynomial.

Step 1. First differences: \(2,4,8,16\). Second: \(2,4,8\). Third: \(2,4\). Differences keep doubling, never constant.

Step 2. Conclude: the data is not polynomial. In fact \(y=2^{x+1}\), an exponential.

  • The method fails if \(x\)-values are not equally spaced — always check the common difference first.
  • Remember \(n!\,h^{n}\) in the leading-coefficient formula, not just \(n!\).
  • Sign matters: if all the constant differences are negative, the leading coefficient is negative.

Curriculum expectation: Strand C, Expectation C1.3 — "describe key features of the graphs of polynomial functions… and identify the connection between the degree of a polynomial function and the corresponding constant finite differences."

1.5 — Graphing from Factored Form
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

When a polynomial is written in factored form, \(f(x)=a(x-r_{1})^{m_{1}}(x-r_{2})^{m_{2}}\dots(x-r_{k})^{m_{k}}\), it tells you exactly where the graph crosses or touches the \(x\)-axis and how it behaves at each zero. The exponent on each factor is the multiplicity of that zero, and multiplicity controls a key visual cue. With multiplicity 1 the curve passes straight through the \(x\)-axis; with multiplicity 2 it bounces off; with multiplicity 3 it flattens out and squeezes through with an inflection.

To produce a quick sketch from factored form, you only need four ingredients: the zeros (with their multiplicities), the \(y\)-intercept (evaluate \(f(0)\)), the end behaviour (from the expanded leading term), and the sign of the function on each interval between zeros. Together these give you a graph accurate enough for any analysis question in this course — no graphing calculator required.

This factored-form perspective also previews polynomial inequalities: the sign of \(f\) changes at zeros of odd multiplicity and stays the same across zeros of even multiplicity. That rule will become the engine of the sign-chart method in Chapter 2.

  • Factored form — \(f(x)=a(x-r_{1})^{m_{1}}\dots(x-r_{k})^{m_{k}}\); each \(r_{i}\) is a zero and each \(m_{i}\) its multiplicity.
  • Multiplicity — the exponent on a factor; controls cross-through (odd) vs. bounce (even) behaviour at the zero.
  • Sign chart — a number line marking zeros and the sign of \(f(x)\) in each resulting interval.
  • \(y\)-intercept — \(f(0)\); the constant produced when every \(x\) is set to zero in factored form.

Worked Example — Sketch from factored form

Sketch \(f(x)=-2(x+3)(x-1)^{2}(x-4)\).

Step 1. Zeros: \(x=-3\) (mult 1, cross), \(x=1\) (mult 2, bounce), \(x=4\) (mult 1, cross).

Step 2. Degree: \(1+2+1=4\). Leading coefficient: \(-2(1)(1)(1)=-2\). Even degree, negative \(a\) — both ends point down.

Step 3. \(y\)-intercept: \(f(0)=-2(3)(1)(-4)=24\), so \((0,24)\).

Step 4. Sign chart: test \(x=-4\to-2(-)(+)(-)=-\); \(x=0\to+\); \(x=2\to-\); \(x=5\to+\)? Recheck \(x=5\): \(-2(8)(16)(1)=-256\). Both ends down, so the right side is negative — \(f(5)\) negative. ✓

Step 5. Curve enters from \(-\infty\), rises through \(x=-3\), passes through \((0,24)\), bounces at \(x=1\), comes back down, and exits through \(x=4\) into \(-\infty\).

Worked Example — Equation from a graph

A cubic touches the \(x\)-axis at \(x=2\) and crosses at \(x=-1\); the \(y\)-intercept is \(-4\). Find the equation.

Step 1. Multiplicity 2 at \(x=2\) and multiplicity 1 at \(x=-1\): \(f(x)=a(x-2)^{2}(x+1)\).

Step 2. Use \(f(0)=-4\): \(a(-2)^{2}(1)=4a=-4\Rightarrow a=-1\).

Step 3. Equation: \(f(x)=-(x-2)^{2}(x+1)\).

  • Multiplicity 2 means bounce, not cross — drawing a cross-through is the most common sketching error.
  • When finding \(a\), include the sign — a missing negative ruins the whole graph.
  • End behaviour comes from the expanded leading term \(a\cdot x^{m_{1}+m_{2}+\dots}\), not just \(a\).

Curriculum expectation: Strand C, Expectation C1.4 — "distinguish polynomial functions from sinusoidal and exponential functions… and compare and contrast the graphs of various polynomial functions with the graphs of other types of functions."

1.6 — Even and Odd Functions
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Symmetry is one of the most powerful shortcuts in mathematics. If a function has a symmetry, you only need to understand half of it — the other half is free. In MHF4U we focus on two algebraic symmetries: even functions, which satisfy \(f(-x)=f(x)\), and odd functions, which satisfy \(f(-x)=-f(x)\). The names match what you see in polynomial powers: \(x^{2}, x^{4}, x^{6}\) are even functions, while \(x, x^{3}, x^{5}\) are odd functions.

Geometrically, an even function is mirror-symmetric across the \(y\)-axis: folding the page along the \(y\)-axis lines up the two halves. An odd function has point-symmetry through the origin: rotating the graph 180° around \((0,0)\) lands it back on itself. Most functions are neither even nor odd, and that is fine — but when symmetry exists, it tells you about zeros (odd functions, if defined at 0, must have \(f(0)=0\)) and about long-run behaviour.

The test is purely algebraic: replace every \(x\) with \(-x\), simplify, and compare to \(f(x)\) and \(-f(x)\). Be careful with signs — a sign error makes a function look like the wrong parity.

  • Even function — satisfies \(f(-x)=f(x)\) for all \(x\) in the domain; graph is symmetric about the \(y\)-axis.
  • Odd function — satisfies \(f(-x)=-f(x)\) for all \(x\) in the domain; graph is symmetric about the origin.
  • Neither — most functions are neither even nor odd; this is the default outcome.

Worked Example — Classify a polynomial

Determine whether \(f(x)=3x^{4}-5x^{2}+7\) is even, odd, or neither.

Step 1. Compute \(f(-x)=3(-x)^{4}-5(-x)^{2}+7=3x^{4}-5x^{2}+7\).

Step 2. Compare: \(f(-x)=f(x)\). The function is even — every exponent on \(x\) is even, including the constant (which is \(x^{0}\)).

Worked Example — A mixed polynomial

Classify \(g(x)=x^{3}-4x\).

Step 1. \(g(-x)=(-x)^{3}-4(-x)=-x^{3}+4x=-(x^{3}-4x)=-g(x)\).

Step 2. So \(g(-x)=-g(x)\): the function is odd. Check at \(x=0\): \(g(0)=0\), as required for an odd function defined at 0.

Worked Example — Neither

Classify \(h(x)=x^{2}+x\).

Step 1. \(h(-x)=x^{2}-x\). Compare to \(h(x)=x^{2}+x\) — not equal.

Step 2. Compare to \(-h(x)=-x^{2}-x\) — also not equal.

Step 3. The function is neither even nor odd. Mixing even and odd power terms breaks both symmetries.

  • For polynomials, the shortcut is: only even powers \(\Rightarrow\) even; only odd powers \(\Rightarrow\) odd; any mix \(\Rightarrow\) neither (provided the constant term is non-zero for the odd test).
  • Watch parity of signs when raising to powers: \((-x)^{4}=x^{4}\), but \((-x)^{3}=-x^{3}\).
  • An odd function defined at 0 must pass through the origin. If \(f(0)\ne 0\) the function cannot be odd.

Curriculum expectation: Strand D, Expectation D1.5 — "determine, through investigation, and compare the properties of even and odd polynomial functions… and determine whether a given polynomial function is even, odd, or neither."

1.7 — Transformations of Power Functions
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Every transformed power function in this course can be written in the form \(g(x)=a\,f\!\bigl(k(x-d)\bigr)+c\). The four parameters \(a\), \(k\), \(d\), and \(c\) control four independent geometric actions: \(a\) stretches or reflects vertically, \(k\) stretches or reflects horizontally, \(d\) translates horizontally, and \(c\) translates vertically. Once you can extract these from any equation, you can graph the transformed function from the parent function's key points without re-plotting.

Two cautions are worth flagging up front. First, vertical stretches "feel" intuitive because the multiplier acts on \(y\): doubling \(a\) doubles every \(y\)-value. Horizontal stretches act the opposite way: dividing \(x\) by \(k\) outside the bracket means each point's \(x\)-coordinate shrinks by a factor of \(\tfrac{1}{|k|}\). Second, you must factor \(k\) out before reading the horizontal shift — \(g(x)=f(2x-6)\) is \(f\bigl(2(x-3)\bigr)\), so the shift is \(3\) right, not \(6\).

The order of operations matters when sketching by hand: apply stretches and reflections before translations. Any key point \((x,y)\) on the parent maps to \(\bigl(\tfrac{x}{k}+d,\;ay+c\bigr)\) on the image.

  • \(a\) — vertical stretch by \(|a|\), with reflection in the \(x\)-axis if \(a<0\).
  • \(k\) — horizontal stretch by \(\tfrac{1}{|k|}\), with reflection in the \(y\)-axis if \(k<0\).
  • \(d\) — horizontal translation: right by \(d\) if \(d>0\), left if \(d<0\).
  • \(c\) — vertical translation: up by \(c\) if \(c>0\), down if \(c<0\).
  • Mapping rule — \((x,y)\to\bigl(\tfrac{x}{k}+d,\;ay+c\bigr)\) transforms each parent point.

Worked Example — Reading parameters

Describe the transformations of \(g(x)=-\tfrac{1}{2}\bigl(2(x+3)\bigr)^{3}-1\) relative to \(y=x^{3}\).

Step 1. Identify: \(a=-\tfrac{1}{2}\), \(k=2\), \(d=-3\), \(c=-1\).

Step 2. \(a=-\tfrac{1}{2}\): vertical compression by \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) and reflection in the \(x\)-axis.

Step 3. \(k=2\): horizontal compression by \(\tfrac{1}{2}\).

Step 4. \(d=-3\): shift 3 units left. \(c=-1\): shift 1 unit down.

Worked Example — Transforming a point

The point \((2,16)\) lies on \(y=x^{4}\). Find its image on \(y=3(x-1)^{4}+5\).

Step 1. Parameters: \(a=3\), \(k=1\), \(d=1\), \(c=5\).

Step 2. Apply mapping rule \((x,y)\to\bigl(\tfrac{x}{k}+d,\;ay+c\bigr)=\bigl(\tfrac{2}{1}+1,\;3\cdot 16+5\bigr)=(3,53)\).

Step 3. Check: substitute into the new equation. \(3(3-1)^{4}+5=3\cdot 16+5=53\). ✓

  • Always factor \(k\) out of the bracket before reading \(d\); failing to do so misplaces the horizontal shift.
  • Horizontal transformations are inverses of what they look like — a factor of \(k=3\) compresses, it does not stretch.
  • Stretch/reflect first, then translate. Switching the order will move points to the wrong place.

1.8 — Average Rate of Change
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The average rate of change of a function \(f\) on the interval \([a,b]\) is the slope of the secant line connecting the points \(\bigl(a,f(a)\bigr)\) and \(\bigl(b,f(b)\bigr)\). Algebraically, \(\text{AROC}=\dfrac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}\). This is just the familiar rise-over-run from Grade 9, but now applied to curves. The big idea is that even when a function is not linear, you can talk meaningfully about how fast it is changing on a stretch of its domain.

Average rate of change is the bridge to calculus. The instantaneous rate of change you study in section 1.9 is the limit of average rates as the interval shrinks. So mastering this computation — including handling units in word problems — sets you up for derivatives next year.

Units are critical in applied problems. If \(f\) measures height in metres and \(x\) measures time in seconds, then the average rate of change has units of metres per second. Always carry the units through; an answer of "5" is meaningless without them.

  • Secant line — a straight line passing through two points on a curve.
  • Average rate of change — slope of the secant: \(\dfrac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}\).
  • Difference quotient — the algebraic form \(\dfrac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\); generalises the AROC formula.

Worked Example — Numerical AROC

Find the average rate of change of \(f(x)=x^{2}-3x\) on the interval \([1,4]\).

Step 1. \(f(1)=1-3=-2\). \(f(4)=16-12=4\).

Step 2. \(\text{AROC}=\dfrac{f(4)-f(1)}{4-1}=\dfrac{4-(-2)}{3}=\dfrac{6}{3}=2\).

Step 3. Interpretation: the function increases by an average of 2 units of \(y\) for every 1 unit of \(x\) on this interval.

Worked Example — Word problem with units

A baseball's height (in metres) at time \(t\) seconds is \(h(t)=-4.9t^{2}+20t+1\). Find the average vertical velocity over \([0,2]\) seconds.

Step 1. \(h(0)=1\); \(h(2)=-4.9(4)+40+1=-19.6+41=21.4\) m.

Step 2. \(\text{AROC}=\dfrac{21.4-1}{2-0}=\dfrac{20.4}{2}=10.2\) m/s.

Step 3. The baseball rises an average of 10.2 metres per second over the first two seconds.

  • Order matters in the formula — be consistent: top and bottom should both go "later minus earlier".
  • A negative AROC means the function decreases on average — not that the function is itself negative.
  • Always state units in applied problems; a numerical answer is incomplete.

Curriculum expectation: Strand D, Expectation D1.7 — "make connections, through investigation, between average rate of change and slope of a secant on the graph of a function, and between instantaneous rate of change and slope of a tangent at a point on a graph."

1.9 — Instantaneous Rate of Change
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The instantaneous rate of change (IROC) of a function at a point is the slope of the tangent line there — geometrically the line that "just touches" the curve at that single point. Without the machinery of calculus, you cannot compute this slope exactly, but you can estimate it to any accuracy by computing average rates of change over shorter and shorter intervals containing the point. This is your first taste of the limit concept.

Two standard estimation methods appear in MHF4U. The preceding-interval and following-interval approach picks a tiny \(h\) and computes \(\dfrac{f(a)-f(a-h)}{h}\) or \(\dfrac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}\). The centred-interval approach uses \(\dfrac{f(a+h)-f(a-h)}{2h}\), which averages the two one-sided estimates and is usually more accurate.

The IROC is the same numerical object that you will call the derivative \(f'(a)\) in MCV4U. Right now you compute it numerically; soon you will compute it symbolically. Understanding the limit-from-secants idea now makes the calculus transition seamless.

  • Tangent line — the unique straight line touching a curve at exactly one nearby point; its slope is the IROC.
  • Centred difference quotient — \(\dfrac{f(a+h)-f(a-h)}{2h}\); a symmetric, more accurate IROC estimate.
  • Limit — the value approached by the AROC as the interval shrinks to zero; this is the exact IROC.

Worked Example — IROC by squeezing intervals

Estimate the IROC of \(f(x)=x^{2}\) at \(x=3\) using \(h=0.1\) and \(h=0.01\).

Step 1. \(h=0.1\): \(\dfrac{f(3.1)-f(2.9)}{0.2}=\dfrac{9.61-8.41}{0.2}=\dfrac{1.20}{0.2}=6\).

Step 2. \(h=0.01\): \(\dfrac{f(3.01)-f(2.99)}{0.02}=\dfrac{9.0601-8.9401}{0.02}=\dfrac{0.12}{0.02}=6\).

Step 3. The IROC at \(x=3\) appears to equal exactly \(6\). (This matches the exact derivative \(2x\) at \(x=3\).)

Worked Example — Velocity at an instant

For \(h(t)=-4.9t^{2}+20t+1\), estimate the instantaneous velocity at \(t=2\) s using \(h=0.001\).

Step 1. \(h(2.001)=-4.9(2.001)^{2}+20(2.001)+1\). Compute \((2.001)^{2}=4.004001\), so \(-4.9(4.004001)=-19.6196049\), giving \(-19.6196049+40.02+1=21.4003951\).

Step 2. \(h(1.999)=-4.9(3.996001)+19.99+1=-19.5804049+20.99=21.4095951\).

Step 3. Centred: \(\dfrac{21.4003951-21.4095951}{0.002}=\dfrac{-0.0092}{0.002}=-4.6\) m/s. The ball is moving downward at about 4.6 m/s at \(t=2\).

  • The IROC is a slope, not a function value — do not confuse \(f(a)\) with \(f'(a)\).
  • One-sided estimates can be biased; the centred difference is usually closer to the true value.
  • For applied problems, the sign of the IROC tells you the direction of change (increasing vs. decreasing).

2.1 — Remainder Theorem & Synthetic Division
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

To solve polynomial equations of degree 3 or higher, you need a systematic way to divide a polynomial by a linear factor and find its roots. The Remainder Theorem states that when a polynomial \(P(x)\) is divided by \((x-c)\), the remainder is exactly \(P(c)\). This is profoundly useful: evaluating \(P\) at \(c\) is far easier than carrying out the long division, and it tells you instantly whether \((x-c)\) divides cleanly into \(P(x)\) — namely, when \(P(c)=0\).

When you do need the quotient (not just the remainder), synthetic division provides a quick shortcut for dividing by a linear binomial. It is just the bookkeeping of polynomial long division compressed into a single row of arithmetic. Synthetic division gives you both the quotient and the remainder, and it sets up factoring of higher-degree polynomials in section 2.2.

The Division Algorithm ties everything together: \(P(x)=(x-c)\,Q(x)+R\), where \(Q(x)\) is the quotient polynomial and \(R\) is the constant remainder. Understanding this identity is what makes both theorems intuitive.

  • Remainder Theorem — for any polynomial \(P(x)\), dividing by \((x-c)\) gives remainder \(P(c)\).
  • Division algorithm — \(P(x)=D(x)\,Q(x)+R(x)\), with \(\deg R<\deg D\).
  • Synthetic division — a streamlined algorithm for dividing \(P(x)\) by \((x-c)\), using only the coefficients.
  • Quotient polynomial \(Q(x)\) — the polynomial obtained from the division, with degree one less than \(P\).

Worked Example — Remainder via the theorem

Find the remainder when \(P(x)=2x^{3}-5x^{2}+x-3\) is divided by \(x-2\).

Step 1. By the Remainder Theorem, the remainder equals \(P(2)\).

Step 2. \(P(2)=2(8)-5(4)+2-3=16-20+2-3=-5\).

Step 3. So the remainder is \(-5\), and \((x-2)\) does not divide \(P(x)\) evenly.

Worked Example — Synthetic division

Divide \(P(x)=x^{3}-4x^{2}+5x-2\) by \((x-1)\) using synthetic division.

Step 1. Coefficients of \(P\): \(1,\,-4,\,5,\,-2\). Divisor root: \(c=1\).

Step 2. Bring down \(1\). Multiply: \(1\cdot 1=1\); add to \(-4\): \(-3\). Multiply: \(-3\cdot 1=-3\); add to \(5\): \(2\). Multiply: \(2\cdot 1=2\); add to \(-2\): \(0\).

Step 3. Bottom row: \(1,\,-3,\,2,\,0\). Quotient \(Q(x)=x^{2}-3x+2\), remainder \(R=0\).

Step 4. Verify: \((x-1)(x^{2}-3x+2)=x^{3}-3x^{2}+2x-x^{2}+3x-2=x^{3}-4x^{2}+5x-2\). ✓

  • For divisor \((x+3)\), use \(c=-3\) in the Remainder Theorem — the sign flips.
  • Synthetic division requires the polynomial to be in descending order; insert \(0\) for any missing degree.
  • The bottom-row entries shift in degree by 1: a cubic dividend gives a quadratic quotient.

2.2 — Factor Theorem & Sum/Difference of Cubes
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The Factor Theorem is a direct consequence of the Remainder Theorem: \((x-c)\) is a factor of \(P(x)\) if and only if \(P(c)=0\). This converts the problem of finding a factor into the easier problem of finding a zero. The Integral Zero Theorem narrows the search: every rational integer zero of a polynomial with integer coefficients must be a divisor of the constant term. The Rational Zero Theorem extends this to fractions \(\tfrac{p}{q}\), where \(p\) divides the constant term and \(q\) divides the leading coefficient.

Once you have one zero \(c\), divide by \((x-c)\) using synthetic division to drop the degree by one. Repeat on the quotient until you reach a quadratic, which you can then factor or solve with the quadratic formula. This factor-and-divide cycle is the engine for solving cubics, quartics, and beyond.

Two special-product identities expand your toolkit. The sum of cubes factors as \(a^{3}+b^{3}=(a+b)(a^{2}-ab+b^{2})\), and the difference of cubes as \(a^{3}-b^{3}=(a-b)(a^{2}+ab+b^{2})\). Memorising these saves time and reveals zeros that trial-and-error would miss.

  • Factor Theorem — \((x-c)\) is a factor of \(P(x)\) iff \(P(c)=0\).
  • Integral Zero Theorem — integer zeros of \(P(x)\) (with integer coefficients) divide the constant term.
  • Rational Zero Theorem — rational zeros take the form \(\tfrac{p}{q}\) where \(p\mid a_{0}\), \(q\mid a_{n}\).
  • Sum / difference of cubes — \(a^{3}\pm b^{3}=(a\pm b)(a^{2}\mp ab+b^{2})\).

Worked Example — Factor a cubic completely

Factor \(P(x)=x^{3}-2x^{2}-5x+6\) completely over the reals.

Step 1. Integer Zero Theorem: candidates are \(\pm1,\pm2,\pm3,\pm6\).

Step 2. Test \(x=1\): \(1-2-5+6=0\). So \((x-1)\) is a factor.

Step 3. Synthetic division by \(1\) on \(1,-2,-5,6\) gives \(1,-1,-6,0\), so \(Q(x)=x^{2}-x-6\).

Step 4. Factor the quadratic: \(x^{2}-x-6=(x-3)(x+2)\).

Step 5. Complete factorization: \(P(x)=(x-1)(x-3)(x+2)\).

Worked Example — Difference of cubes

Factor \(8x^{3}-27\) completely.

Step 1. Recognise \(8x^{3}=(2x)^{3}\) and \(27=3^{3}\), so \(a=2x\), \(b=3\).

Step 2. Apply the identity: \(8x^{3}-27=(2x-3)\bigl((2x)^{2}+(2x)(3)+3^{2}\bigr)=(2x-3)(4x^{2}+6x+9)\).

Step 3. Check the quadratic factor for further real roots: discriminant \(36-144=-108<0\), so it is irreducible over the reals.

  • Watch the middle sign in the cubic identity: it is opposite to the binomial sign — easy to flip in haste.
  • Test small integer candidates first; positive and negative one and two are usually quickest to evaluate.
  • Do not stop after the first factor — the question almost always asks for complete factoring.

2.3 — Solving Polynomial Equations
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

To solve a polynomial equation \(P(x)=0\) you find every value of \(x\) that makes the polynomial vanish. Geometrically these are the \(x\)-intercepts of \(y=P(x)\). Algebraically the strategy is always the same: move everything to one side so the equation reads \(\text{polynomial}=0\), factor completely, and apply the Zero Product Property — if a product equals zero, at least one factor must equal zero.

The factoring step is where the work lives. For quadratics use any of the familiar tools (common factoring, trinomial factoring, difference of squares, quadratic formula, completing the square). For cubics and quartics use the Factor Theorem to find one zero, divide out the linear factor, and recurse on the lower-degree quotient. The Fundamental Theorem of Algebra guarantees that a degree-\(n\) polynomial has exactly \(n\) zeros when counted with multiplicity over the complex numbers, although in this course you usually report only real solutions.

If a quadratic factor is irreducible over the reals (discriminant negative), state that there are no further real solutions — do not invent ones. Multiplicities still count, so report repeated roots clearly: "\(x=2\) (double root)".

  • Zero Product Property — if \(A\cdot B=0\), then \(A=0\) or \(B=0\) (or both).
  • Fundamental Theorem of Algebra — a degree-\(n\) polynomial has exactly \(n\) complex zeros counted with multiplicity.
  • Discriminant — for \(ax^{2}+bx+c\), the value \(b^{2}-4ac\); negative means no real roots.

Worked Example — Solve a cubic

Solve \(x^{3}+x^{2}-4x-4=0\).

Step 1. Try grouping: \(x^{2}(x+1)-4(x+1)=(x+1)(x^{2}-4)\).

Step 2. Factor \(x^{2}-4=(x-2)(x+2)\).

Step 3. Complete factoring: \((x+1)(x-2)(x+2)=0\).

Step 4. Zero Product Property: \(x=-1,\,2,\,-2\).

Worked Example — Solve a quartic

Solve \(2x^{4}-5x^{3}-8x^{2}+17x-6=0\).

Step 1. Rational Zero Theorem: candidates are \(\pm 1,\pm 2,\pm 3,\pm 6,\pm\tfrac{1}{2},\pm\tfrac{3}{2}\).

Step 2. Test \(x=2\): \(2(16)-5(8)-8(4)+17(2)-6=32-40-32+34-6=-12\). Not zero.

Step 3. Test \(x=3\): \(2(81)-5(27)-8(9)+17(3)-6=162-135-72+51-6=0\). So \((x-3)\) is a factor.

Step 4. Synthetic division by \(3\) on \(2,-5,-8,17,-6\): \(2,1,-5,2,0\). Quotient \(2x^{3}+x^{2}-5x+2\).

Step 5. Test \(x=1\) in the cubic: \(2+1-5+2=0\). Divide by \(1\): \(2,3,-2,0\). Quotient \(2x^{2}+3x-2=(2x-1)(x+2)\).

Step 6. Solutions: \(x=3,\,1,\,\tfrac{1}{2},\,-2\).

  • Move everything to one side first — students often forget and try to factor an unequal expression.
  • Repeated factors give repeated roots; count multiplicity to match the Fundamental Theorem total.
  • Check each candidate solution by substituting it back into the original equation.

Curriculum expectation: Strand C, Expectation C3.3 — "determine, through investigation using technology, the connection between the real roots of a polynomial equation and the \(x\)-intercepts of the graph of the corresponding polynomial function."

2.4 — Family of Polynomial Functions
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

A family of polynomial functions is the collection of all polynomials sharing the same zeros. Symbolically the family is \(f(x)=a(x-r_{1})(x-r_{2})\cdots(x-r_{k})\), where \(a\) is any non-zero real number. Every member of the family has identical \(x\)-intercepts; what changes between members is the vertical stretch — controlled entirely by \(a\) — which also affects the \(y\)-intercept and every \(y\)-value.

This idea matters because knowing the zeros alone is not enough to determine the polynomial. You need one additional point — usually the \(y\)-intercept or any other specified point — to pin down \(a\). This is the modelling skeleton: zeros give shape, a single anchor point gives scale.

In modelling word problems you will frequently be told a curve has zeros at certain values and passes through a given point. The recipe is identical to section 1.5 sketching, just in reverse: write the family, substitute the anchor point to find \(a\), and write the unique solution.

  • Family of polynomial functions — all polynomials sharing the same zeros, differing only by the parameter \(a\).
  • Parameter \(a\) — controls vertical stretch and the overall sign; pinned down by one extra point.
  • Anchor point — any non-zero point on the curve used to solve for \(a\).

Worked Example — Quartic family from zeros

Write the family of quartic polynomials with zeros at \(x=-3,\,-1,\,1,\,4\), then find the member passing through \((0,12)\).

Step 1. Family: \(f(x)=a(x+3)(x+1)(x-1)(x-4)\).

Step 2. Use \((0,12)\): \(f(0)=a(3)(1)(-1)(-4)=12a=12\Rightarrow a=1\).

Step 3. Specific member: \(f(x)=(x+3)(x+1)(x-1)(x-4)\).

Worked Example — Repeated zero in a family

A cubic has a double root at \(x=2\) and a simple root at \(x=-1\). It passes through \((1,-4)\). Find the cubic.

Step 1. Family: \(f(x)=a(x-2)^{2}(x+1)\).

Step 2. Use \((1,-4)\): \(a(1-2)^{2}(1+1)=a(1)(2)=2a=-4\Rightarrow a=-2\).

Step 3. Cubic: \(f(x)=-2(x-2)^{2}(x+1)\).

  • Do not solve for \(a\) using a zero — substituting a zero just gives \(0=0\), no information.
  • If the anchor point happens to be a zero, you need a different point on the curve.
  • Keep multiplicities visible in the family — \((x-2)^{2}\), not \((x-2)\), when there is a double root.

Curriculum expectation: Strand C, Expectation C1.7 — "determine, through investigation, and compare the properties of even and odd polynomial functions… and determine an equation to represent a given graph of a polynomial function, using methods appropriate to the situation."

2.5 — Polynomial Inequalities
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

A polynomial inequality asks for all \(x\) where \(P(x)>0\), \(P(x)<0\), \(P(x)\ge 0\), or \(P(x)\le 0\). The most reliable method is the sign chart: factor the polynomial completely to find its zeros, mark them on a number line, and test a sample point in each resulting interval to determine the sign of \(P\) on that interval. Read the solution off the chart in the direction the inequality requires.

Two refinements speed things up. First, the sign of a polynomial can only change at a real zero, and only when that zero has odd multiplicity — even multiplicity bounces, so the sign stays the same. Second, when you write the answer, use the right endpoint conventions: square brackets for strict-or-equal (\(\le, \ge\)), round brackets for strict (\(<, >\)).

Avoid the tempting shortcut of "multiplying through" by a variable — variables can be negative or zero, which can flip or break the inequality. Always rearrange to put zero on one side and factor instead.

  • Sign chart — a number line marked with the polynomial's zeros and the sign of \(P\) on each subinterval.
  • Test point — any convenient value inside an interval, substituted into the factored form to find the sign.
  • Inclusive vs strict inequality — \(\ge, \le\) include the zeros; \(>, <\) exclude them.

Worked Example — Cubic inequality

Solve \(x^{3}-x^{2}-6x>0\).

Step 1. Factor: \(x(x^{2}-x-6)=x(x-3)(x+2)\).

Step 2. Zeros: \(x=-2,\,0,\,3\). Intervals: \((-\infty,-2),(-2,0),(0,3),(3,\infty)\).

Step 3. Test points: \(x=-3:(-3)(-6)(-1)=-18\;(-)\). \(x=-1:(-1)(-4)(1)=4\;(+)\). \(x=1:(1)(-2)(3)=-6\;(-)\). \(x=4:(4)(1)(6)=24\;(+)\).

Step 4. Want \(P>0\): take positive intervals. Solution: \(x\in(-2,0)\cup(3,\infty)\).

Worked Example — Inequality with repeated factor

Solve \((x-1)^{2}(x+3)\le 0\).

Step 1. Zeros: \(x=1\) (multiplicity 2), \(x=-3\) (multiplicity 1).

Step 2. Sign analysis: \((x-1)^{2}\ge 0\) always. So the sign is governed by \((x+3)\): negative for \(x<-3\), positive for \(x>-3\).

Step 3. Want \(\le 0\): need \((x+3)\le 0\) or \((x-1)^{2}=0\). That gives \(x\le -3\) together with the isolated point \(x=1\).

Step 4. Solution: \(x\in(-\infty,-3]\cup\{1\}\).

  • Never multiply both sides by a variable expression — its sign may change.
  • Even-multiplicity zeros are sign-preserving — easy to miss and they often appear on tests.
  • Read the inequality direction at the end: \(P>0\) wants positive intervals; \(P<0\) wants negative ones.

Curriculum expectation: Strand C, Expectation C3.4 — "solve polynomial inequalities (e.g., solve \(f(x)\ge 0\), where \(f(x)=x^{3}-x^{2}+3x-9\)) by graphing the corresponding functions, with or without technology, and identifying the intervals for which the inequality holds."

3.1 — Introduction to Rational Functions
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

A rational function is any function of the form \(f(x)=\dfrac{P(x)}{Q(x)}\), where \(P\) and \(Q\) are polynomials and \(Q\) is not the zero polynomial. The new feature compared with polynomials is the denominator: any value of \(x\) that makes \(Q(x)=0\) is excluded from the domain. These excluded values produce one of two graphical features — a vertical asymptote or a hole — and distinguishing the two is the central skill of Chapter 3.

If a factor of \(Q(x)\) does not cancel with a matching factor of \(P(x)\), the excluded \(x\)-value produces a vertical asymptote: the curve shoots to \(\pm\infty\) as \(x\) approaches that value. If the factor cancels completely, the excluded value produces a hole — a single point removed from an otherwise continuous curve. End behaviour produces horizontal or oblique asymptotes depending on the degree comparison.

Approaching rational functions for the first time, it helps to think of them as polynomials with controlled defects. Most of the curve looks polynomial-like; the asymptotes and holes are localised disruptions you can describe precisely.

  • Rational function — \(f(x)=\dfrac{P(x)}{Q(x)}\), with polynomials \(P,Q\) and \(Q\not\equiv 0\).
  • Vertical asymptote — a vertical line \(x=a\) approached but never crossed by the graph, occurring at non-cancelling zeros of the denominator.
  • Hole — a single removed point, occurring where a factor of the denominator cancels with a factor of the numerator.
  • Horizontal asymptote — a horizontal line approached by the graph as \(x\to\pm\infty\); its location depends on the degrees of \(P\) and \(Q\).

Worked Example — Domain and feature classification

For \(f(x)=\dfrac{x-2}{x^{2}-4}\), state the domain and classify each excluded value.

Step 1. Factor: \(f(x)=\dfrac{x-2}{(x-2)(x+2)}\).

Step 2. Denominator zeros at \(x=2\) and \(x=-2\); both excluded from domain.

Step 3. The factor \((x-2)\) cancels, leaving \(f(x)=\dfrac{1}{x+2}\) for \(x\ne 2\). So \(x=2\) is a hole; \(x=-2\) is a vertical asymptote.

Step 4. Domain: \(\{x\in\mathbb{R}\mid x\ne 2,\,x\ne -2\}\).

Worked Example — End-behaviour comparison

For \(g(x)=\dfrac{3x^{2}-1}{x^{2}+5}\), determine the horizontal asymptote.

Step 1. Compare degrees: numerator degree \(=2\), denominator degree \(=2\). Equal degrees.

Step 2. Horizontal asymptote at the ratio of leading coefficients: \(y=\dfrac{3}{1}=3\).

  • Always factor before declaring asymptotes — cancellations turn would-be asymptotes into holes.
  • The domain comes from the original denominator, not the simplified one.
  • If numerator degree exceeds denominator degree by 1 you get an oblique asymptote, not horizontal.

3.2 — Reciprocal of Linear Functions
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The simplest rational function is the reciprocal of a linear function: \(f(x)=\dfrac{1}{ax+b}\). Its graph is a translated, possibly stretched version of the parent reciprocal \(y=\dfrac{1}{x}\). Two asymptotes appear: a vertical asymptote where the denominator is zero (\(x=-\tfrac{b}{a}\)) and a horizontal asymptote at \(y=0\) (because the numerator's degree is lower than the denominator's). Together they cut the plane into two regions, with a hyperbolic branch in each.

The reciprocal flips magnitudes: where the linear function is large, the reciprocal is small; where the linear function is near zero, the reciprocal is enormous. As you approach the vertical asymptote, the sign of the linear factor determines whether the reciprocal heads to \(+\infty\) or \(-\infty\) — this is the most-asked detail.

Domain, range, asymptotes, and a clean sketch with the correct branch orientations are all you need to fully describe these functions. Practising the sign analysis near the vertical asymptote will pay off in later sections where you build up to more complicated rationals.

  • Reciprocal function — \(f(x)=\dfrac{1}{g(x)}\); each \(y\)-value is the multiplicative inverse of \(g(x)\).
  • Vertical asymptote (VA) — at \(x=-\tfrac{b}{a}\) for \(f(x)=\dfrac{1}{ax+b}\).
  • Horizontal asymptote (HA) — at \(y=0\) because the degree of the numerator is less than the degree of the denominator.
  • One-sided behaviour — \(f\to+\infty\) or \(-\infty\) at the VA, determined by the sign of the linear factor.

Worked Example — Asymptotes and intercepts

Analyse \(f(x)=\dfrac{1}{2x-6}\).

Step 1. VA: \(2x-6=0\Rightarrow x=3\).

Step 2. HA: \(y=0\) (numerator constant, denominator linear).

Step 3. Intercepts: no \(x\)-intercept (numerator never zero). \(y\)-intercept: \(f(0)=\dfrac{1}{-6}=-\dfrac{1}{6}\).

Step 4. Domain: \(\{x\in\mathbb{R}\mid x\ne 3\}\). Range: \(\{y\in\mathbb{R}\mid y\ne 0\}\).

Step 5. Sign near VA: as \(x\to 3^{-}\), \(2x-6\to 0^{-}\), so \(f\to-\infty\); as \(x\to 3^{+}\), \(f\to+\infty\).

Worked Example — Behaviour at far ends

Describe end behaviour of \(g(x)=\dfrac{1}{-x+4}\) as \(x\to\pm\infty\).

Step 1. As \(x\to\infty\), \(-x+4\to-\infty\), so \(g(x)\to 0^{-}\).

Step 2. As \(x\to-\infty\), \(-x+4\to\infty\), so \(g(x)\to 0^{+}\).

Step 3. The graph approaches \(y=0\) from below on the right and from above on the left.

  • The reciprocal has no \(x\)-intercept — the numerator is a non-zero constant.
  • Always check the sign on each side of the VA; reversing the signs places the branches in the wrong quadrants.
  • Domain excludes the VA but range excludes the HA value — these are different exclusions.

3.3 — Reciprocal of Quadratic Functions
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

When you reciprocate a quadratic, three distinct shapes can appear depending on the discriminant. If the quadratic has two distinct real roots, the reciprocal has two vertical asymptotes — one at each root — and a hump or pit between them. If it has a single repeated root, there is one vertical asymptote and the reciprocal opens upward or downward on both sides like a "tower". If the quadratic has no real roots (discriminant negative), the denominator is never zero, so there is no vertical asymptote — the reciprocal is a single smooth curve.

In every case the horizontal asymptote is \(y=0\) (numerator degree is less than denominator degree by at least one). The maximum or minimum of the reciprocal occurs at the vertex of the quadratic: when the quadratic hits its minimum (lowest \(y\)), the reciprocal hits its maximum (highest \(y\)), and vice versa, after attention to sign.

Sketching is a sign-tracking exercise. Determine the sign of the original quadratic in each region between roots, then reciprocate the magnitude while keeping the same sign. Tall narrow towers near the asymptotes, low flat curves elsewhere.

  • Reciprocal of a quadratic — \(f(x)=\dfrac{1}{ax^{2}+bx+c}\); shape determined by the discriminant.
  • Discriminant cases — two VAs, one VA, or no VA, depending on \(b^{2}-4ac\).
  • Reciprocal of vertex — vertex \((h,k)\) of the quadratic maps to \(\bigl(h,\tfrac{1}{k}\bigr)\) on the reciprocal, an extremum.

Worked Example — Two-root case

Sketch \(f(x)=\dfrac{1}{x^{2}-4}\).

Step 1. Factor denominator: \(x^{2}-4=(x-2)(x+2)\). Zeros at \(x=\pm 2\). Two VAs.

Step 2. HA: \(y=0\).

Step 3. Sign chart of denominator: positive on \((-\infty,-2)\) and \((2,\infty)\); negative on \((-2,2)\). Reciprocal mirrors this sign.

Step 4. Vertex of \(x^{2}-4\) at \((0,-4)\), so reciprocal has a maximum (least negative) at \((0,-\tfrac{1}{4})\).

Step 5. Sketch: tall asymptotic towers at \(x=\pm 2\); a downward hump between them peaking at \((0,-\tfrac{1}{4})\); curve approaches \(y=0\) from above on outer branches.

Worked Example — No-real-root case

Analyse \(g(x)=\dfrac{1}{x^{2}+1}\).

Step 1. Denominator zero: \(x^{2}+1=0\) has no real solutions. No VA.

Step 2. Domain: all real numbers.

Step 3. Vertex of \(x^{2}+1\) at \((0,1)\); reciprocal maximum at \((0,1)\). As \(x\to\pm\infty\), \(g\to 0^{+}\).

Step 4. Range: \((0,1]\). Curve is a smooth bell shape symmetric about the \(y\)-axis.

  • Always test the discriminant first — it determines the entire structure.
  • Vertex of the quadratic gives an extremum of the reciprocal; do not confuse the two \(y\)-values.
  • Sign of the reciprocal matches the sign of the original quadratic in each interval.

3.4 — Linear over Linear Rational Functions
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

A linear-over-linear rational function has the form \(f(x)=\dfrac{ax+b}{cx+d}\) with \(c\ne 0\). Its graph is a transformed reciprocal — that is, a translated and stretched hyperbola — because polynomial long division converts the expression into \(f(x)=A+\dfrac{B}{cx+d}\) for some constants \(A,B\). From this form you can read off every feature: a vertical asymptote where the denominator vanishes, a horizontal asymptote at \(y=A=\tfrac{a}{c}\) (the ratio of leading coefficients), and the usual intercept calculations.

The \(x\)-intercept comes from solving the numerator equal to zero: \(ax+b=0\) gives \(x=-\tfrac{b}{a}\). The \(y\)-intercept is just \(f(0)=\tfrac{b}{d}\). Together with the asymptotes these give a fully-determined sketch — only two pieces of information beyond the asymptotes are needed.

One subtlety: if the numerator and denominator share a common factor, the function simplifies and the would-be VA becomes a hole. Always look for common factors before declaring asymptotes.

  • Linear over linear — \(f(x)=\dfrac{ax+b}{cx+d}\), \(c\ne 0\), \(ad\ne bc\).
  • Horizontal asymptote — \(y=\tfrac{a}{c}\), the ratio of the leading coefficients.
  • Vertical asymptote — \(x=-\tfrac{d}{c}\), where the denominator vanishes.
  • Centre of the hyperbola — the intersection of the asymptotes, \(\bigl(-\tfrac{d}{c},\tfrac{a}{c}\bigr)\).

Worked Example — Full analysis

For \(f(x)=\dfrac{2x+1}{x-3}\), find both asymptotes, both intercepts, the domain, and the range.

Step 1. VA: \(x-3=0\Rightarrow x=3\).

Step 2. HA: ratio of leading coefficients \(=\tfrac{2}{1}=2\), so \(y=2\).

Step 3. \(x\)-intercept: \(2x+1=0\Rightarrow x=-\tfrac{1}{2}\).

Step 4. \(y\)-intercept: \(f(0)=\dfrac{1}{-3}=-\dfrac{1}{3}\).

Step 5. Domain: \(\{x\in\mathbb{R}\mid x\ne 3\}\). Range: \(\{y\in\mathbb{R}\mid y\ne 2\}\).

Worked Example — Rewriting in transformation form

Rewrite \(f(x)=\dfrac{3x-7}{x-2}\) in the form \(A+\dfrac{B}{x-2}\).

Step 1. Polynomial long division: \(3x-7=3(x-2)-1\).

Step 2. \(f(x)=\dfrac{3(x-2)-1}{x-2}=3-\dfrac{1}{x-2}\).

Step 3. Read off: HA at \(y=3\), VA at \(x=2\), reflection in the \(x\)-axis (because of the minus sign), no stretch beyond magnitude 1.

  • Watch for common factors — they convert VAs into holes.
  • The HA is the ratio of leading coefficients, not the ratio of any other coefficients.
  • Range excludes only the HA value, not zero — a common confusion with reciprocal functions.

3.5 — Holes and Oblique Asymptotes
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Two features distinguish more complex rational functions from the simple ones in 3.2–3.4: holes and oblique asymptotes. A hole appears at any \(x\)-value where a factor cancels between numerator and denominator. The function is undefined there, but the limit exists — geometrically you draw an open circle at the missing point. To locate the hole's \(y\)-coordinate, simplify the function and evaluate the simplified form at the excluded \(x\)-value.

An oblique (slant) asymptote appears when the degree of the numerator is exactly one more than the degree of the denominator. As \(x\to\pm\infty\) the function behaves like a non-horizontal line. To find the equation of the oblique asymptote, perform polynomial long division: the quotient (ignoring the fractional remainder) is the line \(y=mx+b\) the function approaches.

Knowing how to combine these features with VAs and intercepts means you can sketch any quadratic-over-linear or cubic-over-quadratic rational function from first principles.

  • Hole — a single removed point where a factor cancels in numerator and denominator.
  • Oblique asymptote — a line \(y=mx+b\) approached at infinity, present when \(\deg P=\deg Q+1\).
  • Polynomial long division — the method for splitting \(P/Q\) into quotient \(+\) remainder; quotient gives the oblique asymptote.

Worked Example — Locate a hole

For \(f(x)=\dfrac{x^{2}-x-6}{x-3}\), find any holes, asymptotes, and intercepts.

Step 1. Factor numerator: \(x^{2}-x-6=(x-3)(x+2)\). So \(f(x)=\dfrac{(x-3)(x+2)}{x-3}=x+2\) for \(x\ne 3\).

Step 2. Hole at \(x=3\). Plug into simplified form: \(y=3+2=5\). Hole at \((3,5)\).

Step 3. No VA (the would-be VA cancelled into a hole). The simplified function is a line: \(y=x+2\) with a hole.

Worked Example — Oblique asymptote

Find the oblique asymptote of \(g(x)=\dfrac{x^{2}+3x-1}{x-1}\).

Step 1. Degrees: numerator 2, denominator 1. Difference is 1, so an oblique asymptote exists.

Step 2. Long division: divide \(x^{2}+3x-1\) by \(x-1\). \(x^{2}\div x=x\); multiply \(x(x-1)=x^{2}-x\); subtract: \(4x-1\). \(4x\div x=4\); multiply \(4(x-1)=4x-4\); subtract: \(3\).

Step 3. So \(g(x)=x+4+\dfrac{3}{x-1}\). Oblique asymptote: \(y=x+4\).

Step 4. VA at \(x=1\). No HA (different oblique structure replaces it).

  • Always factor and cancel first — holes hide behind un-simplified expressions.
  • Use the simplified function to compute the hole's \(y\)-coordinate, not the original.
  • If the degree difference is greater than 1, the function has no horizontal or oblique asymptote — only a higher-order curve at infinity.

3.6 — Rational Equations and Inequalities
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

A rational equation contains rational expressions with variable denominators. To solve, identify all values of \(x\) that make any denominator zero — these are restrictions that must be excluded from your final answer. Then multiply both sides by the LCD (least common denominator) to clear fractions, solve the resulting polynomial equation, and check each candidate solution against the restrictions. Any candidate equal to a restricted value is extraneous and must be rejected.

Rational inequalities require more care because multiplying by a denominator may flip the inequality sign — and you cannot know in advance whether the denominator is positive or negative. The safe method is to move everything to one side, combine into a single rational expression, and build a sign chart using the zeros of the numerator and the zeros of the denominator as critical values. Then read off the intervals where the inequality holds, taking care to exclude denominator zeros even when the inequality is non-strict.

The zeros of the numerator are candidate solutions (included for \(\le\) and \(\ge\), excluded for \(<\) and \(>\)); zeros of the denominator are always excluded.

  • Restriction — a value that makes any denominator zero; must be excluded from the domain.
  • Extraneous solution — a candidate that satisfies the cleared equation but violates a restriction; must be rejected.
  • Sign chart for rational inequality — uses numerator zeros and denominator zeros as critical values.

Worked Example — Rational equation

Solve \(\dfrac{2}{x-1}+\dfrac{1}{x+2}=1\).

Step 1. Restrictions: \(x\ne 1,\,x\ne -2\). LCD \(=(x-1)(x+2)\).

Step 2. Multiply: \(2(x+2)+1(x-1)=(x-1)(x+2)\).

Step 3. Simplify: \(2x+4+x-1=x^{2}+x-2\), so \(3x+3=x^{2}+x-2\).

Step 4. Rearrange: \(x^{2}-2x-5=0\). Quadratic formula: \(x=\dfrac{2\pm\sqrt{4+20}}{2}=1\pm\sqrt{6}\).

Step 5. Both \(1+\sqrt{6}\approx 3.45\) and \(1-\sqrt{6}\approx -1.45\) are different from the restrictions. Both accepted.

Worked Example — Rational inequality

Solve \(\dfrac{x+1}{x-2}\le 0\).

Step 1. Critical values: numerator zero at \(x=-1\); denominator zero at \(x=2\).

Step 2. Sign chart on \((-\infty,-1),(-1,2),(2,\infty)\). Test \(x=-2:\dfrac{-1}{-4}=+\). Test \(x=0:\dfrac{1}{-2}=-\). Test \(x=3:\dfrac{4}{1}=+\).

Step 3. Want \(\le 0\): negative interval is \((-1,2)\). Include \(x=-1\) (numerator zero, expression equals 0). Exclude \(x=2\) (undefined).

Step 4. Solution: \(x\in[-1,2)\).

  • Always write down restrictions before doing any algebra — they save you from extraneous solutions later.
  • Do not multiply both sides of an inequality by a variable denominator; build a sign chart instead.
  • Denominator zeros are always excluded — even when the inequality includes equality.

3.7 — Word Problems with Rational Functions
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Word problems modelled by rational functions usually arise from combining rates or expressing a quantity that is naturally a ratio. Classic examples include shared-work problems ("two pipes filling a tank together"), average-cost problems ("cost per unit when fixed costs are spread over many units"), travel-time problems with current or wind, and concentration problems involving dilution. The unifying idea is that rational functions are how mathematics expresses "per" quantities and their interactions.

The setup is the same as any modelling problem: define your variable carefully (units!), express each relevant quantity as a rational expression in that variable, write the equation that captures the problem's constraint, and solve. The pay-off is being able to interpret asymptotes meaningfully — for instance, average cost approaches a horizontal asymptote (the variable cost per unit) as production grows large, with the difference representing the spread of fixed costs.

Always finish by checking that your answer makes sense in context: negative times, lengths, or quantities are usually inadmissible even when they satisfy the equation.

  • Combined rate — for shared work, \(\dfrac{1}{T}=\dfrac{1}{t_{1}}+\dfrac{1}{t_{2}}\), where \(T\) is total time.
  • Average cost — \(\overline{C}(x)=\dfrac{\text{total cost}}{x}\); horizontal asymptote represents variable cost.
  • Contextual restriction — domain restrictions that come from the real-world meaning (e.g., \(x>0\) for physical quantities).

Worked Example — Shared work

Alex paints a room in 6 hours; Mia paints the same room in 4 hours. How long does it take working together?

Step 1. Rates: Alex \(=\tfrac{1}{6}\) room/hour, Mia \(=\tfrac{1}{4}\) room/hour. Combined rate \(=\tfrac{1}{6}+\tfrac{1}{4}=\tfrac{2+3}{12}=\tfrac{5}{12}\) room/hour.

Step 2. Time \(T\) satisfies \(\tfrac{1}{T}=\tfrac{5}{12}\), so \(T=\tfrac{12}{5}=2.4\) hours \(=2\) h \(24\) min.

Worked Example — Average cost interpretation

A company has fixed costs of \(\$5000\) plus \(\$8\) per item. Write the average cost per item and interpret as \(x\to\infty\).

Step 1. Total cost \(C(x)=8x+5000\). Average cost \(\overline{C}(x)=\dfrac{8x+5000}{x}=8+\dfrac{5000}{x}\).

Step 2. HA: as \(x\to\infty\), \(\dfrac{5000}{x}\to 0\), so \(\overline{C}\to 8\).

Step 3. Interpretation: average cost approaches the variable cost of \(\$8\) per item as production grows large; fixed costs spread thinly and become negligible.

  • Always state units when defining your variable; many marks are lost by mismatched units.
  • Check that solutions satisfy contextual restrictions (e.g., positive time, integer number of items).
  • Interpret asymptotes in plain English — that explanation is often the Communication mark.

  • Inequality checklist — restrictions, sign chart, included/excluded endpoints, correct interval notation.
  • Word-problem checklist — variable defined with units, equation set up, contextual restrictions applied, interpretation in words.

Worked Example — Full sketch

Sketch \(f(x)=\dfrac{x^{2}-1}{x^{2}-x-2}\).

Step 1. Factor: numerator \((x-1)(x+1)\); denominator \((x-2)(x+1)\).

Step 2. Cancel \((x+1)\): \(f(x)=\dfrac{x-1}{x-2}\) for \(x\ne -1\). Hole at \(x=-1\): plug into simplified form, \(\dfrac{-2}{-3}=\tfrac{2}{3}\). Hole at \((-1,\tfrac{2}{3})\).

Step 3. VA at \(x=2\). HA at \(y=1\) (equal degrees, ratio \(\tfrac{1}{1}\)).

Step 4. \(x\)-intercept: \(x-1=0\Rightarrow x=1\). \(y\)-intercept: \(f(0)=\dfrac{-1}{-2}=\tfrac{1}{2}\).

Step 5. One-sided behaviour at \(x=2\): from left, denominator \(\to 0^{-}\), numerator \(\to 1\), so \(f\to-\infty\). From right, \(f\to+\infty\).

  • Mark holes with open circles — leaving them as filled points loses Communication marks.
  • Distinguish "no horizontal asymptote" (degree differs by 2+) from "horizontal asymptote at \(y=0\)" (numerator lower degree).
  • For inequalities, never skip the sign chart even if you "see" the answer — show the work.

4.1 — Radian Measure
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

A radian is the angle subtended at the centre of a circle by an arc whose length equals the circle's radius. It is a natural, dimensionless angle measure that arises directly from arc length: the formula \(s=r\theta\) (arc length equals radius times angle in radians) is the reason radians are the standard unit in calculus and physics. Degrees, by contrast, are a historical convention with no geometric content — the choice of 360 traces back to ancient astronomy.

The conversion between the two systems comes from the full revolution: \(360°=2\pi\) radians, so \(1\text{ rad}=\tfrac{180°}{\pi}\approx 57.3°\) and \(1°=\tfrac{\pi}{180}\) rad. You should know the key benchmarks by sight: \(30°=\tfrac{\pi}{6}\), \(45°=\tfrac{\pi}{4}\), \(60°=\tfrac{\pi}{3}\), \(90°=\tfrac{\pi}{2}\), \(180°=\pi\), and so on around the unit circle.

From radian measure you also get formulas for arc length \(s=r\theta\) and sector area \(A=\tfrac{1}{2}r^{2}\theta\), both of which only work when \(\theta\) is in radians. These are favourite exam application problems.

  • Radian — angle whose arc length equals the radius; \(2\pi\) radians make one full revolution.
  • Conversion factors — \(\theta_{\text{rad}}=\theta_{\text{deg}}\cdot\dfrac{\pi}{180}\); \(\theta_{\text{deg}}=\theta_{\text{rad}}\cdot\dfrac{180}{\pi}\).
  • Arc length — \(s=r\theta\) (\(\theta\) in radians).
  • Sector area — \(A=\tfrac{1}{2}r^{2}\theta\) (\(\theta\) in radians).

Worked Example — Convert between units

(a) Convert \(135°\) to radians. (b) Convert \(\tfrac{7\pi}{6}\) radians to degrees.

(a) \(135\cdot\dfrac{\pi}{180}=\dfrac{135\pi}{180}=\dfrac{3\pi}{4}\) rad.

(b) \(\dfrac{7\pi}{6}\cdot\dfrac{180}{\pi}=\dfrac{7\cdot 180}{6}=210°\).

Worked Example — Arc length and sector area

A sector of radius \(8\) cm subtends an angle of \(\tfrac{\pi}{3}\) radians. Find the arc length and the sector area.

Step 1. \(s=r\theta=8\cdot\dfrac{\pi}{3}=\dfrac{8\pi}{3}\) cm \(\approx 8.38\) cm.

Step 2. \(A=\tfrac{1}{2}r^{2}\theta=\tfrac{1}{2}\cdot 64\cdot\dfrac{\pi}{3}=\dfrac{32\pi}{3}\) cm\(^{2}\approx 33.51\) cm\(^{2}\).

  • The formulas \(s=r\theta\) and \(A=\tfrac{1}{2}r^{2}\theta\) require radians — converting to degrees first will give wrong answers.

4.2 — Special Angles and Trig Ratios
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The unit circle is the universal reference for trigonometric values. A point on the unit circle at angle \(\theta\) (measured counter-clockwise from the positive \(x\)-axis) has coordinates \((\cos\theta,\sin\theta)\). From these primary ratios you also get \(\tan\theta=\dfrac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}\), and the reciprocal ratios \(\csc\theta=\dfrac{1}{\sin\theta}\), \(\sec\theta=\dfrac{1}{\cos\theta}\), and \(\cot\theta=\dfrac{1}{\tan\theta}\).

For the special angles \(0,\,\tfrac{\pi}{6},\,\tfrac{\pi}{4},\,\tfrac{\pi}{3},\,\tfrac{\pi}{2}\) and their reflections across the axes, exact values come from two reference triangles: the 30–60–90 (sides \(1:\sqrt{3}:2\)) and the 45–45–90 (sides \(1:1:\sqrt{2}\)). For any angle outside the first quadrant, find the reference angle — the acute angle to the nearest \(x\)-axis — compute the ratio there, and attach the correct sign based on which quadrant the original angle lies in (the "CAST" rule: Cosine in IV, All in I, Sine in II, Tangent in III are positive).

Knowing exact ratios for special angles by sight saves enormous time on tests, where calculators may be restricted or where answers must be in exact form.

  • Unit circle — circle of radius 1 centred at the origin; coordinates of \(\theta\) are \((\cos\theta,\sin\theta)\).
  • Reference angle — the acute angle between the terminal arm and the nearest \(x\)-axis.
  • CAST rule — quadrant-by-quadrant sign chart for the three primary trig ratios.
  • Reciprocal ratios — \(\csc,\sec,\cot\) are the reciprocals of \(\sin,\cos,\tan\).

Worked Example — Exact ratio in quadrant III

Find \(\sin\bigl(\tfrac{7\pi}{6}\bigr)\) exactly.

Step 1. \(\tfrac{7\pi}{6}=\pi+\tfrac{\pi}{6}\) — angle is in quadrant III.

Step 2. Reference angle: \(\tfrac{\pi}{6}=30°\).

Step 3. \(\sin\tfrac{\pi}{6}=\tfrac{1}{2}\). In quadrant III, sine is negative.

Step 4. So \(\sin\bigl(\tfrac{7\pi}{6}\bigr)=-\tfrac{1}{2}\).

Worked Example — Find all six ratios from one point

A point on the terminal arm of \(\theta\) (in standard position) is \((-3,4)\). Find all six trig ratios of \(\theta\).

Step 1. Distance from origin: \(r=\sqrt{9+16}=5\). The angle is in quadrant II (\(x<0,y>0\)).

Step 2. Primary ratios: \(\sin\theta=\dfrac{y}{r}=\dfrac{4}{5}\); \(\cos\theta=\dfrac{x}{r}=-\dfrac{3}{5}\); \(\tan\theta=\dfrac{y}{x}=-\dfrac{4}{3}\).

Step 3. Reciprocals: \(\csc\theta=\dfrac{5}{4}\); \(\sec\theta=-\dfrac{5}{3}\); \(\cot\theta=-\dfrac{3}{4}\).

  • The CAST rule is for the sign only — magnitude always comes from the reference-angle triangle.
  • \(r\) is always positive; only \(x\) and \(y\) carry quadrant signs.
  • Reciprocal ratios are undefined where their base ratios are zero (e.g., \(\csc 0\) and \(\sec\tfrac{\pi}{2}\) are undefined).

4.3 — Equivalent Trig Expressions
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Sine and cosine are cofunctions — they are translates of each other by a quarter-turn (\(\tfrac{\pi}{2}\) radians). This gives the cofunction identities: \(\sin\bigl(\tfrac{\pi}{2}-\theta\bigr)=\cos\theta\) and \(\cos\bigl(\tfrac{\pi}{2}-\theta\bigr)=\sin\theta\). Similar relations hold for tangent/cotangent and secant/cosecant. These identities let you rewrite expressions in sine as expressions in cosine (and vice versa), which is essential for proving identities and solving equations.

You should also know the even–odd identities: cosine and secant are even (\(\cos(-\theta)=\cos\theta\)), while sine, tangent, cosecant and cotangent are all odd (e.g., \(\sin(-\theta)=-\sin\theta\)). These follow directly from the geometry of the unit circle, where reflecting across the \(x\)-axis flips the \(y\)-coordinate but leaves the \(x\)-coordinate unchanged.

Working through "equivalent expressions" exercises trains the algebraic dexterity you need for trig identities later in the chapter. Treat them as transformation puzzles: each rule moves you between equivalent forms, and you choose the path.

  • Cofunction identity — \(\sin\bigl(\tfrac{\pi}{2}-\theta\bigr)=\cos\theta\); each trig function and its co- partner swap under the quarter-turn complement.
  • Even–odd identities — \(\cos(-\theta)=\cos\theta\); \(\sin(-\theta)=-\sin\theta\); \(\tan(-\theta)=-\tan\theta\).
  • Periodicity — sine and cosine have period \(2\pi\); tangent has period \(\pi\).

Worked Example — Rewrite using a cofunction

Rewrite \(\sin\bigl(\tfrac{\pi}{2}-x\bigr)\) using cosine.

Step 1. Direct cofunction identity: \(\sin\bigl(\tfrac{\pi}{2}-x\bigr)=\cos x\).

Worked Example — Combine identities

Express \(\cos\bigl(\tfrac{\pi}{2}+x\bigr)\) in terms of sine.

Step 1. Write \(\tfrac{\pi}{2}+x=\tfrac{\pi}{2}-(-x)\).

Step 2. Cofunction: \(\cos\bigl(\tfrac{\pi}{2}-(-x)\bigr)=\sin(-x)\).

Step 3. Apply odd identity: \(\sin(-x)=-\sin x\).

Step 4. Therefore \(\cos\bigl(\tfrac{\pi}{2}+x\bigr)=-\sin x\).

Worked Example — Use periodicity

Find an equivalent expression for \(\sin\bigl(\theta+2\pi\bigr)\).

Step 1. Sine has period \(2\pi\). Adding a full period leaves the value unchanged.

Step 2. \(\sin(\theta+2\pi)=\sin\theta\).

  • Cofunction identities use \(\tfrac{\pi}{2}-\theta\), not \(\tfrac{\pi}{2}+\theta\) — the latter requires an additional manipulation.
  • The odd identities pick up a minus sign — easy to drop accidentally.
  • Sine and cosine both have period \(2\pi\); tangent has period \(\pi\), not \(2\pi\).

4.4 — Compound Angles
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The compound-angle formulas tell you how to compute the sine, cosine, or tangent of the sum or difference of two angles. They are the workhorse identities of trigonometry, used to derive every other trig identity in the course. The most important ones are:

\[\sin(A\pm B)=\sin A\cos B\pm\cos A\sin B,\qquad \cos(A\pm B)=\cos A\cos B\mp\sin A\sin B.\]

Notice the sign pattern: in \(\sin(A+B)\) the signs match, in \(\cos(A+B)\) they switch. Memorise this carefully — sign errors here ripple through every later proof.

From the sum formulas you can derive the double-angle identities by setting \(B=A\): \(\sin 2A=2\sin A\cos A\) and \(\cos 2A=\cos^{2}A-\sin^{2}A\) (which has two equivalent forms via the Pythagorean identity: \(=1-2\sin^{2}A=2\cos^{2}A-1\)). These are essential for the proofs in section 4.5 and the equation-solving in section 5.4.

The main exam use case is computing an exact value of a non-special angle by decomposing it into a sum or difference of special angles. For example, \(75°=45°+30°\), so you can find \(\sin 75°\) exactly without a calculator.

  • Sum formula for sine — \(\sin(A+B)=\sin A\cos B+\cos A\sin B\).
  • Sum formula for cosine — \(\cos(A+B)=\cos A\cos B-\sin A\sin B\) (note the sign flip).
  • Double-angle for sine — \(\sin 2A=2\sin A\cos A\).
  • Double-angle for cosine — \(\cos 2A=\cos^{2}A-\sin^{2}A=1-2\sin^{2}A=2\cos^{2}A-1\).

Worked Example — Exact value of a non-special angle

Find \(\sin\bigl(\tfrac{7\pi}{12}\bigr)\) exactly.

Step 1. \(\tfrac{7\pi}{12}=\tfrac{4\pi}{12}+\tfrac{3\pi}{12}=\tfrac{\pi}{3}+\tfrac{\pi}{4}\).

Step 2. Apply sum formula: \(\sin\bigl(\tfrac{\pi}{3}+\tfrac{\pi}{4}\bigr)=\sin\tfrac{\pi}{3}\cos\tfrac{\pi}{4}+\cos\tfrac{\pi}{3}\sin\tfrac{\pi}{4}\).

Step 3. Substitute: \(\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\cdot\dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}+\dfrac{1}{2}\cdot\dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}=\dfrac{\sqrt{6}+\sqrt{2}}{4}\).

Worked Example — Using a known angle

Given \(\cos A=\dfrac{3}{5}\) with \(A\) in quadrant IV, find \(\sin 2A\).

Step 1. Sign of \(\sin A\) in quadrant IV: negative. Use \(\sin^{2}A+\cos^{2}A=1\): \(\sin A=-\sqrt{1-\tfrac{9}{25}}=-\tfrac{4}{5}\).

Step 2. \(\sin 2A=2\sin A\cos A=2\cdot\bigl(-\tfrac{4}{5}\bigr)\cdot\tfrac{3}{5}=-\dfrac{24}{25}\).

  • Cosine sum/difference: sign in the formula is opposite to the sign in the argument. Easy to flip in haste.
  • When the angle is given by a Pythagorean condition, always check which quadrant determines signs.
  • Memorise all three forms of \(\cos 2A\) — different forms simplify different identity proofs.

4.5 — Proving Trig Identities
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

A trigonometric identity is an equation in trig functions that holds for every value of the variable for which both sides are defined. Proving an identity means showing — algebraically — that one side is equivalent to the other. Unlike solving an equation, you do not "balance" an identity by manipulating both sides simultaneously. Instead you start with one side (typically the more complicated one) and transform it step by step until it matches the other.

Your toolkit is the entire catalogue of identities: Pythagorean (\(\sin^{2}\theta+\cos^{2}\theta=1\) and its rearrangements), reciprocal, quotient (\(\tan=\sin/\cos\)), cofunction, even–odd, compound-angle, and double-angle. A common strategic move is to convert everything to sines and cosines, simplify algebraically (common denominator, factor, cancel), and then re-package.

The crucial habit is to keep the two sides separated by the word "LS" (left side) and "RS" (right side) and to never use the equality sign between them until the proof is complete — that is, only write "LS = RS" as your final line, never inside the work.

  • Pythagorean identity — \(\sin^{2}\theta+\cos^{2}\theta=1\), with consequences \(1+\tan^{2}\theta=\sec^{2}\theta\) and \(1+\cot^{2}\theta=\csc^{2}\theta\).
  • Reciprocal identity — \(\csc\theta=\dfrac{1}{\sin\theta}\), etc.
  • Quotient identity — \(\tan\theta=\dfrac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}\).
  • Proof structure — manipulate one side independently until it equals the other; do not transpose terms.

Worked Example — Pythagorean substitution

Prove \(\dfrac{1-\cos^{2}\theta}{\sin\theta}=\sin\theta\).

Step 1. Start with the left side: \(\text{LS}=\dfrac{1-\cos^{2}\theta}{\sin\theta}\).

Step 2. Use Pythagorean identity: \(1-\cos^{2}\theta=\sin^{2}\theta\). So \(\text{LS}=\dfrac{\sin^{2}\theta}{\sin\theta}\).

Step 3. Cancel: \(\text{LS}=\sin\theta=\text{RS}\). ✓

Worked Example — Convert to sines and cosines

Prove \(\tan\theta+\cot\theta=\sec\theta\csc\theta\).

Step 1. \(\text{LS}=\dfrac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}+\dfrac{\cos\theta}{\sin\theta}\).

Step 2. Common denominator: \(\dfrac{\sin^{2}\theta+\cos^{2}\theta}{\sin\theta\cos\theta}=\dfrac{1}{\sin\theta\cos\theta}\).

Step 3. Split as reciprocals: \(\dfrac{1}{\sin\theta}\cdot\dfrac{1}{\cos\theta}=\csc\theta\sec\theta=\text{RS}\). ✓

  • Never cross-multiply or move terms between sides — that destroys the proof structure.
  • Always note restricted values (e.g., \(\sin\theta\ne 0\)) when cancelling factors.
  • If you are stuck on one side, work the other side from the bottom up and look for a meeting point.

Curriculum expectation: Strand B, Expectation B3.3 — "recognize that trigonometric identities are equations that are true for every value in the domain… prove trigonometric identities through the application of reasoning and the use of basic identities."

5.1 — Graphing 6 Trig Functions
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The six trigonometric ratios become functions when you let the angle vary over the real numbers (in radians) and plot the ratio versus the angle. The result is six distinctive curves: \(y=\sin x\) and \(y=\cos x\) are smooth waves of amplitude 1 and period \(2\pi\); \(y=\tan x\) is a periodic curve with period \(\pi\) and vertical asymptotes at every odd multiple of \(\tfrac{\pi}{2}\); and the three reciprocal graphs (\(\csc, \sec, \cot\)) have asymptotes wherever their base functions are zero.

You should know each graph's domain, range, period, asymptotes (if any), maximum and minimum, and the locations of its zeros and turning points. For sine and cosine specifically, the five key points per period — start, max, midline crossing, min, end — give an accurate sketch from memory.

These six graphs are the universal reference. Every transformed sinusoid in the next section starts from \(y=\sin x\) or \(y=\cos x\). Internalising their shapes now makes all that follows visual rather than algebraic.

  • Period — the smallest horizontal distance after which the graph repeats; \(2\pi\) for \(\sin, \cos, \csc, \sec\) and \(\pi\) for \(\tan, \cot\).
  • Amplitude — half the difference between max and min; \(1\) for \(\sin\) and \(\cos\), undefined for the others.
  • Asymptotes — vertical lines where the function is undefined; \(\tan\) and \(\sec\) at odd multiples of \(\tfrac{\pi}{2}\); \(\cot\) and \(\csc\) at multiples of \(\pi\).
  • Five key points — for \(y=\sin x\) on \([0,2\pi]\): \((0,0),\bigl(\tfrac{\pi}{2},1\bigr),(\pi,0),\bigl(\tfrac{3\pi}{2},-1\bigr),(2\pi,0)\).

Worked Example — Compare sine and cosecant

Describe how the graphs of \(y=\sin x\) and \(y=\csc x\) relate.

Step 1. \(\csc x=\dfrac{1}{\sin x}\), so reciprocate every \(y\)-value of \(\sin x\).

Step 2. Where \(\sin x=0\) (at \(x=0,\pi,2\pi,\dots\)), \(\csc x\) is undefined: vertical asymptotes there.

Step 3. At max of \(\sin x\) (\(y=1\)), \(\csc x\) is also \(1\) — local min on its arch.

Step 4. Result: \(\csc x\) is a series of U-shaped arches reaching down to \(y=1\) (or up to \(y=-1\)), separated by vertical asymptotes.

Worked Example — Key features of tangent

State the domain, range, period, and asymptote locations of \(y=\tan x\).

Step 1. Tangent is undefined where \(\cos x=0\), i.e., at \(x=\tfrac{\pi}{2}+k\pi\) for integer \(k\).

Step 2. Domain: \(\{x\in\mathbb{R}\mid x\ne\tfrac{\pi}{2}+k\pi\}\). Range: \(\{y\in\mathbb{R}\}\) — all real values.

Step 3. Period: \(\pi\), not \(2\pi\). Asymptotes spaced \(\pi\) apart.

  • Tangent and cotangent have period \(\pi\), not \(2\pi\) — a classic test trap.
  • The reciprocal trig graphs touch (not cross) the original at max/min; mark this clearly.
  • Cosecant and secant have range \((-\infty,-1]\cup[1,\infty)\) — no values strictly between \(-1\) and \(1\).

5.2 — Transformations of Sinusoidal Functions
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

A sinusoidal function takes the form \(y=a\sin\!\bigl(k(x-d)\bigr)+c\) (or with cosine instead of sine). The four parameters carry the same geometric meaning as in section 1.7 — vertical stretch, horizontal stretch, horizontal shift, vertical shift — but each one now has a specific trigonometric interpretation. The amplitude is \(|a|\); the period is \(\dfrac{2\pi}{|k|}\); the phase shift (also called horizontal translation) is \(d\); and the vertical translation \(c\) is the equation of the midline \(y=c\).

From these four numbers you can recover every feature: maximum \(y=c+|a|\), minimum \(y=c-|a|\), period \(\dfrac{2\pi}{|k|}\), and the location of the first cycle. The five key points of the parent wave map to corresponding key points of the transformed wave.

Be careful with the order of operations: always factor \(k\) out of the bracket before reading the phase shift. The expression \(\sin(2x-\pi)\) is really \(\sin\!\bigl(2(x-\tfrac{\pi}{2})\bigr)\), so the phase shift is \(\tfrac{\pi}{2}\) to the right, not \(\pi\).

  • Amplitude — \(|a|\); the vertical distance from midline to peak.
  • Period — \(\dfrac{2\pi}{|k|}\) (or \(\dfrac{360°}{|k|}\) in degree mode); the length of one full cycle.
  • Phase shift — \(d\); horizontal translation, positive \(\Rightarrow\) right.
  • Midline — \(y=c\); the horizontal axis of the wave.

Worked Example — Read parameters from an equation

State amplitude, period, phase shift, and midline for \(y=3\sin\!\bigl(2(x-\tfrac{\pi}{4})\bigr)-1\).

Step 1. \(a=3\): amplitude \(3\). \(k=2\): period \(\dfrac{2\pi}{2}=\pi\). \(d=\tfrac{\pi}{4}\): phase shift \(\tfrac{\pi}{4}\) right. \(c=-1\): midline \(y=-1\).

Step 2. Maximum: \(-1+3=2\); minimum: \(-1-3=-4\).

Worked Example — Factor before reading

Find the phase shift of \(y=\cos(3x+\pi)\).

Step 1. Factor: \(\cos\!\bigl(3(x+\tfrac{\pi}{3})\bigr)\).

Step 2. So \(d=-\tfrac{\pi}{3}\): phase shift \(\tfrac{\pi}{3}\) to the left.

  • Phase shift uses \(d\) after factoring \(k\) out — not the constant inside the bracket.
  • Period scales inversely with \(|k|\): doubling \(k\) halves the period.
  • A negative \(a\) flips the graph over the midline — peaks become troughs and vice versa.

5.3 — Equations of Sinusoidal Functions
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

To write the equation of a sinusoid from a graph or description, find the four parameters in turn. The recipe is mechanical once you know what to look for:

(1) Midline \(c\) — average of max and min: \(c=\dfrac{y_{\max}+y_{\min}}{2}\). (2) Amplitude \(a\) — half the distance between max and min: \(|a|=\dfrac{y_{\max}-y_{\min}}{2}\). (3) Period — the horizontal length of one full cycle; then \(|k|=\dfrac{2\pi}{\text{period}}\). (4) Phase shift \(d\) — choose a reference point on the parent and find how far it has moved.

For sine, the natural reference is the midline crossing where the function is increasing (the start of a fresh cycle on \(y=\sin x\)). For cosine, the natural reference is the maximum. If you cannot identify either cleanly from the data, fit both and choose whichever gives the simpler equation.

Many graphs admit multiple correct equations — sine and cosine forms of the same wave differ only by a \(\tfrac{\pi}{2}\) phase shift. Either is acceptable; verify your equation by plugging in one extra point.

  • Midline — \(c=\dfrac{y_{\max}+y_{\min}}{2}\).
  • Amplitude — \(|a|=\dfrac{y_{\max}-y_{\min}}{2}\).
  • Period to \(k\) — \(|k|=\dfrac{2\pi}{\text{period}}\).
  • Reference points — sine: midline going up; cosine: maximum.

Worked Example — From a description

A sinusoid has max \(7\) at \(x=2\), next min \(-3\) at \(x=5\). Write an equation using cosine.

Step 1. Midline: \(c=\dfrac{7+(-3)}{2}=2\).

Step 2. Amplitude: \(|a|=\dfrac{7-(-3)}{2}=5\). Use \(a=5\) (max above midline).

Step 3. Half-period: from max to next min is half a cycle. \(5-2=3\), so period \(=6\), and \(k=\dfrac{2\pi}{6}=\dfrac{\pi}{3}\).

Step 4. Cosine has max at \(x=d\); given max at \(x=2\), \(d=2\).

Step 5. Equation: \(y=5\cos\!\bigl(\tfrac{\pi}{3}(x-2)\bigr)+2\).

Worked Example — From a graph in seconds

A tide model shows water height oscillating between 1 m and 5 m, completing one full cycle every 12 hours, with high tide at \(t=3\) h. Model with cosine.

Step 1. Midline \(c=\dfrac{5+1}{2}=3\). Amplitude \(|a|=\dfrac{5-1}{2}=2\).

Step 2. Period \(=12\), so \(k=\dfrac{2\pi}{12}=\dfrac{\pi}{6}\).

Step 3. High tide (max) at \(t=3\), so \(d=3\) for cosine.

Step 4. \(h(t)=2\cos\!\bigl(\tfrac{\pi}{6}(t-3)\bigr)+3\).

  • Sine and cosine equations of the same wave are both valid — they just differ by a phase shift.
  • Verify by substituting a known point (max or midline crossing) into your final equation.
  • If max is below midline (descending wave), use \(a<0\) instead of changing the reference point.

5.4 — Solving Trig Equations
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Solving a trigonometric equation means finding every angle (in a specified interval) that makes the equation true. Because trig functions are periodic, equations almost always have infinitely many solutions — one base solution plus any integer number of full periods. Most exam questions restrict the interval to \([0,2\pi)\) or \([0,2\pi]\) and ask for all solutions in that window.

The standard procedure: (1) isolate the trig function (e.g., get \(\sin x=\tfrac{1}{2}\)); (2) find the reference angle using the inverse trig function; (3) use the CAST rule to find every quadrant where the sign matches; (4) compute the actual solutions in the required interval. If the equation is quadratic-in-trig (\(2\sin^{2}x-\sin x=0\)), factor first, then solve each factor's simpler equation separately.

Identity substitution is often required. If you see \(\sin 2x\) and the rest of the equation involves \(\sin x\), use \(\sin 2x=2\sin x\cos x\) to rewrite the equation in one common ratio. Then factor and solve.

  • Reference angle — the acute angle whose trig ratio matches the magnitude; produced by \(\sin^{-1}\), \(\cos^{-1}\), \(\tan^{-1}\).
  • CAST — which quadrants give positive ratios; determines how many solutions in \([0,2\pi)\).
  • General solution — every solution plus integer multiples of the function's period.

Worked Example — Linear-in-trig

Solve \(2\cos x+\sqrt{3}=0\) on \([0,2\pi)\).

Step 1. Isolate: \(\cos x=-\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\).

Step 2. Reference angle: \(\cos^{-1}\bigl(\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\bigr)=\dfrac{\pi}{6}\).

Step 3. Cosine is negative in quadrants II and III. \(\text{Q-II}: \pi-\tfrac{\pi}{6}=\tfrac{5\pi}{6}\). \(\text{Q-III}: \pi+\tfrac{\pi}{6}=\tfrac{7\pi}{6}\).

Step 4. Solutions: \(x=\dfrac{5\pi}{6},\,\dfrac{7\pi}{6}\).

Worked Example — Quadratic-in-trig

Solve \(2\sin^{2}x-\sin x-1=0\) on \([0,2\pi)\).

Step 1. Factor as a quadratic in \(u=\sin x\): \(2u^{2}-u-1=(2u+1)(u-1)=0\).

Step 2. Either \(\sin x=-\dfrac{1}{2}\) or \(\sin x=1\).

Step 3. \(\sin x=1\Rightarrow x=\dfrac{\pi}{2}\). \(\sin x=-\dfrac{1}{2}\): reference angle \(\tfrac{\pi}{6}\), sine negative in Q-III and Q-IV: \(x=\dfrac{7\pi}{6},\,\dfrac{11\pi}{6}\).

Step 4. Solutions: \(x=\dfrac{\pi}{2},\,\dfrac{7\pi}{6},\,\dfrac{11\pi}{6}\).

  • Always state the interval and check that every solution lies in it.
  • Calculators give only one inverse value; do not stop after finding the reference angle.
  • For equations of the form \(\sin(kx)=c\), divide the period by \(k\) when listing solutions — there are more of them per \(2\pi\) interval.

5.5 — Applications of Trig Functions
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Sinusoidal functions model any phenomenon that oscillates periodically about a mean value. Examples include daylight hours through the year, tides through the day, a Ferris-wheel rider's height, alternating current voltage, pendulum motion, and seasonal temperature. The modelling recipe is the same in every case: identify the maximum and minimum to find amplitude and midline, identify the period from one full cycle, and identify a known maximum or midline crossing for the phase shift.

Once you have a model, the typical questions are: "What is the height/temperature/voltage at time \(t\)?" (substitute), "When is the value first equal to \(X\)?" (solve a trig equation), "For how long during one cycle does the value exceed \(X\)?" (solve a trig inequality, typically via the graph), and "How many complete cycles occur in the interval?" (divide time by period).

Always include units in setup and conclusion, and watch the difference between radian-mode and degree-mode calculations. In a Ferris-wheel problem with angular speed in radians per second, your input \(t\) is in seconds and the trig function expects a radian argument; mixing units is a common cost of marks.

  • Mean / equilibrium value — the midline \(c\) of the sinusoid; physical interpretation depends on context.
  • Amplitude — the maximum deviation from the mean; e.g., tidal range divided by 2.
  • Angular frequency — the value of \(k\); for time-based models often expressed as \(\dfrac{2\pi}{T}\) where \(T\) is the period in time units.

Worked Example — Ferris wheel

A Ferris wheel of radius 10 m rotates once every 40 seconds, with the lowest point 1 m above the ground. A rider boards at the lowest point at \(t=0\). Model the rider's height \(h(t)\).

Step 1. Centre of wheel is at \(10+1=11\) m, so midline \(c=11\).

Step 2. Amplitude is the radius: \(|a|=10\).

Step 3. Period \(40\) s, so \(k=\dfrac{2\pi}{40}=\dfrac{\pi}{20}\).

Step 4. At \(t=0\) the rider is at minimum, so use cosine with negative \(a\): \(h(t)=-10\cos\!\bigl(\tfrac{\pi}{20}t\bigr)+11\).

Step 5. Check: \(h(0)=-10(1)+11=1\). ✓ \(h(20)=-10(-1)+11=21\) m at the top. ✓

Worked Example — Solving a model

Using \(h(t)=-10\cos\!\bigl(\tfrac{\pi}{20}t\bigr)+11\), find the first time the rider is 16 m above the ground.

Step 1. Set up: \(-10\cos\!\bigl(\tfrac{\pi}{20}t\bigr)+11=16\Rightarrow \cos\!\bigl(\tfrac{\pi}{20}t\bigr)=-\dfrac{1}{2}\).

Step 2. \(\tfrac{\pi}{20}t=\cos^{-1}\bigl(-\tfrac{1}{2}\bigr)\). First positive solution: \(\tfrac{2\pi}{3}\).

Step 3. \(t=\tfrac{20}{\pi}\cdot\tfrac{2\pi}{3}=\dfrac{40}{3}\approx 13.33\) s.

  • Make sure your calculator is in radian mode for radian-argument models.
  • Sketch the model before solving — a quick graph catches missed solutions and helps interpret context.
  • "First time" means smallest positive \(t\), not the only mathematical solution.

6.1 — Inverse of Exponential Functions
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

An exponential function \(f(x)=b^{x}\) with base \(b>0,\,b\ne 1\) takes a real input \(x\) and produces a positive output \(b^{x}\). The function is one-to-one (each input gives a distinct output), so it has an inverse. The inverse takes a positive number and returns the exponent needed to produce it — that inverse function is the logarithm: \(\log_{b}y=x\) means exactly \(b^{x}=y\).

Graphically, the inverse of \(y=b^{x}\) is its reflection across the line \(y=x\). Every key feature swaps: the exponential's \(y\)-intercept \((0,1)\) becomes the logarithm's \(x\)-intercept \((1,0)\); the horizontal asymptote \(y=0\) becomes a vertical asymptote \(x=0\); the domain of one becomes the range of the other. So \(y=b^{x}\) has domain \(\mathbb{R}\) and range \((0,\infty)\); \(y=\log_{b}x\) has domain \((0,\infty)\) and range \(\mathbb{R}\).

The point of this section is to anchor logarithms as inverses, not abstract symbols. Whenever you see \(\log_{b}y=x\), translate it to "the exponent that takes base \(b\) to \(y\)". That habit makes every later log law feel inevitable.

  • Exponential function — \(f(x)=b^{x}\), \(b>0,\,b\ne 1\); domain \(\mathbb{R}\), range \((0,\infty)\).
  • Logarithm — the inverse of the exponential: \(\log_{b}y=x\iff b^{x}=y\), defined for \(y>0\).
  • Inverse relationship — graphs of \(y=b^{x}\) and \(y=\log_{b}x\) are reflections in \(y=x\).

Worked Example — Find the inverse algebraically

Find the inverse of \(f(x)=3^{x}-2\).

Step 1. Write \(y=3^{x}-2\) and swap \(x\) and \(y\): \(x=3^{y}-2\).

Step 2. Solve for \(y\): \(3^{y}=x+2\), so \(y=\log_{3}(x+2)\).

Step 3. \(f^{-1}(x)=\log_{3}(x+2)\). Domain of inverse: \(x+2>0\Rightarrow x>-2\).

Worked Example — Key features from inverse

State the domain, range, intercepts, and asymptote of \(g(x)=\log_{2}x\).

Step 1. Domain: \((0,\infty)\). Range: \(\mathbb{R}\).

Step 2. \(x\)-intercept where \(\log_{2}x=0\): \(x=2^{0}=1\). Intercept \((1,0)\).

Step 3. No \(y\)-intercept (zero is not in the domain).

Step 4. Vertical asymptote at \(x=0\) (the boundary of the domain).

  • Logarithms only accept positive inputs — the argument of every log must be positive.
  • The base of a logarithm must be positive and not equal to 1; otherwise the inverse fails.
  • Reflecting across \(y=x\) means swapping every \((a,b)\) on the original to \((b,a)\) — useful for sketching by hand.

Curriculum expectation: Strand A, Expectation A1.1 — "recognize the logarithm of a number to a given base as the exponent to which the base must be raised to get the number… and evaluate simple logarithmic expressions."

6.2 — Introduction to Logarithms
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The defining relationship of logarithms is the exponential–logarithmic equivalence: \(\log_{b}x=y\) is the same statement as \(b^{y}=x\). Reading equations both ways — exponential to logarithmic, logarithmic to exponential — is the most fundamental skill in this chapter. It is the move you make every time you evaluate a log, solve a log equation, or apply a log law.

Two special logarithms have shortened names. The common logarithm has base 10 and is written \(\log x\) (no base shown). The natural logarithm has base \(e\approx 2.71828\) and is written \(\ln x\). Both appear constantly in science: \(\log\) shows up in pH, dB, magnitude scales; \(\ln\) shows up in growth, decay, and calculus.

To evaluate a logarithm exactly, ask: "to what power must I raise \(b\) to get \(x\)?" For example, \(\log_{2}8=3\) because \(2^{3}=8\); \(\log_{5}\tfrac{1}{25}=-2\) because \(5^{-2}=\tfrac{1}{25}\); \(\log_{4}2=\tfrac{1}{2}\) because \(4^{1/2}=2\). Recognising these patterns quickly is the goal.

  • Exponential–log equivalence — \(\log_{b}x=y\iff b^{y}=x\).
  • Common logarithm — \(\log x=\log_{10}x\).
  • Natural logarithm — \(\ln x=\log_{e}x\).
  • Key identities — \(\log_{b}1=0\), \(\log_{b}b=1\), \(\log_{b}b^{n}=n\), and \(b^{\log_{b}x}=x\).

Worked Example — Evaluate exact logs

Evaluate (a) \(\log_{3}81\); (b) \(\log_{8}2\); (c) \(\log_{1/2}16\).

(a) \(81=3^{4}\), so \(\log_{3}81=4\).

(b) \(2=8^{1/3}\) since \(8^{1/3}=\sqrt[3]{8}=2\). So \(\log_{8}2=\tfrac{1}{3}\).

(c) Need \(\bigl(\tfrac{1}{2}\bigr)^{x}=16=2^{4}\). Rewrite: \(2^{-x}=2^{4}\Rightarrow x=-4\). So \(\log_{1/2}16=-4\).

Worked Example — Convert between forms

Rewrite \(2^{5}=32\) in logarithmic form, and \(\log_{7}49=2\) in exponential form.

Step 1. \(2^{5}=32\Leftrightarrow\log_{2}32=5\).

Step 2. \(\log_{7}49=2\Leftrightarrow 7^{2}=49\).

  • Negative arguments inside a log are undefined — always check the input is positive.
  • Do not confuse \(\log_{b}(x\cdot y)\) with \(\log_{b}x\cdot\log_{b}y\) — the second is meaningless.
  • \(\log 100=2\), not \(100\) — students sometimes mix up the input and output of a log.

6.3 — Transformations of Log Functions
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The general transformed logarithm is \(g(x)=a\log_{b}\!\bigl(k(x-d)\bigr)+c\). Each parameter does what it always did: \(a\) controls vertical stretch and reflection across the \(x\)-axis; \(k\) controls horizontal stretch and reflection across the \(y\)-axis; \(d\) translates horizontally; \(c\) translates vertically. The parent curve \(y=\log_{b}x\) has its vertical asymptote at \(x=0\) and its \(x\)-intercept at \((1,0)\); these key features move with the transformation.

The most important consequence is the location of the vertical asymptote: it moves with the horizontal shift \(d\). Setting the argument of the log equal to zero, \(k(x-d)=0\), gives the asymptote \(x=d\). The domain restriction also shifts: instead of \(x>0\), you need \(k(x-d)>0\).

When sketching by hand, take two parent points like \((1,0)\) and \((b,1)\), apply the mapping \((x,y)\to\bigl(\tfrac{x}{k}+d,\;ay+c\bigr)\), and connect them following the asymptote at \(x=d\).

  • General form — \(g(x)=a\log_{b}\!\bigl(k(x-d)\bigr)+c\); four parameters, same roles as for other transformations.
  • Vertical asymptote — at \(x=d\) (when \(k>0\)); shifts with the horizontal translation.
  • Domain — solve \(k(x-d)>0\); not always \(x>d\) if \(k\) is negative.
  • Mapping rule — parent point \((x,y)\to\bigl(\tfrac{x}{k}+d,\;ay+c\bigr)\).

Worked Example — Sketch by transformation

Sketch \(g(x)=2\log_{3}(x-1)+4\) from the parent \(y=\log_{3}x\).

Step 1. Parameters: \(a=2\), \(k=1\), \(d=1\), \(c=4\).

Step 2. Vertical asymptote moves from \(x=0\) to \(x=1\).

Step 3. Map parent point \((1,0)\): \(\bigl(\tfrac{1}{1}+1,\,2(0)+4\bigr)=(2,4)\). Map \((3,1)\): \(\bigl(3+1,\,2(1)+4\bigr)=(4,6)\).

Step 4. Sketch: vertical asymptote \(x=1\); curve passes through \((2,4)\) and \((4,6)\), increasing.

Worked Example — Domain with reflection

Find the domain of \(h(x)=\log(3-x)\).

Step 1. Argument must be positive: \(3-x>0\Rightarrow x<3\).

Step 2. Domain: \((-\infty,3)\). Vertical asymptote at \(x=3\); curve approaches from the left.

  • Domain depends on the sign of the argument, not just the value of \(d\). Always solve the inequality.
  • The vertical asymptote remains a critical feature — never draw the curve crossing it.
  • Reflecting horizontally (\(k<0\)) flips the asymptote-to-curve orientation; check by plotting one point.

6.4 — Power Law of Logarithms
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The Power Law of Logarithms says \(\log_{b}(x^{n})=n\log_{b}x\). An exponent inside the logarithm comes out front as a coefficient. The reason is simple if you trace it through the exponential equivalence: \(\log_{b}(x^{n})\) asks "to what power do I raise \(b\) to get \(x^{n}\)?" — and if \(b^{p}=x\), then \(b^{np}=x^{n}\), giving the exponent \(np=n\log_{b}x\).

The power law is the single most useful log identity for solving exponential equations. When you take the log of both sides of \(5^{x}=20\), the \(x\) drops out of the exponent to become a coefficient — converting an exponential equation into a linear one in \(x\). This is the standard move for any equation where the variable lives in an exponent.

The law works in both directions: you can pull exponents out front, but you can also push coefficients up into exponents. For example, \(3\log_{2}x=\log_{2}x^{3}\). Be alert for chances to compress (or expand) expressions to a useful form.

  • Power law — \(\log_{b}(x^{n})=n\log_{b}x\), valid for \(x>0\).
  • Reverse application — \(c\log_{b}x=\log_{b}(x^{c})\); useful for combining log expressions.
  • Change-of-base formula — \(\log_{b}x=\dfrac{\log x}{\log b}=\dfrac{\ln x}{\ln b}\); lets you evaluate any log on a standard calculator.

Worked Example — Simplify using the power law

Simplify \(\log_{2}(32^{3})\) to a single number.

Step 1. Power law: \(\log_{2}(32^{3})=3\log_{2}32\).

Step 2. \(32=2^{5}\), so \(\log_{2}32=5\).

Step 3. Result: \(3\cdot 5=15\).

Worked Example — Solve an exponential equation

Solve \(7^{x}=50\) for \(x\) to four decimal places.

Step 1. Take a common log of both sides: \(\log(7^{x})=\log 50\).

Step 2. Power law: \(x\log 7=\log 50\).

Step 3. Solve: \(x=\dfrac{\log 50}{\log 7}\approx\dfrac{1.6990}{0.8451}\approx 2.0103\).

  • The power law requires the argument to be positive — be careful when \(n\) is even and \(x\) is negative.
  • \(\log_{b}(x+y)\) is not \(\log_{b}x+\log_{b}y\) — that is the next section's law and applies only to multiplication.
  • The change-of-base formula is the bridge to using a calculator for any logarithm.

Curriculum expectation: Strand A, Expectation A2.4 — "recognize the laws of logarithms… and use these laws to simplify and evaluate numerical expressions."

6.5 — Product and Quotient Laws
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The Product Law states \(\log_{b}(xy)=\log_{b}x+\log_{b}y\); the Quotient Law states \(\log_{b}\!\bigl(\tfrac{x}{y}\bigr)=\log_{b}x-\log_{b}y\). Both follow from the fact that logarithms convert multiplication of arguments into addition of exponents — which is the entire reason logarithms were invented in the seventeenth century (to turn multiplication of large numbers into addition, before electronic calculators existed).

Combined with the Power Law from section 6.4, these three laws form the algebraic toolkit for all log manipulation. You will use them in two directions: expand a single complicated log into a sum of simpler logs (useful in calculus), and condense a sum or difference of logs into a single log (useful for solving log equations).

Always state restrictions when you apply log laws. Each input to a log must be positive, so combining \(\log x+\log(x-2)\) into \(\log\!\bigl(x(x-2)\bigr)\) is correct only when both \(x>0\) and \(x-2>0\), i.e., \(x>2\). Forgetting the restriction is a routine cause of extraneous solutions.

  • Product law — \(\log_{b}(xy)=\log_{b}x+\log_{b}y\).
  • Quotient law — \(\log_{b}\!\bigl(\tfrac{x}{y}\bigr)=\log_{b}x-\log_{b}y\).
  • Restrictions — every individual log argument must be positive.

Worked Example — Expand

Expand \(\log_{2}\!\left(\dfrac{8x^{3}}{y}\right)\) into a sum/difference of simpler logs.

Step 1. Quotient law: \(\log_{2}(8x^{3})-\log_{2}y\).

Step 2. Product law: \(\log_{2}8+\log_{2}(x^{3})-\log_{2}y\).

Step 3. Power law: \(\log_{2}8+3\log_{2}x-\log_{2}y\).

Step 4. Evaluate \(\log_{2}8=3\): final expression \(3+3\log_{2}x-\log_{2}y\).

Worked Example — Condense

Condense \(2\log x+\log(x-3)-\log(x+1)\) into a single logarithm.

Step 1. Power law on the first term: \(\log x^{2}+\log(x-3)-\log(x+1)\).

Step 2. Product law: \(\log\!\bigl(x^{2}(x-3)\bigr)-\log(x+1)\).

Step 3. Quotient law: \(\log\!\left(\dfrac{x^{2}(x-3)}{x+1}\right)\). Restrictions: \(x>3\) (the most restrictive of \(x>0,\,x>3,\,x>-1\)).

  • The product law is \(\log(xy)\), not \(\log(x+y)\) — confusion between these is the most common log error.
  • State the combined restriction (the intersection of individual restrictions) after condensing.
  • Coefficients become exponents before products combine — apply the Power Law first when condensing.

6.6 — Logarithmic Scales
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Many physical quantities span enormous ranges — earthquake energies vary by factors of millions, sound intensities cover \(10^{12}\) orders of magnitude, hydrogen-ion concentrations in water range over ten powers of ten. Logarithmic scales compress these vast ranges into manageable numbers by reporting the exponent rather than the raw value.

The three scales you must know in MHF4U: pH defined as \(\text{pH}=-\log[H^{+}]\), where \([H^{+}]\) is the hydrogen-ion concentration in mol/L. Richter magnitude: \(M=\log\!\bigl(\tfrac{I}{I_{0}}\bigr)\), where \(I\) is amplitude and \(I_{0}\) is a reference. Sound intensity in decibels: \(\beta=10\log\!\bigl(\tfrac{I}{I_{0}}\bigr)\), where \(I_{0}=10^{-12}\) W/m\(^{2}\) is the threshold of hearing.

Because the scales are logarithmic, each unit increase represents a constant multiplicative factor: pH down by 1 means concentration up by 10×; Richter magnitude up by 1 means amplitude up by 10× (and energy roughly 32×); dB up by 10 means intensity up by 10×. Interpreting these ratios is the heart of the application questions.

  • pH scale — \(\text{pH}=-\log[H^{+}]\); each pH unit corresponds to a 10× change in concentration.
  • Richter scale — \(M=\log\!\bigl(\tfrac{I}{I_{0}}\bigr)\); each magnitude unit corresponds to a 10× change in amplitude.
  • Decibel scale — \(\beta=10\log\!\bigl(\tfrac{I}{I_{0}}\bigr)\); the factor of 10 means each 10 dB corresponds to 10× intensity.

Worked Example — Earthquake magnitude ratio

An earthquake of magnitude 6.3 is how many times more intense (in amplitude) than one of magnitude 4.8?

Step 1. Difference in magnitude: \(6.3-4.8=1.5\).

Step 2. Each magnitude unit is a factor of 10 in amplitude, so ratio \(=10^{1.5}\).

Step 3. \(10^{1.5}=10\cdot 10^{0.5}\approx 31.6\). The first earthquake's amplitude is about 32 times the second's.

Worked Example — pH to concentration

A solution has \(\text{pH}=3.4\). Find the hydrogen-ion concentration.

Step 1. \(3.4=-\log[H^{+}]\Rightarrow\log[H^{+}]=-3.4\).

Step 2. Convert to exponential: \([H^{+}]=10^{-3.4}\).

Step 3. \(10^{-3.4}\approx 3.98\times 10^{-4}\) mol/L.

  • The pH formula has a leading minus sign — easy to drop, and it changes everything.
  • "How many times louder" in dB needs careful interpretation: a 10 dB increase is 10× intensity, not 10 dB more loudness perceived.
  • Always state units (mol/L for concentration, W/m\(^{2}\) for intensity).

Chapter 7: Solving Exp. & Log. Equations

Techniques for solving exponential and logarithmic equations

📚 Strand A: Exponential & Logarithmic ⏱️ ~10h 📹 8 lessons
7.1 — Solving Exponential Equations
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

An exponential equation has the variable in an exponent. Two situations arise. If you can rewrite both sides as powers of the same base, equate the exponents and solve algebraically. For instance, \(2^{x+1}=8\) becomes \(2^{x+1}=2^{3}\), giving \(x+1=3\). This works whenever the constants are obvious powers of a common base.

When a common base is not available, take the logarithm of both sides — using any base, though \(\log\) (base 10) or \(\ln\) (base \(e\)) is most convenient on a calculator — and apply the Power Law to bring the exponent down. The equation \(5^{x}=12\) becomes \(x\log 5=\log 12\), so \(x=\dfrac{\log 12}{\log 5}\).

More complex equations may need substitution. The equation \(4^{x}-6\cdot 2^{x}+8=0\) becomes the quadratic \(u^{2}-6u+8=0\) under the substitution \(u=2^{x}\). Recognising this pattern — exponential-quadratic — is a recurring exam skill.

  • Common base method — rewrite both sides as \(b^{\text{something}}\), then equate exponents.
  • Take a log method — apply \(\log\) or \(\ln\) to both sides, then Power Law to free the exponent.
  • Substitution method — let \(u=b^{x}\) when the equation is quadratic in the exponential.

Worked Example — Common base

Solve \(3^{2x-1}=27^{x+2}\).

Step 1. Rewrite 27 in base 3: \(27=3^{3}\), so \(27^{x+2}=3^{3(x+2)}=3^{3x+6}\).

Step 2. Equate exponents: \(2x-1=3x+6\).

Step 3. Solve: \(-7=x\), so \(x=-7\).

Worked Example — Take a log

Solve \(2^{x+1}=15\) to two decimal places.

Step 1. \(\log\bigl(2^{x+1}\bigr)=\log 15\). Power Law: \((x+1)\log 2=\log 15\).

Step 2. \(x+1=\dfrac{\log 15}{\log 2}\approx\dfrac{1.1761}{0.3010}\approx 3.9069\).

Step 3. \(x\approx 2.91\).

Worked Example — Quadratic substitution

Solve \(9^{x}-4\cdot 3^{x}+3=0\).

Step 1. Note \(9^{x}=(3^{x})^{2}\). Let \(u=3^{x}\): \(u^{2}-4u+3=0\).

Step 2. Factor: \((u-1)(u-3)=0\), so \(u=1\) or \(u=3\).

Step 3. Back-substitute: \(3^{x}=1\Rightarrow x=0\); \(3^{x}=3\Rightarrow x=1\).

  • Exponential expressions are always positive — reject any substitution solution \(u\le 0\).
  • When taking logs, you can use any base; the calculator's \(\log\) (base 10) or \(\ln\) (base \(e\)) is fine.
  • Always verify the final answer by substituting back into the original equation.

Curriculum expectation: Strand A, Expectation A3.1 — "recognize equivalent algebraic expressions involving logarithms and exponents, and simplify expressions of these types"; A3.3 — "solve exponential equations in one variable… by determining a common base, and by using logarithms."

7.2 — Solving Logarithmic Equations
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

A logarithmic equation has the variable inside a logarithm. The strategy is to convert the equation into a form you already know how to solve, using two main moves. If the equation looks like \(\log_{b}(\text{expression})=c\), convert it to exponential form: \(\text{expression}=b^{c}\). Then solve the resulting polynomial or rational equation. If the equation contains multiple logs on different sides, use the laws of logarithms to condense each side into a single log; then the one-to-one property says \(\log_{b}A=\log_{b}B\Rightarrow A=B\) (assuming \(A,B>0\)).

You must always state restrictions before solving and check every candidate against them at the end. Logarithms accept only positive arguments, so the algebra may produce a "valid" candidate that violates a restriction — that candidate is extraneous and must be rejected. Forgetting this check is the single most common cause of lost marks in Chapter 7.

For equations with logs of different bases, use the change-of-base formula to convert everything to a single base first.

  • Exponential conversion — \(\log_{b}A=c\Leftrightarrow A=b^{c}\); the basic move for log equations.
  • One-to-one property — \(\log_{b}A=\log_{b}B\) implies \(A=B\) (with both positive).
  • Extraneous solutions — candidates that satisfy the converted equation but violate the original log's domain.

Worked Example — Convert to exponential

Solve \(\log_{2}(x-3)=4\).

Step 1. Restriction: \(x-3>0\Rightarrow x>3\).

Step 2. Convert: \(x-3=2^{4}=16\).

Step 3. Solve: \(x=19\). Check restriction: \(19>3\). ✓

Worked Example — Condense then one-to-one

Solve \(\log(x+2)+\log(x-1)=\log 4\).

Step 1. Restrictions: \(x+2>0\) and \(x-1>0\), giving \(x>1\).

Step 2. Product Law: \(\log\!\bigl((x+2)(x-1)\bigr)=\log 4\).

Step 3. One-to-one: \((x+2)(x-1)=4\), so \(x^{2}+x-2=4\), giving \(x^{2}+x-6=0\).

Step 4. Factor: \((x+3)(x-2)=0\). Candidates \(x=-3,\,2\).

Step 5. Check restriction \(x>1\): reject \(-3\); accept \(2\). Solution: \(x=2\).

  • Always write restrictions before solving; checking them is non-negotiable.
  • The one-to-one property requires both sides to be single logarithms — condense first.
  • When converting \(\log_{b}A=c\) to exponential form, the result is \(A=b^{c}\), not \(b\cdot c\).

Curriculum expectation: Strand A, Expectation A3.2 — "solve simple logarithmic equations in one variable algebraically (e.g., \(\log_{3}(5x+6)=2\), \(\log_{6}(x+4)=2-\log_{6}(x-2)\))."

  • Conversion fluency — \(\log_{b}A=c\Leftrightarrow A=b^{c}\) in both directions.
  • Three laws — product, quotient, power; restrictions on each argument.
  • Equation toolkit — common base, take a log, exponential conversion, one-to-one.
  • Real-world scales — pH, Richter, decibels; interpret each unit change as a multiplicative factor.

Worked Example — Doubling-time application

A bacteria population doubles every 5 hours. If the initial population is 200, how long until it reaches 1500?

Step 1. Model: \(P(t)=200\cdot 2^{t/5}\). Set \(P(t)=1500\): \(200\cdot 2^{t/5}=1500\).

Step 2. Isolate: \(2^{t/5}=\dfrac{1500}{200}=7.5\).

Step 3. Take a log: \(\dfrac{t}{5}\log 2=\log 7.5\), so \(\dfrac{t}{5}=\dfrac{\log 7.5}{\log 2}\approx\dfrac{0.8751}{0.3010}\approx 2.907\).

Step 4. \(t\approx 14.5\) hours.

  • Watch the difference between \(\log\) and \(\ln\) when setting up; either works but be consistent.
  • Word problems often have a "round to a sensible value" instruction — note units (hours vs days) at the end.
  • Always check restrictions on log equations, even when the algebra looks clean.

8.1 — Sums and Differences of Functions
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Given two functions \(f\) and \(g\), the sum function is defined as \((f+g)(x)=f(x)+g(x)\), and the difference function as \((f-g)(x)=f(x)-g(x)\). These new functions have a domain equal to the intersection of the original domains: a value of \(x\) must be valid for both \(f\) and \(g\) before \(f+g\) can be evaluated there.

Combining functions lets you build complex models from simple components. A noisy signal might be modelled as a sinusoid plus a polynomial trend; a profit function is revenue minus cost; the height of a damped oscillator is exponential decay times sine. Understanding how to graph and analyse the combined function is the gateway to function composition in section 8.3 and to early calculus modelling.

To sketch \(f+g\) from the graphs of \(f\) and \(g\), use ordinate addition: at each \(x\), the height of \(f+g\) equals the sum of the heights of \(f\) and \(g\). Where one function is zero, the sum equals the other; where they are equal in magnitude but opposite in sign, the sum is zero.

  • Sum function — \((f+g)(x)=f(x)+g(x)\), with domain \(D_{f}\cap D_{g}\).
  • Difference function — \((f-g)(x)=f(x)-g(x)\), same domain rule.
  • Ordinate addition — graphical technique: add \(y\)-values at each \(x\).

Worked Example — Combine algebraically

For \(f(x)=x^{2}-1\) and \(g(x)=\sqrt{x}\), find \((f+g)(x)\), \((f-g)(x)\), and state the domain.

Step 1. \((f+g)(x)=x^{2}-1+\sqrt{x}\). \((f-g)(x)=x^{2}-1-\sqrt{x}\).

Step 2. Domain of \(f\): \(\mathbb{R}\). Domain of \(g\): \([0,\infty)\). Intersection: \([0,\infty)\).

Worked Example — Application

A company's revenue is \(R(x)=20x\) and cost is \(C(x)=0.5x^{2}+5x+50\). Find the profit function and the break-even points.

Step 1. Profit \(P(x)=R(x)-C(x)=20x-(0.5x^{2}+5x+50)=-0.5x^{2}+15x-50\).

Step 2. Break-even: \(P(x)=0\), so \(0.5x^{2}-15x+50=0\), or \(x^{2}-30x+100=0\).

Step 3. Quadratic formula: \(x=\dfrac{30\pm\sqrt{900-400}}{2}=\dfrac{30\pm\sqrt{500}}{2}=15\pm 5\sqrt{5}\approx 3.82,\,26.18\).

  • Domain of a sum is the intersection — both originals must be defined at \(x\).
  • Even and odd properties: sum of two even functions is even; sum of two odd is odd; sum of even and odd is neither.
  • Graphical sums can introduce new zeros where the two functions cancel each other.

Curriculum expectation: Strand D, Expectation D2.1 — "determine, through investigation using graphing technology, key features… of the graphs of functions created by adding, subtracting, multiplying, or dividing functions."

8.2 — Products and Quotients of Functions
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The product function is defined as \((fg)(x)=f(x)\cdot g(x)\), and the quotient function as \(\bigl(\tfrac{f}{g}\bigr)(x)=\dfrac{f(x)}{g(x)}\). The product's domain is the intersection \(D_{f}\cap D_{g}\). The quotient's domain is the intersection minus any values where \(g(x)=0\). These zeros of the denominator typically introduce vertical asymptotes or holes in the quotient — just as in Chapter 3.

Multiplying functions is the standard way to model phenomena that combine two effects multiplicatively. A damped oscillation \(y=e^{-t}\sin t\) is the product of exponential decay and a sinusoid; its envelope shrinks like \(e^{-t}\) while it oscillates inside. A package's variable price might be \(\text{(price per unit)}\times\text{(units sold)}\); seasonal patterns multiply trends in many applications.

Sign analysis of a product is straightforward: the product is positive where both factors share a sign, negative where their signs differ, zero where either factor is zero. This lets you sketch the product graph from the sign chart of its components.

  • Product function — \((fg)(x)=f(x)g(x)\); domain \(D_{f}\cap D_{g}\).
  • Quotient function — \(\bigl(\tfrac{f}{g}\bigr)(x)=\dfrac{f(x)}{g(x)}\); domain \(D_{f}\cap D_{g}\setminus\{x:g(x)=0\}\).
  • Sign of a product — positive when factors agree in sign, negative when they differ.

Worked Example — Product domain and zeros

For \(f(x)=x-2\) and \(g(x)=\sqrt{x+1}\), find \((fg)(x)\), state the domain, and find zeros.

Step 1. \((fg)(x)=(x-2)\sqrt{x+1}\).

Step 2. Domain of \(g\) requires \(x+1\ge 0\Rightarrow x\ge -1\). Domain of \(f\) is \(\mathbb{R}\). Intersection: \([-1,\infty)\).

Step 3. Zeros: either \(x-2=0\) or \(\sqrt{x+1}=0\), so \(x=2\) or \(x=-1\).

Worked Example — Quotient with restrictions

For \(f(x)=x^{2}-9\) and \(g(x)=x-3\), find \(\bigl(\tfrac{f}{g}\bigr)(x)\) in simplified form and state the domain.

Step 1. \(\dfrac{x^{2}-9}{x-3}=\dfrac{(x-3)(x+3)}{x-3}\).

Step 2. Cancel: simplified form is \(x+3\) for \(x\ne 3\).

Step 3. Domain: \(\{x\in\mathbb{R}\mid x\ne 3\}\). The graph is a line with a hole at \((3,6)\).

  • Always check denominator zeros — they restrict the quotient's domain even if they cancel.
  • Products of functions of opposite parity are usually neither even nor odd.
  • For damped oscillations, the envelope is the absolute value of the decaying factor.

Curriculum expectation: Strand D, Expectation D2.2 — "recognize real-world applications of combinations of functions… and solve related problems graphically."

8.3 — Composite Functions
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

The composite of two functions, written \((f\circ g)(x)=f\!\bigl(g(x)\bigr)\), means "apply \(g\) first, then apply \(f\) to the result". Composition is the deep operation that lets you build a function from simpler pieces: every transformation you have studied — \(y=af(k(x-d))+c\) — is a composition with linear inner and outer functions. Calculus, when you reach it, builds further: the chain rule is the differentiation rule for composites.

Composition is generally not commutative: \(f\circ g\) and \(g\circ f\) are usually different functions. The order matters because the inner function's output becomes the outer function's input, and "do A then B" is not the same as "do B then A" in most situations.

The domain of \(f\circ g\) requires two conditions: \(x\) must lie in the domain of \(g\), and \(g(x)\) must lie in the domain of \(f\). Skipping the second check is a frequent error and can produce extraneous values.

  • Composite function — \((f\circ g)(x)=f\!\bigl(g(x)\bigr)\); read right-to-left as "\(g\), then \(f\)".
  • Non-commutative — generally \(f\circ g\ne g\circ f\).
  • Domain of composite — \(\{x\in D_{g}\mid g(x)\in D_{f}\}\).
  • Identity composition — \(f\circ f^{-1}=f^{-1}\circ f=x\), the identity on the appropriate domain.

Worked Example — Compose two ways

Let \(f(x)=x^{2}+1\) and \(g(x)=2x-3\). Find \((f\circ g)(x)\) and \((g\circ f)(x)\).

Step 1. \((f\circ g)(x)=f(2x-3)=(2x-3)^{2}+1=4x^{2}-12x+9+1=4x^{2}-12x+10\).

Step 2. \((g\circ f)(x)=g(x^{2}+1)=2(x^{2}+1)-3=2x^{2}-1\).

Step 3. Different functions — confirms non-commutativity.

Worked Example — Domain of a composite

For \(f(x)=\sqrt{x}\) and \(g(x)=x-4\), find \((f\circ g)(x)\) and its domain.

Step 1. \((f\circ g)(x)=\sqrt{x-4}\).

Step 2. Domain of \(g\) is \(\mathbb{R}\); need \(g(x)\in D_{f}=[0,\infty)\), so \(x-4\ge 0\Rightarrow x\ge 4\).

Step 3. Domain: \([4,\infty)\).

  • Composition order matters — read \(f\circ g\) as "\(g\) first, then \(f\)".
  • Domain of a composite is not always the intersection of domains; check the inner function's output landing inside the outer's input set.
  • For inverse functions, composing them recovers the identity — a key diagnostic test.

Curriculum expectation: Strand D, Expectation D3.1 — "determine, through investigation, and explain some properties (i.e., odd, even, or neither; increasing/decreasing behaviours) of functions formed by adding, subtracting, multiplying, dividing, and composing two functions."

8.4 — Modeling with Combined Functions
📖 Lesson Notes — Ontario Curriculum

Real-world phenomena rarely match one of the six function families exactly. More often you need to combine several base functions — through addition, multiplication, or composition — to capture the behaviour. Modelling with combined functions is the culmination of MHF4U: every algebraic and graphical tool from Chapters 1 through 7 comes together here.

The modelling cycle has three stages. Identify the underlying behaviours: is there a trend (polynomial or exponential)? A periodic component (sinusoidal)? A multiplicative envelope (decay × oscillation)? Build a candidate model by combining the relevant base functions. Validate by checking the model against known data points and reasoning about long-run behaviour. Iterate as needed.

Common archetypes to recognise: damped oscillation (\(y=e^{-kt}\sin\omega t\)), seasonal trend (\(y=\text{linear}+\text{sinusoid}\)), profit (revenue minus cost), and probabilistic decay (\(y=\text{polynomial}\cdot\text{exponential}\)). Each maps cleanly to a combination operation you have studied.

  • Modelling cycle — identify behaviours, build a candidate, validate against data.
  • Damped oscillation — product of decay and sinusoid; amplitude shrinks toward zero.
  • Trend plus seasonality — sum of a slow function (linear, polynomial, exponential) and a periodic function.
  • Envelope — the graph of the slow factor of a product, bounding the oscillating factor's amplitude.

Worked Example — Damped oscillator

A pendulum's displacement from rest is modelled by \(d(t)=10\,e^{-0.1t}\cos(2t)\) cm with \(t\) in seconds. Find \(d(0)\), describe long-run behaviour, and predict the displacement at \(t=5\) s.

Step 1. \(d(0)=10\,e^{0}\cos 0=10\cdot 1\cdot 1=10\) cm.

Step 2. As \(t\to\infty\), \(e^{-0.1t}\to 0\), so \(d(t)\to 0\). The oscillation amplitude decays — the pendulum gradually stops.

Step 3. \(d(5)=10\,e^{-0.5}\cos 10\approx 10\cdot 0.6065\cdot(-0.8391)\approx -5.09\) cm.

Worked Example — Seasonal temperature model

A city's average monthly temperature is modelled by \(T(m)=15\sin\!\bigl(\tfrac{\pi}{6}(m-4)\bigr)+10\). Average temperature increases by 0.05°C/month due to climate change. Write a model for the temperature including the trend.

Step 1. Original model has midline 10. Add linear trend \(0.05m\) to make midline rise.

Step 2. New model: \(T(m)=15\sin\!\bigl(\tfrac{\pi}{6}(m-4)\bigr)+10+0.05m\).

Step 3. After 60 months: midline rises by \(0.05\cdot 60=3\) °C; seasonal oscillation continues with amplitude 15.

  • Always verify your model at a known point — if it does not match, the form is wrong.
  • Long-run behaviour comes from the dominant component (exponential beats polynomial beats sinusoidal).
  • Note units throughout — degrees, metres, seconds, and so on.

Curriculum expectation: Strand D, Expectation D3.3 — "solve problems involving the composition of two functions, including problems arising from real-world applications."

Worked Example — Cross-chapter synthesis
